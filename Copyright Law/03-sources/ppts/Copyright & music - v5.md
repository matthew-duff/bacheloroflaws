In the case of a song with lyrics, there will be two (and possibly more) forms of protected subject matter:
the lyrics – which are a literary work;
the music (i.e. sound, melody, harmony, rhythm) – which is a musical work; and
(if recorded) - a sound recording (protected under Part IV)
Any particular performance style is not covered by the copyright in the musical work

Musical Works 
What constitutes a musical work is an “abstract concept” – although it might be evidenced by a noted musical score or a sound recording, neither of these is the work itself: EMI Songs Australia Pty Ltd v Larrikin Music Publishing Pty Ltd (2011) 191 FCR 444 at [10], Boomerang Investments Pty Ltd v Padgett (Liability) (2020) 383 ALR 202 at [63]
While lyrics are separately protected as a literary work, the sound of those lyrics being sung is (probably? maybe?) part of the musical work: Boomerang at [65]-[77] per Perram J.

# Slide 8

8

Sound recordings
note that the exclusive right in relation to sound recordings is (amongst other things) the right ”to make a copy of the sound recording”.  
Deliberate replication of the sound of someone else’s sound recording does NOT amount to making a copy of the sound recording itself: CBS Records Australia Ltd v Telmak Teleproducts (Aust) Pty Ltd (1987) 79 ALR 604
This highlights the narrow nature of Part IV protection (does not protect the underlying work – have to rely on Part III protections for that) - Part IV protection is only against copying of the actual sound recording, film etc

Part IV – subject matter other than works

LAWS5206: Copyright Law

# Slide 9

9

Sound recordings
When a record label wants to make a sound recording of a song, it needs to obtain the copyright permission of the owner of the copyright in the relevant musical work and, if any, the lyrics (which are a literary work)
When the record label creates a record by recording an artist perform the song, the record label owns the copyright in the sound recording (unless there is another contractual arrangement)
This is a narrow (Part IV) copyright – only prevents others from copying a substantial part of that actual recording
Someone else can make another, similar sounding, recording without infringing the copyright in the first sound recording (though it might infringe the copyright in the underlying musical work or literary work)

On the issue of causal connection, the Court said:
legal burden rests with the person alleging infringement but … the evidential burden shifts to the alleged infringer if there is proof of sufficient similarity and proof of access: at [24];
in every case, it must be a question of fact and degree whether the extent of an alleged infringer’s access to the original work, combined with the extent of the similarities, raises a sufficient possibility of copying to shift the evidential burden: at [25].
But, in any event, infringement requires there to have been actual copying – and that requires actual access.  
The Court must therefore be satisfied that there was actual access (which might be indirect) – but such actual access might be inferred from a combination of objective similarity and the possibility of access – and it may not be necessary to prove the actual method of access
