# Slide 5

Copyright Act protects two broad categories of subject matter:
Part III: “works” – these include literary, artistic, dramatic and musical works –note “literary works” includes computer programs: s 10
Part IV: “subject matter other than works” – sound recordings, films, television & sound broadcasts, published editions of works

Subject matter must fall into one of these two categories to be protected
There is also separate protection for performers’ rights in their live performances

Subsistence – what is protectable?

5

# Slide 6

“Subsistence” just means that copyright exists in the relevant subject matter.
Note that subsistence and ownership are presumed, unless contested: s 126.
If subject matter is protected, that protection has a scope and a duration
SCOPE – refers to the extent of the exclusive rights – that is, what can the copyright owner prevent others from doing?
DURATION – how long does the copyright last? 
Both of these depend on the nature of the subject matter.
Remember that (unlike some other jurisdictions) there is no need to register copyright in Australia – copyright simply exists (or doesn’t exist) from the time of creation of the relevant subject matter

# Slide 7

If the relevant material falls within one of the categories of Part III or Part IV, there are 4 requirements for copyright protection:
there must be a relevant connection to Australia (also called ‘jurisdictional attachment’);
must be original (Part III) or not copied (Part IV);
must be reduced to material form (Part III and most Part IV); and
must be sufficiently substantial. 

If all these requirements are met, copyright protection arises automatically upon creation (c.f. patents, designs, trade marks)

Requirements for protection

# Slide 8

In order to attract protection:
The author or maker must be a “qualified person”: ss 32, 89, 90, 92, 189 (definition of “performer”), 191B; OR
The work or other subject matter must have been first published in Australia: ss 89-92; OR
In the case of sound recordings, films and broadcasts, that they are made in Australia: ss 89, 90, 91
Reciprocity rules generally mean that a work protected in any Berne Convention country (or other relevant treaty) will also qualify for protection in Australia:        s 184 and Copyright (International Protection) Regulations 1969 (Cth)

Section 32 requires works to be “original”

# Slide 11

Originality (continued)

11

This requirement first introduced by the Copyright Act 1911 (UK) – adopted by the 1912 Australian leg’n
“Original” is not defined in the Act 
The terms “original” and “author”, as used in the Act, are “correlative”.  A work is “original” when it “originates” from the author in the sense of not being copied from elsewhere: see Sands & McDougal v Robinson (1917) 23 CLR 49 at 54-55 per Isaacs J 
Does not have to be “novel” or “inventive” in the patent sense.

# Slide 12

Originality (continued)

12

IceTV Pty Ltd v Nine Network Australia Pty Ltd (2009) 239 CLR 458 at [33] per French CJ, Crennan and Kiefel JJ:

Originality for this purpose requires that the literary work in question originated with the author and that it was not merely copied from another work. It is the author or joint authors who bring into existence the work protected by the Act. In that context, originality means that the creation (that is, the production) of the work required some independent intellectual effort, but neither literary merit nor novelty or inventiveness as required in patent law

# Slide 18

Originality (continued) – what about compilations of facts?

18

Copyright protects forms of expression, not facts or ideas
BUT a particular compilation of facts – e.g. in a timetable, map or set of sporting results – can be original in the required sense.
Generally, some (low) degree of skill, effort and judgment in selection, compilation, collocation or arrangement is required which produces ”some original result”: Victoria Park Racing at 511 per Dixon J.
This form of originality, however, is generally regarded as quite “thin” – there is no protection for the underlying facts – infringement will require close copying of the compilation

# Slide 47

Idea/information v expression of the idea/information

47

One of the key concepts in copyright law
Easy to understand, sometimes very hard to apply

Copyright does not protect facts or ideas – it only protects the particular way in which that information is expressed

# Slide 48

Idea/information v expression of the idea/information

48

Baker v Selden, 101 US 99 (1879)
Plaintiff sought to assert copyright in a particular system of bookkeeping 
Bradley J: But there is a clear distinction between the book as such and the art which it is intended to illustrate.  
Copyright protects the particular expression of the idea, but not the idea itself – the latter is the province of patent protection
Baker would be decided the same way in Australia in 2026.

# Slide 49

Idea/information v expression of the idea/information

49

This is usually most relevant to the question of infringement…
See Krueger Transport Equipment Pty Ltd v Glen Cameron Storage & Distribution Pty Ltd (2008) 78 IPR 262 (appeal dismissed (2009) 83 IPR 1) at [123] per Gordon J, referring to Baker and, at [130]:
The claim of copyright over the Krueger sketch… fails at a more basic level. While the Krueger sketch and the Vawdrey drawings both depict one or more sliding side posts with “things hanging off them”, the form in which they are both expressed differs significantly. They do not look similar; one is a hand drawn sketch of very few lines showing a concept whereas the other is a detailed technical drawing expressing that concept in a practical form. Krueger’s claim of copyright infringement amounts more to a claim for the appropriation of the concept of Slide-A-Gate. Again, however, there is no copyright protection for concepts, and thus no breach of copyright subsisting in the Krueger sketch. To put it another way, because the Krueger sketch expresses little more than the concept (which is unprotected), very little of the sketch is protected.

# Slide 57

Feist Publications Inc v Rural Telephone Service Co Inc (1991) 499 US 340 (cont)

57

On appeal, SCOTUS noted that the case concerned the interaction of two well-established principles:
Facts are not copyrightable; and
Compilations of facts generally are copyrightable

The key to resolving the tension lies in understanding why facts are not copyrightable

The answer lies in originality.  The facts do not originate from the author of the compilation.

# Slide 58

Feist Publications Inc v Rural Telephone Service Co Inc (1991) 499 US 340 (cont)

58

Compilations of facts may have a degree of originality – e.g. the author chooses which facts to include, how to arrange them etc – but this does not extend to the underlying facts themselves
Only the original part is protected
Subsequent compilers are free to take the facts

Court then reviewed jurisprudential history of the compilation cases in the US and dismantled the “sweat of the brow” doctrine

# Slide 59

Feist Publications Inc v Rural Telephone Service Co Inc (1991) 499 US 340 (cont)

59

On the facts of the case at hand:
Copyright in the directories as a whole was admitted
BUT the Court held that no original element had been taken
What had been taken (1,309 names, towns and telephone numbers) did not originate from Rural and was therefore unprotectable raw data
Rural’s selection, coordination and arrangement of its white pages did not meet the minimum requirements for protection:
“The end product is a garden-variety white pages directory, devoid of even the slightest trace of creativity”

# Slide 60

Desktop Marketing Systems Pty Ltd v Telstra Corporation Limited (2002) 119 FCR 491

60

Australian rerun of Feist, 10 year later
Desktop produced directories on CD-ROM using Telstra’s data
Telstra sued for copyright infringement & was successful at first instance
FCAFC held, dismissing the appeal, that Telstra’s white pages and yellow pages directories were original compilations in which copyright subsists
