## AI Summary

**Feist v Rural Telephone** (US Supreme Court) holds that **white pages telephone directories are not copyrightable** because they lack the **originality** required by the Constitution and the Copyright Act. **Facts are not copyrightable**; compilations of facts are only protected to the extent they display **original selection, coordination, or arrangement**. Effort alone (“sweat of the brow”) does not create copyright.

- **Issue**: Whether Rural could sue Feist for copying names, towns, and telephone numbers from Rural’s white pages for Feist’s area-wide directory.
- **Holding**: Rural’s white pages are **not** entitled to copyright. Feist’s use did **not** infringe.
- **Reasoning**: 
- (1) **Facts** (names, towns, numbers) do not “owe their origin” to an author—they are discovered, not created—so they are uncopyrightable. 
- (2) A **compilation** is only copyrightable if the facts are “selected, coordinated, or arranged” in an **original** way (17 USC § 101). (3) Rural’s “selection” was to list every subscriber (and was **required by state law**); the **arrangement** was **alphabetical by surname**—a routine, expected choice with no “minimal creative spark.” 
- (4) The Court **rejected the “sweat of the brow” / “industrious collection”** doctrine: copyright rewards **originality**, not labour. 
- (5) **Thin copyright**: even when a compilation is protected, only the **selection and arrangement** are protected; the **facts may be copied** by others who use a different selection/arrangement.
- **Takeaway**: Key US authority that **originality** (independent creation + minimal creativity) is constitutionally required; **alphabetical listing of raw factual data** does not meet that standard. Often cited in Australian discussions of **IceTV** and originality in compilations.

## Facts

- **Rural** was a telephone utility in Kansas, required by state regulation to publish an annual directory (white pages + yellow pages). White pages listed subscribers’ names, towns, and numbers in alphabetical order.
- **Feist** published area-wide directories covering multiple service areas. It sought licences from 11 telephone companies; **Rural refused**. Feist used Rural’s white pages without consent, verifying and adding data (e.g. street addresses). **1,309 listings** in Feist’s directory were identical to Rural’s (including four fictitious “trap” listings Rural had inserted).
- Rural sued for **copyright infringement**. District Court and Tenth Circuit held the directory copyrightable and ruled for Rural. Supreme Court granted certiorari.

## Issues

- Are telephone directory white pages (names, towns, telephone numbers) copyrightable?
- Does Feist’s use of that information constitute infringement?

## Holding

- Rural’s **white pages are not entitled to copyright**.
- Feist’s use of the listings **does not constitute infringement**.
- **Reversed** (Court of Appeals).

## Reasoning

- **Originality is constitutionally required** (Art I § 8 cl 8): “authors” and “writings” presuppose **independent creation** plus a **modicum of creativity**. Facts do not owe their origin to authorship; they are discovered, not created. **Compilations** of facts may be copyrightable only if the **selection, coordination, or arrangement** of the facts is **original** (independently made and minimally creative).
- **Copyright Act 1976**: § 102(a) protects “original works of authorship”; § 102(b) excludes ideas, facts, etc.; § 101 defines “compilation” as a work in which pre-existing materials or data are “selected, coordinated, or arranged” so that the work “as a whole constitutes an **original** work of authorship.” **“Sweat of the brow”** (rewarding effort alone) was rejected; the statute envisions that some compilations lack sufficient originality.
- **Application**: Rural’s raw data are uncopyrightable facts. The “selection” (all subscribers, as required by law) and “arrangement” (alphabetical by surname) lacked any minimal creative spark—they were obvious and routine. Therefore the white pages did not meet constitutional or statutory requirements for copyright. Feist copied nothing that was “original” to Rural.

## Ratio / Takeaways

- **Facts** are never copyrightable; **compilations** are protected only to the extent of **original selection, coordination, or arrangement**.
- **Originality** = independent creation + minimal creativity (low but non-zero threshold).
- **“Sweat of the brow”** is not the touchstone; copyright rewards **originality**, not effort.
- **Thin protection**: copyright in a compilation does **not** extend to the facts themselves; others may use those facts in a different selection/arrangement.
- **Alphabetical listing** of factual data (e.g. names and numbers) is not sufficiently original.
