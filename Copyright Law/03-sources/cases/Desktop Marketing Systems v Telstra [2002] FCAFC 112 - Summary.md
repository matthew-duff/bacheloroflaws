Full judgment: [[Desktop Marketing Systems Pty Ltd v Telstra Corporation Limited [2002] FCAFC 112]]

**Court:** Full Court of the Federal Court of Australia (Black CJ, Lindgren J, Sackville J)  
**Date:** 15 May 2002  
**Result:** Appeal dismissed; Desktop to pay Telstra’s costs.

---

## 1. Overview

Telstra sued Desktop for infringing copyright in its **White Pages** and **Yellow Pages** directories and in its **Headings Books**. Desktop had copied listing data (names, addresses, telephone numbers and, from Yellow Pages, headings) from those works into its CD‑ROM products (Marketing Pro, Australian Phone Disk, CD Phone Directory). The Full Court upheld the primary judge’s findings that copyright subsisted in the directories and headings books and that Desktop had infringed that copyright.

---

## 2. Facts in brief

- **Telstra** publishes White Pages and Yellow Pages for 55 regions and maintains unpublished Headings Books used to classify Yellow Pages. It spends large sums on systems (e.g. Axis, Condor), editing, verification and production.
- **Desktop** took data from the directory sections of the White and Yellow Pages (not information pages, ads or artwork), had it keyed in overseas, and stored it in databases on CD‑ROMs. The CD‑ROMs allowed searching and display by postcode, name, “line of business” (headings), etc., rather than in the same page layout as the printed directories. Desktop admitted copying from the directories and using Telstra’s headings (about 95% identical).
- The directories are **“whole-of-universe”** compilations: all subscribers in each region (except silent numbers), with no selection, and effectively only one obvious arrangement (alphabetical by name).

---

## 3. Issues

1. **Subsistence of copyright (originality)**  
   For compilations of factual information, does the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth) require:
   - only **labour and expense** (“industrious collection”), or  
   - some **intellectual effort / creativity** in selection, arrangement or form of expression?

2. **If copyright subsists**  
   Did Telstra’s labour and expense make the White Pages, Yellow Pages and Headings Books **original literary works**?

3. **Infringement**  
   Did Desktop **reproduce a substantial part** of those works when it put the data (and headings) into its CD‑ROMs, even though the storage format and presentation (e.g. by postcode, on screen) differed from the printed directories?

---

## 4. Holdings

### Subsistence of copyright (originality)

- **First issue:** The requirement of **originality** for a literary work (including a compilation) under Australian law is satisfied by **substantial labour and expense** in collecting, verifying, recording and assembling the data. It is **not** necessary that there be a “creative spark” or intellectual effort in selection, arrangement or mode of expression. The Court declined to follow the US approach in *Feist Publications Inc v Rural Telephone Service Co Inc* 499 US 340 (1991), which requires a minimal level of creativity in selection or arrangement.
- **Second issue:** Telstra’s labour and expense (systems, editing, verification, compilation, Headings Committee work, etc.) were sufficient. The White Pages, Yellow Pages and Headings Books were **original literary works** in which copyright subsisted. That conclusion was not altered by: statutory obligations to publish; free distribution; monopoly position; or the fact that, in practice, others could not replicate the data without copying Telstra.
- **Headings Books:** Copyright also subsisted in the Headings Books based on the labour and expense of creating and maintaining the compilation of headings (and by analogy to *Lamb v Evans* [1892] 3 Ch 462).

### Infringement

- Desktop had **reproduced a substantial part** of each of Telstra’s copyright works. The “substantial part” inquiry focuses on the **quality** (including originality) of what is taken, not merely quantity. Here, the originality of the works lay in the labour of compiling the data (and headings); Desktop took that compiled information in bulk.
- **Reproduction** does not require **visual similarity** between the copyright work and the alleged infringement. Section 10(1) defines “material form” to include storage (including electronic) from which the work or a substantial part can be reproduced. Desktop’s CD‑ROMs stored the same listing data and effectively the same headings; users could reproduce from the CD‑ROMs alphabetical listings closely corresponding to the directories. That was enough for reproduction in a material form and for infringement.

---

## 5. Key legal points

- **Originality in compilations (Australia):** Anglo-Australian authority (e.g. *Kelly v Morris*, *Morris v Ashbee*, *Walter v Lane*, *Sands & McDougall v Robinson*, *Ladbroke v William Hill*, *Waterlow v Rose*) supports copyright in factual compilations based on **industrious collection** — labour and expense in gathering and compiling facts — even where there is no selection, no creativity in arrangement (e.g. alphabetical listing), and no scope for different expression. *Feist* and *Tele-Direct* (Canada) were distinguished or not followed as depending on different statutory and constitutional contexts.
- **Substantial part:** Assessed by the **importance** of the part taken to the work (e.g. *Autodesk Inc v Dyason (No 2)*). For these compilations, the compiled data (and headings) were the essential, original features; taking them in bulk was taking a substantial part.
- **Reproduction / material form:** For compilations, the focus is on whether the **information** that constitutes the compilation (or a substantial part of it) has been reproduced, not on whether the infringing product looks like the original. Electronic storage from which the same information can be reproduced is reproduction in a “material form” (s 10(1)).

---

## 6. Significance

- **“Industrious collection”** remains the standard in Australia for originality of factual compilations: substantial labour and expense can suffice without creativity or intellectual effort in selection or arrangement.
- **Feist** is not applied in Australia for compilations; the “sweat of the brow” / industrious collection line of authority is preferred.
- **Infringement** of compilation copyright can occur when the defendant takes the compiled data and stores it in a different format (e.g. database/CD‑ROM) and presents it differently (e.g. by postcode, on screen); visual similarity to the original is not essential.

---

## 7. Legislation

- *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth): ss 8, 10(1) (“literary work”, “material form”), 14(1)(b), 31(1)(a)(i), 32(1)–(2), 35(2), 36(1).

## 8. Key cases cited

- *Feist Publications Inc v Rural Telephone Service Co Inc* 499 US 340 (1991) — considered, not followed (US “creativity” standard).
- *Kelly v Morris* (1866) LR 1 Eq 697; *Morris v Ashbee* (1868) LR 7 Eq 34 — industrious collection.
- *Walter v Lane* [1900] AC 539 — labour/skill sufficient for authorship.
- *Sands & McDougall Pty Ltd v Robinson* (1917) 23 CLR 49 — “original” not inventive.
- *Ladbroke (Football) Ltd v William Hill (Football) Ltd* [1964] 1 WLR 273 — substantial part.
- *Data Access Corporation v Powerflex Services Pty Ltd* (1999) 202 CLR 1 — referred to on originality/substantial part.
- *Autodesk Inc v Dyason (No 2)* (1993) 176 CLR 300 — substantial part.
- *Lamb v Evans* [1892] 3 Ch 462 — copyright in directory headings.
