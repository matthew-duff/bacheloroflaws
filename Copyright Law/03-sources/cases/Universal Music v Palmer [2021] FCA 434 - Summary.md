## AI Summary

**Universal Music v Palmer** (Federal Court, Katzmann J) holds that Clive Palmer infringed copyright in Twisted Sister’s song “We’re Not Gonna Take It” by using a **strikingly similar** campaign song “Aussies Not Gonna Cop It” for the United Australia Party. The Court applied a **qualitative** substantial-part test from the perspective of an ordinary reasonably experienced listener, found the chorus was an **original** and qualitatively important part of the work (rejecting Palmer’s argument that it copied “O Come, All Ye Faithful”), and rejected defences of **parody** and **satire**. Palmer was ordered to pay **$500,000** compensatory damages and **$1,000,000** **additional damages** for **flagrant** infringement—having sought a licence, been quoted a fee and refused, and used the work anyway.

- **Issue**: Did Palmer’s “Aussies Not Gonna Cop It” reproduce a **substantial part** of “We’re Not Gonna Take It,” and were Palmer’s defences made out?
- **Holding**: Yes—infringement. Defences rejected. **$1.5m** total damages ($500k compensatory + $1m additional).
- **Reasoning**: (1) **Substantial part** is assessed **qualitatively** (structure, melody, harmony, prominence to an ordinary listener), not by note-for-note quantity. (2) The chorus was **indisputably original** and a key part of the work; Palmer’s “O Come, All Ye Faithful” argument failed. (3) Minor lyrical changes (“we’re not gonna take it” → “Aussies not gonna cop it”) were **immaterial**. (4) **Parody/satire** defence did not apply on the facts. (5) **Flagrancy** justified substantial additional damages under the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth).
- **Takeaway**: Important on **substantial part** in musical works (qualitative test, ordinary listener), **originality** as a bar to infringement, limits of **parody/satire** defences, and **additional damages** for flagrant infringement.

## Facts

- **“We’re Not Gonna Take It” (WNGTI)** was composed by Dee Snider (Twisted Sister) in 1984. **Universal Music Publishing** held the Australian copyright in the musical and literary works.
- **Clive Palmer** (United Australia Party) authorised creation and use of **“Aussies Not Gonna Cop It” (ANGCI)** in UAP campaign advertisements in 2018. The music and lyrics were noticeably similar to WNGTI, especially the **chorus**.
- Palmer’s team **sought a licence** from Universal; they were quoted **$150,000** plus approval from Snider. Palmer **declined** and used the song anyway.
- Universal sued for **copyright infringement**, alleging ANGCI reproduced a **substantial part** of WNGTI. Palmer argued (1) the chorus of WNGTI was **not original** because it copied the musical contour of “O Come, All Ye Faithful,” and (2) copying it therefore did not amount to reproducing a substantial part.

## Issues

- Did ANGCI reproduce a **substantial part** of the musical and/or literary work in WNGTI?
- Was the chorus of WNGTI **original** (or did it copy “O Come, All Ye Faithful” so that taking it did not take “original” expression)?
- Did a **parody or satire** defence apply?
- What **remedies** (including additional damages for flagrancy) were appropriate?

## Holding

- **Copyright was infringed**: ANGCI reproduced a **substantial part** of WNGTI.
- Palmer’s **originality** argument was **rejected**: the chorus was original and qualitatively important.
- **Parody/satire** defence was **rejected**.
- **Damages**: **$500,000** compensatory; **$1,000,000** additional damages for **flagrant** infringement (**$1.5m** total).

## Reasoning

- **Substantial part**: Assessed **qualitatively**, not by counting bars or notes. The court considered structure, melody, harmony and other features from the perspective of an **ordinary reasonably experienced listener**. The chorus was the most prominent feature of both works; the two songs were **strikingly similar**. It was “ludicrous to suggest” ANGCI was independently created.
- **Originality**: Palmer’s argument that the WNGTI chorus copied “O Come, All Ye Faithful” was rejected. The chorus was **indisputably original** and a qualitatively significant part of the work. Copying it therefore reproduced a substantial part of original expression.
- **Lyrical changes**: Replacing “we’re not gonna take it” with “Aussies not gonna cop it” was **immaterial** and did not avoid infringement.
- **Parody/satire**: The statutory defence for parody or satire did not apply on the facts; the use was not accepted as parody or satire in the circumstances.
- **Additional damages**: Palmer’s **deliberate** conduct—seeking a licence, being refused on clear terms, and using the work anyway—supported a finding of **flagrant** infringement and justified **$1m additional damages** under the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth).

## Ratio / Takeaways

- **Substantial part** (musical works): qualitative assessment; ordinary reasonably experienced listener; prominence and importance of the part taken (e.g. iconic chorus).
- **Originality**: even if a defendant argues the part taken was “unoriginal,” the court may find it original and qualitatively important; that argument failed here.
- **Parody/satire**: statutory defences are construed and applied on the facts; political campaign use of a near-copy did not attract the defence.
- **Remedies**: **additional damages** for **flagrancy** (s 115(4) *Copyright Act 1968*) can significantly increase the award where the defendant knowingly infringes after being refused a licence.
