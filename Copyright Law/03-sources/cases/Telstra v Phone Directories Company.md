Full judgment: [[Telstra Corporation Limited v Phone Directories Company Pty Ltd [2010] FCAFC 149]]

**Court:** Full Court of the Federal Court of Australia (Keane CJ, Perram J, Yates J)  
**Date:** 15 December 2010  
**Result:** Appeal dismissed; Telstra/Sensis to pay respondents’ costs.

---

## 1. Overview

Telstra and Sensis (publishers of the White Pages and Yellow Pages directories) appealed from a primary judge’s finding that **copyright did not subsist** in the relevant White Pages and Yellow Pages compilations. The Full Court **dismissed the appeal**, holding that the directories were not “original” literary works because they were brought into existence in their material form by an **automated process**, not by a human author (or authors) exercising sufficient independent intellectual effort or effort of a literary nature. The Court **followed *IceTV*** and effectively **overruled *Desktop Marketing*** (2002) to the extent it had held that labour and expense in collecting and assembling data alone could establish originality in a compilation.

---

## 2. Facts in brief

- **Telstra/Sensis** publish White Pages Directories (WPDs) and Yellow Pages Directories (YPDs) for multiple Australian regions. Production involved: (1) a **Collection Phase** — maintaining and updating a database (Genesis) with listing information, with significant human input (editors, sales, quality control); (2) an **Extraction Phase** — a largely **automated** “Book Extract” routine that extracted data from the database and produced a “galley file” (the form in which the directory would appear); (3) a **Production Phase** — typesetting and printing.
- The **galley file** was created almost entirely by software. Publishing co-ordinators (e.g. Vormwald and Cooper) ran the Book Extract routine by selecting parameters (year, region, product code); they did not shape or direct the content or arrangement of the directory. The primary judge found the production of the galley file was “almost entirely automated.”
- **Phone Directories Company** and others (respondents) had been sued by Telstra/Sensis. The separate question of whether copyright subsisted in the directories was determined first; the primary judge (Gordon J) held it did not.

---

## 3. Issues

1. **Subsistence of copyright (originality)**  
   Are the WPD and YPD compilations **original** literary works under the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth)?

2. **Authorship and material form**  
   Must the **author’s** effort be directed at the **creation of the material form** of the work (the form of the directory), or is labour and expense in the **collection** of the data sufficient?

3. **Computer-generated form**  
   Where the “work” (the compilation in the form in which it is published) is produced by an automated computer process, with humans only setting high-level parameters, is there a **human author** for the purposes of the Act?

4. **Section 128 presumption**  
   Where a work is first published in Australia by an identified publisher, does s 128 presume copyright subsists, and was the “contrary” established on the evidence?

---

## 4. Holdings

### Subsistence of copyright (originality)

- **Copyright did not subsist** in the WPD and YPD compilations. They were not “original” literary works because there was no **human author** who brought the work into existence in its **material form** with the required **independent intellectual effort** or **sufficient effort of a literary nature** (*IceTV*).
- **Authorship and material form:** The effort that counts for authorship must be directed at the **reduction of the work into material form** (the particular form of expression), not merely at gathering or organising the underlying data. Labour and expense in the Collection Phase may show the work was not *copied*, but they do not establish that a human *authored* the work in the sense of creating its material form. *Desktop Marketing* was **overruled** on this point as inconsistent with *IceTV* (Perram J at [671]).
- **Computer-generated form:** The directories first took material form when the **galley file** was generated by the Book Extract routine. That process was almost entirely automated. The persons who ran the software (e.g. Vormwald and Cooper) did not “shape or guide” the material form; they set only high-level parameters (year, region). Controlling software that performs the creative assembly is not enough where the person does not direct or fashion the material form — “a plane with its autopilot engaged is flying itself.” No human author of the material form could be identified; therefore the works were not “original” and copyright did not subsist (Perram J).
- **Section 128:** The presumption (copyright subsists where first publication in Australia by identified publisher) did not apply because the **contrary was established**: the evidence proved on the balance of probabilities that copyright did not subsist (no human author of the material form). Section 128 does not create a presumption that competes with actual evidence; it dictates an outcome only “unless the contrary is established.”

### Identification of authors

- The Act does not require that each author be **named**; it requires that there be an **author or authors** (i.e. that such persons exist). Here, the evidence showed that no human exercised sufficient authorial effort in creating the material form, so there was no author.

---

## 5. Key legal points

- **Originality and authorship (*IceTV*):** For a literary work (including a compilation), “original” means **originated** by an **author** — a human who brings the work into existence in its **material form** with **independent intellectual effort** or **sufficient effort of a literary nature**. The author must be a natural person (s 33, duration by reference to author’s life).
- **Material form:** Copyright protects the **form** of the work, not the ideas or information. Effort in gathering data (Collection Phase) is relevant only to show the work was not *copied*; it does not by itself constitute authorship of the *material form*. *Desktop Marketing*’s “industrious collection” approach is inconsistent with *IceTV* and was overruled.
- **Computer-generated works:** Where the material form is produced by software and the human operator only sets parameters without shaping or directing the form, that person is not the “author.” Australian law has no equivalent to the UK’s s 9(3) Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 (computer-generated works). If no human authored the material form, copyright does not subsist.
- **Section 128:** “Unless the contrary is established” means unless it is proved on the balance of probabilities that copyright does not subsist. Once the evidence establishes that, the presumption is not enlivened.

---

## 6. Significance

- **Reversal of *Desktop Marketing*:** The Full Court explicitly **overruled *Desktop Marketing*** (2002) insofar as it held that labour and expense in collecting, verifying and assembling data could by themselves establish originality. After *IceTV* and this case, **authorship** must be directed at the **creation of the material form** of the work.
- **Phone directories and automation:** Where directories are produced by heavily automated systems (database → automated extract → galley file), with humans only maintaining data and running programs without shaping the final form, **copyright may not subsist** in the resulting compilations. This contrasts sharply with *Desktop Marketing*, where the same type of directories were held to attract copyright.
- **IceTV applied:** The decision confirms that *IceTV* governs originality and authorship for compilations: focus on **material form**, **human author**, and **effort directed at that form**, not “sweat of the brow” in collection alone.

## 8. Key cases cited

- ***IceTV Pty Limited v Nine Network Australia Pty Limited*** (2009) 239 CLR 458 — **followed** (originality, authorship, material form, independent intellectual effort).
- ***Desktop Marketing Systems Pty Ltd v Telstra Corporation Ltd*** (2002) 119 FCR 491 — **overruled** (to the extent it held industrious collection alone could establish originality).
- ***Walter v Lane*** [1900] AC 539; ***Sands & McDougall Pty Ltd v Robinson*** (1917) 23 CLR 49; ***Ladbroke (Football) Ltd v William Hill (Football) Ltd*** [1964] 1 WLR 273 — cited on originality/authorship.
- ***Kelly v Morris*** (1866) LR 1 Eq 697; ***Morris v Ashbee*** (1868) LR 7 Eq 34 — cited (but *Desktop* approach rejected).
- ***Feist Publications Inc v Rural Telephone Service Co Inc*** (1991) 499 US 340 — cited (US “creativity” standard; not applied in Australia).
- ***Microsoft Corporation v PC Club Australia Pty Ltd*** (2005) 67 IPR 262 — “established” = proved on balance of probabilities (s 128).
- ***Waterlow Publishers Ltd v Rose*** (1989) 17 IPR 493 — s 128 and compilations.
- ***Farah Constructions Pty Ltd v Say-Dee Pty Ltd*** (2007) 230 CLR 89 — intermediate appellate court bound by High Court dicta.
