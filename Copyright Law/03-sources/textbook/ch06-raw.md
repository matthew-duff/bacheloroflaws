Direct copyright infringement of copyright can be understood as requiring

number of exclusive rights attached to different subject matter varies markedly

within Part III and Part IV. Literary, dramatic and musical works receive the
most exclusive rights, seven; sound recordings have four; artistic works and
cinematograph films have three; broadcasts (sound and television) have two
and published editions merely one.

While exclusive rights are sometimes expressed in slightly different ways
(most notably “to reproduce” for Part III and “to make a copy” for Part IV
sound recordings and cinematograph films) these variations reflect the more
fundamental distinctions between the Parts explained in Chapter 5. For the
purpose of explanation below, like exclusive rights will be explained with like
regardless of whether they are attached to Part III or Part IV subject matter. That
is because many of the contours of these like exclusive rights are essentially the
same (notwithstanding linguistic variation) so long as regard is given to the

difference in application of the derivation and substantial part requirements
as between Part III works and Part IV other subject matter.

Finally, it should be noted that the nature of exclusive rights and subject
matter parameters taken together define the proprietary interest for any copy-
right owner or plaintiff. Critically, anything done in relation to subject matter
that does not implicate an exclusive right is outside the scope of the copyright
property. Privately reading out loud a literary work does not implicate any
exclusive right, whereas reading the same work out loud before a paying
audience will implicate the performance in public right. An exclusive right in
copyright is the right to exclude the whole world from the designated activity
in relation to a substantial part of the subject matter to which that right is
attached. As a personal property right it is a right higher in order than a purely
contractual right or a non-proprietary tort right, and lower in rank thana right
in real property. Because copyright is property it has boundaries: it is not a
system of protection by “wide generalization” ®

6.2 To reproduce / To copy / To film or record a
broadcast / To make a facsimile copy

Each copyright subject matter recognised in Parts III and IV of the 1968 Act has
aright attached to exclude others from making, however expressed, a physical
copy. This is the most basic and cardinal exclusive right, which was the founda-
tion of the Statute of Anne there expressed for the authors of books as the “sole
right and liberty of printing” ° An aspect of the 2017 reforms was the insertion
in Part IVA (which rationalised exceptions for persons with a disability; librar-
ies, archives and key cultural institutions; and educational institutions) of a
new expression “copyright material” for that Part. This is defined in s 10(1) to
mean “anything in which copyright subsists”. Consequential to the adoption
of the term “copyright material” for Part IVA, the term “copy” was defined in
s 10(1) to refer to both a reproduction of a work and a copy of subject-matter
other than works - i.e. the physical result of an exercise of the exclusive right in
issue.” With one exception all existing provisions of relevant exclusive rights
as they related to the different subject matter types were inscribed into the
“copy” definition, so that no substantive reform was intended. For reasons
not entirely clear, while published edition was included within the s 10(1)
“copyright material” definition, its exclusive right of “to make a facsimile

an

copy” was omitted from the s 10(1) definition of “copy”.

The provisions creating liability for dealings in articles the making
of which “constituted an infringement of the copyright” - discussed in
Chapter 7 (see: 7.5) - sit largely as adjuncts to these reproduction and copy-
ing rights.” This connection is a foundational feature of statutory copyright.
So that the Statute of Anne, aside from conferring on the authors of books
“the sole right and liberty of printing”, extended infringement liability upon
those selling books known to have been printed “without the consent of the
proprietors”.

6.2.1 Degree of exactitude

A salient distinction between Part III and Part IV in relation to all exclusive
rights is that an act may be in respect to a substantial part of Part III subject
matter without mechanistic taking, whereas for Part IV the doing of an act
to a substantial part requires firstly that act is done in respect of the actual
images or sounds or in the case of the published edition, as a facsimile.‘ While
this distinguishes all Part III exclusive rights from those in Part IV, it is a
distinction that can sometimes be easiest to understand in cases of physical
copying. Indeed the very language of the cardinal exclusive right reflects this
substantive difference. In Part III for all works the exclusive right concept
is expressed in s 31(1)(a)(i) and (ii) as “to reproduce the work in a material
form” - where “to reproduce” is understood to implicitly require less exacti-
tude than the language of “to make a copy”. In Part IV for sound recordings
and cinematograph films the counterpart right is “to make a copy” of the
subject matter in ss 85(1)(a) and 86(b) respectively, for broadcasts it is to make a
sound recording or a film of the broadcast in s 87(a) and (b), and for published
editions it is “to make a facsimile copy of the edition” in s 88 - in each case the
Part IV language is read as implicitly requiring a copy made with exactitude
by technical means.

6.2.2 Deeming provisions
6.2.2.1 Reproduction-specific deeming rules

The 1968 Act includes in s 21 a variety of deeming provisions which can have
importance in clarifying whether or not conduct constitutes an act of repro-
duction or copying. Largely, however, the provisions state propositions that
would likely be arrived at in their absence. They consist of four types:

Filming or sound recording works or their adaptations: this deems the
inclusion within a cinematograph film or sound recording of a work,
or an adaptation thereof, to be a reproduction in a material form.’
Versions of artistic works into different dimensions. This deems a
three-dimensional version (e.g. a teapot) of a two-dimensional artistic
work (e.g. a technical drawing for the making of the teapot) to be a
reproduction of the two-dimensional work.” Conversely, it deems a
two-dimensional version (e.g. a photograph) of a three-dimensional
artistic work (e.g. a sculpture) to be a reproduction of the three-
dimensional work. The deeming provision is made expressly subject
to Division 7 of Part III which contains an assortment of artistic work
exceptions, although perhaps even more significant here are the copy-
right/design overlap exceptions discussed in Chapter 10 (see: 10.5) and
found in Division 8 of Part III.”

“Versions” of computer programs from source code into object code
(e.g. compilation) or from object code into source code (e.g. decompila-
tion): these versions are deemed to be reproductions of the computer
program they were derived from.” This should be considered against
the scope of the adaptation right which includes making a “version”
of a computer program whether or not in a different programming
language and “not being a reproduction of the work” It seems clear
enough, in view of this definition of adaptation (see: 6.8.2), that the
s 21 deeming provision is directed to automated processor activities of
compiling or decompiling which do not adapt a program but which are
deemed to reproduce it. This is an outcome that is consistent with the
logic of the drafting of the 1968 Act and related High Court authority
which require authorial contribution for there to be an adaptation: any
adaptation must itself be a work.”

Conversions of works, their adaptations, sound recordings and
cinematograph films into or from digital form: such conversion is
deemed to be a reproduction (for works and adaptations) or a copy
(for records and films). While this appears self-evident, the provision
was inserted to placate a parliamentary committee which had recom-
mended that there should be a new right of “first digitisation” which

could then be outside the scope of certain exceptions. Because this
recommendation was resisted by the government, as an olive branch
it inserted the deeming provision and appended a legislative note
to make clear “first digitisation” was included within the concept of
reproduction.»

Critically, these deeming provisions remain at all times subject to the over-
arching and controlling role of s 14. That is, they deem that reproduction or
copying is possible in the stipulated form, not that any such particular form
takes a substantial part of a work or other subject matter.

6.2.2.2 Broader deeming rules for sound recordings and broadcasts

In addition, and situated within Part IV, are similar (yet broader) deeming
provisions that apply to the s 101(1) liability that arises from the unlicensed
doing of any act comprised in the copyright or the unlicensed authorisation
of the doing of such an act. The first, s 101(3), relates to sound recordings
and provides that the s 101(1) liability provision applies to “an act done in
relation to a sound recording whether the act is done directly or indirectly
making use of a record embodying the recording” - seemingly ensuring for
the avoidance of doubt so that (for example) making a copy of a copy of a
sound recording and causing a copy of a copy of a sound recording are both
exercises of exclusive rights. The second, s 101(4), relates to sound and televi-
sion broadcasts and provides that s 101(1) applies to acts done by reception
of the broadcast or “by making use of any article or thing in which the visual
images and sounds comprised in the broadcast have been embodied” . In TCN
Channel Nine v Network Ten a case discussed in Chapter 3 (see: 3.4) and Chapter
5 (see: 5.3.3) the effect of this provision as it applied to the exclusive right “to
re-broadcast” a broadcast was neatly explained by Hely J:
It follows from s 101(4) that the notion of ‘re-broadcasting’ is not confined to
the re-direction or ‘pulling down’ by one station of another's signal. Ten’s
transmission of the [excerpts from the Nine programmes] which it had
captured on videotape involved a ‘re-broadcasting’ of the [excerpts from
the Nine programmes].”

Similar to the s 21 deeming rules, both s 101(3) and (4) deeming provisions
are subject to the role played by the need for any act to be done in relation toa
substantial part of the subject matter in issue, assessed as both a quantitative
and qualitative matter.”

6.2.3 Relevant actor

In 2012 the Full Court considered NRL Investments v Singtel Optus which
involved the defendant's cloud-computing implemented service branded as
TV Now.” It gave rise to the issue of who is the relevant actor which exercises
aright in the setting of a service provider performing the technical acts of copy-
ing at the behest of a subscriber. The TV Now service, offered by the defendant,
permitted a subscriber to instruct the defendant to record broadcasts on remote
servers for the purpose of then streaming that subject matter to the subscriber
for so-called time-shifted viewing.” Who exercised the right of making the
copy: the subscriber, the defendant or both? The Full Court, contrary to the trial
judge Rares J who considered the subscriber to be the sole responsible actor,
indicated that the exercise of the right was undertaken either by the defendant
alone or, more likely, by both the defendant and the subscriber jointly. The Full
Court reasoned, in unanimously expressing its preference for a joint exercise
of right, that:
The subscriber, by selecting the programme to be copied and by confirming
that it is to be copied, can properly be said to be the person who instigates
the copying. Yet it is Optus which effects it. Without the concerted actions
of both there would be no copy made ...*
Applied across different cloud and network computing settings, this principle
places responsibility for the exercise of the copying right either wholly or
jointly upon the entity providing the cloud service or the network provider.

6.2.4 Material form of the reproduction/copy and reproducibility
from it
For Part III to reproduce “in a material form” includes any form of physical
storage of a work, its adaptation, or a substantial part of either, whether or
not the work or adaptation is visible to the human eye from that storage.
Therefore magnetic audio tape, for example, could suffice as a material form
reproduction of a literary work. Similarly for the sound recordings, cinemato-
graph films and broadcasts of Part IV a fundamental requirement for there to
be a copy is some form of embodiment: By s 24 sounds or visual images are

deemed to be embodied in an article or thing if it is “so treated in relation to
those sounds or visual images that those sounds or visual images are capable,
with or without the aid of some other device, of being reproduced from the
article or thing”.

A significant aspect of law (and law reform) relating to the exercise of
reproduction and copying exclusive rights has centred around data copied
into the random access memory (RAM) of a device, so as to have it available
for processing by a processor within the device. This may occur, for example,
when a disc with digitally stored content is played by the device or when.
content made available online is streamed to a device. Whereas read only
memory (ROM) storage is like archived file cabinet storage of the whole of
content items, RAM storage is like a white board onto which parts of an item's
content (or items’ contents) can be rendered for a temporary period for imme-
diate use. Data representing digital file content is sometimes said to “flush”
through RAM, and the advances in RAM technologies and capacity has helped
facilitate the availability of streaming platforms such as Netflix. The emergent
issue, and addressed by the Commonwealth legislature in the shadow of the
US-Australia Free Trade Agreement, has been whether the transitory flushing
of data representing parts of work or other subject matter could amount to a
reproduction of a work in a “material form” for Part III, or a copy sufficiently
“embodied” in RAM of sounds or visual images for Part IV purposes.

The issue arose acutely in litigation spanning 2002 to 2005 which involved
the first iteration of Australia’s technical protection measures regime and gener-
ally referred to as Sony v Stevens.» The plaintiff's technology was a gaming
device, the Sony PlayStation, which was designed to connect to a television
and run video games stored as computer programs on disc media. A disc,
upon being inserted into the PlayStation, encountered an integrated circuit (a
computer chip) that performed a gatekeeper role: it detected and decoded an
encrypted code that should be present on the disc only if it was an author-
ised copy. Conventional technologies facilitating the making (i.e. “burning”)
of unauthorised copies from authorised discs were incapable of copying the
encrypted code onto the copy made. The PlayStation device was configured
such that discs would be permitted to boot and run only if the encrypted code
was present on the disc; unauthorised copies should be inutile. Stevens ran a
small business installing modified chips within PlayStation devices so that discs
would boot and run regardless of whether the encrypted code was present.

Clearly Stevens was in the business of circumventing the Sony technol-
ogy, however, any liability in Stevens for supplying a “circumvention device”
under the 1968 Act required determination of whether the Sony technology
comprised under it a “technological protection measure” (TPM). This was
because liability only arose from the supply of a device or service which

circumvents a TPM. At the time of the litigation a TPM was defined as a meas-
ure “designed ... to prevent or inhibit the infringement of copyright ina work
or other subject-matter” .** Did the PlayStation’s gatekeeper solution comprise a
TPM? Sony’s arguments included that the playing of any disc resulted in data
being read from the disc and fed into the device’s RAM, thus reproducing “in
a material form” the computer program encoding for the game, and copying
by RAM embodiment the cinematograph film which was the audio-visual of
the game. The High Court unanimously rejected both propositions: transitory
RAM storage was neither a material form nor sufficient embodiment.

The definition of “material form” at the time of the litigation was that it
included “any form (whether visible or not) of storage from which the work ...
ora substantial part of the work ... can be reproduced”. The evidence was such
that any reproducibility from the PlayStation RAM would require the develop-
ment of new hardware to enable it. The High Court endorsed the conclusion of
the trial judge and the Full Court majority that it was “unrealistic and strained”
to treat the words “can be reproduced” to encompass “could be reproduced if
an additional device, not supplied with the console and not yet available, were
to be manufactured and attached to it”.*’ On the cinematograph film copying
point, the trial judge’s conclusion was that “the ephemeral embodiment” of a
small proportion of images in the RAM could not constitute the act of making
a copy of the video game as a cinematograph film in view of the definition
of “cinematograph film” in s 10(1) and the provisions of s 24 both requiring
some form of embodiment of the film in “an article or thing”, combined with
the overarching requirement of at least substantial part copying imposed by
s 14. This was accepted by the High Court: “Sony failed to lay the necessary
evidentiary basis for a finding in its favour on substantiality.”** This conclusion
fed into an overarching characterisation that the gatekeeper technology of the
PlayStation was not a TPM and therefore the modified chips Stevens supplied
were not circumvention devices.

The coincidence of this litigation with the negotiation of the US-Australia
Free Trade Agreement provides some context to the 2004 reforms that followed
inits wake. So that the first copyright-focused article of the US-Australia Free
Trade Agreement made clear that reproduction and copying “in any manner
or form, permanent or temporary (including temporary storage in a material
form)” was the nature of the minimum standard required.» This obligation
was considered in the context of Sackville J’s trial decision in the litigation, later
endorsed by a unanimous High Court, that the transitory storage through the
PlayStation RAM had neither comprised a material form for Part II reproduc-
tion nor sufficient embodiment for Part IV copying.” It was thought that the

FTA obligation required statutory amendment of the 1968 Act to attempt to
make clear that storage in RAM was capable of comprising a material form
for the Part III reproduction rights and could suffice for the Part IV copying
rights. As seen in the Sony PlayStation case, under the then definition of
material form only reproduction in a form of storage from which the work
could be reproduced could amount to a rights exercise. Under the 2004 new
definition, to be in “material form” the work (or its adaptation) no longer needs
to be capable of reproduction from that storage. The definition now provides
that such storage qualifies “whether or not the work ... can be reproduced”
from it.* For Part IV sound recordings, cinematograph films and broadcasts, a
similar outcome was arrived at by the addition in 2004 of s 10(5) and (6). Those
provisions provide that copies include any form (whether visible or not) of
storage of the relevant subject matter, or a substantial part thereof, “whether or
not the copy of the subject matter, or a substantial part, can be reproduced”.
However, no reform occurred at that time or since in relation to s 24, so that
reproducibility continues on as a necessary element for embodiment to be
deemed under that provision.

The result of the 2004 reforms should not be overstated. There is continued:
insistence upon physical reproductions or copies; flexibility as to the forms they
might take; use of the term “storage” to describe those forms; and the necessity
that a substantial part be so stored. The change has been to the requirement of
reproducibility from the storage. In Part III the prior position was reversed and
in Part IV the legislative intent appears to have been the same - leaving aside
any uncertainty an unamended s 24 might create.“ It should be also added that
at this time two new exceptions were added to the 1968 Act in ss 43B and 111B
to ensure that any temporary reproductions of works or temporary copies of
Part IV subject matter would not infringe copyright if made “incidentally ... as
a necessary part of a technical process” of using an authorised copy.

It is useful to consider how this new landscape would apply to the
computer program reproduction and the cinematograph film copying argu-
ments in the Sony PlayStation case itself. Given the unchallenged finding of
the trial judge that a substantial part of the computer program embodied in a
game disc was temporarily stored in the RAM of a PlayStation device while
the game was being played, it seems that under the new legislative regime
that storage would comprise a material form of reproduction of the work,
notwithstanding any non-reproducibility from RAM.* However with the

cinematograph film the trial judge, supported by the High Court, could not
find on the balance of probabilities that a substantial part of the audio-visual
item had been embodied in RAM. That finding would mean that even under
the 2004 reforms, the temporary RAM storage would not comprise a copy
of the film.** Finally, given that any reproduction or copies were made from
playing a non-authorised copy of a game disc, the ss 43B and 111B defences
would not apply.

6.3 Works subject matter examples: “to reproduce”

In the previous chapter the broad concepts of derivation and substantial part
(including the degree to which those two can become blurred for works in
certain cases) were set out in settings which almost always involved repro-
duction or copying. It is useful to provide some illustrative case examples of
where exercises of the reproduction or copying right in each category of subject
matter was either established or seriously put into issue.

6.3.1 Literary works (other than computer programs,
compilations and tables)

Chapter 2 (see: 2.5) and Chapter 5 (see: 5.3.2) reveal that many of the leading
Anglo-Australian authorities on the treatment of works and substantial part
involve literary works in the form of computer programs, or compilations and
tables.” There are few modern Anglo-Australian cases involving a finding of
a reproduction of a more conventional literary work by a subsequent literary
work, a finding, that colloquially at least is akin to plagiarism. In Baigent v
Random House the UK courts considered whether the famous literary work The
Da Vinci Code was a substantial part reproduction of an earlier literary work
of speculative history titled The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail (HBHG).* Both
works dealt with a conjectured theory that Jesus Christ had fathered children
and a connection of that bloodline through the centuries to Leonardo da Vinci.
Reproduction here was alleged on the basis that plot elements representing
the central theme of HBHG had been appropriated by the author of the later
work. In upholding the trial judge’s rejection of substantial part reproduction,
Lloyd LJ writing as a member of a unanimous Court of Appeal, observed that:
[T]he judge had to consider:
() what relevant material was to be found in both works;
(i) how much, if any, of that had been copied from HBHG;
(iii) whether what was so copied was on the copyright side of the
line between ideas and expression; and

(iv) whether any of the material that was copied and did qualify
as expression, rather than as ideas, amounted to a substantial
part of HBHG.

The third and fourth of these issues, as often, are interconnected.?

While the author of the later work had been found to have taken from HBHG
an assortment of historical facts, ideas and theories found in the earlier literary
work, these takings were also found to be at a too abstract level to amount toa
taking of a substantial part of the expression of the earlier work. The case can
be seen as an example of the proposition that copyright protects expression
rather than mere facts or broad ideas.

However, this does not mean that thematic takings can never amount to
a substantial part of the expression of the plaintiff's work. For literary works
of fiction in which the author's imagination and writing are responsible for
creating the characters and the entire universe that those characters inhabit,
the appropriation of plot elements may more readily be held to amount to a
substantial part reproduction. So that in the immediate wake of the Baigent v
Random House Kitchin J was asked to consider an application brought by the
defendants, publisher of the Harry Potter series of books and their author
professionally known as J K Rowling. The defendants sought summary judg-
ment against a plaintiff who claimed one book in the Harry Potter series, Harry
Potter and the Goblet of Fire (HPGE), comprised a substantial part reproduction
of an earlier literary work of children’s fiction titled Villy the Wizard (WTW).”
The application was in part on the basis that Allen, the trustee for the estate of
the author of WIW, had no real prospect of success in his copyright infringe-
ment claim. That claim at its most basic was the taking in HPGF of the core
plot architecture of WIW. As summarised by the court these most basic five
elements were:

(i) the main characters of WIW and HPGF are wizards who are to
compete in a wizard contest which they ultimately win (element 1);
(ii) the main characters are required to deduce the exact nature of the
main task (element 2);
(iii) the main characters uncover the nature of the main task covertly in
a bathroom (element 3);
(iv) the main characters complete the main task using information
gained from helpers (element 4);
(v) the main task for the main characters involves the rescue of human
hostages imprisoned by a community of half-human, half-animal
creatures (element 5)"
In such a case issues of idea and expression become more fraught, where the
ideas (as such) are so confected. Arguably, protecting the fruits of literary
imagination of this sort is a central role that copyright law should perform.
In any event, Kitchin J rejected the application by the publisher and author

of HPGF for the claims to be struck out by summary judgment. This was in
part on the basis of expert evidence that “the similarities comprise ideas and
devices which are original and unusual and constitute ... plots and themes of
WTW’ and the trial judge’s acceptance that the plaintiff had “a real prospect of
establishing that collectively they represent the core of [WTW’s] architecture”.
However the court also concluded that while the claim may succeed “it is
improbable it will do so”, and the matter was eventually struck out on another
basis: the plaintiff's failure to meet a security for costs order.

6.3.2 Dramatic works

The case of Zeccola is discussed in Chapter 3 when distinguishing the natures
of Part III and Part IV subject matter.™ Relevant here is the status of the case
as involving a reproduction of a dramatic work, the Jaws screenplay, by the
making of a later film released in Australia under the title Great White, in which
the same “easily recognisable characters appear and recognisable incidents
occur”.® There was no word-for-word appropriation by the later film, and
the defendants asserted their film was merely in the same genre as Jaws (that
of a savage monster menacing a community) and indeed the defendants
further asserted that the Great I Vile screenplay was derived not from Jaws but
from other source material: a Spanish novel by Ramon Bravo called Carnada.
However, the Victorian Supreme Court's approach was informed by the
following dicta taken from Sargant J at trial in Corelli v Gray:

That there is nothing very striking or original either in the novel or the sketch
Taccept. But the combination of these ordinary materials may nevertheless
be original, and when such a combination has arrived at a certain degree
of complexity it becomes practically impossible that it should have been
arrived at independently by a second individual. The notes in music or the
letters of the alphabet are the common materials of musicians or authors,
as the case may be, and a musical or literary passage is but a combination
of these common materials. But nevertheless no one doubts that identity
or extreme similarity between two musical or literary passages, even of
quite moderate length, may be practically conclusive evidence in favour
of common origin and against independent creation. In my judgment the
similarities and coincidences in this case are such as, when taken in combi-
nation, to be entirely inexplicable as the result of mere chance coincidence.

This dicta from Corelli v Gray (itself involving an infringement by the adapta-
tion of a novel into a stage play) when coupled with what the court considered
to be the vast array of plot and character similarities between the two films,
led Gray J at trial to conclude that Great White “could not have been made
independently of Jaws” ’ In so doing the two issues of substantial part and
derivation were essentially rolled into one and an interlocutory injunction
against the distributors further publicly exhibiting Great !Vhite was made.* On
appeal to the Full Federal Court the injunction was maintained. While the Full
Court noted that “our own viewing of the films did not instil in us the same
degree of conviction” on the issues of derivation and substantial part, it was
content with the trial judge’s conclusions on the following basis:

Atits narrowest the major issue is whether or not the appellants’ film Great

TWhite substantially reproduced the novel Jaws or the screenplay based upon

that novel from which the respondent's film Jaws was made. In the resolu-

tion of that question it is appropriate to have regard to the operation of

s 21 of the Act which deems cinematographic films to be reproductions of

literary or dramatic works in certain circumstances.”
The reference here to s 21 (discussed above) and the operation of the extended
reach of the adaptation right in s 31(1)(a)(vii) (discussed below) can be brought
together here in a way that is more formalised than was undertaken in the
interlocutory hearing. The plaintiff was the copyright owner of rights in the
Jaws literary work, the dramatic work/screenplay adapted from that first liter-
ary work, and - although not relevant to the analysis — the cinematograph film
based on that screenplay. The cinematograph film Great !Vhite reproduced a
substantial part of the Jaws dramatic work/screenplay being the s 31(1)(a)(i)
reproduction right, a conclusion buttressed by the combined operation of ss 14
and 21(1),(2). Once that conclusion is reached, if Great WVhite is caused to be seen
in public by the defendants that act will almost certainly involve them exercis-
ing the s 31(1)(a)(iii) public performance right in the Jaws dramatic work/
screenplay. Then, because the Jaws dramatic work/ screenplay is an adaptation
of the Jaws literary work, and because the same plaintiff owns copyright in that
literary work, under s 31(1)(a)(vii) any exercise of s 31(1)(a)(i) or (iii) rights in
the adaptation by the defendants will automatically comprise an exercise of
those same two rights in the underlying literary work and permit copyright
ownership of the literary work to also ground the plaintiff's action. However,
in this elaboration of the analysis the critical juridical issues remain: (i) is Great
White derived from the Jaws screenplay and if so, (ii) does Great White comprise
a substantial part reproduction of the Jaws screenplay?

So that in Telstra v Royal & Sun Alliance the plaintiff had run a television

advertising campaign for its directory services featuring a distinctive male

Scottish-born actor as a character (“Mr Goggomobil”) who sought to find
by telephone a mechanic for his Goggomobil, an uncommon vintage motor
vehicle.” The defendant used the same actor in essentially the same “Mr
Goggomobil” character in its television advertisement for its automobile insur-
ance services in which the character now sought to insure his Goggomobil
over the telephone. Did the defendant's television advertisement comprise
a substantial part reproduction of the plaintiff's dramatic work/ screenplay?
Merkel J held that it did not:
The common concepts or themes feature a memorable character (Mr
Goggomobil), an unusual and distinctive motor vehicle (a Goggomobil),
the use of the telephone to help solve an obscure problem in relation to
that vehicle and the telling of that story in a humorous manner. However,
those resemblances relate to the ideas and concepts copied rather than to
their expression, and are not sufficient to constitute the ‘reproduction’ of
a substantial part of the first Goggomobil advertisement. Plainly, the ...
[defendant's] advertisement conjures up the first Goggomobil advertise-
ment and its ideas and concepts but does not reproduce a substantial part
of the substance or expression of the ‘dramatic’ events comprising that
advertisement.*
The holding must be correct and serves as a neat counterpoint to the holding in
Zeccola. The use of the “Mr Goggomobil” character in a different, but related,
setting is a far cry from the Great Vhite where an array of sequenced plot
elements and characters were taken by the defendant in such a way so as to
serve as a substitute for the plaintiff’s work.

6.3.3 Musical works

English judges have, since the 1835 case of D’Almaine v Boosey, adopted a proxy
test for musical works in that a recognisable part will generally amount to
a substantial part.‘ It can be seen that 21st century Australian judges have
adopted this approach in three cases involving popular music.

In Larrikin Music Publishing v EMI Songs Australia the defendants were
found liable for reproducing in a material form an iconic musical work for

children, Kookaburra, by incorporating its first two bars (of a total of four bars
in Kookaburra) within a flute riff of the also iconic popular song Down Under.
Ultimately, (i) derivation was able to be inferred from the failure to call the
musician responsible for contributing the flute riff; (ii) that the first two of
the four bars represented a substantial part of Kookaburra was never likely
to be seriously doubted; (iii) the case turned on whether the defendants had
truly reproduced that part. Within the Down Under flute riff the two bars from
Kookaburra were interrupted by a short bar referred to as the basic hook. In this
interrupted form the bars from Kookaburra had remained largely unrecognised
in Down Under from the early 1980s until 2007 when a broadcast television
program identified the provenance of those two bars in the flute riff. Only
after that broadcast did the copyright owner of the Kookaburra musical work
begin an infringement action, alleging (inter alia) that the defendants had
reproduced its work in a material form by the composition of Down Under.
The trial judge, Jacobson J, faced with competing expert evidence found in
favour of the plaintiff on the third issue. The court did this by directly applying
the recognisability test of D’Almaine v Boosey (for which Jacobson J observed
that “the emphasis upon the quality of what is taken introduces a subjective
element”) the court found that there was a reproduction by the taking “of
two bars or phrases” “ This holding was unanimously upheld on appeal. One
member of the Full Court, Jagot J, concisely distilled the issues on the question
of reproduction:
Kookaburra and Down Under are different genres, but that does not over-
come the fact that the bars of Kookaburra have been borrowed for the flute
riff. Similarly, although the works are of a different structure, the bars of
Kookaburra are nevertheless present in the flute riff and recognisable as
such.®
Across time, style, genre and structure in a musical reproduction case recognis-
ability to the ear of a substantial part of the plaintiff owners work emerges as
the crucial touchstone. So that when the trial judge and the Full Court were
required to consider whether a “languid orchestral” arrangement of Down
Under for use in the soundtrack to a Qantas television advertisement also
reproduced a substantial part of Kookaburra by incorporating its second bar
only, again the absence of recognition loomed large. The essential determina-

to detect the second bar was regarded by the Full Court to be an “important
factual finding”

Another somewhat iconic 1970s Australian popular song, Love is in the Air,

In the Full Court, Finkelstein J summarised the evidence in this way:
[D]epending on the particular game being considered, somewhere between
5% and 15% of the object code in a CD is downloaded in the RAM at any

6.5 To publish

The exclusive right “to publish the work” is attached to all work types in
Part III and to no Part IV subject matter. Its nature and its relationship to
whether a work is deemed “to have been published” and also “what does not
constitute publication of the work” - both exhaustively defined in s 29(1)(a)
and (3) - was a more arcane area of Australian copyright law.” Until the
High Court's 1990 decision in Avel v Multicoin Amusement there existed legal
ambiguity as to whether “to publish”, the exclusive right in s 31, was controlled
by the s 29(1)(a) and (3) concepts. Those concepts clearly have application
in respect of provisions dealing with special ownership cases, term duration
and jurisdictional attachment." The question was did they also control the
exclusive right “to publish” in s 31.

A reason for the ambiguity is rooted in copyright legal history which
features the different natures of a divesting publication and a vesting publi-
cation prior to the 1911 Act. In the 19th century common law copyright in
unpublished works and an entitlement to statutory protection upon due publi-
cation were easy to lose in one foul swoop by any public disclosure of the work
prior to securing statutory rights: a divesting publication. Thus, in Jefferys v
Boosey, Lord Brougham rhetorically asked: “if [the author] makes his composi-
tion public, can he retain the exclusive right which he had before?”. To this,
the following answer was given: “whatever may have been the original right
of the author, the publication appears to be of necessity an abandonment” *”
This was reinforced in Caird v Sime: prior public disclosure rendered the subject
matter publici juris in copyright in the same way as an anticipatory publication
of a secret invention would destroy both its confidentiality and any prospective
patent rights. Conversely in this era the statutory right to copy was much
more difficult to attract, requiring that an author while resident in the UK first
printed and had copies published (i.e. sold or distributed) in the UK by the
engagement of a UK printer: a vesting publication.”” The High Court in Avel
v Multicoin Amusements, as the House of Lords had earlier held in Infabrics v
Jaydex, interpreted the exclusive right “to publish” as meaning action akin to
what would amount to (under that 19th century law) a divesting publication:
making public in the territory a work not previously made public in the terri-
tory. In other words, the first public disclosure of the work in Australia, and
it is axiomatic that such an exercise in relation to a work can only occur once.
This approach was consistent with the way the “to publish the work” exclusive

right was first inserted into modern copyright law in the 1911 Act (“if the work
is unpublished, to publish the work or any substantial part thereof”) makes it a
quite distinct and independent concept from “publication” in the s 29 sense."

Legal history aside, the two modern interpretive factors that reinforced
this conclusion in both the House of Lords and the High Court are quite
difficult to refute. In terms of the 1968 Act they were coherence with
s 38 liability and consistent application of the s 14 substantial part test. On
the former, both courts could observe that the type of indirect liability for
selling items made in infringement of a work (i. s 38 of the 1968 Act) was
conditioned on a knowledge requirement not found for liability for directly
exercising any exclusive right If liability arising from the exercise of the
“to publish the work” exclusive right (ie. s 31(1)(a)(i) and (1)(b)(ii) of the
1968 Act) was defined (i.e. by s 29 of the 1968 Act) to mean the unauthorised
supply of copies of the work regardless of state of mind, it would essentially
leave the indirect liability provision with nothing to do. This incoherence was
considered unlikely to have been intended by the parliaments, so that if there
was a plausible interpretation to be given to the exclusive right “to publish
the work” to avoid such incoherence, it should be applied to give effect to
presumed parliamentary intention. Also, if the specific deeming provisions
on the concepts of “to have been published” and “what does not constitute
publication of the work” (ie. s 29(1)(a) and (3) of the 1968 Act) controlled
the exclusive right “to publish the work” this would remove this exclusive
right alone from the reach of the substantial part test. This was because those
deeming provisions included that, for the purpose of determining whether a
work should be deemed to have been published, s 14 “does not apply”. There
was no obvious policy reason why the substantial part test ought not apply
to that exclusive right alone, and indeed as was mentioned above when the
right was first introduced in the 1911 Act and attaching to unpublished works
alone, it was expressed as “to publish the work or any substantial part thereof”.

Given the meaning of “to publish the work” is to make public in the copy-
right territory (i.e. the Australian jurisdiction) a work not previously made
public in that territory and this is far wider than what is deemed publication
under s 29, a question arises whether it is also wider than the circumstances
in which a work is “made public” under the more recent s 29A. While s 29A
concepts are indeed closer to the exclusive right meaning of “to publish the
work’, it seems the exclusive right concept eclipses s 29A. To give one example:
a previously undisclosed, private letter (ie. the artefact) might be made open
to public inspection as part of the collection of a library or archive. Quite
clearly this conduct by the library or archive on its own does not amount
to publication of any literary work under s 29(1)(a). However, that conduct

also does not expressly fall within the concept of making the literary work
public under s 29A, under which only artistic works are made public by being
“exhibited in public”. In contrast, by virtue of the Avel v Multicoin Amusement
and Infabrics v Jaydex authorities, it seems clear enough that the library or
archive has by its conduct exercised the right of “to publish the work” insofar
as it has first disclosed to the public the literary work that the letter embodies.

6.6 To perform in public / To cause to be heard or
seen in public

The original text of the 1886 Berne Convention recognised a right of “public
representation of dramatic or dramatico-musical works” 4 English copyright
law, however, is rooted in the right to reproduce a work in a material form,
such that prior to 1833 neither the common law right nor any expansive inter-
pretation of rights under the Statute of Anne recognised any right of public
performance.” In 1833 dramatic works (whether unpublished or published)
had conferred upon them a statutory public performance right under the
Dramatic Literary Property Act 1833. Likewise, under the Publication of Lectures
Act 1835, certain public lectures delivered in accordance with statutory notice
requirements also had conferred upon them a statutory performance right.”
Then in 1842 a provision in the Copyright Act extended the public performance
right to musical works and curtailed the length of performance rights provided
under the 1833 Act.’ In view of this statutory incrementalism of the 19th
century, the 1911 Act's inclusion of a public performance right was less dictated
by Berne obligation and more a consolidation. The 1911 Act provided that
“copyright means ... to perform, or in the case of a lecture to deliver, the work
or any substantial part thereof in public”. Since its enactment in 1968, the
exclusive right “to perform the work in public” applies to literary, dramatic
and musical (but not artistic) works in Part III. Sound recordings and
cinematograph films in Part IV have attached to them the analogous exclusive
right expressed as “to cause” a recording to be heard in public or a film to be
seen or heard in public." While television broadcasts also received under the

1956 UK Act an exclusive right to cause the broadcasts being seen or heard “by
a paying audience”, no such right was ever extended under the 1968 Act.

Section 27 of the 1968 Act provides a series of provisions intended to
provide greater certainty around the issue of what amounts to performance,
causing images to be seen or sounds to be heard for the relevant exclusive
rights in Part III and Part IV. It provides four substantive points of clarifica-
tion for practical purposes:

(1) A performance of a work may occur mechanically - such as by the
operation of reception equipment, or the exhibition of a cinemato-
graphic film, or the use of a sound recording.“

(2) Both performance of a work and causing images to be seen or sounds

to be heard are distinct from the communication of subject matter,

such that the latter is mutually exclusive from the former two.”

Related to (2), the activity of communicating matter to reception equip-

ment (as opposed to operating that reception equipment) does not

constitute performance, causing images to be seen or sounds to be
heard."

The occupier of premises who consents to situating equipment on the

premises by the operation of which a performance occurs mechani-

cally, images are caused to be seen or sounds to be heard, is deemed
to be the person giving the performance, causing images to be seen or
sounds to be heard.”

An implicit theme running through s 27 is that a central feature of performance
in public, or causing matter to be seen or heard in public, is the existence of a
geographically proximate audience. This distinguishes those “in public” exer-
cises of right from the communication “to the public” exclusive right which
has as its hallmark geographically dispersed points of reception.

There are two leading Australian authorities: one on causing a cinemato-
graph film to be seen or heard in public and the other on the question of what
amounts to a performance of a musical work in public. Both are consistent
with each other, and have been persuasive authorities on the scope of exer-
cises of exclusive rights “to the public”. In Rank Film Production v Dodds the
plaintiff obtained a declaration that the defendant had unlawfully caused the
plaintiff's films to be seen and heard in public and an injunction restrain-
ing further infringement. The defendant operated a suburban motel and
centrally transmitted to televisions sets in rooms the plaintiff's films by means

(3)

(C)

of a video cassette recorder connected to those sets by cabling. Rath J held that
this was causing those films to be seen or heard in public because:

The relevant character of the audience is not its character of an individual
or individuals in a private or domestic situation, but in its character as a
guest or guests of the motel. In that latter character the guest pays for his
accommodation, and the benefits (in-house movies) that go with it. Ina real
sense he is paying the proprietor of the motel for presentation to him in the
privacy of his room of an in-house movie. He is in this character a member
of the copyright owner's public.”
In Australasian Performing Right Association v Commonwealth Bank of Australia
the defendant bank had played to 11 staff members in a suburban bank
branch, and before the branch had opened to its customers, a bank-produced
training and motivational video.” The video soundtrack included what was
conceded to be a substantial part of a musical work whose public performance
exclusive right was owned by the plaintiff. Equally clear was that the playing
of the video by the defendant bank was capable of being a performance of the
work. The only question was whether the defendant bank had undertaken
that performance “in public” given the nature of the audience. That question
was answered in the affirmative in a first instance decision of Gummow J,
then sitting on the Federal Court. Reliance was placed on the analysis of a
Singaporean textbook writer Wei who had concluded that: “if the performance
occurs as an adjunct to some commercial activity, then almost certainly the
performance can be regarded as public”. Applying Wei’s analysis, and look-
ing to the ties that bound the members of the audience to the performance,
Gummow J found that the bank had exercised the public performance right
on the basis that:
The occasion of the performance was the imparting of information by the
employer to its employees and the musical work was used to facilitate that
process. The audience was brought together by the commercial purposes
of the respondent and their public lives as employees.
The determinative consideration in both cases of whether the audience was
a “public” one was not the size of the audience, but the commercial nature of
the audience's setting. In both cases an audience comprising either a solitary
motel guest or a solitary bank employee would be an “in public” exercise of

rights.

6.71 Introduction

Attaching to all work types in Part III and to all Part IV subject matter except
published editions, the exclusive right “to communicate the [work or relevant
subject matter] to the public” was the centrepiece of the Digital Agenda
Reforms.* An express intention of inserting the right was to give effect
to a treaty obligation in the 1996 }VIPO Copyright Treaty (WCT). WCT art 8
relevantly provides that:
[Aluthors of literary and artistic works shall enjoy the exclusive right of
authorizing any communication to the public of their works, by wire or
wireless means, including the making available to the public of their works
in such a way that members of the public may access these works from a
place and at a time individually chosen by them.“

When first enacted, the 1968 Act included two exclusive rights for all works
that were related to their electronic communication. These were the rights “to
broadcast the work” and “to cause the work to be transmitted to subscrib-
ers to a diffusion service”, for which each right had their own set of defined
terms. On enactment in 1968, cinematograph films received both these rights,
whereas sound recordings, sound broadcasts and television broadcasts merely
had attached to them the former right which, in the case of broadcasts, was
expressed as “to re-broadcast it”. Hence on enactment - and as will be seen
this still remains somewhat the case in current law - the word “broadcast” was
used both as a noun in the Part IV subject matter sense, and as a verb in the
exclusive right sense across both Parts III and IV. So that when s 87(c) states
that copyright in relation to a television broadcast or sound broadcast is, inter
alia, “the exclusive right ... to re-broadcast it” the provision uses and brings
together both the noun and verb usage of the word “broadcast”.

The Digital Agenda Reforms - aside from the re-broadcasting of broad-
casts - removed these two 1968 electronic communication exclusive rights
and subsumed them within the new exclusive right. The defined term
“communicate” was introduced in those reforms and has been reformed only
to accommodate the expanded protection for performers effectively required
by the US-Australia Free Trade Agreement. Today it provides that:

communicate means make available online or electronically transmit
(whether over a path, or a combination of paths, provided by a material

substance or otherwise) a work or other subject-matter, including a perfor-
mance or live performance within the meaning of this Act.”

The 1999 Explanatory Memorandum circulated with the Digital Agenda
Reform Bill elaborated that:

The term ‘communicate’ is limited to the availability of material online or
through electronic transmissions. It is not intended to cover the physical
distribution of copyright material in a tangible form, which is adequately
covered by existing provisions of the Act. The definition makes clear that
an electronic transmission may occur as a result of a combination of paths
or delivery mechanisms. For example, a communication over the Internet
may involve a transmission that travels across copper wire, optic fibre cables
and microwaves."

Then, when dealing with the right per se in its s 31(1)(a) context, that Explanatory
Memorandum set out that:

The amendment introduces the new technology-neutral right to communi-
cate literary, dramatic and musical works to the public. The new right will
provide copyright owners with greater protection for their material in the
new digital environment and is the keystone to the reforms provided by
this Bill. The new right replaces and extends the existing technology-specific
broadcasting right which currently only applies to “wireless” broadcasts.
The new right of communication to the public also replaces the limited right
to transmit to subscribers to a diffusion service (i.e. cable diffusion right).
The new right of communication to the public encompasses the making
available of copyright material on-line, so as to provide protection to
copyright material made available through on-demand, interactive trans-
missions. An example of the exercise of this right would be the uploading
of copyright material onto a server which was connected to the Internet.

Thus, the exclusive right is intended to cover both traditional “push” electronic
communication of broadcasting and the new “pull” internet-age platforms
whereby subject matter is made available to a public who can access it when
they choose. The intent was also to capture, as much as possible, comparable
over-the-horizon communication technologies.

In this context, given the quite seismic nature of this reform, the transition
provisions in the Digital Agenda Reforms are worthy of note. First, the transi-
tion provisions make clear that the new exclusive right “applies in relation to
all works and other subject-matter (other than published editions of works) in
which copyright subsisted immediately before the commencing day”, which
was 4 March 2001.1” Second, the provisions state that:

A licence, contract or arrangement (including an assignment of copyright)

that was in force immediately before the commencing day continues to
have effect on and after that day in so far as it relates to the broadcasting

right or cable transmission right [i.e. to cause transmission to subscribers
to a diffusion service] in a work or other subject-matter, but subject to any
contrary intention, as if the Copyright Act had not been amended by this
Act, and the Copyright Act applies in relation to the licence, contract or
arrangement accordingly."
Thus, for example, a prior, continuing contractual licence to broadcast - to
transmit wirelessly to the public - a work or other subject-matter continued
to be effective after 4 March 2001, but only in respect of that aspect of the
new communication to the public right which the licence implicated: wireless
transmission to the public.

Given that the new right is intended to be broad-based, the exclusion of
published editions from its breadth clearly underscores what appears to be
the strictly print-based and physical nature of published edition copyright?

More obscure is why, for television and sound broadcasts, the exclusive
right was crafted in s 87(c) in the following terms: “to re-broadcast it or commu-
nicate it to the public otherwise than by broadcasting it”.** The reason why
broadcasts did not receive the straightforward exclusive right to communicate
the broadcast to the public was explained at the time as ensuring that “the
right of communication does not include a right to broadcast” .™ The rationale
for this was seemingly tied to the operation of s 22(5),(6) discussed below
(see: 6.7.3.3). These provide different rules for determining the relevant person
who broadcasts as opposed to who otherwise communicates, and preserve
the nature of protection applied in TCN Channel 9 v Network 10 when one
broadcaster exploited the broadcasts of a direct rival as explained in Chapters
3 (see: 3.4) and 5 (see: 5.3.3). In TCN Channel 9 v Network 10, which involved
s 87(c) prior to its Digital Agenda Reforms, the expression “to re-broadcast”
was accepted by both parties to involve either simultaneous retransmission or
broadcasting ata later time froma copy of the broadcast - an outcome buttressed
by the s 101(4) deeming rule discussed above (see: 6.2.2.2). At the time of the
reforms the right “to broadcast” (as a copyright exclusive right) was removed
from the Act, including the then definition of “broadcast” which had the verb
meaning of “transmit by wireless telegraphy to the public” .1* In its place,

the noun definition of “broadcast” now in s 10(1) and discussed in Chapter 3
(see: 3.4) was inserted. The use of the words in s 87(c) “to re-broadcast” and
“otherwise than by broadcasting it” appear to be intended to make a verb
usage of the current noun definition - a matter dealt with indirectly ina sound
recording licensing dispute in Phonographic Performance Company of Australia v
Commercial Radio Australia which was discussed in Chapter 3 (see: 3.4). In that
case a non-exclusive contractual licence to exploit the plaintiff’s sound record-
ings was delineated by the contract to be for the “Broadcasting Right” itself
contractually defined as “the right, from time to time, to broadcast the Sound
Recordings in Australia”. The Full Court noted by way of background that:

It is agreed between the parties that the term broadcast is to be understood
as having the meaning specified in s 10(1) of the Copyright Act. Section 10(1)
provides that broadcast means a communication to the public delivered by
a broadcasting service within the meaning of the [Broadcasting Services Act
1992 (Cth)]27
This led the Full Court to conclude, given that internet delivery is expressly
excluded by ministerial determination from what is a broadcasting service
under the Broadcasting Services Act 1992 (Cth), that the licence did not include
internet-streaming of radio programming: “the delivery of the radio program
by transmission from a terrestrial transmitter is a different broadcasting service
from the delivery of the same radio program using the internet” * The latter
was (using the language of s 87(c)) “to communicate it to the public otherwise
than by broadcasting it”. Therefore, to the extent “to broadcast to the public”
(or indeed “to re-broadcast” a broadcast) today remains as a distinct aspect
of the communication to the public right, it is a sub-right exercisable only by
broadcasting services delivering a “broadcast” as defined in the 1968 Act since
4 March 2001.

6.7.2 What is the meaning of “to the public”?

As discussed above (see: 6.6) the meaning of “in public” for performance in
public and kindred rights is largely informed by the single instances decisions
in Rank Film Production » Dodds and Australasian Performing Right Association
v Commonwealth Bank of Australia: a physically proximate audience, whatever
its size, in a commercial setting.

Shortly prior to the Digital Agenda Reforms the High Court considered
the appeal in Australasian Performing Right Association v Telstra Corporation.
In it, a question was whether the provision of music-on-hold by the defend-
ant corporation to in-coming (and queued) mobile telephone callers was to
broadcast that music under the rights structure prior to the Digital Agenda

Reforms, insofar as it was a transmission by wireless telegraphy “to the
public”. The High Court unanimously found that the provision of music-on-
hold to such callers was “to the public”, rejecting Telstra’s submission that it
was “the antithesis of broadcasting” because “each transmission goes only to
the one caller”. Dawson and Gaudron JJ, with the concurrence of Toohey
and McHugh JJ, expressed their conclusion in these terms:

What is important is the nature of the audience constituted by those who
receive music-on-hold. Lying behind the concept of the copyright owner's
public is recognition of the fact that where a work is performed in a
commercial setting, the occasion is unlikely to be private or domestic and
the audience is more appropriately to be seen as a section of the public.

Kirby J, consistent with that conclusion, expressed his conclusion by explicitly
inscribing Gummow J's analysis (itself drawn from Wei) from Australasian
Performing Right Association v Commonwealth Bank of Australia:

Music-on-hold is played largely by businesses and other organisations
to entertain, placate or distract customers or clientele, in a way hoped
to be congenial to them, while the telephone lines are engaged. ... It is
undoubtedly provided as ‘an adjunct to a commercial activity’. It cannot be
described as ‘private’ or ‘domestic’ in character. A telephone conversation
may be private. However, when callers are placed on hold ... [tJhey hear
the music because it is intended that any member of the public who calls
the engaged number, will hear the music. A caller hearing music-on-hold,
must therefore be part of that ‘public’ which the owner of the copyright in
the music contemplates to be part of its audience.

Australasian Performing Right Association v Telstra Corporation, which carried
forward the essential logic of the “in public” authorities of Rank Film Production
v Dodds and Australasian Performing Right Association v Commonwealth Bank
of Australia, emerges as the leading authority for the proposition that public
exploitation means commercial exploitation.

The Digital Agenda Reforms introduced as an overlay upon this a defini-
tion of “to the public” as meaning “to the public within or outside Australia”.
The Explanatory Memorandum here stated:

Currently the Act does not provide copyright owners with the exclusive
right to control transmissions that originate from Australia but are intended
only for reception by the public outside Australia, as they are not broad-
casts to the public in Australia as required by the Act. The inclusion of
this definition will mean that Australian copyright owners can control the
communication of material from Australia directed to overseas audiences.

As explained in Chapter 7 (see: 7.2.4) the communication right creates some
complex territoriality issues. For present purposes, when the actor exercising

the right is physically situated within jurisdiction, the public implicated by
the communication right may be a global one. In an infringement setting the
monetary remedial consequences of this feature of Australian copyright law
might be considerable. This is especially so in view of the recent acceptance
of general availability of notional lost licence fee damages under the user
principle.* The augmented definition highlights the distinction between “in
public” exercises of right and “to the public” exercises: the former is an exercise
directed to a physically proximate audience, the latter is an exercise directed
to points of reception or access which may be geographically (indeed globally)
dispersed. As noted above (see: 6.6), this distinction is made explicit in s 27
which provides that communication “to the public” constitutes neither of the
“in public” types of exclusive rights; performance of works nor causing other
subject matter to be seen or heard.

6.7.3 Who exercises the communication right?
6.7.3.1 General case

The issue of the actor responsible for the exercise of the communication to
the public right is dealt with in the general case (ie. excluding programming
delivered by broadcasting services) by a pair of provisions: s 22(6) and (6A).

Section 22(6) is a straightforward deeming provision: a communication
is undertaken by “the person responsible for determining the content of the
communication” - the uploader or person who exercises editorial control. The
Explanatory Memorandum indicates that the intent of the deeming provision
was to have “the effect that communications carriers and Internet Service
Providers will not be directly liable for communicating material to the public
via their networks if they are not responsible for determining the content of
that material” 2 In Universal Music Australia v Cooper, in a holding not chal-
lenged on appeal, Tamberlin J could comfortably apply the language of s 22(6)
to conclude that providing a hyperlink (a uniform resource locator address)
to third-party hosted content was not determining the content communicated
in the relevant sense. Thus the defendant who provided hyperlinks on his
website was not exercising the communication to the public right:

Ido not think it can be said that a person who is the proprietor, manager or
operator of a website which provides hyperlinks to other websites on which
the sound recordings are hosted determines the content of the communica-
tion. It is artificial in the extreme to suggest that the person or body who
facilitates access from the website to a remote site and provides a trigger
which enables sound recordings to be downloaded from that remote site is
responsible for the content of the communication from the remote website.

The conclusion represents current copyright law on the question of remotely-
hosted subject matter.

To understand s 22(6A) it is necessary to spell out the theory of liability
sketched by the applicant before the Copyright Tribunal in Copyright Agency v
Queensland Department of Education. This case arose shortly after Tamberlin J's
holding in Universal Music Australia v Cooper and was a dispute as to whether
educational institutions were exercising the communication to the public right
under a statutory licence when (say) teachers in their teaching materials “tell
students to view” matter on the internet - such as by providing students with
a hyperlink.” The Copyright Agency advanced a theory of liability that s 22(6)
applied here in relation to the electronic transmission caused by a student
clicking on a hyperlink. The theory went that when a member of the public,
such as a student, clicks on a hyperlink to cause subject matter to be rendered
on his or her device, that was a “subsequent act of viewing (and download-
ing) requires an actual transmission of the work to, or towards, someone (the
recipient)”. Therefore, the person clicking on the hyperlink was “determin-
ing the content” of that communication (i.e. electronic transmission) to him or
herself as a member of the public. Therefore, on that submission, because the
student was the person who is determining the content of the communication
within the meaning of s 22(6), the student was making the communication “on
behalf of” the school in question by “acting on the direction of the school as its
agent” “° However, rather than seek that the Tribunal itself resolve this issue,
it was reported that “the parties are considering whether it may be preferable
for the Tribunal to refer the issues to the Federal Court in a case stated under
s 161” 3”

Prior to resolution of this issue by any reference of it to the Federal Court
as a question of law, the Parliament intervened later in 2006 in a manner
that emphatically rejected the theory of liability advanced by the Copyright
Agency. This was by the addition of s 22(6A) which provides:

To avoid doubt, for the purposes of subsection (6), a person is not respon-

sible for determining the content of a communication merely because the

person takes one or more steps for the purpose of:

(a) gaining access to what is made available online by someone else in the
communication; or

(b) receiving the electronic transmission of which the communication
consists.

Example: A person is not responsible for determining the content of the
communication to the person of a web page merely because the person
clicks on a link to gain access to the page.

The accompanying Explanatory Memorandum elaborated on the new provi-
sion with a cryptic reference to the dispute in Copyright Agency v Queensland
Department of Education. It observed that although “it was never intended that
a person doing no more than merely accessing copyright material online could
be considered to be exercising the communication right in relation to what was
accessed, some have argued that this interpretation is possible” 17

6.7.3.2 Digital platform settings

The question arises where suppliers of content to a digital platform make
copyright subject matter available through the platform who is exercising the
communication to the public right: the suppliers, the platform or both? Two
first instance decisions have found the relevant exerciser of the right to be the
platform. In Pokémon Company International v Redbubble discussed in Chapter 7
(see: 7.4.1.2) the defendant was a web-based platform through which artists’
works were offered to be printed upon merchandise on demand. The artists
were “members” of the defendant who argued it was the artists and not it who
were exercising the communication to the public right in the works - some of
which infringed the plaintiff's copyright in its Pikachu work. Pagone J applied
s 22(6) to hold that the defendant digital platform was indeed the relevant
actor:

In the present case Redbubble does not provide the content of the commu-
nications in the sense of being the originator of any of the 29 images on its
website said to be infringements of the Pikachu work. In each case the origi-
nator was the artist who had placed the image on the Redbubble website.
Redbubble, however, was responsible for determining that content through
its processes, protocols and arrangements with the artists.”

A similar result was arrived at in Boomerang Investments v Padgett discussed

above (see: 6.3.3) where an issue was who was exercising the communication

to the public right when I Varm in the Winter (which reproduced the plaintiff's
musical work) was licensed by owners of }Varm in the Winter for streaming
and download on third-party platforms such as iTunes. The plaintiffs’ Further

Amended Statement of Claim (FASOC) at §11 pleaded: “From a date presently

unknown to the First to Third Applicants, the Glass Candy members have

made available, or caused to be made available for download and streaming,
by internet users, including in Australia, one or more recordings of a Glass

Candy song known as Warm in the Winter (Warm in the Winter recording).”

This was particularised by “The Warm in the Winter recording has been made

available on the internet including through iTunes, Google Play, Spotify, Apple

Music, YouTube” 2? Perram J forcefully rejected this claim without referring

tos 22(6):

There is no doubt that Glass Candy did literally not do any of these acts.
The persons who did the acts were the relevant online music providers
or, in the case of the actual act of download, the online music provider's
downloading customer. [The Applicants’] pleaded case at FASOC §11 was
that Glass Candy did these acts but I reject this on the facts. At best, the only
way Glass Candy can be drawn into these events is on the assumption that
what the online music services did was done on behalf of Glass Candy but
no matter how that suggestion is dressed up at FASOC §11 this is a case that
Glass Candy had authorised what had happened.”
Notwithstanding these cases it is far from clear in that it is a question of either
the platform or the supplier of content. As was discussed above (see: 6.2.3) in
NRL Investments v Singtel Optus the Full Court offered obiter in the compa-
rable setting of a subscriber to a cloud storage service that there could be a
joint exercise of an exclusive right: “our preferred view would be that both
Optus and the subscriber, acting together, were the makers of the copies”. A
platform and supplier of content joint exercise offers some cogency, especially
when considered together with a general principle of joint tortfeasor liability
discussed in Chapter 7 (see: 7.7).27°

6.7.3.3 Broadcasting

Finally, for exercises of the communication to the public right that also fall
within the scope of the definition of “broadcast” within the 1968 Act (i.e. the
delivery of programs by broadcasting services under the Broadcasting Services
Act 1992) there is a separate deeming provision as to who is exercising the
right. Section 22(5) provides that “a broadcast is taken to be made by the person
who provided the broadcasting service by which the broadcast was delivered”.
This was intended as a carve-out from s 22(6) so as to avoid “the potential
for conferring copyright on channel providers and production companies
who may determine the content of the broadcast” .1” Thus, the provision is
directed to the operation of the s 99 broadcast copyright ownership provision
in Part IV; a sound or television broadcast as copyright subject matter. In this
an important feature of Australian copyright law should be highlighted. When
a broadcast is made for the purpose of Part IV copyright subsistence (i.e. as
a “noun” as explained at the start of the chapter), the broadcasting service is
exercising the communication to the public right (ie. a “verb”) by that same
transmission in any subsisting Part III works and Part IV other subject matter
that is included in the broadcast.

6.7.4 Communication to the public and its relation to other
copyright rights of action

While s 27 amplifies the separate natures of the “in public” rights from the
communication to the public right, the latter right straddles diverse activi-
ties and content. If communicated in Australia, the provision of a library of
cinematograph films for streaming or the provision of a catalogue of literary
works for download are both exercises of the right. The right gives rise to many
issues of coherence in copyright policy, as it collapses and brings together
both 20th century broadcasting-type exploitation and distribution of copies
(the latter formerly only effected by High Street store retail of chattels such as
books, CDs and DVDs) into the one exclusive right. In so doing, it reflects a
reality that in the 21st century for many copyright-dependent industries the
distribution of physical copies has been supplanted by the provision of online
services.
When introduced in the Digital Agenda Reforms, the Explanatory

Memorandum was explicit in stating:

The term “communicate” is limited to the availability of material online or

through electronic transmissions. It is not intended to cover the physical

distribution of copyright material in a tangible form, which is adequately

covered by existing provisions of the Act.

In the civil provisions, physical distribution of “articles” for commercial dealing
is covered by s 38 for Part III works, and s 103 for Part IV subject matter - as
discussed in Chapter 7 (see 7.5). In 2006, however, an article was defined in
these provisions by amendment to include a “reproduction or copy of a work
or other subject matter, being a reproduction or copy in electronic form” 2”
This was done to ensure that the definition of “article” in the civil provisions
“is consistent with the definition in the related criminal offences” which were
introduced at the same time.“ The intent was to avoid the outcome that the
“online distribution of ... an infringing electronic reproduction may have
constituted a criminal offence, but not necessarily a civil infringement” “** The
relevant criminal provision, s 132AI, entailed a prohibition upon the person
who “distributes an article”. However unlike ss 38 and 103, for s 132AI “distrib-
ute” was expressly defined: “includes distribute by way of communication”
Section 132AI can be understood as the closest the 1968 Act comes to attaching
criminal liability to the communication to the public right. So that while it
seems clear enough that s 132AI creates criminal liability for the communica-
tion of electronic copies, whether corresponding civil liability arises (or should
arise) under ss 38 and 103 is not clear. First, while ss 38 and 103 deploy the
language of “distribution” it is neither defined to include communication nor
should be interpreted to include communication. Moreover, as a policy matter

it is not clear why ss 38 and 103 should create such civil liability for commu-
nication, which in all cases requires a mental element, if that liability overlaps
with liability arising from a direct exploitation of the communication to the
public right which in no case requires a mental element. The new definition of
“article” in ss 38 and 103 should be interpreted in a more confined way to refer
merely to dealings with physical electronic copies (such as compact discs and
DVDs) as chattels.®° While this does seem contrary to the 2006 statutory intent,
it equally seems that the 2006 drafting creates absurdity and inconsistency
with the equally clear 2001 intent underpinning the term “communicate”. The
2006 inclusion of the definition of article in ss 38 and 103, in the context of its
accompanying Explanatory Memorandum, leads to incoherence that can only
be adequately addressed by further statutory amendment.

Another issue of coherence that the sheer breadth of the communication
to the public right creates is its relationship to authorisation liability. While
authorisation liability will be discussed in Chapter 7 (see: 7.4) it should be
noted here that a person who exploits copyright by its communication to the
public may be doing so in such a way that the end recipient is also exploit-
ing the right to reproduce the work in a material form or to copy the subject
matter communicated. Depending upon the circumstances, it could be open
to characterise the person communicating the subject matter as also author-
ising the reproduction or copying by the end recipient. So that in Universal
Music Australia v Cooper, the mere provision of a webpage - <www.mp3s4free
.net> — of aggregated hyperlinks to remotely-hosted files was sufficient to
find in the defendant liability for authorising the downloads made by those
who clicked on the hyperlinks provided. Had the defendant uploaded the
infringing material made available via <www.mp3s4free.net>, and thereby
more obviously infringed by communication to the public, there seems no legal
or logical reason why the defendant would not similarly incur allied liability
for authorising the resulting downloads.**

6.8 To make an adaptation and to reproduce, publish,
perform, or communicate the adaptation

Sections 31(1)(a)(vi) and (vii) entail a bundled regime of exclusive rights attach-
ing to literary, dramatic and musical works in respect of the making of certain
adaptations of those works and further exploitations of the adapted work. The
former provision in paragraph (vi) presents difficult questions of overlap - or
at least blurred edges - with the reproduction right, while the latter provision,
paragraph (vii), is not well understood.

6.8.1 Adaptation: traditional works

For the purposes of paragraph (vi), an adaptation is exhaustively defined in
s 10(1) as comprising one of six things and the High Court has intimated that
for anything to qualify as an adaptation it must itself be a work.’ While some
are oddly drafted, all are derived from the 1956 Act except for the computer
program related aspect of the adaptation definition which was added in 1984.
Leaving aside the addition of computer programs (dealt with below) the
substance of the “traditional” aspects of the definition can be explained as
follows.”

Paragraphs (a) and (b) respectively define adaptation as: a dramatic
work (e.g. a screenplay) from a non-dramatic literary work (e.g. a novel), or
anon-dramatic work (e.g. a student study guide) from a dramatic work (e.g.
a libretto). These paragraphs are premised on a clear distinction between the
concepts “dramatic” and “non-dramatic” which may be less than clear as
observed in Chapter 2 (see: 2.6).

In the English decision Hodgson v Isaac a screenplay was held to comprise
an adaption of an autobiographical (non-fiction) literary work titled Flipper’s
Side on the basis of the trial judge’s conclusion that:

Having read Flipper’s Side and the script, as Ihave, the elements reproduced
are not merely a generic story about a disabled football fan but are recognis-
able as part of the particular story told in Flipper’s Side. The details and
incidents which are reproduced and their interpretation are a key part of
what makes Flipper’s Side itself an original work.

In Sillitoe v McGraw-Hill (another English case) the adaptation was from
dramatic form to literary form. George Bernard Shaw’s play Saint Joan was
the subject of a set of student study notes, published by the defendant, which
included a summary of each of the play’s scenes. The trial judge observed
about the study notes that:

Each scene summary recounts or describes, and in some detail, the events
and conversations that are to be found in the corresponding scene in the
play. These scene summaries run together, seem to me to constitute more
than a synopsis of the play ... After reading the text of Saint Joan and then
the scene summaries, my view is that the author of the scene summaries
has, in writing these scene summaries, converted the play Saint Joan into a
non-dramatic work; and has accordingly made an ‘adaptation’ 1

There is no reason to conclude that either of these two English trial decisions
would be analysed differently under the 1968 Act.

Under paragraph (c)(i) for either a literary or dramatic work, a transla-
tion of the work into a different language is an adaptation. Translation, as a
recognised exclusive right, has a pedigree which dates to the inception of the
Berne Convention in 1886.1”

Paragraph (c)(ii) provides that for either a literary or dramatic work, an
adaptation is a version in which “a story or action is conveyed solely or prin-
cipally by means of pictures”. In its 1956 Act form this type of adaptation was
elaborated with the additional words “in a form suitable for reproduction in a
book, or in a newspaper, magazine or similar periodical” As such, the right
in the UK has been referred to as the “strip cartoon” right. The background
to the Australian enactment fails to explicate its more abbreviated drafting,
although no evidence has been put forward to suggest there was any intent in
the 1968 Australian Parliament to diverge from the operation and effect of the
UK progenitor provision.

Paragraph (d) provides that in relation to a musical work, an adaptation
is an arrangement or transcription. The meaning of this has been considered
in a somewhat obscure setting. In Re Transistor Music and Australian Trade
Commission the issue was whether Wilson, a composer of advertising jingles,
was entitled to an export development grant.** The relevant legislation author-
ised export grants to facilitate the licensing of existing intellectual property, but
not grants to facilitate the commissioning of new subject matter. In this context
Senior Member McMahon of the Australian Administrative Appeals Tribunal
was asked to consider whether three works of Wilson, used by Singapore
Airlines, were adaptations of one of his existing musical works. If they were
adaptations, the Singaporean usage was the licensing of an existing work and
a business development trip to Singapore was grant-eligible. If they were not
adaptations, and thus regarded as the commissioning of fresh works, the trip
was grant-ineligible. In making his determination, McMahon SM relied upon
a passage from The Oxford Companion to Music (9th edition) on “Arrangement
or Transcription”:

By ‘arrangement’ in music is meant the adaptation to one musical medium
of music originally composed for another - the recasting of a song as a
piano piece, of an orchestral overture as an organ piece, and so on. Such a
process, if seriously undertaken, commonly involves much more than the
transferring from the one medium to the other of passages as they stand,
since many passages that are effective on one instrument would sound
ludicrous, or at any rate much less effective, on another. It is necessary, then,
that the arranger should consider, not so much how nearly he can reproduce

a given passage in the new medium, as, rather, how the composer would
have written it, had that medium been the original one.

In relation to the three musical works used by Singapore Airlines, the Senior
Member considered that:

The derivatives were different from the original. Their common authorship
was apparent. Their common origin from the same recording studio and
production company could not be denied. Their indebtedness in mood,
colour, texture and presentation to [Wilson’s earlier musical work] was
incontrovertible. Yet, nevertheless, they could not be said to consist of
arrangements or transcriptions of the original piece within the meaning of
the definitions that I have quoted above.

In view of this conclusion, a trip to Singapore made by Wilson related to the

Singapore Airline transaction was ineligible for an export grant under the

government scheme.

6.8.2 Adaptation: computer programs

Paragraph (ba) provides for a computer program literary work that an
adaptation is any version of the work (whether or not in its own or different
programming language or code) not being a reproduction of the work. As
noted above this was introduced in 1984 (in the midst of the Apple Computer
v Computer Edge litigation) and is the only adaptation type that expressly
distinguishes itself from reproduction.’” At the time of enactment, the
Explanatory Memorandum observed that of the existing adaptation categories
“only translation is likely to be relevant to adaptation of programs and there
are legal doubts as to whether this refers only to translations between human
languages” .** It then went on to explain the reform in these terms:

It is also possible for a program to be converted from object code into source
code, or between different languages of similar level. In some circum-
stances this process will result largely in a substantial reproduction of the
original program. In other cases, however, such as compilation followed
by de-compilation, the differences may be so substantial that one cannot
speak of a reproduction although the final product is clearly derived from
the original. The new definition of adaptation is intended to cover such
situations.”

The provision, with its express exclusion of reproduction, needs to be consid-

ered in tandem with the s 21(5) deeming provision (introduced later in 2000)

for what amounts to a reproduction of a computer program which is discussed

above.* As also observed above (see: 6.8.1) an adaption (unlike a reproduc-
tion) seems to require authorship. Moreover, the concern that functionality
protection does not fall within the copyright domain applies with equal force
here. So that in Data Access Corporation v Powerflex Services the High Court joint
judgment relied upon the above-quoted passages from the 1984 Explanatory
Memorandum to conclude that: “Parliament did not intend the word ‘version’
to cover situations where, although the functionality of a computer program
was copied, original code has been written to perform that function” 2

6.8.3 Section 31(1)(a)(vii) and the rights of the adaptor

The operation of s 31(1)(a)(vii) is not well understood: it is a clear “reach
through” right for the owner of the work that is the subject of the adaption. It
does not allocate rights to an author who has made an adaptation, such as the
screenwriter who adapts a novel. The screenwriter, as author of a dramatic
work, receives his or her full bundle of direct exclusive rights under s 31(1)(a)
and (c). Rather, in this example, s 31(1)(a)(vii) applies as one of the rights
attaching to the copyright owner of the literary work comprising the novel.
It applies so that any of: to reproduce the screenplay in a material form; to
publish the screenplay; to perform the screenplay in public, or to communicate
the screenplay to the public is the exercise of an exclusive right in the novel.
This was explained in 1982 by Robert Goff J in the English case Redwood Music
v Chappell & Co decided under the 1956 UK Act:
An author composes a work: the copyright in the original work vests in
him. He then licenses another person to arrange or adapt it - for example, to
base a film script or a play upon a book. The copyright in the arrangement
then vests in the arranger, who has originated it. Normally, of course, the
licence to make the arrangement or adaptation will carry with it a licence,
for example, to perform the adaptation; but theoretically, if it did not do so,
a performance of the adaptation could be restrained by the owner of the
copyright in the original work as an infringement of that copyright. But it
does not of course follow that the owner of the copyright in the original work
owns the copyright in the arrangement, for example the film script or play.”
This analysis is based upon a reading of the counterpart language to
s 31(1)(a)(vii).* It creates overlapping sets of exclusive rights in an authored
work that is an adaptation of a work in which copyright subsists. To return
to the example above, the novelist and the screenwriter will each in tandem
own their own set of exclusive rights in the screenplay in relation to the: repro-
duction, publication, performance and communication rights. However the
screenwriter, alone, will also own the adaptation rights in the screenplay - i.e.
those under s 31(1)(a)(vi) and (vii).

6.9 To enter into a commercial rental arrangement

TRIPS introduced obligations to confer upon copyright owners of computer
programs, sound recordings and cinematograph films the right to control the
“commercial rental to the public” of copies of their subject matter.“ The obliga-
tion to confer this right in relation to cinematograph film was attenuated insofar
as a WIO member need not introduce such a right until such time as “such
rental has led to widespread copying” of films.» Australia took advantage of
that flexibility — the widespread commercial rental of pre-recorded magnetic
video tapes in Australia had not given rise to endemic tape-to-tape copying
in part because of the prevalence of anti-copying technology (Macrovision’s
Analog Protection System) which degraded resultant copies. Also, the obli-
gation under TRIPS in relation to computer programs did not apply where
“the program itself is not the essential object of the rental” so as to avoid the
absurdity of the right being triggered by (say) the rental of such chattels as a
modern motor vehicle or refrigerator. The primary mischief that TRIPS was
directed at was the prevalence of commercial lending libraries with holdings
of copies of sound recordings and computer programs being loaned out for
short periods where an expectation existed that a purpose of the transaction
was to facilitate the borrower making a retained copy. Such activities have
been overtaken by the internet age. The commercial rental right presents as a
clumsy dead-letter right and a reminder of the limited shelf-life of law reform
based on technological specificity.

The Australian implementation, while excluding cinematograph film as
permitted under TRIPS, extended the right to beyond the TRIPS requirements
by conferring the rental right (expressed as “to enter into a commercial rental
arrangement” so as to implicate both contracting parties) not merely upon
sound recordings and computer programs as a literary work subset, but also
upon other literary works, all dramatic and all musical works reproduced in
a sound recording.” It is a right that straddles Parts III and IV and in s 30A
is a general definition of “commercial rental arrangement” which expressly
excludes public lending activities, intended to encompass settings such as
“where a school requires students to pay a deposit which is refunded in full
when the copy of the sound recording is returned” 2° However, the definition
is equally intended to include so-called “buy-back” schemes which legally
involve the buying and selling of copies of sound recordings or computer
programs, but which are in substance commercial rentals. Here the distinc-

tion between the commercial rental right and the basic cause of action under
ss 38 and 103 warrants mention. The former is a direct exclusive right attaching
to the nominated subject matter per se, applying whether or not the rental
copies were made in infringement, applying to both contracting parties and
requiring no mental element for liability. The latter - which includes the letting
for hire of copies — is indirect liability and applies only in respect of copies
made in infringement of copyright, only to the person running the hire busi-
ness and only if that person “knew, or ought reasonably to have known, that
the making of the article constituted an infringement of copyright” 2

There has been only one case in which the commercial rental right has been
litigated which involved the rental of DVDs of cinematograph films. It will be
recalled that the TRIPS flexibility of excluding cinematograph film from the
rental right was availed of by Australia, and the TRIPS agreement itself was
concluded just prior to the displacement of magnetic video tape copies by
DVDs in commercial lending libraries. In relation to the commercial lending
right a dispute arose between the libraries and film production companies as
to whether the libraries’ activities implicated the commercial lending right
insofar as the DVDs included copies of two types of computer programs: (i)
the navigational program that permitted a user to access content via a menu;
and (ii) the digital file storage of the film per se. In relation to the latter, and
as was explained in Chapter 2, the court rejected that the digital file storage
fell within the definition of a “computer program” on a purposive reading of
the legislation.” As also noted in Chapter 2, on the jurisprudence post-IceTV
its characterisation as a computer program literary work is likely to be rejected
today on the clearer basis of simply being non-authorial.2 In relation to the
former, the navigational computer program, the court did not consider that
program to be “the essential object of the rental” of the DVDs in issue“ Rather,
the essential object of the rental of the DVDs was to access the cinematograph
films thereon copied - which was the very type of rental that Australia had
chosen to exclude from the scope of the rental right. As such, the video libraries
were found to have no commercial rental right liability to the film production
companies arising from their rental of DVDs.
