9.1 Introduction

A feature of the 1968 Act is its reliance upon compulsory licensing, which is
also commonly referred to as statutory licensing. In it there are two basic types
of compulsory licensing - those which do not designate a central administering
body and those which rely on such a body, typically a declared collecting
society which has been so declared by an exercise of Commonwealth executive
power. The first type without a central administration body comprises: Part
IIL, Division 6 (recording of musical works and any related lyrics which has a
pedigree dating to the 1911 Act); s 108 (causing published sound recordings
to be heard in public countenanced by the Rome Convention art 12); and s 109
(including published sound recordings in broadcasts also countenanced by
the Rome Convention art 12). There are also some minor, related provisions
(ss 47(3), 70(3), 107(3)) which convert a free exception for broadcasters to copy

works and sound recordings (to permit the making of a 12-month retention
copy) into a remunerated exception if the copy is included in a third-party
broadcast. The second type of compulsory licence relies ona central administra-
tion body and comprises: Part IVA, Division 4 (copying and communication by
educational institutions and which rationalises two earlier compulsory licences
in Part VA and Part VB); Part VC (retransmission of free-to-air broadcasts); and
Part VD (re-broadcasts in regional areas by satellite broadcasters). In relation
to compulsory licences an administrative body, the Copyright Tribunal of
Australia, has conferred upon it decision-making powers that include a power
to determine or adjust the royalty rates. It may also under s 161 refer questions
of law arising under these powers to the Federal Court of Australia.

Aside from the compulsory licences there also exists in Part VII,
Division 2 an eminent domain exception that permits the exploitation of
third-party exclusive rights by the Commonwealth or a State where State here
includes the Australian Capital Territory, the Northern Territory and Norfolk
Island.* The exception provides for the relevant government giving notice to,
and coming to terms with, affected rights holders. Jurisdiction is conferred
upon the Copyright Tribunal of Australia to fix terms in default of agreement.
Moreover for the making by Commonwealth or State governments of copies
(and reproductions) that fall within classes administered by one or more
declared collecting societies (“government copies”?), the notice and coming
to terms with are dealt through the relevant collecting society with Copyright
Tribunal oversight. Again, questions of law arising under these powers can be
referred to the Federal Court of Australia under s 161.

There also exists quite important powers in the Copyright Tribunal over
a “licence scheme”, defined to mean a scheme formulated by a licensor which
sets out the classes of cases in which the licensor can grant licences and the
applicable charges. Licence (since 2006) means “a licence granted by or on
behalf of the owner or prospective owner of the copyright in a work or other
subject-matter to do an act comprised in the copyright”. Questions of law
that arise in the exercise of this power can also be the subject of as 161 referral
to the Federal Court of Australia. The breadth of this power in the Tribunal,
and the role of the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission in the
exercise of that power, requires some explanation.

9.2 Compulsory licences in general

A compulsory licence is, within its typically legislated form, a public law
mandatory offer of a licence to exercise exclusive rights on conditions that
include most prominently that those who elect to avail themselves of the
licence are obligated to make royalty payments to affected copyright owners.
Acompulsory licence is a legal setting which plots a mid-way course between
an exclusive right and a free exception, so that within the scope of the licence an

exclusive right is converted into a right to remuneration. However, the reasons
why such a mid-way setting is selected (as opposed to full liability or a free
exception) are sometimes not so easy to state as a matter of general principle.
Since the mid-1990s navigating the TRIPS three-step test is a discipline that
policy-makers must consider for virtually all copyright exceptions and compul-
sory licences are a means to assuage concerns about unreasonable prejudice to
rights holders’ legitimate interests. However, as the treatment below reveals
each of the current compulsory licences has its own drivers that represent an
instant-specific mix of economic, historic, social and political factors. To the
extent that any identifiable common-denominator matters of principle emerge
they lie in the coincidence of: (i) a desire to facilitate an activity seen as a good
thing for society; and (ii) the existence of discrete economic value in the hands
of the actor exploiting copyright who deliver that good thing.

On first enactment the 1968 Act had three distinct compulsory licence
regimes - leaving aside the minor exceptions relation to broadcasters’ copies.‘
The first, for the recording of musical works, pre-dates the 1968 Act and
indeed has a pedigree that originates in the 1911 Act. The other two related
to so-called “secondary uses” of sound recordings which were introduced at
the time of the 1968 Act.* Disputes over these two compulsory licences were
blamed by Sawyer, writing in 1969, for contributing to the “scandalous delay”
in the enactment of the 1968 Act, and were regarded by him as having “no
relationship to the general principles of copyright law” and “unintelligible
unless put in the context of the complex commercial arrangements which they
serve” .’ The compulsory licences added to the 1968 Act by amendments are all
regimes that rely upon a central administering body or bodies: for education
(introduced in the 1980s and rationalised in 2017), for retransmission (added
in 2000) and for satellite re-broadcasting within regional areas (added in 2010).

9.3.1 Recording musical works: Part Ill, Division 6

Prior to 1908 the making of a sound recording - for example, a pianola roll - of
a musical work was not regarded within the Berne Convention as an exercise
of copyright in the musical work. With its origins in protecting the Swiss
music box industry, a protocol to the original 1886 Convention stated: “It is
understood that the manufacture and sale of instruments for the mechani-
cal reproduction of musical works in which copyright subsists shall not be
considered as constituting an infringement of musical copyright.”* Judicial

decisions in the USA and the UK prior to 1908 had buttressed this political
exclusion by holding that a pianola roll could not under general principles
comprise a reproduction of a musical work because it was merely part of a
mechanical contrivance for producing musical notes and could not be read
as music by the human eye.’ It was not until the Berlin revision to Berne
in 1908 that the protocol excluding mechanical reproduction was removed
from the Convention. Thus in revised art 13(1) was a new recognition that
musical work owners have the right of “authorizing the adaptation of those
works to instruments that can reproduce them mechanically”. However to
cushion phonograph manufacturers against the impact of having to pay
copyright licence fees for the first time, and to guard against the establishment
of the monopolistic aggregation of musical work titles by large phonograph
manufacturers, the decision was made to introduce a compulsory licensing
flexibility. So that in art 13(2) - the substance of which remains today in the
Berne Convention - that right could be made by Berne Union members subject
to local compulsory licences: “Reservations and conditions relating to the
application of this article may be determined by the legislation of each country
in so far as it is concerned; but all such reservations and conditions shall apply
only in the countries which imposed them.”

Notwithstanding a rejection of the idea by the Gorell Committee, the UK
Parliament ultimately decided late in the law-making process to include such
a compulsory licence in the 1911 Act when conferring the mechanical repro-
duction right upon musical works." The Parliament accepted that the vested
interests of manufacturers (which had operated for decades in a copyright-
free environment) could only be preserved by the inclusion of a compulsory
licence. This comprised s 19 of the 1911 Act - which was by far the longest and
most complex provision of the Act. The licence became operative if the musical
work's rights holder had previously consented or acquiesced to the mechanical
reproduction of the work and the licensee paid the prescribed royalty “on the
ordinary retail selling price” of its mechanical reproduction.

That royalty rate was set at 5% with a power in the Board of Trade, if it
considered after holding a public inquiry that the rate “is no longer equitable”
to order a decrease or increase to the rate but any such order had no effect
unless confirmed by the UK Parliament.» In 1928 the Board of Trade ordered,
and the UK Parliament confirmed, an increase of that rate to 6.25%. However,
that increase had no effect in Australia, with the High Court holding in 1958

that the UK Act confirming the royalty increase was not intended to have
“Imperial” effect, so that s 19 of the 1911 Act continued its Australian operation
at the 5% rate.4* The Spicer Committee in 1959, while recommending the carry-
ing forward of the licence into the 1968 Act, framed the licence as an example
where “history perhaps more than logic” explained the licence and its modern
continuation. Re-enacted in 1968 as Division 6 of Part III, a royalty rate of 5%
of retail selling price was carried forward from the 1911 Act's regime There
was, however, a power in the Attorney-General to ask the newly established
Copyright Tribunal to hold an inquiry into the rate and report the result of
its inquiry to the Attorney-General who, armed with the report, could make
regulations adjusting the rate. Under these arrangements, the Copyright
Tribunal was given its very first job to do. Ina lengthy December 1979 report
the Copyright Tribunal recommended increasing the rate to 6.75% of retail
price.” When the report was published in mid-1980, controversy erupted.®
It was not until two elections later and a change of government that a new
Attorney-General in November 1983 made regulations prescribing a rate at
6.25% of retail price. After the controversy had died down, the whole experi-
ence had proved sufficiently scarring for the Attorney-General’s Department
that s 55 of the 1968 Act was amended in 1989 so that the power was shifted to
the Copyright Tribunal itself to determine a rate in the absence of agreement,
with a floor rate set in the legislation at 6.25% of retail price.” Hence today,
the “prescribed royalty” is that agreed between the manufacturer and the
copyright owner, or as determined by the Tribunal, or if neither agreement
nor determination, 6.25% of retail selling price of the record.

Aside from rate controversies the salient features of the compulsory licence
ins 55 and 59 are:

* The licence applies to musical works, and any literary or dramatic
works sung or spoken in association with the music (e.g. lyrics or
libretto), where those works have been previously incorporated in
commercial sound recordings made in Australia or in a Berne/ UCC/

WCT/WTO member country, or imported to Australia or to a Berne/
UCC/WCT/WTO member country, by or with the permission of the
relevant copyright owners of the works.

* The licence is only in relation to making a “record” of those works,
defined for the Division to mean “a disc, tape, paper or other device in
which sounds are embodied”. This is a subset of the general definition
of “record” for sound recording purposes which is defined in s 10 to
also include an “electronic file”. An Explanatory Memorandum to the
2006 reforms that created this definitional distinction reveals that the
intent was to exclude from the operation of the statutory licence “digi-
tal downloads and ring-tone sales”.” The licence is to manufacture
chattels which exist essentially to embody sounds.

+ The licence requires the giving, prior to any making of a record under
it, a prescribed notice to the copyright owners or their agents directly or
if their identities are unknown, a public notice in the Commonwealth
Government Gazette. The licence also requires payment of the
“prescribed royalty” explained above. Since 1979 a peak body estab-
lished to administer its members’ so-called “mechanical reproduction
rights” in their musical, dramatic and literary works is the Australasian
Mechanical Copyright Owners Society (AMCOS) - which since 1997
has operated in close alliance with its sister society the Australasian
Performing Right Association (APRA).

+ Itisclear enough that an Australian manufacturer who has duly given
the prescribed notice and acted within the licence will have not created.
any s 38 liability in those who then deal with the resulting records
of the musical works and associated literary or dramatic works. The
making of those records did not constitute an infringement of that Part
Ill copyright. Moreover the language of ss 37 and 38 is such that an
importer of sound recording chattels may, if the licence applies to the
works, give the prescribed notice to avoid enlivening liability under
either s 37 for the importation or under s 38 for subsequent dealings
of the imported records. That is because the provisions speak of, in
the case of importation, liability only if the dealing were undertaken
in the knowledge that “if the article had been made in Australia by
the importer” it would have constituted an infringement. Thus an
importer who had duly given a prescribed notice and submitted to
the compulsory licence was indeed licensed to make those articles.

On the final point concerning importers it is useful to note the Chapter 7
(see: 7.5.1.3) discussion of Raben Footwear v Polygram Records.» There the Full
Court’s analysis of knowledge in the context dealings liability exposed the
limits of the compulsory licence for the importer of the compact discs in issue.

Submission to the compulsory licence in that case would have effectively
cleared all the rights in the Part III works, but because the discs were parallel
imports, and because the importation occurred in 1995 (i.e. prior to the 1998
sound recordings parallel importation exception”’) the compulsory licence
provided no answer to liability then arising under s 102 dealings liability in
respect of Polygram’s sound recording rights.

For reasons that will be explained below (see: 9.6.3) when discussing licence

licence in the internet age is worthy of a serious review.

9.3.2 Causing published sound recordings to be heard in
public: section 108

In 1933 Maugham J held that the playing by a Bristol coffee shop of a record of
public domain music for the enjoyment of its patrons amounted to an exercise
by the shop of a hitherto uncertain right of public performance attached to
the copyright in the sound recording.” Discussed in Chapter 3 (see: 3.2) was
how under the regime of the 1911 Act “copyright shall subsist in records,
perforated rolls, and other contrivances by means of which sounds may be
mechanically reproduced, in like manner as if such contrivances were musical
works”, and the 1933 Bristol coffee shop decision interpreted this as including
the same performance in public right as was then attached to musical works.
The decision was regarded as effectively settling the question of whether
the public performance right so attached, and it was consistent with a 1929
Australian decision that had also confirmed the radio broadcasting of a sound
recording also in that era involved the public performance exclusive right in
a sound recording.” These decisions meant that parallel sets of performance
rights could subsist in both a sound recording and the musical work embod-
ied therein. Thus, the creation of expressly enumerated exclusive rights for
sound recordings in s 85 of the 1968 Act (consistent with s 12(5) of its 1956 UK
counterpart Act) was a development to which transitional provisions applied.°
In 2012 the High Court unanimously interpreted the regime change and the
transitional provisions as abolishing the old rights in sound recordings and
substituting for them “distinct and fresh rights” which were, in the cases of
the public performance and broadcast exclusive rights, subject to compulsory
licences.

The s 108 compulsory licence applies to the s 85(1)(b) exclusive right (“to
cause the recording to be heard in public”) although for reasons explained
in Chapter 13 this exclusive right does not attach to all subsisting sound

recordings.” The compulsory licence, and the more significant licence pertain-
ing to the s 85(1)(c) exclusive right in s 109 (discussed next) were, as Sawyer
alludes to, compromises that permitted the government to expressly enact the
exclusive rights in s 85(1)(b) and (c) without unduly disrupting other stake-
holders - most notably broadcasters. The possibility for compulsory licences
being attached to such so-called “secondary uses” of sound recording was
also expressly countenanced in the 1961 Rome Convention although this was
merely a background matter to the drafting of the 1968 Act.** The licences were
neither recommended by the Spicer Committee nor included in the 1956 Act,
and (not unlike the licence for the mechanical reproduction right of musical
works inserted to the 1911 Act) these licences were added late in the law-
making process in what might be colloquially called a political bun fight.

The s 108 licence subjects the s 85(1)(b) exclusive right to a compulsory
licence available to anyone who (failing agreement on terms with the owner)
undertakes to pay equitable remuneration to the owner as determined by the
Copyright Tribunal which has jurisdiction for that purpose under s 151. To
date, that jurisdiction has not been exercised. Rather it seems that the sound
recording public performance right has only come before the Tribunal pursu-
ant to its jurisdiction in respect of voluntary licence schemes under ss 154 and
155 (see: 9.6).23

9.3.3 Including published sound recordings in broadcasts:
section 109

The big cousin to the s 108 licence is the s 109 licence for the “making of a
broadcast” of a sound recording, which is today in respect of a subset of the
s 85(c) exclusive right “to communicate the recording to the public” .*° Much of
the background to this licence has been covered in the discussion of s 108 above
(see: 9.3.2) and issues as to its scope in relation to subsisting sound recordings
will be explained in Chapter 13.” It broadly follows the logic of ss 108 and 151.

The s 109 licence subjects the s 85(c) exclusive right to a compulsory licence
available to a maker of a broadcast who, failing an agreement as to terms with
the owner, undertakes to pay the owner equitable remuneration as determined
by the Copyright Tribunal which has jurisdiction for the purpose under s 152.
However, here there is a salient difference with the ss 108 and 151 regime:
whereas the Tribunal’s jurisdiction as to quantum of equitable remuneration
in s 151 for the s 108 licence is more or less entirely a matter for Tribunal
determination, the s 152 jurisdiction for s 109 is subject to two price caps. For
acommercial radio broadcaster no amount ordered under s 152 can exceed 1%
of its gross earnings. For the public broadcaster, the Australian Broadcasting
Corporation, no amount ordered under s 152 in respect of its sound broadcasts
can exceed “one-half of one cent” multiplied by the official estimate of the
Australian population.”

The compulsory licence caps of ss 109 and 152 (a hard-fought compro-
mise“) have obviously stuck in the craw of the recording industry. This is
well evidenced by a 2012 constitutional case brought by the sound recording
industry to challenge the validity of the 1% cap pertaining to commercial radio
broadcasters, which unsuccessfully claimed it to be an acquisition of property
in pre-1969 sound recordings not on just terms." Today the caps do appear as
involving anachronistic price controls that belong to another era, and provide
the commercial context to what was at stake in Phonographic Performance
Company of Australia v Commercial Radio Australia discussed in Chapter 3
(see: 3.4) and Chapter 6 (see: 6.7.1) on the issues of the legal characterisation
the internet streaming of a radio broadcast. The holding that such streaming
was not broadcasting took the activity outside the immediate clutches of the
1% cap, but as discussed below (see: 9.6.4.2), did not entirely remove it from
the influence of the cap. Like ss 108 and 151, sitting adjacent to ss 109 and 152
is the voluntary licence scheme jurisdiction in the Tribunal under ss 154 and
155 which is discussed later in this chapter (see: 9.6).

9.3.4 Third-party broadcasting of a broadcaster’s copy:
sections 47(3), 70(3), 107(3)

Summarised in Chapter 10 (see: 10.8) are unremunerated exceptions that
permit a broadcaster (the primary broadcaster) to make copies of its broadcasts
for retention for 12 months. Two exceptions apply to works (s 47 for literary,
dramatic or musical works and s 70 for artistic works) and the other (s 107)
applies to sound recordings. An adjunct aspect of all three exceptions are
compulsory licences operating in favour of a third-party broadcaster of that
copy on condition that the third party has paid to the owners of copyright in
the works or sound recording (as the case may be) an agreed amount or an
undertaking to pay an amount determined by the Copyright Tribunal in which
relevant jurisdiction is separately conferred* Significantly, these compulsory
licences do not apply to the copyright in broadcast made by the primary
broadcaster.

9.441 Copying and communication by educational institutions:
Part IVA, Division 4

The outcome of University of New South Wales » Moorhouse discussed in
Chapter 7 (see: 7.4) has been said to have disabused Australian universities
of their “unarticulated assumption that they were either in a copyright-free
environment or that their broader educational and public purposes somehow
removed them from potential liability” “ The finding of authorisation liabil-
ity was the spark that gave rise to the 1976 Copyright Law Committee on
Reprographic Reproduction, known as the “Franki Committee” named after
its chair who was a Justice of the Australian Industrial Court. One of its recom-
mendation was for the introduction of “a statutory licence scheme permitting,
a non-profit educational establishment to make multiple copies of parts of a
work and in some cases of whole works for classroom use or for distribution
to students, subject to recording any copying taking place under the scheme
and an obligation to pay an appropriate royalty if demanded by the copyright
owner or his agent”. This Franki Committee recommendation, focused on
photocopier use in teaching, was the basis of the 1980 reforms adding inter alia
the (since repealed) s 53B (“Multiple copying under statutory licence by educa-
tional institutions”) and s 149A (“ Applications to Tribunal for determination of
remuneration payable to owner of copyright for copies made under statutory

licence”) regime. While burdened by cumbersome reporting obligations on
educational users, this regime had as its model the sound recording statutory
licence regimes added to the 1968 Act. However, this model was ultimately
seen to be unfit for purpose in the educational context.

With the proliferation of magnetic tape-recording technologies - and
in particular off-air video recording — issues of educational use of broad-
cast copying soon emerged in the early 1980s. In 1986 the Senate Standing
Committee on Education and the Arts considered a proposal by Senator
Durack (a former Attorney-General) that copyright in television and sound
broadcasts of the Australian Broadcasting Corporation (ABC) or the Special
Broadcasting Service (SBS) would not be infringed by the making of copies by
educational institutions for their educational purposes.” This proposal was
rejected by the Standing Committee in favour of a more comprehensive solu-
tion proposed by the Attorney-General’s Department. The proposal involved
a compulsory licence for the educational copying of broadcast programmes
and that “for the purposes of the scheme, copyright owners would be required
to exercise their rights through a single collecting society (approved by the
Attorney-General)”.

This Attorney-General’s Department suggestion was to be a stone that
killed two birds: it provided a model for a new audio-visual broadcast copying
licence for education and a model that overcame the shortcomings of the 1980
educational reprographic licence. It gave rise to 1989 reform adding a pair of
statutory licences based on comprehensive coverage and administration by
central collecting societies declared by the Attorney-General to be authorised
to so administer: Part VA for the copying of broadcasts by educational institu-
tions and Part VB for essentially reprographic copying of works and published
editions by educational institutions.* For both licences the basic obligation
on educational institutions (defined at all levels of tuition and with the initial
exclusion of “for-profit” bodies which was removed in 1998*) is to undertake
to pay “equitable remuneration” by giving to the declared society a remu-
neration notice. Equitable remuneration in turn is a quantum determined by
agreement or by the Tribunal in lieu of agreement (typically a royalty rate per
“equivalent full-time student unit”), arrived in either case without prescriptive
rates or caps legislated.*

This 1989 reform had an inauspicious beginning insofar as it was ruled
unconstitutional as a result of it also including the ill-fated (and now repealed)
compulsory licence for the use of blank tapes for private and domestic copy-
ing.” Part VA and Part VB were re-enacted in 1993.* In 2001 by the Digital
Agenda Reforms they were extended in scope so as to include most notably
exercises of the communication right by educational institutions for teaching
purposes.» In 2006 Copyright Tribunal oversight jurisdiction over the declara-
tion and administration of the declared societies themselves was introduced,
providing, for example, copyright owner members an opportunity for external
review of such things as distribution arrangements. Consistent with a 2013
ALRC recommendation, in 2017 further reform was undertaken to rationalise
(but not substantively alter) the educational licences.* They are now located
within Part IVA, Division 4 with the crux of the former Part VB now found in
s 113P(1), the crux of the former Part VA now s 113P(2), and the conferral of
all Copyright Tribunal jurisdiction (both rate determination and oversight) for
the purposes of both licences is provided for in s 153A. As revised, the new
provisions provide a quite non-prescriptive means for measuring the extent of
educational use under the statutory licences: essentially as agreed or as deter-
mined by the Tribunal.” The licences have been respectively administered by
the same two declared collecting societies since their inception.*

9.4.2 Retransmission of free-to-air broadcasts: Part VC

Retransmission is defined in the Berne Convention as “any communication to
the public, by wire or by rebroadcasting of the broadcast of the work, when this
communication is made by an organization other than the original one”.” With
broadcasting right it is expressly amenable to compulsory licensing within a
member country so long as such licensing is prejudicial to neither authors’
moral rights nor to their “right to obtain an equitable remuneration”. While
retransmission of free-to-air broadcasts by subscription television services
occurred as a mainstream activity in Europe and America since the 1960s,
because subscription television in Australia commenced in the 1990s, the issue

did not arise locally until then." Ultimately, Australia adopted a compulsory
licence for retransmission of Australian free-to-air broadcasts in the Digital
Agenda Reforms that was loosely modelled on the then current Part VA
compulsory licence. The Part VC compulsory licence for retransmission entails
reliance upon a sole declared collecting society for its administration and the
provision to it of a remuneration notice by any retransmitter seeking to avail
itself of the licence. The basic requirement for retransmitters is to undertake
by the notice to pay equitable remuneration which, similar to the educational
compulsory licences is not a prescribed rate but rather determined by agree-
ment or by the Tribunal in lieu.

Retransmission here should be understood in the technologically neutral
terms of that part of the communication right that deals with to “electroni-
cally transmit (whether over a path, or a combination of paths, provided by
a material substance or otherwise)”. In other words, “retransmitter” for the
purposes of Part VC statutory licence is not limited to mean “subscription
broadcaster”. However, an important limitation upon the scope of Part VC
is its non-application to retransmission that “takes place over the internet”.
Moreover, and unlike settings in other countries, Australian broadcasting law
in general prohibits the retransmission of a broadcast outside of its intended
broadcast footprint unless regulatory approval is given. Thus, in general, the
retransmission that occurs in Australia under Part VC is “local area” retrans-
mission. This is reflected by what is covered and what is not covered by the
statutory licence: copyright in all works, sound recordings and cinematograph
films included in a free-to-air broadcast is within the scope of the licence, but
not the copyright in the free-to-air broadcast itself. For the exercise of the
communication right in the broadcast by retransmission, a broader statutory
exemption within the Broadcasting Services Act 1992 applies to the extent the
retransmitter operates within the restrictions of that regime.”

9.4.3 Re-broadcasting into regional and remote areas by
satellite: Part VD

The final (and most recent) compulsory licence to be enacted, and which
follows the declared collecting society model, is found in Part VD of the 1968
Act and which deals with re-broadcasts by those who hold a special type of
satellite broadcasting licence under s 38C of the Broadcasting Services Act 1992.
This broadcasting licence under that 1992 Act was enacted at around the time
of the shut-down of terrestrial analogue broadcasting and reflected concern
about remote and regional areas which would not be adequately served by
commercial digital broadcasting. In reaction to that concern, a “section 38C
licensee” was created to be the beneficiary of a compulsion attached to other
commercial broadcasters to provide it with a simultaneous (or “as soon as
practicable”) feed of their broadcast programming for the purpose of it satellite
re-broadcasting the same into designated remote and regional areas. The
satellite re-broadcast should be subject to an approved conditional access
system so that only those who reside in or travel into (e.g. in satellite-equipped
campervans) the designated areas should be able to avail themselves of the
re-broadcasting service.® To facilitate this re-broadcasting it seems a belts and
braces approach was adopted to shield a s 38C licensee from any licensing
hold-ups. First, as a constitutional matter, s 43AD in the Broadcasting Services
Act 1992 provides that if the reforms result in an acquisition of property not on
just terms the s 38C licensee is liable to pay a reasonable amount of compensa-
tion to the person whose property is acquired. Second, in 2010 Part VD was
inserted into the 1968 Act to provide a compulsory licence which attaches
to all copyright subject matter included in a compelled broadcast, and the
broadcast subject matter itself.” Curiously, however, more than 10 years after
the commencement of the Part VD compulsory licence, no collecting society
has ever been declared to administer it. Therefore it most likely offends a
principle that should guide the creation of such a licence: that it should be
responsive to an actual, rather than an imaginary or confected, need.”

9.5 Crown exploitation of copyright: Part VII, Division 2
9.5.1 Background to section 183

As is discussed in Chapter 4 (see: 4.4), Crown ownership provisions of a type
seen in Part VII, Division 1 have existed in statutory copyright law since their
inclusion within the 1911 Act. While a Crown use compulsory licence has
existed in UK patent law since 1883 (where Crown liability coupled with the
compulsory licence replaced a prior setting of Crown immunity from patent
grants”) no similar provisions addressing Crown exploitation of copyright
existed in UK law at the time of the Gregory Committee in 1951. Observing this
to bean anomaly, the Gregory Committee considered that a measure should be
introduced to confer upon the Crown a right of compulsory use of third-party
copyright for national defence purposes. The Gregory Committee justified
the limited nature of the exception (subsequently introduced in an elliptical
manner in the UK through the Defence Contracts Act 1958”) on the basis that
“copyright covers a wide field over much of which, for example, copyright in
musical works, the needs for Crown use without the prior consent of the owner
are not the same, for example, of matters relating to military equipment”.
When the Spicer Committee reconsidered the issue, it commenced by
putting forward the then current view that: “The 1911 Act does not appear
to bind the Crown and, therefore, the Crown is not, in Australia, liable for
infringement”.” Implicit in what followed all members favoured the express
creation of civil copyright liability in the Crown - given effect by s 7 of the 1968
Act - albeit coupled with some form of exception. Three members favoured
the position put by the Commonwealth Solicitor-General for a broad Crown
use exception: “the Commonwealth and the States should be empowered to
use copyright material for any purposes of the Crown, subject to the payment
of just terms to be fixed, in the absence of agreement, by the Court”.* Two
members favoured the adoption of the UK position: “the Crown’s right to
use copyright material without the consent of the copyright owner should be
confined to use for defence purposes only”.” By the enactment of the s 183(1)
exception in the 1968 Act it was the former view that prevailed, and marked a

point of clear difference with UK law. The fundamental exception in s 183(1)
has not been amended in the life of the 1968 Act to date and provides in sweep-
ing terms that copyright in any work or other subject matter “is not infringed.
by the Commonwealth or a State, or by a person authorized in writing by the
Commonwealth or a State, doing any acts comprised in the copyright if the
acts are done for the services of the Commonwealth or State” - where “State”
is deemed under the 1968 Act to include the Australian Capital Territory, the
Northern Territory and Norfolk Island.*

9.5.2 Operation

The s 183 regime is not a compulsory licence. Leaving aside constitutional issues,
the exception in s 183(1) is not conditional upon the giving of notice or even the
payment of a reasonable royalty. The exception operates automatically within
its scope to ensure that any Commonwealth or State government's exploita-
tion (in the copyright territory”) of third-party copyright for the government's
provision of services is non-infringing. While s 183(4) requires the government
to provide a notice to the copyright owner “as soon as possible” after the
exploitation (the nature of the contents of the notice being prescribed®) and
s 183(5) provides that the ev post terms for the exploitation are those “agreed
between [the relevant government] and the owner of the copyright, or in
default of agreement, as are fixed by the Copyright Tribunal” (its jurisdiction
vested by s 153E), the s 183(1) exception is conditional on neither."

So that in a 1996 Copyright Tribunal determination under s 183(5), where
the government had failed to provide any s 183(4) notice, the exception was
presumed to apply and a determination was made.” In 2019, where a govern-
ment raised s 183(1) by way of one of its alternative defences to an infringement
action it also gave a conditional s 183(4) notice to Prodata Solutions (the copy-
right owner plaintiff) in the following terms:

THE STATE OF SOUTH AUSTRALIA HEREBY GIVES NOTICE pursu-
ant to s 183(4) of the Copyright Act 1968 (Cth) (the Act) that to the extent
that it is agreed, or the Federal Court (the Court) determines in action
no. SAD338/2017 (the proceedings), that copyright in any literary work
or compilation of such works owned by Prodata Solutions Pty Ltd

(ACN 058 014 823) (the claimant) the subject of the proceedings has been,
or would have been but for the operation of s 183(1) of the Act, infringed by
any act done by or on behalf of the South Australian Fire and Emergency
Commission (the agency), then ...°
The attack by Prodata Solutions on the validity of the notice was in support
of an argument that the notice’s invalidity made s 183(1) unavailable to the
government respondent. This was rejected by Charlesworth J in these terms:
In my view, the jurisdiction of the Copyright Tribunal to make an assess-
ment of the value of any use of the applicant’s copyright does not depend
upon the issue of a ‘valid’ (that is, unconditional) notice under s 183(4).
Properly construed, s 183(4) is facilitative in the sense that it brings to the
attention of a copyright owner the fact and extent of use, acknowledged
by the user, being actual use that might otherwise remain unknown to the
owner.
More generally Charlesworth J expressed the view that the notice, in the
circumstances of the case, was appropriate and fit for purpose, and that if it
were not, it could be cured by a fresh notice in due course.® The history, text
and limited jurisprudence reveal the s 183 regime to be better thought of as an
eminent domain power rather than an exception akin to a compulsory licence.

9.5.3 Scope

The sweeping nature of s 183(1) has a consequence that an Australian
quasi-autonomous public body might seek to fall within the ambit of “the
Commonwealth” or “a State” for s 183 purposes, and thereby immunise itself
from copyright infringement liability under the 1968 Act. This possibility
was foreseen by the Spicer Committee majority and which attempted its
foreclosure: “Our recommendation in this regard is, of course, confined to
the Crown in the strict legal sense of that term and is not intended to cover
statutory corporations, such as the Australian Broadcasting Commission and
the Australian National Airlines Commission, which are not entitled to the
privileges and immunities of the Crown.”* This view is consistent with Lord
Reid's observation a few years later that “nationalised industries for example
are not services of the Crown” .” However in that case the House of Lords also
held by majority that once a conclusion was arrived at that an entity was indeed
the Crown, anything done by that entity within its remit must be considered to
be done for the services of the Crown.® The implication of that House of Lords
majority view for (say) the Australian Broadcasting Commission (ABC) was

that if it was indeed held to be the Commonwealth, then axiomatically all its
broadcasting activities were for the services of the Commonwealth.

So that notwithstanding the views of the Spicer Committee majority, in
1982 the ABC did attempt to establish s 183(1) coverage in relation to its broad-
casting activities. This was met with a unanimous holding by the Full Court,
upon scrutiny of the statute establishing the ABC, that it neither fell within the
concept of “the Commonwealth” in s 183(1) nor was it an agent or instrumen-
tality of the Commonwealth for the purposes of s 183(1).° Sheppard J found
that the list of matters to be considered included the question of:

+ whether the Commission fulfils a governmental or non-governmental
function;

* the capacity of the Government to control the Commission’s activities;

+ the Commission’s financial autonomy;

+ the right of appointment and dismissal of the members of the
Commission and of its staff by the Government;

¢ whether it has duties to furnish information or accounts to the
Government;

* the Commission's power over assets in its ownership or control;

+ whether the property of the Commission isheld for the Commonwealth”

However, a body such as the Trade Practices Commission (the TPC which
became the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission or ACCC) at
around this time was held to fall within the protection of s 183 when it sought
to copy documents pursuant to its law enforcement remit." Sheppard J readily
concluded that the TPC was an agency or emanation of the Commonwealth
and that therefore the copyright exploitations in issue were for the services of
the Commonwealth.” The closer a public body comes to performing functions
or delivering services that are also undertaken by a private actor, the less likely
the public body will be regarded as the Commonwealth or the State for s 183
purposes. Any other approach would clearly offend competitive neutrality
principles by immunising one from copyright infringement liability while
leaving the other fully exposed.

In 2012 a case arose where Apotex, a private generic drug manufacturer,
had reproduced the literary work owned by a pioneer drug company to
comply with a Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) regulatory require-
ment to supply product information documentation (PID). It was argued
that Apotex could assert s 183 protection on the basis that the TGA was the
Commonwealth and therefore Apotex was “a person authorized in writing
by the Commonwealth”. In rejecting this argument the Full Court observed:

[T]he argument ... is not without its difficulties. Even if one were to
accept that the TGA’s practice authorised the reproduction of the same

product information, the assumption that the TGA should be taken to have
authorised the reproduction of a substantial part of the first respondent's
expression of the first respondent's PID, as distinct from the substantive
information contained in it, would require clear proof of the fact, after a
fair contest. More importantly for present purposes, no finding of fact was
sought or made before the primary judge that the TGA purported to give
such an authorisation to [Apotex] by reason of its practices in relation to
approving PIDs.®

However, the ventilation of the issue in this litigation triggered a specific new

free exception to copyright being introduced to the 1968 Act in 2011.

Where a person has been duly authorised in writing to exploit copyright
on behalf of a government, there are two specific provisions that may apply.
First, s 183(3) permits retrospective coverage in the approval of past acts,
including those licensed by the copyright owner. Second, s 183(6) requires that
any agreement with a copyright owner that fixes for the purposes of s 183(5)
the terms of an authorised person's exploitation must be approved by the
relevant Minister with responsibility for copyright. This provision precludes
any other part of government, or indeed the person authorised, from binding
the government to s 183(5) terms unless the “copyright” Minister’s consent is
first obtained.

Finally, while it has been presumed that local governments are not within
the scope of “a State” for the purposes of s 183 (and indeed for the purposes of
the ownership provision of Part VII*) there has been no definitive decision so
holding and indeed there are indicia which suggest that presumption is open
to doubt.» The issue awaits either legislative or judicial clarification.

9.5.4 Relationship with prior infringement proceedings

The nature and role of the Australian exception is illustrated by the Copyright
Tribunal determination in the exercise of its s 183(5) jurisdiction in Pocketful of
Tunes v Commonwealth. (This is a particularly fitting example given it involves

the very type of exploitation that the Gregory Committee in 1951 considered
should not fall within any UK Crown use exception.”) The Commonwealth
through the engagement of the firm Radiowise Media Network, had produced
an Australian citizenship video montage in DVD format for use in Australian
citizenship ceremonies. In it the musical work and lyrics of the iconic song [Am
Australian was synchronised so as to comprise the montage’s soundtrack. This
exploitation occurred without obtaining a synchronisation copyright licence
in relation to the works.® In early 2013 the copyright owners commenced
infringement proceedings in the Victorian registry of the Federal Court, and
having filed a defence which pleaded s 183(1) in mid-2013 the Commonwealth
made a “Without Prejudice Except as to Costs” offer of compromise (a so-called
Calderbank offer) of $250,000 plus $50,000 for costs. This was not accepted
by the copyright owners. Rather, the owners made a fresh application under
s 183(5) to the Copyright Tribunal.

In 2015 the amount fixed by Bennett J, sitting as the Tribunal President,
pursuant to s 183(5) was approximately $150,000, to which approximately
$40,000 was added by agreement for interest." The matter then became
derailed when a costs order was made by the Tribunal for the copyright
owners to pay the Commonwealth’s costs on an indemnity basis from the date
of the 2013 Calderbank offer. A judicial review by the Full Court of that costs
order identified error. The Calderbank offer had not been made in the s 183(5)
application before the Tribunal but rather in the earlier infringement proceed-
ings before the Federal Court. A line of prior authorities had held that offers
of compromise in proceedings other than the immediate proceeding were not
relevant, and thus the Calderbank offer in the infringement action could not be
relied upon before the Tribunal.“ On remit the Tribunal constituted by Jagot
as Deputy President observed that in the two prior Tribunal determinations
under s 183(5) - which had both involved States - Sheppard J and Lockhart J
had each made cost awards in favour of the copyright owner applicants.
Jagot J also observed that while the norm in the Tribunal was for parties to
bear their own costs (because most “disputes before the Tribunal involve a
collecting society which controls copyright in numerous works and a large
scale user of such works”) determinations under s 183(5) were more akin
to a hearing on quantum in civil litigation.™ As such Jagot J suggested that

copyright owners costs should ordinarily be paid by the Commonwealth or
State:
[G]iven the relatively small amounts involved and that the applicants
had no choice but to come to the Tribunal to have terms fixed, it would be
unjust for the applicants to be at risk of the amount obtained as a result of
the Tribunal’s order being materially consumed or even exceeded by the
costs the applicants have had to expend to have those terms determined.
Applicants under s 183(5) of the Act should not be placed in such an
invidious position. The nature of proceedings under s 183(5) indicates that
it would be consistent with ‘the subject matter and the scope and purpose’
of the legislation for the Commonwealth or State to pay the costs of the
proceeding, at least where there has been no disentitling conduct on the
part of such an applicant
This principle is guided by the ordinary role played by offers of compromise
(i.e. Calderbank offers) in facilitating settlements through the discipline of costs
if such offers are made in Tribunal proceedings. Because in Pocketful of Tunes it
was found ultimately that no such offer had been made by the Commonwealth
in the s 183(5) Tribunal proceedings (as opposed to the earlier infringement
proceedings), Jagot J awarded that the copyright owners’ costs in the Tribunal
proceedings be paid by the Commonwealth.”

9.5.5 Collective administration for government copies:
section 183A

In 1998 the s 183 regime was amended with the express purpose to “streamline
the system for owners of copyright in works and other subject matter to be
paid when their materials are copied by Commonwealth, State and Territory
governments”. The amendment achieved this by relieving those govern-
ments of obligations under s 183(4) and (5) and substituting them ins 183A with
“an administratively simple procedure for calculating and making payments
of equitable remuneration to copyright owners for the use of their copyright
materials by the governments” »” This procedure applies for the making by
a government of a copy of: (i) any copyright subject matter except “a liter-
ary work that consists of a computer program or a compilation of computer
programs”; and (ii) where that copy belongs to a class of copies for which a
relevant collecting society has been declared by the Copyright Tribunal under
153F" Notably, and unlike the compulsory licence for educational institutions

in Part IVA, Division 4, the operation of s 183A is not predicated upon a State
or the Commonwealth government providing to a relevant collecting society
a remuneration notice. Once triggered by the creation of a relevant collecting
society by Tribunal declaration, s 183A operates automatically within the scope
of any declaration which may be in relation to “all government copies” or “a
class of government copies”. Importantly, a “government copy” here means
“a reproduction in a material form of copyright material made under subsec-
tion 183(1)”, where “copyright material” is defined to encompass all Part III
and Part IV subject matter. As it stands s 183A has no role to play outside of
the exclusive rights to reproduce or to copy — however as discussed below, a
reform to this limitation has been mooted (see: 9.5.8).

To date two collecting societies have reasonably wide ranging declarations
under s 153F that make s 183A operative, and two applications for declarations
have been denied. One declaration, made by the Tribunal on 18 December
1998, declares the Copyright Agency Limited to be the collecting society “in
relation to government copies of works and published editions of works, other
than works that are included in a sound recording, cinematographic film or
a television or sound broadcast” and which necessarily excludes computer
programs or compilations of computer programs."” The other declaration, first
made on 4 May 2000 and revised on 16 October 2015, declares the Audio-Visual
Copyright Society (trading as Screenrights) to be the collecting society:

... in relation to government copies of the following classes of copyright
material:
(a) sound recordings;
(b) cinematograph films;
(©) television broadcasts or sound broadcasts
(together Audio-Visual Items); and
(d) works included in an Audio-Visual Item,
where the government copy was made:
() from a communication to the public; or
(ii) from any copy made from a communication to the public.

Pursuant to s 183A(2) a method of quantifying equitable remuneration to
be paid must be agreed between the relevant collecting society and relevant
government, or in lieu of agreement, determined by the Tribunal with juris-
diction vested in the Tribunal by s 153K.“ The machinery provisions, while
largely non-prescriptive, do require in s 183A (3) that any methodology “specify
the sampling system to be used” for estimating the quantity of government
copies made.**

9.5.6 Sections 183 and 183A combined

After an array of matters concerning ownership and implied licences were
resolved by the Federal Court and the High Court - discussed in Chapter 4
(see: 4.4.2) and Chapter 10 (see: 10.2.2) - the State of New South Wales could
only accept that s 183 applied to its online provision of electronic copies of land
survey plans (involving an exercise by it of the communication to the public
tight), and s 183A applied to its bricks-and-mortar sale of hard copies of those
plans (involving an exercise by it of the reproduction right), being plans that
had been lodged with it pursuant to its Torrens title system. However, the
quantum to be paid to affected copyright owners (typically the land surveyors
who authored the plans) could not be agreed upon for either exploitation.
Because the plans were being commercialised by the State through distinct
exercises of two exclusive rights meant that both aspects of the exception:
s 183(5) to the communication right and s 183A(2) to government copies. The
Copyright Agency, the relevant collecting society in respect of the government
copies, sought a determination on behalf of the copyright owners in the plans
from the Copyright Tribunal in the exercise of its jurisdiction under both s 153E
in relation to the communication right exercise and under s 153K in relation
to the copies. In determining a base rate of 12.5% of retail sale price for both
types of exploitations the Tribunal stated:
[T]hat copying and communication should otherwise be treated on the same
basis. Although the statutory inquiries under ss 183(5) (terms) and 183A (2)
(equitable remuneration) are different, it was not submitted by either party
that this should result in different outcomes in the tribunal's deliberations
... The result of that conclusion is that the legal standard to be applied under
both ss 183(5) and 183A (2) will be, at least in this case, the same. In saying
that, the tribunal does not suggest that, legally, the standards erected by the
two provisions are necessarily the same; rather, the point is that in this case

the tribunal concludes that appropriate terms under s 183(5) would be terms
providing for equitable remuneration ... [I]t is difficult to identify any prin-
cipled reason why the rate of equitable remuneration for the reproduction of
registered survey plans should differ from the equitable remuneration rate
for an electronic communication to a member of the public of the same."*
Importantly, while the Copyright Agency had blanket repertoire coverage in
respect of government copies by virtue of the operation of s 183A, for the
communication right its standing under s 183(5) was confined only to those
copyright owners who had voluntarily appointed it to act on their behalf.”

9.5.7 Copyright owners and the Commonwealth or a State as
copyright users

This gives rise to another important point. As has been stated above, the
s 183 regime means that the Commonwealth or a State, and those with writ-
ten authority from either, cannot infringe Australian copyright when doing
acts for the services of the relevant government. Therefore any infringement
claims in copyright litigation that are within the scope of the s 183 regime
should be struck out. The best the copyright owner can achieve is to come
to terms with the relevant government (or obtain a Tribunal determination)
pursuant to s 183(5), but that is unavailable if the exploitation is the making
of a government copy within the operation of s 183A by virtue of a s 153F
declaration. In cases where s 183A is enlivened by the declaration coverage of
arelevant collecting society, both litigation and recourse to s 183(5) are closed
off. The copyright owner is left with compulsory collective administration of
the right. In the absence of a distribution from the relevant collecting society,
which is subject to being detected under a sampling system, it appears the best
a copyright owner can readily achieve is to successfully apply to the relevant
collecting society for a discretionary ex gratia payment.”* Whether such an
outcome accords with community expectations, good principle, constitutional
“just terms” or indeed the requirement not to “unreasonably prejudice the
legitimate interests of the right holder” under TRIPS could all be debated. It
suffices to give one example. If reproduction occurred today of the work of

an author by the Reserve Bank (“the Commonwealth”) in a case similar to
that of David Malangi (whose artistic work in 1966 was reproduced, without
the licence of the artist, on Australia’s one dollar paper currency) is it at all
satisfactory that the author’s economic rights in Australian copyright law
could amount to no more than an ex gratia payment from a collecting society
declared under s 153F?#"

9.6 Licence scheme oversight

9.6.1 Background

The origins of the powers in the Copyright Tribunal of Australia over collective
voluntary licensing that today reside in Part IV, Division 3, Subdivision H of
the 1968 Act (“References and applications relating to licences and licence
schemes”) can be historically understood in the institution of the Australasian
Performing Right Association (APRA) in 1926. APRA itself was a local version
of earlier European societies, the first of which being the French Société des
Auteurs, Compositeurs et Editeurs de Musique (SACEM) established in 1851
and the closest to APRA culturally being the UK’s Performing Right Society
(PRS) which was established in 1914. As Cornish explains:

Composers of most music ... found it impossible to enforce their performing
rights, work by work, against users such as concert promoters and theatre
proprietors. The only practicable system was to found a single organ - in
Britain the Performing Right Society - for the collective enforcement of
rights. It then became possible to organize licensing schemes for the differ-
ent categories of user and to set various rates for them.’
Through reciprocal arrangements with sister societies around the world
such as PRS, SACEM and the American Society of Composers, Authors and
Publishers (ASCAP), Lahore could explain in 1977 that “a licence granted by
APRA authorizes the use of its entire repertoire which comprises almost all
music performed in Australia” + This aggregation of rights (as opposed to
any individual right) creates a degree of monopoly power in APRA that has
long troubled Australian policy-makers. So that in 1932, a mere six years after
APRA commenced operations, the Commonwealth Parliament established
a Royal Commission on performing rights chaired by Justice Owen of the
New South Wales Supreme Court. The Owen Royal Commission “reported
that APRA was, to all intents and purposes, a super-monopoly controlling or
claiming to control most of the music which users in public must use and was
able to dictate its own terms” 2 The Commission recommended a compulsory
arbitral mechanism be enacted to curb APRA’s market power. However, doubt
raised by APRA and its sister societies about the Berne Convention compliance
of such reform led to the enactment in 1933 of a softer measure: s 13A was
inserted into the 1912 Australian Act to provide a mechanism for the voluntary
arbitration in disputes concerning the public performance of works. In 1959
the Spicer Committee concisely noted: “as far as we are aware no use has
been made of that provision” °° However the Spicer Committee also observed
that APRA now strongly supported the creation of a permanent tribunal with
compulsory arbitral powers over it, a support that can be understood in the
context of such a body (the Performing Right Tribunal) being already estab-
lished in the UK under the 1956 Act”

9.6.2 Foundations of the regime

As first enacted Part VI of the 1968 Act, aside from the jurisdiction mentioned.
above,” conferred upon the Copyright Tribunal of Australia jurisdiction over

certain voluntary licence schemes in ss 154-159. The definition of “licence”
most critically controlled the scope of this jurisdiction and essentially spanned:
(a) alicence granted under the authority of a copyright owner of a literary,
dramatic or musical work to perform the work in public, to broadcast
the work, to make a sound recording or cinematograph film of the work
for the purpose of broadcasting the work; or
a licence granted under the authority of the owner of copyright in a
sound recording to cause the recording to be heard in public or to make
a record embodying the recording for the purpose of broadcasting the
recording
Three basic types of Tribunal jurisdiction over such licences were enacted in 1968
and theseremain the basis of currentlaw: (i) referral ofaproposedlicencescheme#
(ii) referral of an existing licence scheme;** and (iii) an application by a person
aggrieved typically from a failure to be granted a licence under a licence
scheme or an assertion that the terms of a licence offered under such a scheme
were unreasonable.** Importantly, upon the Tribunal confirming or varying
an extant licence scheme, a person who exercises copyright within the scope of
such a licence scheme is deemed to be the “holder of such a licence” * Finally,
and critically, the jurisdiction is confined to a “licence scheme” (“whether
called a scheme or tariff”) formulated by a licensor or licensors setting out
the classes of cases in which each licensor is or licensors are willing to grant
licences. (“Licensor” is itself defined as a copyright owner member based
body whose main business is the granting of licences.) As will be explained
below (see: 9.6.4) the Full Federal Court in 2019 emphatically interpreted this
regime as being confined to voluntary licensing schemes; it created no jurisdic-
tion in the Tribunal to craft adjunct compulsory licences. Also, and while this
is not made explicit, implicitly the drafting logic excludes one-off transactional
licensing from the Tribunals jurisdiction.*”

Aside from jurisdiction over APRA schemes that related to the performance
in public and the broadcast to the public of musical works and any related lyrics,
jurisdiction extended to similar licensing in relation to sound recordings. The
1968 Act put into a codified form so-called secondary use rights of performance

)

in public and broadcast to the public for sound recordings, and as explained
above (see: 9.3.2 and 9.3.3) these rights were made subject to the ss 108 and 109
compulsory licences - the latter involving price controls. This jurisdiction over
voluntary collective licences in respect of causing sound recordings to be heard
in public was enacted in overlapping conjunction with the s 108 statutory
licence. At the time the 1968 Act commenced, a collecting society based in the
recording industry was established - the Phonographic Performance Company
of Australia (PPCA) - with the mission of administering these new secondary
use rights in sound recordings, within the frameworks of the ss 108 and 109
statutory licences and the Tribunal jurisdiction over voluntary licences.

Re APRA and ABC was the first reported exercise by the Tribunal of its
154 jurisdiction.™ In this 1985 case the Tribunal interpreted its powers under
the jurisdiction were to order variation, confirmation, or a denial of confirma-
tion, of a licence scheme before it but no holus-bolus power to substitute anew
licence scheme of Tribunal making.” The Tribunal in 1985 varied an APRA
licence scheme (which was for the exercise of the then broadcast exclusive
right in musical works within the APRA repertoire) so as to both reduce the
royalty rates but to broaden the financial base upon which that reduced rate
applied.

9.6.3 Modernisation and reform

In 2000 a review of the jurisdiction and procedures of the Tribunal was
published by the Copyright Law Review Committee (CLRC) and it unani-
mously recommended that the Act be amended to extend the jurisdiction of
the Copyright Tribunal to all voluntary licence schemes offered by a collect-
ing society. In the same year, the Australian Competition Tribunal had
granted an authorisation to APRA to continue as a natural monopoly subject
to relatively minor adjustments to its membership relations.“ The primary
adjustment was that composer and author members who are obliged (as an
aspect of APRA membership) to assign to APRA relevant existing and future
rights were to now have an entitlement to require APRA to grant the member
a non-exclusive “licence back” to permit the member to directly sub-license
those rights in Australia. The Competition Tribunal had observed earlier in
the course of its determination:

Given the existence of a natural monopoly for the purpose of enforcing

copyright in music composition, and given that it is not in society's interest

to have more than one such collecting society, it is imperative that there is
appropriate regulation to ensure competition in the provision of policing of
copyright where possible and an acceptable balance between, in this case,
the rights of writers and the rights of the rest of society. We agree with
the parties that ACCC and this Tribunal has the power, if necessary, to
regulate competition, but we consider the Copyright Tribunal is the body
with primary responsibility to adjudicate an acceptable balance between the
private and social interests.

However, whether or not the jurisdiction of the Copyright Tribunal remained
adequate was an open question in view of the CLRC recommendation, a matter
made more pressing because the Competition Tribunal had placed most of
the burden on the Copyright Tribunal to act as a public law regulator. These
matters came to a head in 2003 when the Copyright Tribunal observed its own
jurisdictional limitations. In Re Powercom Interactive Media the applicant sought
licences to reproduce and communicate musical works owned by APRA and
AMCOS as mobile phone ringtones, where each collecting society offered a
respective licence scheme for the communication rights and the reproduction
rights of the same musical works. Powercom asserted the societies levied
unreasonable charges and sought that the Tribunal, pursuant to s 155, vary
the schemes to more reasonable charges. Lindgren P refused the application
insofar as it was clear that s 155 gave the Tribunal no jurisdiction to consider
any matter in dispute to the extent that it relates to the terms of a licence
scheme, such as that offered by AMCOS which related to the reproduction
right. In his concluding remarks Lindgren P pointedly stated:

It is unfortunate that I have felt constrained to reach this conclusion. As
noted [above], it is not a result that either party desires. Moreover, in its
report Jurisdiction and Procedures of the Copyright Tribunal made in December
2000, the Copyright Law Review Committee unanimously recommended
that the jurisdiction of the Tribunal be extended to cover just such a case
as the present one ... Moreover, those submissions made to that committee
which commented on the matter unanimously favoured extension of the
Tribunal's jurisdiction so as to cover ‘collectively administered non-statutory
licences’ and no submissions opposed that extension.

This set the stage for reform of the jurisdiction in 2006 which significantly
expanded it. The definition of an amenable licence was replaced from one that
was essentially tied to public performance and broadcasting exclusive rights
in musical works and causing sound recordings to be heard in public, to the
far broader definition of “a licence granted by or on behalf of the owner or
prospective owner of the copyright in a work or other subject matter to do an
act comprised in the copyright”. The power of the Tribunal now explicitly
included a power of substitution, albeit in an attenuated way: the Tribunal may

now confirm or vary the scheme, or substitute it “for another scheme proposed
by one of the parties” ”

Also in 2006, the Tribunal was given power to make the ACCC a party
to any reference or application made under the Tribunal’s licence scheme
jurisdiction. Moreover these reforms required the Tribunal to have regard
to any guidelines published by the ACCC if such regard was requested by a
party. In 2019 the ACCC published relevant guidelines. These guidelines
recommended the making of copyright price determinations by benchmarking
(:e. comparable actual market bargains) and/or the construction of hypotheti-
cal bargains (which are populated by notional “willing but not anxious” buyers
and sellers), but notably the ACCC eschewed endorsing or recommending
“judicial estimation” - which is a method whereby the Tribunal takes account
of all matters considered relevant and applies a degree of intuitive synthesis
to determine a rate. The existence of (since 2006) expanded jurisdiction and
the publication of the ACCC guidelines (in 2019) begs the question about the
public policy utility of persisting with legacy statutory licence schemes such
as s55.

9.6.4 Significant recent referrals

Since the 2006 reforms three quite significant matters have attracted the
Tribunal’s exercise of the licence scheme jurisdiction: digital downloads in
2009, internet-based simulcasting in 2015 and 2016 and subscription broadcast-
ing over 2016-2019.

9.6.4.1 Digital downloads

The first of these, Re APRA and AMCOS, involved revisiting the terrain of
Re Powercom Interactive Media in the wake of the 2006 reforms which now
facilitated the type of joint licensing able to be offered by the allied collect-
ing societies APRA and AMCOS.™ The licence in issue was being offered.
to entities such as Apple and Telstra which provided digital music services,

including services that made available song downloads. A download involves
the conflation of the communication to the public right, the authorisation of a
reproduction and the potential for dealings liability.“ The licence before the
Tribunal was contractually defined, leaving aside certain exclusions, in the
following way:

Licence means a non-exclusive licence to:

(a) reproduce AMCOS Works;

(b) authorise the reproduction of AMCOS Works;

(c) communicate in the Territory the APRA Works (including authorising

customers);

(d) authorise Your Affiliates to communicate the APRA Works to custom-

Having observed that “[e]ach of APRA and AMCOS is a licensor” within the
meaning of the regime, the Tribunal noted that the licence fees in the licence
scheme jointly proposed by the pair was the greater of 12% of the retail price
ora “track rate” of 12 cents for a single track downloaded. The form of the
scheme confirmed by the Tribunal, and ultimately consented to by all parties,
saw those rates reduced to 9% and 9 cents respectively.

9.6.4.2 Internet simulcasting and the relevance of the section 109 cap

In 2015 and 2016 a sequel to the 2013 Full Court decision in Phonographic
Performance Company of Australia » Commercial Radio Australia discussed in
Chapter 3 (see: 3.4) and Chapter 6 (see: 6.7.1) played out in two parts in the
Tribunal. As explained in Chapter 3 the Full Court had held that a simulcast
on or over the internet of a radio broadcast was not in and of itself a “broad-
cast”. This mattered commercially insofar as such a simulcast was taken by
that 2013 holding outside of the s 109 statutory licence and therefore beyond

the tendentious price caps of s 152. As noted above (see: 9.3.3) the commer-
cial price cap in s 152 is 1% of a broadcaster's gross earnings and in 1983 the
Tribunal under its s 152 jurisdiction had arrived at a rate of 0.45% of the gross
earnings of a commercial radio broadcaster.** The Full Court holding meant
that any licence scheme offered by the PPCA for internet-based simulcasting
was unconstrained by the s 152 cap. However any rate under such a scheme
undeniably existed in the context of that cap. Whereas the PPCA sought a rate
couched in terms of a fixed amount each time a sound recording was streamed
via an internet connection (“per stream”), Commercial Radio Australia (CRA)
sought a carry-over of a percentage of revenue approach similar to that upon
which the s 152 cap is itself predicated. The parties appeared to be intractably
wedded to their respective positions. After some 14 hearing days in 2015, the
Tribunal denied confirmation of any scheme for the key reason that “none of
the schemes are reasonable in that they require payment to be made on either a
per stream basis or a percentage of revenue basis and do not give radio stations
the choice of which payment system they prefer” ” The parties, who were
described as adopting an “unhelpful position” were required to return to the
Tribunal in 2016." Notwithstanding the Tribunals 2015 determination, in 2016
the PPCA maintained that radio stations “be required to pay on a per stream
basis from 1 July 2020”. This the Tribunal wearily rejected in the following
terms: “We consider it clear from our principal reasons that we believe that for
so long as radio stations must pay for the broadcast right on a percentage of
revenue basis, they should be able to pay for the simulcast right on that basis
if they so wish.”* The outcome was the Tribunal signalling to the parties
that it would confirm an internet simulcast licence scheme which permitted
radio stations to elect to pay the PPCA on the basis of either: (a) 0.35% of gross
revenue (a concept which the Tribunal also marked out for the parties) or
(b) $0.00059 (i.e. 0.059 cents) each time a PPCA sound recording was streamed.“

9.6.4.3 On-demand exploitation outside of voluntary licence

The third major and recent referral under s 154 also involved the PPCA in
protracted Tribunal litigation, this time with the subscription television
provider Foxtel. After the delivery of multiple determinations by the Tribunal,
the matter was ultimately escalated to the Full Court on judicial review.
An aspect of the Tribunal determination was to confirm a licence scheme
that extended beyond the scope of any voluntary licence that the PPCA

was authorised under its so-called “input” agreements with its copyright
owner members to grant licences on their behalf. In the context of a grant
of a licence to Foxtel for subscription broadcast and certain related wider
uses, the extended exploitations confirmed by the Tribunal but unauthorised
by any copyright owner included “the On-demand Offering Rights, to the
extent that those rights apply to Programs or feature films that have not
previously been Broadcast or will not in the next 30 days be Broadcast by the
Subscription Television Provider” * The Tribunal, consisting of Perram DP and
McMillian M reasoned that the Tribunal was unconstrained by any voluntary
licence in what it “may, or may not, determine by way of a scheme under
s 154(4)” and that the “point of the scheme of Pt VI is to create the ability to
license rights against their owners’ will, just as it is in the case of any decision-
maker which, as here, regulates monopoly asset pricing” In the Tribunal’s
view it was s 159(1) (which deemed a person who exercises copyright within
the scope of a confirmed licence scheme to the holder of a licence) which
underscored the conferral of this power on the Tribunal to essentially legislate,
at least at the edges of voluntary licence schemes, compulsory licences. The
Full Court unanimously rejected this characterisation of the regime. The core
definition “licence scheme” made its voluntary nature manifest.*® Because
the regime operated only to regulate those insofar as they were “willing” to
grant a licence:
The starting point mandated by s 154(1) is the voluntary offer of a licence
scheme by the licensor on behalf of its members. It is not a scheme for the
compulsory license of rights, which are addressed in other provisions of
the Act. ... The consequence of our construction is that the Tribunal does
not have power to vary the scheme proposed by a licensor insofar as it
affects the classes of cases in respect of which the licence is proposed to be
granted.
This 2019 decision of the Full Court has cogency: as an administrative body, the
Tribunals jurisdictional stream can never rise higher than its sources in Part VI
of the 1968 Act. To the extent that regime confers any power on the Tribunal
of compelling copyright owners to grant a licence, it can be found in a more
limited way in s 157(1) and (6A)(b). These provision, taken together, empower
the Tribunal to countermand a refusal by a licensor to offer a prospective
licensee a licence under an existing voluntary licence scheme. These provi-
sions reveal no more or less than a sound legislative policy that in respect of
voluntary licensing schemes, non-discriminatory, universal licensing obliga-
tions apply.
