1.1. Origins and justifications

Incentive, human rights and international trade concerns adequately summa-
rise the three main justifications for the existence of the Copyright Act 1968
(Cth) which is referred to in this book as the “1968 Act”. Firstly, copyright
law in the Anglo tradition is the promise of property rights in authorial works
in order to provide incentive for authorship. This idea of copyright remains
as the core justification of modern Australian law. Secondly, the Continental
tradition of copyright law, which has influences on modern Australian law
particularly through the Berne Convention, conceives of the conferral of rights
in copyright as a natural and just consequence of the subject matter being
human expression.’ Thirdly, since the 1990s a new and striking influence upon

Australian law has arisen by the integration of all intellectual property, includ-
ing copyright law, as an aspect of international trade law.’ This trade hue can
be explained by a desire especially in developed nations to appropriate value
from the comparative advantages they hold in fields dependent on intellectual
property such as popular culture and technology.

Until the British Copyright Act of 1911, English copyright law was a
farrago of Acts and legislative instruments.* A UK Royal Commission held in
1878 with the aim of supplying an acceptable blueprint for a new, cohesive and
singular piece of legislation had failed to achieve that goal in England. It was,
however, the kernel of the short-lived first Australian national legislation - the
Copyright Act 1905 - which replaced the State Copyright Acts.° Closely adher-
ing to UK law, the Australian statute protected copyright in books upon their
publication, and inscribed English common law to confer proprietary rights
in unpublished literary works.’ The 1905 Act created a national Registrar of
Copyrights and a Copyright Office to assimilate and replace the defunct State
counterparts, with registration of copyright rights being required by a plaintiff
prior to bringing any infringement action.

The 1905 Australian Act applied for less than a decade, swept away by
changes that were triggered by the reform of international copyright norms
within the Berne Convention, to which Australia was a member by virtue of
being part of the British Empire. The Berne Convention, the original text of
which dates back to 1886, has a profound importance in shaping the basic
contours of Australian law. Copyright law, similar to mainstream intellectual
property regimes, is national law. Its exclusive rights are territorially confined
to activities occurring within national territories: in this case the Australian

copyright territory. Therefore, copyright treaties and most notably the Berne
Convention seek to achieve a degree of harmonisation of laws between member
states. This relies on two pillars.’ The first is minimum standards of copyright
protection. The second is national treatment which obliges Australia to ensure
that (foreign) authors from Berne member countries are treated in Australian
copyright law no less favourably than Australian (local) authors.

The 1908 Berlin revision to the Berne Convention is a critical moment
that shaped the logic and contours of Australian copyright law throughout
the 20th century and into the 21st century in three salient respects: strength-
ened recognition of an obligation to protect rights of foreign authors prior
to publication; the rejection of formalities requirements such as registration;
and the introduction of a minimum term of protection of “life of the author
and fifty years after his death” 1° The impetus for the British 1911 Act, which
achieved a consolidation of the law that the 1878 Royal Commission failed to
achieve, was largely sparked by the reforms that the Berlin Revisions obliged.
It went beyond mere consolidation, to codify the common law protection that
attached to unpublished works. However, the form of the legislation was
also influenced by the intention for it to have imperial effect. The British
colonies, and particularly the Dominion of Canada, wanted to ensure that the
lengthened copyright term - prior to the 1911 Act the minimum post-mortem
term in British law was only seven years - did not prejudice the public’s ability
to obtain on reasonable terms a supply of books and public performances.”

The 1911 Act was passed in a form suitable for adoption by the various
British Dominions, which Australia did by the passing of the Copyright Act 1912
(Cth) which brought the 1911 Act into local effect on 1 July 1912. The 1912 Act
both repealed the 1905 Act and inscribed the terms of the British 1911 Act (as
a schedule) to be its primary operative provisions. In 1928 the Australian High
Court unanimously held that the operative provisions of the 1911 Act were
enacted not as an exercise of power supported by s 51(xviii) of the Constitution
(which is the head of power with respect to “copyrights, patents of inventions
and designs, and trade marks”), but rather to bring the Imperial Act into force
in Australia. Indeed the express words of the 1912 Act were held to reveal that
the intention of the Commonwealth Parliament was to bring the 1911 Act into
force in Australia and not to enact its provisions as a federal law.’ The regime

of the 1911 Act, which had as its object to protect literary, dramatic, musical
and artistic works of original human authorship, remained extant for some 57
years (and was amended on only three occasions") until the commencement
of the current 1968 Act on 1 May 1969.

1.2 The provenance of the 1968 Act

The 1968 Australian Act takes its bones from the UK Copyriglt Act 1956, which
itself was wholly repealed in the UK in 1988."° While the Australian Act has
lasted much longer than its source legislation, it is often necessary to consider
the origins of the 1956 UK Act to understand the underlying drafting logic
of its 1968 Australian successor. The 1956 UK Act's origins lie in a 1952 UK
government-commissioned report written by a committee chaired by the senior
civil servant Sir Henry Stanley Gregory (Gregory Committee).” It had been
asked to consider what copyright reforms were desirable in light of technical
developments and the 1948 Brussels revision to the Berne Convention. The
1956 UK Act was in turn central to the review of Australian law undertaken by
another government-commissioned committee chaired by Sir John Armstrong
Spicer (Spicer Committee), a former Senator and at the time of the review in
1959 had been appointed the first Chief Judge of the Commonwealth Industrial
Court.®

The most significant reform recommended by the Gregory Committee in
1952, implemented in the 1956 UK Act, accepted by the Spicer Committee in
1959 and then adopted within the 1968 Australian Act, was the addition of
a new subject matter type.” So that beyond the authorial literary, dramatic,
musical and artistic works that hitherto had been the scope of copyright
law, subject matter also comprised sound recordings, cinematograph films,
broadcasts and published editions of works. These four new categories were
conceived of in a way distinct from the works of original authorship that had
been the subject matter protected in the prior regime. These were productions
that did not need to originate from a human author. They essentially arose in
response to technological change and to regularise the past stretching of 1911
Act works categories to accommodate such subject matter.

In the 1956 UK Act the two types of subject matter, works originating from
human authors and non-authorial productions, were dealt with in Part 1 and
Part 2 of the Act respectively. In the 1968 Australian Act the counterpart treat-
ments were consigned to Part 3 (“Copyright in Original Literary, Dramatic,
Musical and Artistic Works”) and Part 4 (“Copyright in Subject Matter Other

Than Works”) respectively. In 1988 the UK Act did away with the sharp
distinction between the two subject matter types, and such a distinction has
never been made in US copyright law. However, the distinction between the
two subject matter types remains a feature of modern Australian copyright
law.”

1.3. The evolution of the 1968 Act by tranches of reform

In the absence of a wholesale root and branch revision, the 1968 Act has been
subjected to waves of incremental reforms and additions.“ The main tranches
can be identified and, as summarised below, these can be seen to be occurring
with increasing frequency.

1.3.1. Franki Committee Reforms 1980

The first major set of reforms were fundamentally sparked by the High Court
decision in University of New South Wales v Moorhouse.” This established
authorisation liability, discussed in Chapter 7 (see: 7.4), in a university for the
reproductions made by the public using photocopying equipment placed in
the university library. An expert committee, chaired by Franki J, a judge of the
Commonwealth Industrial Court, was given the following terms of reference:

To examine the question of the reprographic reproduction of works
protected by copyright in Australia and to recommend any alterations to
the Australian copyright law and any other measures the Committee may
consider necessary to effect a proper balance of interest between owners
of copyright and the users of copyright material in respect of reprographic
reproduction. The term ‘reprographic reproduction’ includes any system or
technique by which facsimile reproductions are made in any size or form.”

The resulting recommendations formed the basis of the first major instalment
of amendments to the 1968 Act in 1980, and these included reform to research
or study fair dealing exceptions, reform of the library exceptions and the first
introduction of a statutory licence for educational institutions to undertake
multiple copies of parts of a work (and in some cases of whole works) for
teaching purposes.™

1.3.2 First Computer Program Reforms 1984

Similar to the condition sparking the 1980 reforms - litigation - a trial judge’s
decision on 7 December 1983 that a computer program (whether in source
or object code) did not fall within the concept of a literary work in copyright
law sparked the 1984 reforms involving the express inclusion of computer
programs as literary works together with a “computer program” definition.”
The definition was to be overhauled in 2001 with the implementation of a
Copyright Law Reform Committee (CLRC) 1994 recommendation.”

1.3.3. Performers’ Rights Reforms 1989

Aside from the Berne Convention, another treaty that has had a significant
impact upon the contours of Australian copyright law is the 1961 Rome
Convention for the Protection of Performers, Producers of Phonograms and
Broadcasting Organisations (Rome Convention).” While the 1968 Act as first
enacted gave protections for phonogram (i.e. sound recording) producers and
broadcasters that met the Rome Convention standards, it omitted protection
to performers. The omission precluded Australia from Rome Convention
membership. In 1987 a Copyright Law Review Committee recommended the
conferral of performers’ rights protection, albeit by a personal rather than a
proprietary right.** This recommendation was adopted in 1989 by the addition
of new Part XIA (Performers’ Protection to the 1968 Act), and that addition
in turn permitted Australia to join the Rome Convention in 1992.?° Part XIA
entailed a 20-year period in which performers could control the fixation and
communication of their performances. As will be explained sound performers’
rights were subsequently bolstered as a consequence of obligations in TRIPS
and the US-Australia Free Trade Agreement.

1.3.4 TRIPS Reforms 1994

The Australian reforms required by TRIPS, which itself largely inscribed the
economic articles of the Berne Convention as so-called “level playing field”
trading norms within the World Trade Organization (WTO), were quite
minor.*° This was because Australia had previously enacted laws intended to
adhere to the Berne articles, and one of the most controversial aspects of the
TRIPS copyright reforms was TRIPS art 10 which obliges WTO members to

protect computer programs “as literary works under the Berne Convention”,
and Australian reforms had in 1984 effectively conferred that level of protec-
tion on computer programs “in any language, code or notation”, expanding the
protection found by the High Court to exist under the prior law for programs
written in source code.” As will be noted below (see: 1.3.7), the definition of
what is a computer program was to undertake further reform. All that was
left for Australia to do to make its copyright law TRIPS compliant was to
undertake a few reforms.** Most notably these included adding rental right
protection to certain subject matter (sound recordings and works therein, and
computer programs) that could be readily copied by customers of commercial
rental libraries; bolstering the enforcement powers of customs seizure of goods,
the making of which amounted to copyright infringement; and extending the
term of sound performers’ protection from 20 to 50 years.

Aside from these specific matters TRIPS introduced a new generalised
treaty discipline for the creation of exceptions to all intellectual property rights.
This was the three-step test, that itself had its origins with the 1967 Stockholm
revisions to the Berne Convention, there applicable only to the (then-
introduced) reproduction right in copyright. Under TRIPS art 13 limitations
or exceptions to the exclusive intellectual property rights covered by TRIPS
must be confined to (i) “certain special cases” which (ii) do not conflict with “a
normal exploitation” and (iii) “do not unreasonably prejudice the legitimate
interests of rights holders”.

1.3.5 Second Computer Program Reforms 1999

Following the 1994 Copyright Law Review Committee Report on Computer
Software Protection, added to the Act in 1999 was a new set of exceptions
(in Part III, Division 4A) setting out acts that did not constitute infringement
of copyright in computer programs.™ These included acts for the purpose of
study of computer programs, back-up copies, making interoperable products
and security testing. Notably some of these are the only exceptions in the 1968
Act expressly protected from being excluded or limited by contract.* Another
major recommendation of the 1994 CLRC report relating to subject matter was
enacted as part of the Digital Agenda Reforms.

1.3.6 Moral Rights Reforms 2000

Although since 1928 Berne obligations have existed to protect authors against
derogatory treatment of their works, and to ensure due authorial attribution
is made in relation to those works, Australia resisted giving specific legislative
effect to those obligations.*° There was limited protection in Part IX of the
1968 Act on first enactment against a person falsely attributing themselves as
an author of another’s work and limited protection against debasement of a
musical work under a compulsory licence provision.*” Some fair dealing excep-
tions, most notably criticism or review, have long conditioned their coverage
on the requirement that “a sufficient acknowledgement of the work is made” .*®
But the more general resistance can be seen within a wider Anglo tradition of
conceiving of copyright policy as more of a microeconomic policy lever rather
than a question of human rights. Thus, the moral rights art 6bis of the Berne
Convention was not inscribed into TRIPS in 1994. Prior to that a 1988 CLRC
by a majority decision rejected the introduction of moral rights protections in
Australia.*” Notwithstanding the CLRC’s position, and the omission of moral
rights obligations from TRIPS, impetus to enact moral rights protections began
in the Commonwealth Parliament in the mid-1990s. However, that reform
effort was bogged down over the question of to what extent authors would
have capacity to waive their moral rights and how should cinematograph
film be treated. (Film is Berne Convention authorial subject matter but which
is protected under the 1968 Act in Part IV as a non-authorial production.)
Ultimately in 2000 these issues were resolved with the introduction of a scheme
of moral rights protection within a radically revised and expanded Part IX.*®

1.3.7 Digital Agenda Reforms 2001

In 1996 the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) concluded two
new copyright treaties. The first was the WIPO Copyright Treaty (WCT), a
special agreement within the meaning of the Berne Convention insofar as it
granted more extensive rights to authors and was not contrary to the Berne
Convention.* The second was the WIPO Performances and Phonograms Treaty
(WPPT) which conferred upon performers (in particular, sound performers)
stronger rights in sound recordings made of their performances, stronger than
those required by the Rome Convention.# While Australia expeditiously moved

to reform its copyright law to comply with the WCT - and these reforms were
branded the Digital Agenda Reforms - it was not clear that Australia would
join the WPPT until bilateral Free Trade Agreements required it.* The Digital
Agenda Reforms, consistent with the WCT, comprised two major additions to
the rights of copyright owners. The first was the introduction of a broad-based
and technologically neutral communication to the public exclusive right. This
new right was intended to assimilate and extend existing rights tied to wireless
broadcasting and cable diffusion, effecting an expansion to include any means
by which copyright subject matter was transmitted electronically or made avail-
able online to the public. The second was to provide legal protections against
the circumvention of technical controls placed upon copyright subject matter
and against the removal of embedded rights management information.* The
anti-circumvention provisions were litigated and before a constrained inter-
pretation given to them by the High Court could take wider effect, they were
overhauled in a manner consistent with the more prescriptive set of obligation
in the US-Australia Free Trade Agreement noted below.* The other significant
reforms of the Digital Agenda Round related to computer programs as liter-
ary works. There was a refinement of the definition of computer program to
mean “a set of statements or instructions to be used directly or indirectly ina
computer in order to bring about a certain result” .*” This was consistent with
the 1994 CLRC recommendation which had suggested the adoption of the
language of US copyright law.

1.3.8 US-Australia Free Trade Agreement Reforms 2004

The US-Australia Free Trade Agreement (FTA) of 2004 triggered one of the
most expedited and expansive tranches of copyright reform.* It comprised
three main substantive areas. Two involved exporting aspects of the Digital
Millennium Copyright Act 1998 (US) (DMCA) into Australian law. First was the
extension by 20 years of the copyright terms for all works, sound recording and
films that were still within copyright or to come into existence.” The second,
also drawn directly from the DMCA, was the so-called safe harbour regime.
This moderated copyright liability on certain online intermediaries so long as
they comply with a separate enforcement system (typically involving the take-
down of infringing content) within the regime.” Australia’s initial adoption
of this was confined to admit only carriage service providers (telecommu-
nications companies) and not more general online service providers such
as Facebook or Google that are beneficiaries of the DMCA regime. In 2018

this was somewhat expanded to include a wider range of actors involved in
public service activities - but notably not actors such as Facebook or Google.*
Finally, the FTAs obliged Australia to join the WPPT which, in turn, required
the vesting of economic rights to sound performers in the sound recording of
their performances, confining exceptions to the performers’ rights of sound
performers and extending moral rights protection to sound performers.”

1.3.9 Exceptions and Enforcement Reforms 2006

The FTA triggered a review of exceptions. A view was expressed that because
so much of US copyright law (and in particular the 20-year term extensions) had
been effectively exported to Australia by the FTA, why not go the whole hog
and also adopt the more open-ended US fair use doctrine? In 2006 a compro-
mise of sorts was arrived at by introducing a range of new exceptions for such
things as parody or satire, broadcast copying for private viewing or listening at
a time more convenient and copying to new media forms - such as from audio
CD to MP3. Also added was a new fallback exception available for certain
public service interests (education, libraries and those assisting the disabled)
whose public service use of copyright did not fall within existing exceptions
regime and satisfied the TRIPS three-step test. This provision, s 200AB, was as
far as policy-makers were prepared to go to satisfy institutional-user demands
for Australian fair use.

The criminal law regime within the Act was greatly expanded in both
scope and complexity. Closely mapping the structure of the Criminal Code
Act 1995 (Cth), an expanded array of indictable, summary and strict liability
offences (with a range of penalty options) was introduced.”

Also included was reform to the anti-circumvention regime. Australia had
in its Digital Agenda Reforms attempted to meet the WCT obligations in that
regard. However, litigation involving the modification of the Sony PlayStation
(which enabled the playing of unauthorised copies of video games) revealed
that the circumvention of an access control could fall outside the scope of
liability under Australian law.* Australia, more closely adhering to the DMCA
model put forward in the FTA, closed off that possibility by requiring the
creation of liability for the circumvention of an access control technological
protection measure.”

1.3.10 Resale Royalty Right for Visual Artists Reforms 2009

After a long period of consideration, in 2009 the government gave effect in
the Resale Royalty Right for Visual Artists Act 2009 (Cth) to a type of protection
known most commonly by its French description: the droit de suite.** It is an
authorial right found in the Berne Convention, which is an optional obliga-
tion.°® The 2009 reforms did not involve amendment to the 1968 Act, but rather
comprised their own statutory regime. In certain respects, it can be understood
as a right that makes up for the absence within Berne (and therefore the 1968
Act) of any sort of “public performance” economic right for visual artists — i.e.
there is no economic right in visual artists to control the public exhibition of
their works. The Australian droit de suite regime applies only in relation to
artwork sales that occur after the regime’s commencement on 9 December
2009. The royalty is determined by the 2009 Act to be 5% of the sale price of
visual art within the scope of the Act, when commercially resold after its first
commercial sale. The right to the royalty in visual artists is an inalienable
right. Liability falls primarily upon the seller. The right is administered by a
government-declared collecting society - the Copyright Agency was declared
to be the responsible society in 2010.

1.3.11 Offshore site blocking injunctions 2015 and 2018

Loosely following a 2003 UK copyright provision, in 2015 Australia conferred
jurisdiction in the Federal Court to order carriage service providers to block
access to an offshore online location that facilitates infringement and has the
primary purpose of doing so. While this power is contained in a single provi-
sion of the Act -s 115A - it can be seen as a significant reform measure. Rights
holders were prompt to use this enforcement avenue and a court decision
determined a cost-sharing split between service providers and copyright appli-
cants. In 2018 the jurisdiction was augmented with a power to also require
online search engine providers to take reasonable steps to not provide a search
result that refers users to the online location.

1.312 |ALRC Copyright and the Digital Economy Related
Reforms 2017 and ongoing

The 2013 ALRC Report Copyright and the Digital Economy was greeted enthusi-
astically by many user groups and in a more sceptical way by many of those
in copyright-dependent industries. Central to the ALRC Report was the
recommendation in favour of Australia adopting US-style fair use, and failing

that, a significant expansion of specific fair dealing purposes from the then five
purposes to 11 purposes.

The government's response to the ALRC Report has been cautious, and
fair use has to date not been adopted. There have been piecemeal reforms that
have occurred in response to it, with the abolition of perpetual copyright for
unpublished subject matter, the introduction of a new fair dealing purpose
to facilitate access for people with a disability and the simplification of the
educational statutory licensing introduced in 2017 — all regarded as less contro-
versial ALRC recommendations.*® It is likely that further reforms will occur
following consultations with stakeholders on more difficult topics. In October
2020 a limited fair dealing exception for quotation has been flagged, together
with a remedial limitation regime to facilitate exploitation of so-called orphan
works.® It seems unlikely, however, that US-style fair use will be adopted by
Australia in the near term. On a separate front the “right to repair” movement
(itself heavily informed in Europe by environmental concerns) has given rise
to the Productivity Commission shaping recommendations to introduce a fair
use exception (so as to tap into US jurisprudence that has found that in certain
circumstances facilitating repair or maintenance is a fair use purpose) or a
repair-specific fair dealing exception.”

1.3.13 Other reforms

The summary above captures merely what might be considered headline
reform initiatives. Other substantial rafts of reform have often related to
miscellaneous exceptions. For example, detailed waves of reform have focused
on both the parallel importation of goods and the overlap of copyright with
designs protection. The end result of over 50 years of incrementalism is a
complex body of statute law that has not been the subject of an overarching
revision. The complexity of Australian copyright law has been compounded
by some intensely reasoned High Court authorities in the 21st century, all
involving information technologies.”

Most recently an ACCC Review of Digital Platforms has had the attention
of public policy and this has in 2021 translated into some particularly quirky
unfair competition reform as outlined in Chapter 15 (see: 15.6).

1.4

The framework of Australian copyright law

Australian copyright law is essentially a collection of distinct yet intercon-
nected regimes within the 1968 Act and one distinct stand-alone regime found
in the Resale Royalty Right for Visual Artists Act 2009 (Cth).

The regimes based in the 1968 Act can be summarised as:

Proprietary copyright: The first and central regime of copyright is the
economic property rights in works of authorship and non-authorial
productions. This primarily consists of Parts III and IV and Part V,
Division 2 of the Act, and all the other regimes are attached to the
proprietary regime in one way or another. In this book, Chapters 2-6
and most of Chapter 7 deal with the essential elements of the propri-
etary copyright regime.

Liability relating to technological protection measures and rights
management information: The liability regime in Part V, Division 2A
for circumvention of technological protection measures relies on the
concepts of copyright formed in Parts III and IV of the 1968 Act. In this
book, Chapter 7 (see: 7.6) deals with this regime.

Performers’ rights: The performers rights regime in Part XIA creates a
personal right (rather than a property right) in performers in non-copy-
right subject matter: performances. It nonetheless deploys definitions
and concepts that have been formed in the context of Parts III and IV
of the Act. In this book Chapter 11 deals with this regime. Since the
US-Australia Free Trade Agreement 2004 the rights of performers have
reached into proprietary copyright attaching to Part IV sound record-
ings discussed in Chapter 4 (see: 4.3.1) and as explained in Chapter 12,
sound performers have also obtained moral rights protections.

¢ Moral rights: The moral rights regime in Part IX is connected with both
Parts III and IV of the Act insofar as authors of works and the director,
producer and screenwriter of cinematograph film (which is Part IV
subject matter) all have personal rights of integrity and attribution.
Since the US-Australia Free Trade Agreement reforms of 2004, the
moral rights regime also has a connection with Part XIA in that sound
performers too have rights of integrity and attribution. In this book,
Chapter 12 deals with the Part IX moral rights regime.

¢ Criminal liability: The criminal law regimes of Part V, Division 5 and
Part XIA, Division 3 creates various criminal liabilities around activi-
ties that mirror civil copyright and circumvention liability when these
are done on a commercial scale and with the requisite criminal intent.
There also exists powers of customs seizure in Part V, Division 7. There
are also obligations in Part X, Division 3 put upon publishers, backed
by criminal liability, relating to National Library deposit obligations.
In this book, Chapter 15 (see: 15.7) touches upon these regimes.
