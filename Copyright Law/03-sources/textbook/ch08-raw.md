8.1 Introduction

By s 196(1) of the 1968 Act copyright “is personal property” and as such its
title is alienable (transferable) by voluntary assignment. Indeed since 2005
the possibility of non-alienable economic copyright property rights has been
foreclosed: art 17.4(6) of the US-Australia Free Trade Agreement obliges the
parties to provide that a holder of any “economic right in a work, performance,
or phonogram” may freely transfer that right by contract to another person.’
Copyright is also transmissible by will or by operation of law — such as by
a sequestration order in bankruptcy — in much the same way as any other
personal property. In Chapter 4 the rules that establish first ownership of
copyright property were set out. In Chapter 7 it was explained how infringe-
ment of copyright occurs in all cases where conduct involving an exclusive
right (either directly or otherwise) occurs without the licence of the owner of
the copyright. A licence is a permission by an owner to exercise a property
right that would, in the absence of the licence, give rise to liability. This chapter
deals with the legal architecture around the assignment and other transmis-
sions of copyright title, and the voluntary licensing of the rights attached to

copyright. In Chapter 14 special rules of evidence and procedure that touch
upon establishing good copyright title in litigation are explained. The follow-
ing chapters discuss forms of involuntary licensing: by statute in Chapter 9 and
by courts (so-called “implied licences”) in Chapter 10 (see: 10.2). However, not
examined in this book is the Personal Property Securities Act 2009 (Cth) regime
of registration and prioritisation of security interests attached to collateral,
save for flagging here that both Part III and Part IV copyright property and
transferable copyright licences are regarded as collateral under that regime in
respect of which security interests may be registered?

The 1968 Act makes explicit in s 196(2) that copyright may be partially
assigned, limited “in any way”, and implicitly the possibility of lawful licens-
ing must as a matter of logic be at least as flexible as alienations by assignment
that are expressly permitted. Three illustrative examples are given in s 196(2):

(1) anassignment of only certain exclusive rights (such as the public perfor-
mance right traditionally assigned by composers for the purposes of
collective administration);

(2) anassignment limited to exploitations in a part of Australia (such as an
assignment of copyright that applies only to Norfolk Island); and

(3) anassignment for only a part of the period of the copyright term (such
as under a reversionary interest).

Also, s 197 facilitates the assignment of copyright prior to its existence (“future
copyright”) whereby a person who upon the subject matter’s subsistence would
be the first owner of copyright, may lawfully assign it to a third person. In rela-
tion to this divisibility of copyright property, s 30 seeks to avoid any doubt for
the operation of other provisions of the Act by expressly acknowledging that
there can be multiple owners of copyright in a work according to place, time
or the acts involved. As Yates J has explained s 30 “does not, however, provide
the machinery by which particular ownership arises” but rather “proceeds on
the basis that separate and distinct copyright ownership has been acquired
and requires the Copyright Act to be applied accordingly” + Thus, an author
and first copyright owner A who then assigns in her dramatic work merely
the performance in public right to B will be able to action her reproduction
right against B if B exercises that right without A’s permission. Likewise, B can
action its performance in public right against A if she performs the work, or
authorises its performance, in public without B’s permission.

8.2 Formalities for assignments and exclusive licences

Section 196(3) provides that: “An assignment of copyright (whether total or
partial) does not have effect unless it is in writing signed by or on behalf of
the assignor” and a similar rule applies in s 197(1) to assignments of future
copyright: This Statute of Frauds type of requirement also extends to exclusive
licences. Section 10(1) defines an exclusive licence to mean “a licence in writ-
ing, signed by or on behalf of the owner or prospective owner of copyright,
authorizing the licensee, to the exclusion of all other persons, to do an act that,
by virtue of this Act, the owner of the copyright would, but for the licence, have
the exclusive right to do”. As is explained below (see: 8.2.6) and in Chapter
14 (see: 14.2) an exclusive licensee under such a licence has a statutory right
to bring infringement proceedings.* By virtue of this right such an exclusive
licensee is effectively filling the shoes of the owner, and the broad uniformity
of the requirement of writing in the 1968 Act for assignment and exclusive
licensing reveals them to be loosely regarded as equivalents.

8.24 Relation to Berne no-formalities rule

The Berne Convention norm requires that, as an aspect of national treatment,
a Berne member must ensure that “enjoyment and the exercise” of the rights
of foreign Berne Union authors are equivalent to the member's own authors
and their recognition “shall not be subject to any formality”? This Berne norm
has existed since the 1908 Berlin revision, and reflected in Australian law since
the 1912 adoption of the British 1911 Act which adhered to the no-formalities
principle for both domestic and foreign authors. The no-formalities approach is
also implicit in the way in which an array of deeming rules vest initial owner-
ship of copyright. To take one example, a person who employs an author
under an oral contract of service will, in the absence of any prior countervailing

express assignment, be the owner of copyright in the literary, dramatic, musi-
cal or artistic works produced by the author pursuant to the contract

However, subsisting copyright is intangible personal property, as is all
other statute-based intellectual property, and is classified in law as a chose
(“thing”) in action: property enforceable by legal action alone, and unlike
chattel property, not enforceable (for example) by effecting physical recap-
tion. This makes copyright property more akin to property rights pertaining
to other choses in action such as debts or company shares. For such intangible
property rights the desire for legal certainty and the prevention of fraud long
ago resulted in assignments or transfers at law being required by statute to
be in writing and signed.? Harmonised treatment of copyright property, as a
chose in action, when it is voluntarily assigned helps to explain why signed
writing is required. Thus, notwithstanding the Berne no-formalities rule and
the related complete absence of written formalities attaching to default first
ownership rules, Anglo-Australian copyright law has long adhered to the
necessity of a signed written instrument for there to be a valid assignment
at law of copyright.° As will be explained, equity provides the possibility of
transfer in certain cases where there is a failure of formalities.

8.2.2 Recognition of formality requirements: business to
business settings

In deciding whether the s 196(3) formality is satisfied, courts in business to
business settings have accepted irregular forms if the circumstances around the
writing revealed clear enough intent to assign copyright. So that in Greenfield
Products v Rover-Scott Bonnar a sale of business agreement defined the subject
matter of the sale as: “The goodwill, motor vehicles, plant and equipment,
patents, loose tools, trade debtors and stock of the business conducted by the
Vendor as manufacturer and distributor of industrial mowers conducted by the
Vendor at 1101 Beaudesert Road, Archerfield.”" While the copyright owned by
the vendor was not expressly mentioned in the written sale agreement, Pincus]
was willing both to imply into the agreement that copyright was included in
order to give effect to the parties’ clear intention “that all the assets used in
the business” should be subject to the sale, and to hold that the agreement so
interpreted then satisfied s 196(3): “documents which do not in terms assign
copyright can suffice” 2? Similarly in Murray v King the Full Court, affirming
the trial judge, regarded the written agreement for the sale of a business by

a receiver was effective to assign the copyright owned by the business even
though that copyright was not specifically referred to in the agreement.
The Full Court also took a relaxed approach to written terms in Peter Vogel
Instruments v Fairlight.au where a clause of a formal agreement (clause 20)
provided that:
Upon receipt of the final payment from [PVI], F.au agrees that the software
and its source components developed by F.au pursuant to this agreement
will transfer to [PVI]. The exact definition of IP to be transferred will be
defined in a separate document.

The trial judge had considered the clause to be ineffective because no “separate
document” was ever created to define the copyright subject matter of the
assignment. The Full Court overturned this holding on the basis that:

In our view, the language of clause 20 makes tolerably clear that the
intellectual property rights to be transferred are those that flow from the
payment of $200,000. Namely, the software that PVI commissioned Fairlight
to develop. The unfulfilled requirement that the “exact” definition of intel-
lectual property to be transferred will be defined in a separate document,
does not diminish the clarity of the meaning that is conveyed.*

In other words the Full Court, similar to Pincus J in Greenfield Products v Rover-

Scott Bonnar, was prepared to accept some margin of imprecision in a formal

assignment provision so long as contractual intent was otherwise manifest.

Recently in Chhabra v McPherson, a stricter approach to formalities was
adopted in a business to business setting. At trial there were no less than three
transactions which raised the question of whether s 196(3) had been satisfied
and the analysis of the trial judge, Yates J, was unanimously upheld on appeal.

To start with was the transaction that involved the terms of engagement
by a company, which was the vehicle seeking to establish an international
network of law firms to be branded as Kaden Boriss, with a Singaporean
branding agency (Pulse Creative). The 2010 terms of engagement under which

Pulse Creative produced the logo artwork included:

The Agency agrees to assign to the Client any present and future copyright,
designs, inventions, trade marks and any other intellectual property in the
Works upon payment of all Fees relating to the creation of such Works. The
Agency agrees to execute all documents and do all things necessary to give
effect to the assignment in this clause.”

The fees were fully paid on 29 September 2011. Yates J interpreted this to be no

more than a promise to execute an assignment at some future time rather than

a clause which effected an assignment at law, even upon full payment. As is

explained below (see: 8.3) the terms could lead to no more than an equitable

interest in the copyright."* The legal ownership of the copyright remained with
Pulse Creative.
Next, Pulse Creative executed on 12 January 2017 a confirmatory deed
in which it “assigned to the First and Second Assignees all its right, title and
interest in and to the Kaden Boriss Corporate Brands, including (a) all current
and future copyright rights, and rights in the nature of copyright, throughout
the world in the Kaden Boriss Corporate Brands; and (b) all accrued rights of
action involving the Kaden Boriss Corporate Brands, throughout the world,
with effect from 29 September 2011”. The two assignees were Sunil Lal Chhabra
(who was referred to as Sunil Lal and was the lead plaintiff) and Hemant Batra
(a central figure although not party to the litigation) who were the principals
of the network of law firms initiative, and Yates J held that:
[T]he Confirmatory Deed does fulfil the requirements of s 196(3). Although
expressed in “confirmatory” terms, the operative clause, properly construed,
should be taken to be a present assignment of the copyright in the Kaden
Boriss Logos made on 12 January 2017, with retrospective operation from
29 September 2011. On this basis, Mr Lal and Mr Batra are the co-owners of
the Australian copyright in the logos with title to exercise those rights from
29 September 2011, as the Confirmatory Deed provides.”

As far as the disposition of the ownership was concerned Yates J held that

chain of title commenced with Pulse Creative “and ends with the Confirmatory

Deed”.

However, the plaintiff Lal claimed that notwithstanding the terms of the
Confirmatory Deed, an email sent from Batra to Lal on 11 January 2017 (being
the day prior to the execution of the Confirmatory Deed) or an earlier email
dated 4 August 2010 also from Batra to Lal, or both emails taken together,
were effective to assign to Lal any Australian copyright interest Batra was
to acquire under the Confirmatory Deed to Lal. The text of the 2017 email
included the following passages: “Unquestionably, Australia territory was
yours re: Kaden Boriss”; “As Australia was completely under your domain,
control and prerogative, there is no denying of the fact that it was you who
identified / authorized Brisbane on the above premise” and “ Australia territory
was yours, which is now sadly subject matter of litigation” In the 2010 email,
when dealing with the incorporation of the commercialisation entities, Batra
replied to assertions about who “the brand name will be the property” of,
with “Your understanding is correct regarding exclusivity and ownership in
Australia with Sunil Lal” ” Either or both emails were argued by the plaintiff
Lal to constitute an agreement in writing in relation to the ownership of future
copyright implicating s 197(1), discussed below (see: 8.2.5). Aside from diffi-
culty in giving this correspondence any operative force on its face as a legal

assignment, characterisation of the correspondence as any assignment was also
“not reflected in the Confirmatory Deed” with Yates J finding that:
Further, general assertions about the exclusive right to use a ‘brand’ say
little about the ownership of copyright in an artistic work. Even further, the
email correspondence does not meet the formal requirements of s 197(1).
Whatever agreements Mr Lal and Mr Batra might have come to in that
correspondence, it cannot be construed as ‘an agreement made in relation to
a future copyright’ and it is not ‘signed’, let alone signed by any person who
would have been the owner of the Australian copyright in the Kaden Boriss
Logos on its coming into existence (the parties agree that [Pulse Creative]
was the first owner).>
It was this final issue that Lal focused upon on appeal to the Full Court. In
particular Lal argued that the 4 August 2010 email satisfied the requirement
of an assignment of future copyright by application of s 197(1). The appeal
ground was rejected by the Full Court on the same basis as the trial judge but
with more elaboration. First, the correspondence was dealing with “brand”
ownership in the future and not artistic work copyright ownership.* Implicitly,
the looser approaches taken to assignment language in the earlier cases was not
applied.” Second, s 197 requires the signature as assignor of “the person who
would, apart from this section, be the owner of the copyright on its coming
into existence”. The Full Court could bluntly observe, consistent with the
Confirmatory Deed and the trial judge’s unchallenged finding, that Batra was
not such a person, but that Pulse Creative was.”
This judicial analysis, driven largely by recognition of formality require-
ments, resulted in the holding that the plaintiff Lal and Batra were the joint
owners of the Australian copyright pursuant to the Confirmatory Deed.

8.2.3 Recognition of formality requirements: independent
contractor settings

As discussed in Chapter 4 (see: 4.2.2) the formality of the requirement of
signed writing for an assignment of copyright ownership in a work from an
independent contractor to a commissioning party is put into stark contrast
with the automatic effect of s 35(6) in the case of an employee author of a
work. In the former unless the commissioning party has a signed copyright
assignment from the contractor the commissioning party will not be the
owner. In the latter the default rule is that the employer is the owner of the
copyright. While the commissioning party absent taking copyright ownership
under a formal assignment, might be able to establish an equitable title to the
copyright, more commonly they will be merely licensees with a permission
to exercise rights defined by purpose of the commission. Often matters that

end in litigation reflect a degree of ignorance in commissioning parties about
the limits of s 35(6) and the requirements of s 196(3) and s 197(1). Moreover,
Australian jurisprudence reveals in these cases an unwillingness to confect an
intent to assign from contractual dealings or to extend wide flexibility when
interpreting the formalities provisions.

In 1984 the Western Australian Supreme Court had before it a plaintiff who
had engaged the defendant as an independent contractor, by an exchange of
letters, to provide meteorological forecasts.” Specifically the defendant was
engaged “as a seasonal forecaster at the rate of $2000 for the initial forecasts
and $500 per calendar month to provide the update and amendment service” 2°
The court rejected the plaintiff's claim to copyright ownership in the resulting
reports by dint of a written assignment arising from the letters on the basis that
Olney J “was unable to extract from the correspondence anything approaching
an assignment or the granting of an exclusive licence” ”

In Wilson v Weis Art Hill J rejected a submission - based largely on UK
authorities - that “the cases show that the courts ‘strive’ to find that a particular
contract operates as an assignment, rather than as a licence”.*° Rather, Hill J
considered that “[w]hat these cases demonstrate, however, is merely that the
question whether there has been an assignment is one of construction of the
particular instrument in the light of the particular circumstances”! The facts
before Hill J involved a contract with Wilson, a visual artist, to use her works
“on shopping bags, caps and T shirts which were promotional adjuncts” to the
garments sold by Peter Weiss Pty Ltd — a third party to the case, but a related
entity to the defendant company Weiss Art Pty Ltd. Hill J was “inclined to
the view that a licence was intended” * The characterisation of the licence
by Hill J was as one to use the artwork as essentially advertising logos. This
was coupled with Hill J's rejection of any implied right in Peter Weiss Pty Ltd
to assign the benefit of that licence, or presumably to grant a sub-licence, to
the defendant company Weiss Art. This meant that Weiss Art's decorative
use of Wilson's artwork on a large range of merchandise (including “towels,
sheets, pillowcases, beach towelling robes, large framed prints, deckchairs,
greeting cards and paper, yo-yos, underwear, tissue holders, make-up purses
and T-shirts”) was done without the artist's licence and therefore infringed
her artistic copyright.* That is, the use was to ornament goods as items of
merchandise rather than merely comprise adjunct promotional use which was
a purposive limitation of the licence. This ornamental use on merchandise was
both beyond the scope of the license and undertaken by Weiss Art which was
a third party to the licence agreement.

Most recently in 2019 the defendant in University of Sydney v ObjectiVision
had brought a counter-claim against the plaintiff relying upon the defendant's
ownership of the copyright in a computer program known as OPERA v2.3
which was asserted to be a work of joint authorship of up to nine individuals
— none of whom were employees of ObjectiVision. On Burley J's analysis, of
the nine only one had executed in adequate form a written agreement which
assigned his ownership interest to ObjectiVision» For another individual, an
email to him by ObjectiVision attaching misaddressed assignment terms was
regarded by Burley J as being insufficient to effect any assignment for the
purposes of s 196(3), notwithstanding his written reply to the email signifying
his agreement to other terms in the body of the email. His electronic signature,
in the circumstances, could not be regarded under the Electronic Transactions
Act 1999 (Cth) as being a signature applied to the attached terms. This was
especially so because in the body of the email ObjectiVision had plainly
required execution of the attached terms by his wet signature on paper, and
such execution had not occurred.*

The upshot of the Australian authorities is clear enough: if an entity wants
ownership of the copyright works authored by an independent contractor
in the discharge of that contractor's contractual duties, a written contract of
engagement should expressly assign the copyright in written terms signed
by the contractor that satisfy the formalities provisions. There has been little
judicial appetite in cases involving independent contractor authors to accept
irregular forms in the way that has occurred in some of the business to business

cases.%¢

8.2.4 Assignments of past causes of action

Since the Australian High Court decision in Equuscorp v Haxton existing rights
of action in respect of assigned property are capable themselves of being
contemporaneously assigned with that property. While assignments of bare
causes of action have traditionally created policy concerns rooted in the old
crimes and torts of maintenance and champerty, under Equuscorp v Haxton,
if rights of action are assigned along with the relevant property they are not
regarded as a bare cause of action because they are legitimately adjunct to
the subject matter being transacted.” More generally, it seems to be broadly
accepted that so long as a genuine commercial interest can be established in

the assignee enforcing the past rights of action assigned, such an assignment
will be recognised and not regarded as contrary to public policy.**

In Insight SCR IP Holdings v ACER there was: a June 2009 written assign-
ment from the author, who had been copyright owner since 1992, to a first
related company; then an October 2009 written assignment from that company
to another holding company related to the author; and, also in October 2009,
a written exclusive licence conferred by the holding company upon a third
company related to the author. All these transactions were regarded to be effec-
tive by the trial judge, a view supported on appeal. More hotly contested was
whether rights of action against the defendant, ACER, that arose from infring-
ing conduct in the period 2006-2009 were also capable of being assigned. In
May 2011, and shortly prior to the trial of the matter, the author and his three
related companies entered into a chain of curative deeds that sought (inter alia)
to assign past rights of action from author to the first related company, from
that company to the holding company and from the holding company a grant
of power was then conferred upon the third company (the exclusive licensee)
to enforce those past rights of action. The trial judge, supported by the Full
Court, concluded that these May 2011 assignments and grant relating to the
rights of action for past infringements were not contrary to any public policy
because since 2009 the assignees had a legally recognised interest in the subject
matter of the litigation by virtue of copyright ownership or exclusive licence.”

More recently Bennett J, sitting as the Copyright Tribunal, applied both
the High Court decision in Equuscorp and the Full Court decision in Insight
SCR IP to conclude that a right to claim compensation for Crown use was
similarly capable of such assignment in conjunction with the relevant copy-
right property."

8.2.5 Future copyright

Prior to the 1968 Act, Australian law reflected the position under the 1911
Act that only recognised the assignment of legal title in subsisting copyright.
Future copyright could not be legally assigned, so that any valid agreement
to assign copyright to come into existence could only be recognised in equity,

much like certain informal assignments in current law discussed below
(see: 8.3). This former treatment of future copyright detracted from the type
of legal certainty that aggregators of copyright property such as publishers,
rights management organisations and film studios considered ideal.” This was
remedied in the 1956 UK Act so as to permit assignment of copyright rights at
law prior to those rights coming into existence, and this provision was readily
endorsed by the 1959 Australian Spicer Committee and found its way into the
1968 Act as s 197.5

In Chhabra v McPherson (see: 8.2.2), aside from making the point discussed
above on the necessity of the same formalities requirements as apply for exist-
ing copyright being met for assignments of future copyright, Yates J and the
Full Court made a salient point about the scope of s 197. Future copyright does
not include existing copyright subject matter, the property rights of which are
to be acquired in the future. The language of the provision makes plain that it
is dealing with subject matter that at the time of the assignment is yet to come
“into existence”. So in Clihabra v McPherson, a party who anticipated taking
an ownership stake in existing copyright owned by a third party pursuant to
a future assignment could not be a person who would be the owner on the
copyright “coming into existence”, being the status required of any assignor
for the provision to be enlivened.#

8.2.6 Exclusive licences

The 1911 Act, while it recognised the right of an owner to grant a licence, did
not provide any special recognition of exclusive licensees.” This in turn gave
rise to ambiguities as to the entitlement of an exclusive licensee to sue in its
own name -a problem that courts resolved with some dexterity by interpreting
the licence agreement as a partial assignment of the right in issue, and thereby
conferring an entitlement in the licensee-cum-assignee to sue.“° The Gregory
Committee considered it desirable “to make clear that an exclusive licensee
is entitled to sue for infringement of the right comprised in his licence”,
however in the “normal case ... the copyright owner should be a party to the
proceedings” .*” This recommendation was the basis for the enactment of s 19
of the 1956 UK Act which in turn was reflected by Part V Division 3 of the

1968 Act which provides a regime that both confers an entitlement to sue in
an exclusive licensee and sets some procedural rules around that entitlement.
Most notably the regime includes: the right in an exclusive licensee to seek
economic remedies for infringement; the requirement that the owner be joined
as a party unless a court dispenses with that requirement or interlocutory
injunctive relief is being sought; and that any defence available to the defend-
ant as against the owner is equally available against the exclusive licensee.
What qualifies as an exclusive licensee under the statutory definition
(set out in part above, see: 8.2) has been judicially considered several times.
Bowen CJ has held that “the licence must be exclusive of all other persons,
including the grantor of the licence, and this must appear from the words of
the licence” * In Avel v Multicoin Amusements the High Court unanimously
rejected that a “sole and exclusive” Australian distributor of new Williams
pinball machines (i.e. chattels) was by virtue of that status also an “exclusive
licensee” in respect of any associated Williams copyright. In a holding tied to
the court's similarly unanimous view about the limited nature of the exclusive
tight “to publish” discussed in Chapter 6 (see: 6.5) it found that:
The distribution agreement did not make the distributor an ‘exclusive
licensee’ of the copyright in the artwork or the computer programmes of
the particular models. The exclusive rights of local sale conferred upon
the distributor in respect of machines of those models did not encompass
exclusive rights of first or other publication”

The distributor Avel had no standing under the 1968 Act to sue Multicoin
Amusements, an importer of second-hand Williams pinball machines. This
holding can be contrasted with a distributor of computer programs (software)
who was granted by the copyright owner, Broderbund, “an exclusive license
in the territory to use Broderbund’s copyright in the distribution and sale
of Broderbund’s software products and all trade marks, designs and logos”.
Here the nature of software distribution at the time (late 1980s) involved both
a chattel aspect and the authorising of computer program reproduction by
installation on customer hardware. As such Beaumont J held that the distribu-
tor “had the right conferred on it by Broderbund, to the exclusion of all other
persons, to reproduce the program in Australia”.

8.3 Informal purported assignments

Notwithstanding the necessity imposed by ss 196(3) and 197(1) for a writ-
ten assignment signed by the assignor, as is typical in other settings (e.g. the
doctrine of part performance) where there has been a failure of formalities in

respect of an otherwise valid contract transacting property, equity may step
in to effect redress. This occurs not so much to permit assignment in equity,
but rather “[i]t is normally the consequence of such a transaction that, value
having been given, an equitable interest in the subject thereof arises in the party
giving it”. As Smithers J went on to explain:
In such a case the equitable interest arises not by way of transfer but by
activation in equity of the conscience of the receiver of the valuable consid-
eration. A trust is created; there is not a transfer or assignment; there is no
transmission of an equitable interest.

In other words the relations give rise to a resulting trust, with the transacted
copyright the trust asset, the assignor its trustee and the assignee its beneficiary.
While Smithers J’s comments on the operation of equity arose in 1984 in the
context of a pre-trial skirmish over pleadings, three decades later these same
equitable principles were given effect by Crennan J in Gold Peg International
v Kovan Engineering where it was held, and upheld on appeal, that Gold Peg
was the equitable owner of the copyright in certain technical drawings based
ona course of commercial dealings between it and Kovan:
Iam satisfied on the evidence that while there was no express agreement
between the parties as to the ownership of copyright in manufacturing
drawings produced by [Kovan], there was a term which was partly oral and
which can partly be implied as a matter of business efficacy, that the intel-
lectual property rights in manufacturing drawings based on [an earlier set
of drawings] or any modification or enhancement of them in manufacturing
drawings created by [Kovan] would be owned by [Gold Peg].

On this basis Gold Peg was entitled to have that equitable ownership perfected
by anassignment from Kovan of the legal ownership. Thus, Kovan was ordered
to “forthwith execute a deed of assignment transferring legal ownership” of
the copyright in the relevant technical drawings to Gold Peg.* Needless to say,
arriving at this outcome for Gold Peg was considerably more involved (and
costly) than an ex ante written assignment duly executed by Kovan. Moreover,
under general principles, an equitable interest in copyright could not have
been enforced against a bona fide purchaser of the title to copyright under a
formal assignment from the legal owner.

8.4 Transmission under law

Section 196(1) makes clear that copyright is transmissible by will and by opera-
tion of law. So in Corby v Allen & Unwin, ownership of copyright in one of
the artistic works (a photograph) in suit had been passed by inheritance to
four people, one of whom was a plaintiff who was consequently a 25% owner

of the copyright in that work, holding her share as a tenant in common.® In
Boyapati v Rockefeller Management Kenny J could readily identify a fundamental
impediment to an asserted infringement action brought by way of cross-claim:
the copyright property in the cross-claim works in suit had either vested in
the trustee at the time one of the cross-claimants had become a bankrupt or, if
acquired during his period of bankruptcy, had vested in the trustee as after-
acquired property.*

There exists one complication that applies to copyright in works
bequeathed under a will if the works are not published at the time of the death
of the testator. Where the testator has bequeathed under will the manuscript
embodying a not published literary, dramatic or musical work, or in the case of
anot published artistic work, the created artefact, s 198 of the 1968 Act creates a
presumption that the bequest includes the copyright in the unpublished work
embodied in the manuscript or artefact “unless a contrary intention appears
in the testator’s will” *” This provision seems to have as its genesis a desire in
the mid-1800s in Britain to foreclose the repetition locally of the outcome in the
then-recent US litigation of Folsom v Marsh where unpublished private letters
of the late President Washington had been purchased by the US government
“at a great price, for the benefit of the nation”, however the government had
not acquired any copyright by virtue of that purchase.** Thus in the Copyright
Law Amendment Act of 1842 the issue was addressed by an absolute rule:
ownership of copyright in a book published after the death of its author “shall
be the property of the proprietor of the author’s manuscript” .” This absolute
rule was replaced in the 1911 Act with a presumptive rule applying to works
in manuscript form that had been neither published nor performed in public.”
The 1956 UK Act carried that presumptive rule forward, expanding it to all
unpublished works, and it is this which has been essentially inscribed into the
1968 Act. For those drafting wills the provision means that it is important that
if chattel property embodying unpublished works is intended to be devolved
separate from the works as copyright property, to clearly say so. Without
expressing that intent, the copyright property will likely pass with the chattel
property.”

8.5 Voluntary licensing

As has been explained in Chapter 7 (see: 7.3, 7.4.1.3 and 7.5.1.2) infringement
of copyright in Australia requires the doing of a relevant act “without the
licence of the owner of the copyright”. There are three basic ways in which
such a licence (permission) can arise: (i) voluntarily from the copyright owner;
(i) compulsorily from statutory mandate; (iii) judicially from the finding of an
implied licence. The discussion that follows relates only to the first of these.
Chapter 9 deals in large part with compulsory (statutory) licensing which has a
relatively prominent role in the 1968 Act. Implied licences, which are essentially
estoppel-based free exceptions, will be considered in Chapter 10 (see: 10.2)
which covers the more generally applicable free exceptions to copyright.

As a general commercial matter, copyright licensing under contract has
none of the licensing complications that arise under the authorised use provi-
sions of the Trade Marks Act 1995 (Cth) - which require actual quality control
- because the interests served by the copyright system are quite different from
those of the registered trade mark system. Whereas the former at its most
basic is the facilitation of creative and informative matter, the latter is the
protection of the mark as an ongoing signal of origin. However, any copyright
licensee party to a contractual licence with the owner would expect to be
assured that it is dealing with the copyright owner and would generally expect
to be assured that any exploitation under the licence would not infringe any
third-party rights. More generally the interpretation and construction of the
terms of such licence are governed by general contract law principles (includ-
ing relevant equitable doctrines), and this body of law predominately control
issues around its breach.“ So that in Chhabra v McPherson discussed above
(see: 8.2.2 and 8.2.5) Yates] at trial, unanimously supported on appeal, applied
general contract law to find that a licence term contractual licence could be
implied in respect of the Kaden Boriss logo. That implied term operated in
that “for so long as [the defendants] were participants in the network, [the
defendants] were entitled to use ... the Kaden Boriss Logos” .*° The defendants’
use of the artistic works was within the scope of this contractual licence and
therefore not infringing the plaintiffs’ copyright. With a contractual licence,
as with any similar intellectual property licence, as a general matter a licensee
in breach is both creating for itself contractual liability at general law and

liability for infringing the underlying intellectual property that is the subject
of the contract - although double damages are not available, merely the most
favourable remedy.

8.5.1 Bare licences and their revocation

Far more complex issues come into play when considering a revocation by
the licensor of a bare (gratuitous) licence, unsupported by consideration. The
threshold question is whether the licence is indeed gratuitous and therefore
non-contractual in nature.® In Chhabra v McPherson discussed above (see: 8.2.2,
8.2.5, 8.5) this was hotly contested: the plaintiffs asserting a bare licence with
the defendants (who had used the works as an aspect of their firm, MVM Legal,
rebranding as “Kaden Boriss Brisbane”) asserting contractual. Yates J, upheld
on appeal, found it was a contractual licence: the terms of a 25 April 2013 email
revealed that the licence was supported by consideration, which included an
obligation in the defendants to pay a 20% referral fee for any clients referred
to them by a participating firm in the Kaden Boriss network.”

Ifa bare licence, the starting position is that it may be revoked at will, or at
least, on giving the licensee reasonable notice.* Where there is co-ownership
Yates J in Chhabra v McPherson, having regard to the nature of copyright
co-ownership which is discussed in Chapter 4 (see: 4.6), suggested by way of
obiter at trial that:

Given the fact that one co-owner of copyright cannot act independently o}
another co-owner (or at least independently of that co-owner’s consent) ir
granting a licence to use the copyright work, the better view must be that
similarly, one co-owner cannot act independently to revoke a licence thal
each or all have consented to grant.

The Full Court, referred to the issue and expressed no disagreement with
Yate J's suggestion, while not expressing for itself a concluded view because it
was unnecessary for it to decide the question in the context of the appeal.” The
obiter view of Yates J appears both cogent and correct as a matter of principle.

Representations in a bare licence, such as that it is “irrevocable”, might not
create contractual obligations in the licensor. However modern Australian
principles of promissory estoppel, at least in cases where detrimental reliance
can be established in a licensee, might attenuate any power in a licensor of
such a licence to revoke it - at least to the extent necessary to avoid licensee
detriment. Thus the Full Court observed in Computermate Products (Aust)
Ozi-Soft that: “where the bare licence has been acted upon by the licensee to
the detriment of the licensee, in an appropriate case there may be an estoppel
against the licensor preventing the revocation of the licence, either at all or
otherwise than upon notice”.” For this proposition the Full Court cited the
famous promissory estoppel decision in Valtons Stores (Interstate) » Maher
as authority.” In Chhabra v McPherson Yates J relied on both Computermate
Products (Aust) v Ozi-Soft and Waltons Stores (Interstate) » Maher to find, by
way of obiter, that:

If the licence to use the Kaden Boriss Logos was a gratuitous or bare licence,
it was nevertheless a promise by [the licensors] that the respondents could
use the logos in and for the purpose of their participation in the network.
It was also a promise, on which the respondents undoubtedly acted with
[the licensors’ ] knowledge, that while that purpose was being fulfilled, their
permission to use the Kaden Boriss Logos would not be withdrawn, at least
not without just cause being established or, perhaps, reasonable notice being
given ... Put another way, the licensors would have been estopped from
denying the continuing right of the respondents to use the logos as members
of the Kaden Boriss network.*

In agreeing with this position adopted by Yates J, the Full Court put an even
finer point on it:
The appellants conducted their case on the assumption that a gratuitous
licence was revocable at will. That assumption was wrong in principle. If
the licence was not contractual, then the conditions in which the gratuitous
licence may be revoked was always a fact specific matter to be determined.
at trial.”

These estoppel conclusions must be correct as a matter of law and principle,
and should be to the forefront of the minds of any copyright owner who wishes
to gratuitously licence on “irrevocable” terms such as those under the Creative
Commons movement and of any licensee user who relies upon such terms.”
In these Chhabra v McPherson conclusions a connection also exists with the
estoppel backbone of the implied licence exception explained in Chapter 10

(see: 10.2.1) and future developments in copyright law might find organising
ideas that bring them into closer alignment.

8.5.2 Proving absence of licence and tolerated use

As noted above (see: 8.5) and in Chapter 7 (see: 7.3, 7.4.1.3 and 7.5.1.2) infringe-
ment requires a relevant act to be done “without the licence of the owner of
the copyright”: absence of permission. It is possible for permission or consent
to be “implied from the circumstances of a particular case”.” Moreover, the
plaintiff has “the burden of showing, inter alia, the absence of a licence, rather
than placing upon the other side the burden of showing a licence”.”* The two
notions can give rise to complex and finely balanced analysis as the Avel v
Multicoin Amusements litigation reveals.

In Avel v Multicoin Amusements the copyright owner, Williams, was not a
party and was indifferent about the proposed conduct of Multicoin Amusements
to import from the USA second-hand Williams pinball machines.” Had Avel,
in defending a groundless threats action brought by Multicoin Amusements,
established that Multicoin’s proposed conduct would have been “without the
licence” of Williams and thereby would have constituted infringement? The
majority joint judgment reasoned that Avel had not discharged the onus of
proving that in the event that the proposed importation and resale of the used
pinball machines had gone ahead “it would have been without the licence of
Williams” ®° Dawson J, also in the majority but writing separately, made the
point more explicitly:

[T]he onus would be upon Avel to prove that importation by Multicoin ...
would be without Williams’ licence because lack of licence, or consent, is a
constituent element of infringement under s 37; it is not by way of proviso,
saving, exception or excuse, so that the onus of proving it is upon the person
seeking to raise it. It goes to the substance of an infringement under s 37
that there should be no licence by the owner and, consequently, as Avel
must satisfy the court that the acts he complained of would constitute an
infringement, the burden of establishing the absence of consent would lie
upon Avel.!
In contrast McHugh J, while agreeing with the principle that the onus of
establishing absence of permission was on Avel, considered that Avel had
indeed discharged that onus by proving that Williams was “neutral”: on
McHugh J's view Avel had proved that the copyright owner “did not consent

to the importation of the machines” “* On McHugh J's minority view Avel did
not need to prove a negative, proving the absence of positive consent would
suffice.

The proposition that emerges from the majority view in Avel v Multicoin
Amusements, logically applicable to all the infringement provisions in Parts III
and IV, is that proving the relevant conduct was “without the licence of the
owner of the copyright” is an evidentiary burden on copyright plaintiffs that
can only be met by establishing on balance of probabilities the act was not
permitted by the copyright owner. It cannot be established merely by proving
the absence of express permission where all the circumstances paint a picture
of ambiguity as to the copyright owner's position. This is because consent
can be implied from the circumstances. Where the copyright owner's posture
resembles that of Williams and thus tolerates a third party exploitation of
copyright, but does not expressly permit it, that exploitation as a matter of logi-
cal application of Avel v Multicoin Amusements is non-infringing. Put another
way, under this principle a tolerated use of copyright is not an infringing use.

8.5.3 Sub-licensing and reliance on licence
Section 15 of the 1968 Act provides that:
For the purposes of this Act, an act shall be deemed to have been done with

the licence of the owner of a copyright if the doing of the act was authorized
by a licence binding the owner of the copyright.

The provision appears to be derived on a more tightly drafted provision in the
1956 UK Act which made clear that the doing of anything authorised by the
grantee of a licence within the terms of a licence “shall for the purposes of this
Act be taken to be done with the licence of the grantor” .® In other words, the
1956 Act provision gave statutory recognition of the scope and effect of sub-
licensing.‘ There is no explanation of the provenance of the Australian or UK
provision by either the Spicer Committee or Gregory Committee respectively.
Lahore, writing in 1977, considered that the Australian provision more or less
mapped the operation of its UK counterpart: statutory recognition of sub-
licensing.® It is a matter for the owner granting a written copyright licence on
what terms sub-licensing should occur, if at all.
Ina 2006 case dealing with the revocability of an implied licence discussed

in Chapter 10 (see: 10.2.1) Gummow ACJ stated:

Section 15 is an important provision for this case. It accommodates instances

of what ordinarily would be called a sub-licence. [Citing Lahore, 1977.] The

section also encompasses cases where the existence and scope of an effective

licence is found in a consent binding the copyright owner other than by

reason solely of the principles of contractual consideration and privity.

The third sentence in this passage is cryptic. It seems to raise the possibility
of s 15 having effect in settings where a non-contractual licence is enforceable
against the owner because it is coupled with an equitable obligation created.
For example, by estoppel where, relying on the licence, a person alters her posi-
tion in a way that would be to her detriment if the licence were withdrawn.”

8.5.4 Enforceability of licence against future owners

Section 196(4) marks a point of clear distinction between the 1968 Act and the
1956 UK Act which was its blueprint. The Australian provision provides that:

A licence granted in respect of a copyright by the owner of the copyright
binds every successor in title to the interest in the copyright of the grantor
of the licence to the same extent as the licence was binding on the grantor.

The 1956 Act provision on which s 196(4) is based had excluded from the first-
in-time priority rule an assignee of the copyright title who was “a purchaser in
good faith for valuable consideration and without notice (actual or construc-
tive) of the licence or a person deriving from such a purchaser”. The Spicer
Committee rejected this exclusion in the following terms:

Wecan see no good reason for this exception. Whatever the position regard-
ing other forms of property, we think that a licence and an assignment of
copyright are interests of a similar order and that the first in time ought to
prevail ®

This recommendation is reflected in s 196(4) and it is a crucial provision

for those licensing or taking assignments of Australian copyright property.

Voluntary licences granted by the assignor may, depending upon the terms

of the licence, bind assignees including those taking in good faith and without

notice in much the same way a duly registered security attached to the copy-
right will be enforceable against such assignees - but in the case of copyright
licences, automatically so under s 196(4) without a registration requirement.”

This creates issues when drafting copyright licences: should either the owner

seek to reserve a right to revoke, or the licensee reserve a right to terminate,

prior to any voluntary assignment or other transmission of the copyright?

More critically perhaps, it also creates issues when considering the acquisition

of copyright property: prospective assignees should (aside from searching the

personal property security register) undertake due diligence regarding prior
licence grants and seek protection (such as through warranties and indemni-
fication) in the contract of assignment in respect of any undisclosed licensing.

8.5.5 Sound recording and unlocatable co-owners

As noted in Chapter 4 (see: 4.3.1), the manner in which Australia met its WPPT
obligations in the 2004 US-Australia Free Trade Agreement reforms meant
that economic rights could be vested in sound performers retrospectively. A
concern arose from the position of a sound producer (first owner) who may
not be able to locate a co-owning party (e.g. a performer) and who wishes
to enter into a licence agreement in respect of a published sound recording.
Section 113C deems such a co-owner to have granted the first owner permission
to grant such a licence if “the first owner, after making reasonable inquiries,
cannot discover the identity or location of the other owner”, and that missing
co-owner cannot thereafter countermand that licence.“ However, this deemed
licence comes with an obligation to hold the missing owner's licence fee share
on trust for four years in the event the missing owner comes forward or is
located in that period.”

8.6 Assignments pre-dating the 1968 Act

Within the Part XI transitional provisions of the 1968 Act is s 239 which in
part deals with assignments that occurred under the 1911 Act, prior to the
commencement of the 1968 Act. Section 239(1) provides for such assignments
that the 1911 Act’s formalities requirements apply. Those requirements were
that the owner of the copyright in any work “may assign the right ... but no
such assignment or grant shall be valid unless it is in writing signed by the
owner of the right in respect of which the assignment or grant is made”. This
is essentially the same rule as under current law, and was treated more or less
as such by Jacobson J in Larrikin Music Publishing » EMI Songs Australia. In
that case the defendants sought to impugn the copyright ownership of the
plaintiff. The plaintiff claimed its title derived from either a 1987 assignment
from the author to the Library of South Australia and from the Library to it,
or an assignment from the author's estate to it, or both. The defendant argued
that the author and first owner Marion Sinclair had in 1934 fully assigned her
copyright to a third party, the Victorian Girl Guides Association. The defend-
ants’ position was an application of nemo dat quod non habet: the plaintiff had
no copyright ownership because after the alleged assignment in 1934, Sinclair
had no title to pass to anyone else. Sinclair had entered her works into a 1934
Victorian Girl Guides Association competition between writers of songs that
were (in essence) suitable for singing around a campfire. Rule (d) of the compe-
tition published in an Association printed circular provided that: “All matter
entered to become the property of the Guide Association” . Sinclair had signed
her submitted manuscript, titled Kookaburra. Was Sinclair’s submission to the

competition a signed assignment in writing from author to the Association, in
accordance with the defendants’ submission, thereby divesting Sinclair of her
title? Jacobson J's analysis of the setting led him to conclude that it was not,
for a variety of reinforcing reasons: (i) the competition rules were not proven
to be incorporated in any agreement between the author and the Association;
(ii) the author's signature served no purpose other than to identify the work’s
authorship; (iii) subsequent dealings failed to create any compelling evidence
of an intention by the author to assign her copyright; and (iv) in any event,
the Guide Association’s rule (d) should be interpreted as relating to no more
than chattel property.”

Had Jacobson J found a valid assignment by Sinclair to the Association
in 1934, under an aspect of the 1911 Act’s regime which s 239 of the 1968 Act
“grandfathers”, the capacity of an author and first owner such as Sinclair to
assign her copyright was constrained.® In what can be properly understood
as a mechanism under the 1911 Act’s regime to protect an author who had
assigned away his or her copyright in their life, a mandatory reversionary
interest was automatically created on the death of the author. Under it, 25
years after the death of the author the balance of the copyright term (which
today for works is 45 years) reverts to the author's estate. Because she died on
15 February 1988 in Sinclair's case this would have occurred on 15 February
2013, subject to any prior dealing with the interest by the executor or trustee
of the estate.”

Also, and as noted above (see: 8.2.5), the possibility of a valid at law assign-
ment of future copyright was recognised for the first time under the 1968 Act.
Such assignments under the 1911 Act were regarded as informal purported
assignments because under s 5(2) of the 1911 Act assignments at law could
only occur under the hand of the owner of an existing right. Future assign-
ments by contract could be enforced in equity insofar as the agreement gave
rise, for the assignee’s benefit, an equitable interest in the copyright when the
copyright came into existence. The assignee would then have the right to have
that title perfected by an order mandating the assignor execute a formal deed
of assignment.** Section 239 makes clear that the 1968 Act provision permitting
assignment of future copyright has no retrospective effect so that assignments
of future copyright under the 1911 Act’s regime fall to be assessed under the
law that applied to that regime.”

Finally, Division 5 of Part XI is a regime dealing with pre-1912 works. As
explained in Chapter 4 (see: 4.8.5) since 1 January 2019 this will have more
limited scope because copyright subsistence would have ceased in unpublished
pre-1912 works if the relevant author has died prior to 1948, and otherwise

continued subsistence will be quite finite. The regime is largely explained in
Chapter 13 (see: 13.7) to the extent that it pertains to jurisdictional attachment.
For present purposes the regime includes in s 248 a provision which translates
how assignments prior to the 1911 Act should apply.*” The section also makes
provision for the possibility of the reversionary interest that was an aspect of
the 1911 Act (discussed above) applying to such assignments.’
