Motorola Solutions, Inc. v Hytera Communications Corporation Ltd (Liability) (2022) 172 IPR 221 (Perram J)
Subsistence was conceded – see [1173] - main issue was whether the respondents had taken a substantial part of the relevant copyright works 
R contended that what had been taken was not original (and therefore could not be a substantial part) because it was based on earlier versions of the same program
BUT Perram J considered that “review and revision of code, whether resulting in new code or in parts of the code being left unchanged, is an act of origination under the Copyright Act and the product of that act of origination was itself an original work”: [1240]

“There is no doubt that Hytera’s software engineers engaged in industrial scale harvesting of Motorola's source code and that that they set out to make this as difficult to detect as as possible.”

Artistic works – works of artistic craftsmanship
Works of artistic craftsmanship are a special kind of artistic work
Unlike other artistic works they do have to display a degree of skill or craftmanship and they do have to be artistic. 
Typical examples include jewellery, furniture pieces etc - does not necessarily have to be hand-made
Whether a work is a “work of artistic craftsmanship” will sometimes be critical to the question of whether one particular exception to copyright infringement applies; namely, s 77. (We will come back to this exception)

Artistic works – works of artistic craftsmanship
In Burge v Swarbrick (2007) 232 CLR 336, HCA set out criteria to be met for a work to be regarded as a work of artistic craftsmanship.  
The question “turns on assessing the extent to which the particular work’s artistic expression, in its form, is unconstrained by functional considerations”: at 364
In the case before it, the HCA considered that the designs of the plug and mould (for the manufacture of the hull and deck of a racing yacht) were directed to functional issues of speed and water dynamics, rather than aesthetics and therefore were not works of artistic craftsmanship

the work must have a “real or substantial artistic element”
“artistic craftsmanship” does not mean “artistic handicraft”
prototype can be a work of artistic craftsmanship even if it is then thrown away
not incompatible with machine production
no antithesis between “utility” and “beauty” or between “function” and “art”
special training, skill and knowledge, pride and sound workmanship are all implied in “craftsmanship” along with rejection of the shoddy, the meretricious, the facile
evidence from the creator is admissible but not determinative or necessary
beauty or aesthetic appeal is not determinative
