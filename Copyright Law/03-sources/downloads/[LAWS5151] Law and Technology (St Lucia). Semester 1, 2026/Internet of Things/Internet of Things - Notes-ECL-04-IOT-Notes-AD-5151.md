The Internet of Things

LAWS5151 Notes 2026

IoT

Internet of Things (IoT) is a conceptual framework that utilises devices, interconnection solutions, and physical objects to build a shared information base that supports applications operating across people and digital objects (Atzori et al., 2017, p137). Technically an infrastructure that connects things over interoperable information and communication to enable improved services with security-privacy standards. (Lopez-Torres, 2020).

IoT Model

For manufacturing companies, IoT systems use technology for sensing and operating on physical-world entities and interact through a digital network (IEC, 2024, p7), like production lines, self-directed vehicles, etc

Manufacturing company IoT (Manyika et al., 2015, p67)

Risks

Security, Privacy and Confidentiality

IoT can harm personal privacy and confidentiality of organisational data. It increases the number of connected devices, means more access points, raising security exposure for both industry and national scales (Manyika et al., 2015, p105).

Interoperability & Supplier lock-in

IoT depends on seamless interoperability, making integration failures a huge risk (Manyika et al., 2015, p. 104). Case evidence (Weldcorp & SupDigit), shows suppliers use proprietary components that lock-in manufacturers, creating compatibility problems, delaying delivery and raising cost (Ferreira & Lind, 2022, pp.1340-1341).

Lifecycle/device weaknesses.

IoT devices that lack unique identification, secure configuration, data protection, and updating methods are materially easier to breach (Fagan et al., 2020). Attackers can compromise a weak industrial device to target others across zones (Beyrouti et al., 2024, p13).

Skills bottlenecks

IoT required hiring scarce specialists and significant internal training, increasing time and budget pressure (Ferreira & Lind, 2022 p1339).

Australia’s Regulation

Code of Practice.

A voluntary 13-principle for IoT security, with 3 priorities; no duplicated/weak passwords, public vulnerability-disclosure policy, and secure software updates (DoHA, 2020, pp. 3-4). It calls out secure credential storage, enhances communication security, software integrity and outages resilience.

Cyber Security Strategy.

The national strategy sets a 2030 vision with six “cyber shields,” including “safe technology” to ensure Australians can trust digital products and software, and commits to co-designing landmark legislative reforms with industry to strengthen the cyber shields (DoHA, 2023, p6).

Australian Cyber Shield

Cyber Security Act 2024.

The Cyber Security Act 2024 (Cth) creates mandatory security standards for connectable products to be acquired in Australia, with manufacturer/supplier duties that must meet the standard and supply a statement of compliance and enforcement tools.

ASD Essential Eight.

The Australian Signals Directorate maintains the Essential Eight as the minimum, prioritised mitigation strategies with maturity levels and implementation guidance (ASD, 2023, p1).

Australia relies on a voluntary consumer IoT Code supported by general cyber baselines (Essential Eight) and cyber security strategy & act, for new products, creating a soft-law baseline & hard-law where risk is highest. By contrast, the EU requires all products with digital elements to meet mandatory security-by-design and vulnerability-management duties and pass conformity assessment marking before market access, stating the hard-law for the whole market (EUR-Lex, 2024). The Internet of Things (IoT) comprises physical devices fitted with sensors, software, and network connections that facilitate data sharing—often with little human input (International Telecommunication Union [ITU], 2012, p. 1). According to the ITU, IoT is “a global infrastructure for the information society, enabling advanced services by connecting (physical and virtual) things through existing and evolving interoperable information and communication technologies” (ITU, 2012, p. 1). This recommendation also highlights essential features and a high-level reference model for IoT (ITU, 2012, ss 6–7).

The majority of IoT devices are manufactured with weak default settings, limited update options, and inadequate security features. Risks results in vulnerabilities exploited by cyber-attackers. Downstream application services exacerbate impacts across the uses.  Australian law makers have advocated a security-by-design approach for consumer IoT to mitigate these risks, requiring vendors to include secure updates, authentication, configuration, and vulnerability handling.

IoT devices potentially collect or infer information about identifiable individuals such as location and usage patterns) The Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) applies to "personal information", defined in section 6 - information or an opinion about an identified or reasonably identifiable individual. The Office of the Australian Information Commissioner (OAIC) has cautioned that consumer devices can gather substantial volumes of data—including sensitive data—requiring robust controls across the lifecycle.

Operational and data integrity are crucial. Manufacturing use cases increasingly automate decisions, such as quality gates and delivery-versus-payment triggers based on sensor telemetry; inaccurate, unavailable, or manipulated data can disrupt operations.

Australia currently relies on voluntary standards as it works toward mandatory product regulations. The 2020 Code of Practice: Securing the Internet of Things for Consumers details 13 voluntary principles for manufacturers, importers, and retailers, including no default passwords, vulnerability disclosures, and software updates.

The Australian Privacy Principles (APP) govern privacy compliance under the Privacy Act 1988 (Cth), which oversees how IoT providers handle personal information. The OAIC's submission emphasises privacy and security concerns related to IoT and the importance of APP compliance.

Consumer guarantees under the Australian Consumer Law (ACL), plus product safety and recall procedures, provide further safeguards.

2023–2030 Australian Cyber Security Strategy and its 2024 explanatory document deal with mandatory standards and labelling, and considers implementing a mandatory cybersecurity standard for consumer smart devices, along with an industry-led voluntary labelling scheme. Consumers should expect proof of secure design and vulnerability management in IoT devices that the firm deploys or sources.

The Cyber Security Act 2024 commenced on 4 March 2026.  It provides a significant advance in dealing with the risks of IoT devices and technologies. The Act is the next step forward from the Code of Conduct.  It implements mandatory security standards and enhanced compliance requirements

The Act provides for the following:

Minimum cyber security standards for IoT devices
Products must comply with mandatory cyber security standards (set out in rules) and be accompanied by a formal statement of compliance. Enforcement tools include compliance, stop, and recall notices.

Mandatory ransomware payment reporting
Certain entities will be required to report ransomware payments or face civil penalties.

National cyber security coordinator
A new coordination role within government, including a voluntary information-sharing regime for significant cyber incidents.

Cyber incident review board
A statutory board empowered to review major cyber incidents and provide actionable recommendations to government and industry.

The new smart device security regime

Cyber Security Act 2024 (Cth) Part 2 provides for a dedicated regime for security standards for smart devices sold in Australia.

All IoT and network-connectable products must meet minimum cyber security standards.
Manufacturers are required to issue a statement of compliance with each product.

Where a device does not comply, the government has the power to issue:

Compliance notices – to address non-compliance

Stop notices – to prevent continued supply

Recall notices – to remove products from the market

The regime has extraterritorial reach – applying to overseas manufacturers and suppliers whose products are sold in the Australian market.

The regime applies to relevant connectable products, which include both internet-connectable and network-connectable devices, that are manufactured or supplied on or after 4 March 2026.  This includes smart devices that can connect directly or indirectly to the internet or other devices over a network.

Exemptions apply to specific devices or product categories, depending on the criteria set out in the legislation.

Manufacturers and suppliers of relevant connectable products (RCPs) have obligations under the legislation. These roles are defined in line with the Australian Consumer Law, and the obligations are set out in Division 2 of Part 2 of the Cyber Security Act 2024, primarily in sections 15 and 16.

Section 15 requires that RCPs must be manufactured in compliance with the applicable security standard if they are to be acquired in Australia in specified circumstances.

Manufacturers must ensure their products are built to meet the relevant security standards.

Suppliers must ensure the products they supply in Australia were manufactured in accordance with those same standards.

These obligations only apply where the manufacturer or supplier knows or could reasonably be expected to know that the product will be acquired in Australia.

Section 16 – provides an obligation in relation to the statement of compliance that must accompany relevant connectable products (RCPs) offered for sale in Australia.

Manufacturers must prepare and provide a statement of compliance with the security standard applicable to the relevant class of products that will be acquired in Australia in specified circumstances.

Suppliers must ensure that any RCPs they supply in Australia are accompanied by this statement of compliance.

Enforcement

Division 3 of Part 2 of the Cyber Security Act 2024 establishes the enforcement mechanisms applicable to non-compliance with the smart device security regime. There are no civil penalties.  The legislation is intended to be light touch from an enforcement perspective. The framework provides the Secretary with escalating powers to issue regulatory notices where an entity fails to meet its obligations. The three primary types of notices, compliance, stop, and recall, each serving a distinct enforcement function. A summary of the notice types and their key features is set out below.  Also note the Cyber Security (Security Standards for Smart Devices) Rules 2025.

The Secretary must provide written notice of the intention to do so and allow the relevant entity at least 10 days to make submissions as to why the notice should not be issued.  The Act also empowers the Secretary to appoint an independent expert to assess whether a product and/or its associated statement of compliance meets the requirements of the regime. The Secretary may issue a written request to the relevant manufacturer or supplier, requiring the provision of:

the product in question (and the identity of the manufacturer, if known), and

the corresponding statement of compliance.

The notice must also set out relevant procedural information, including:

the period for response,

the expected duration of product retention,

the applicable security standards for testing,

a description of the testing or analysis to be conducted, and

the potential consequences for failing to comply with the request or the Act more broadly.

Manufacturers or suppliers who are required to provide products for examination are entitled to reasonable compensation. Once received, the appointed expert may undertake a range of testing and analysis activities to assess compliance with the security standard and accuracy of the compliance statement.

Review is facilitated prior to the issuance of a notice, entities are also entitled to seek an internal review of any decision to issue or vary a compliance, stop, or recall notice under Part 2 of the Act.

Manufacturers and suppliers of smart devices should engage in an audit and review to ensure compliance.

Assess whether the products the manufacture or supply fall within the definition of a relevant connectable product (RCP). This includes both internet-connectable and network-connectable products, which are essentially, any consumer-grade device that can communicate with other devices or networks.

Consider whether the organisation is reasonably expected to know that the product will be acquired in Australia — this is the threshold that triggers the regime’s obligations.

Comply with the mandatory security standards set out in the Cyber Security (Security Standards for Smart Devices) Rules 2025 and compare them against your current product design, firmware policies, and support lifecycle.

Prepare a template statement of compliance that includes all required information required by section 16.
