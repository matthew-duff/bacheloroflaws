Illegality: Improper Exercises of Power

The core question of whether a legislative provision provides legal support for government action has been considered thus far (in workshop five) in respect of three grounds of judicial review (codified and enumerated under s 5(1)(b)-(d) and s 6(1)(b)-(d) ADJR Act 1977). These grounds were unified under the heading illegality, specifically as ‘simple ultra vires’.

When interpreting and applying legislation other (relatively subtle) issues can arise, in the course of reviewing executive action and deciding whether administrative power was lawfully exercised. For instance, have statutory powers been used for only lawful purposes, have decisions been tainted because relevant matters were overlooked or alternatively because irrelevant matters were considered in the course of acting? Furthermore, were discretionary powers exercised in a way that was legally reasonable, that is to say rationally, logically, and not capriciously?

We can term these types of review grounds as examples of ‘extended’ forms of ultra vires (or ‘excess’ of jurisdiction) that require the courts to go beyond the text of an Act and examine the context and purpose of statutory powers. You can find these grounds of review (and others) set out in s 5(1)(e) (and s 6(1)(e)) ADJR Act, which provides that “the making of the decision was an improper exercise of the power conferred by the enactment in pursuance of which it was purported to be made”.

This provision is to be understood in light of the grounds stipulated in s 5(2) ADJR Act 1977.  This subsection states: “The reference in paragraph (1)(e) to an improper exercise of a power shall be construed as including a reference to:

(a)  taking an irrelevant consideration into account in the exercise of a power;

(b)  failing to take a relevant consideration into account in the exercise of a power;

(c)  an exercise of a power for a purpose other than a purpose for which the power is conferred;

(d)  an exercise of a discretionary power in bad faith;

(e)  an exercise of a personal discretionary power at the direction or behest of another person (‘acting under dictation’);

(f)  an exercise of a discretionary power in accordance with a rule or policy without regard to the merits of the particular case (‘fettering of discretion’);

(g)  an exercise of a power that is so unreasonable that no reasonable person could have so exercised the power;

(h)  an exercise of a power in such a way that the result of the exercise of the power is uncertain; and

(j)  any other exercise of a power in a way that constitutes abuse of the power.”

We will focus on those grounds in bold above, in this workshop.

Key case-law regarding grounds cited above

Before turning to those grounds cited above, in turn, it is necessary to scrutinise one of the most important judicial review cases emanating from England over 70 years ago. The case – Associated Provincial Picture Houses v Wednesbury Corporation – concerned the limits on the broad discretionary power of a Local Authority to impose conditions on the grant of a licence to APPH to use a building for cinematographic purposes on a Sunday. In and of itself not riveting case-material, but the principles distilled in the judgment of Lord Greene MR are important.

The Cinematographic Act 1909 enabled relevant local authorities to grant licences subject “to such conditions as the authority thinks fit to impose”, and Wednesbury Corporation saw fit to grant a licence to the plaintiffs subject to the condition that children under 15 years of age should not be admitted to Sunday performances, either with or without an adult. The Court held that this was not an abuse of power, it was not unreasonable, and so the condition was valid. In reaching that conclusion Lord Greene MR set out some core statements of principle governing judicial review grounds, and the limited role of the courts. These principles warrant restatement in full:

What, then, is the power of the courts? They can only interfere with an act of executive authority if it be shown that the authority has contravened the law. It is for those who assert that the local authority has contravened the law to establish that proposition. On the face of it, a condition of the kind imposed in this case is perfectly lawful. It is not to be assumed prima facie that responsible bodies like the local authority in this case will exceed their powers; but the court, whenever it is alleged that the local authority have contravened the law, must not substitute itself for that authority. It is only concerned with seeing whether or not the proposition is made good. When an executive discretion is entrusted by Parliament to a body such as the local authority in this case, what appears to be an exercise of that discretion can only be challenged in the courts in a strictly limited class of case. As I have said, it must always be remembered that the court is not a court of appeal. When discretion of this kind is granted the law recognizes certain principles upon which that discretion must be exercised, but within the four corners of those principles the discretion, in my opinion, is an absolute one and cannot be questioned in any court of law. What then are those principles? They are well understood. They are principles which the court looks to in considering any question of discretion of this kind. The exercise of such a discretion must be a real exercise of the discretion. If, in the statute conferring the discretion, there is to be found expressly or by implication matters which the authority exercising the discretion ought to have regard to, then in exercising the discretion it must have regard to those matters. Conversely, if the nature of the subject matter and the general interpretation of the Act make it clear that certain matters would not be germane to the matter in question, the authority must disregard those irrelevant collateral matters. There have been in the cases expressions used relating to the sort of things that authorities must not do, not merely in cases under the Cinematograph Act but, generally speaking, under other cases where the powers of local authorities came to be considered. I am not sure myself whether the permissible grounds of attack cannot be defined under a single head. It has been perhaps a little bit confusing to find a series of grounds set out. Bad faith, dishonesty - those of course, stand by themselves - unreasonableness, attention given to extraneous circumstances, disregard of public policy and things like that have all been referred to, according to the facts of individual cases, as being matters which are relevant to the question. If they cannot all be confined under one head, they at any rate, I think, overlap to a very great extent. For instance, we have heard in this case a great deal about the meaning of the word "unreasonable."

It is true the discretion must be exercised reasonably. Now what does that mean? Lawyers familiar with the phraseology commonly used in relation to exercise of statutory discretions often use the word "unreasonable" in a rather comprehensive sense. It has frequently been used and is frequently used as a general description of the things that must not be done. For instance, a person entrusted with a discretion must, so to speak, direct himself properly in law. He must call his own attention to the matters which he is bound to consider. He must exclude from his consideration matters which are irrelevant to what he has to consider. If he does not obey those rules, he may truly be said, and often is said, to be acting "unreasonably." Similarly, there may be something so absurd that no sensible person could ever dream that it lay within the powers of the authority. Warrington LJ in Short v Poole Corporation gave the example of the red-haired teacher, dismissed because she had red hair. That is unreasonable in one sense. In another sense it is taking into consideration extraneous matters. It is so unreasonable that it might almost be described as being done in bad faith; and, in fact, all these things run into one another.

Aspects of Lord Greene MR’s judgment are reflected in the terms (grounds of review) set out in the judicial review statutes in Australia, as outlined below. These relate to securing the proper exercise of legal authority (often discretionary powers vested in the executive branch of government: public entities or officials).

Unauthorised purpose

An exercise of a statutory power other than for a purpose for which the power is conferred is ultra vires. This common law ground of review has deep roots in our legal system and is enshrined in s 5(2)(c) ADJR Act 1977 as a ground of review. It is fundamental to the rule of law that government decision-makers cannot enlarge by their own acts, the scope of power conferred on them by a parliament. But how do the courts determine authorised decision-making purposes? And further, how do they ascertain whether an improper (or, ulterior) purpose has animated the government decision-maker?

Where statutory purposes are not disclosed the courts will identify, and imply lawful purposes from the title, structure and text of the Act, and may have regard to relevant extrinsic materials if necessary. Schlieske v Minister for Immigration illustrates how powers were defined with reference to implied statutory purposes. It also serves to illustrate how the court identified (by inference) what the actual purpose, motivating a decision, was.

Schlieske was a German national who had used a false Swiss passport to enter Australia, from the Philippines and was, therefore, deemed (under s 18 of the Migration Act 1958 (Cth)) to be a prohibited non-citizen and liable to deportation.  Germany sought his extradition from Australia for the alleged commission of several drug-related crimes, but his extradition failed because the legal requirements for extradition were not met. An official in the Immigration department then made a deportation order under the Migration Act s 18 which was not carried out pending the outcome of a fresh extradition request, which also failed.

So, with extradition proceedings finalised (unsuccessfully) German embassy officials and Australian government officers discussed the implementation of the pending deportation order. The Order was then served, and a seat booked on a Lufthansa flight and pursuant to s 22 of the Migration Act an immigration official purported to require the airline to receive Schlieske on board. Failing to receive the deportee and effect their conveyance to a specified place, in breach of s 22, would leave Lufthansa facing a financial penalty. Arrangements were made for two German police officers to come to Australia with the intention of meeting Schlieske when he boarded the Lufthansa flight. Effectively the Australian government were delivering Schlieske to the German Government at Sydney airport. The Immigration Department were aware of these arrangements.

It was argued that the deportation order was unlawful because it was a ‘disguised extradition’ and not, therefore, a power exercised for proper purposes under the Migration Act. The Federal Court agreed, Wilcox and French JJ stated:

Section 18 of the Migration Act 1958 authorises the Minister to order the deportation of prohibited non-citizens. That authorisation is an expression of the power of the Parliament of the Commonwealth to make laws with respect to immigration and emigration: Constitution s 51(xxvii). The authority so conferred is not expressly qualified or conditioned in any way. Like all statutory discretions, however, it is to be exercised in accordance with the scope and purposes of the enactment which is its source … .

… [I]n recent times and corresponding with the developing willingness of the courts to confine the exercise of statutory powers to the purpose for which they were granted, the courts of both countries [Australia and the United Kingdom] have generated a distinction — which is difficult of practical application — between a deportation for the purpose of extradition (“disguised extradition”) and a deportation for immigration control purposes which incidentally effects a de facto extradition. …

[I]t is plainly extraneous to the decision to deport a person, and to deport that person to a particular country, that the person is wanted by the government of that country upon criminal charges. It is not one of the purposes of the Migration Act to aid foreign powers to bring fugitives to justice. There is a distinct head of constitutional authority — namely the external affairs power — and a distinct mechanism — the extradition legislation — under which that object may be pursued. As to the statutory powers in aid of the deportation process which are conferred by s 22 of the Act, those powers must also be used only for their proper purpose, namely to give effect to a deportation order in force. …

The foregoing does not mean that the Minister is precluded from deporting a person to a country where, to the Minister's knowledge, the person is likely to face criminal proceedings. Having regard only to immigration considerations, deportation of the person to that country may be a proper course; most obviously so in a case, such as the present, in which the proposed deportee is a national of that country and has travel documents valid only for entry into that country.

In the present case a finding has been made that particular actions taken by officers of the Department of Immigration and Ethnic Affairs in July 1987 amounted to a “sham” extradition; that is that, although the officers purported to be acting in the enforcement of the Migration Act, they were at that time in fact actuated by the purpose of delivering Mr Schlieske to the West German authorities in order that he might be tried upon the charges pending against him in that country. That finding does not mean that Mr Schlieske cannot now be deported to the Federal Republic. It does mean that, in implementing the deportation order, the Minister and his officers must pursue only the purpose of deportation.

So here the golden rule is that Australian officials can enforce the Migration Act, through (inter alia) use of their powers of deportation, but they cannot go beyond that and, in purported exercise of Migration Act powers, seek to effect the delivery of an alleged criminal into the hands of a foreign country: that amounts to an unlawful extradition. Australia could lawfully deport Schlieske for immigration control purposes, but not for the purpose of surrendering him to prosecuting authorities in Germany.

This case clearly illustrates the procedural nature of judicial review because the desired substantive outcome – Schlieske’s return to Germany –still eventuated following the Federal Court’s decision, but the process by which it was realised changed.  That said, ensuring public powers are only used for lawful purposes can have dramatic (substantive) effects, as illustrated by the High Court case of R v Toohey; ex parte Northern Land Council (1981).

Relevant Considerations

Turning to the ‘relevancy’ grounds which are grounds of review that are codified in s 5(2)(a)-(b) ADJR Act. A decision may be unlawful where irrelevant matters are taken into account or where government decision-makers overlook relevant considerations.

Generally, a decision will be invalid where an irrelevant consideration has been taken into account by a decision-maker, unless the reviewing court determines that the consideration is insignificant. This entails a court first ascertaining what matters were weighed up by the decision-maker before taking action, and second, determining whether any of the matters taken into account were irrelevant.

A leading case is Murphyhores v Commonwealth (1976), in which the plaintiffs, who held mining leases over parts of Fraser Island/K'gari, had sought permission to export mineral concentrates. The Minister for Minerals and Energy announced that before deciding to grant an export approval an inquiry into the effect of sand mining would be held under the Environment Protection Act 1974 (Cth).

The plaintiffs argued (inter alia) it was unlawful for the Minister to consider the environmental aspects of mining on the island. This (they argued) was an irrelevant (or extraneous) consideration vis-à-vis a decision to permit the export of concentrates.

The High Court disagreed, in so doing the Court turned its attention to the legislation that conferred power on the Minister. There were no express limitations written into the legislative instrument (Commonwealth Customs Regulations) governing the Minister’s decision-making powers. And there was no suggestion that the Minister had not exercised the power in a bona fide manner (meaning there was no suggestion or evidence he’d exercised the power for personal or whimsical reasons for example). Given the absence of criteria that could be inferred from the legislative instrument that would serve to limit the considerations that officials may have regard to when making export approval decisions, the Minister was free to pay regard to the environmental effect of sand mining operations on Fraser Island/K'gari in deciding whether or not to allow the export of the plaintiff’s mineral concentrates.

To determine those matters that a decision-maker is required to turn their mind to and consider, the courts will examine the express terms in the power-conferring legislation. However, statutes do not always stipulate exhaustively what the mandatory relevant considerations are; they are often open-textured or may embody expansive notions, such as ‘the national interest’ (which is a broad and, largely, political question).

Therefore, the courts, frequently, have to consider what is to be inferred as relevant – given the language, object and purpose of the statute, nature of the power being exercised and the character of the decision-maker. Thus, whether a particular consideration is a mandatory matter is, typically, a question of statutory construction. Then having identified what is (and is not) legally relevant, there is a separate question about what constitutes adequate consideration of the matter.

Legal unreasonableness

Judicial review on the ground of unreasonableness is justified on the basis that the courts do not attribute to the legislature an intention to authorise the exercise of a power unreasonably. A seminal authority on unreasonableness is the British case of Associated Provincial Picture Houses v Wednesbury Corp referred to above. This common law principle is reflected in the terms of s 5(2)(g) ADJR Act.

In that case, counsel for APPH challenged the exercise of discretionary power of the Wednesbury Corporation on the basis it was ‘unreasonable’ – that is to say, perverse, absurd, or capricious. Counsel used ‘unreasonableness’ as an independent ground of judicial review, not because the Local Authority had, for example, considered ‘irrelevant’ matters, or acted in ‘bad faith’.

Counsel appeared to make the argument that it was the court that was the ultimate arbiter of what was reasonable, not the Local Authority. This argument was treated as fallacious by the Court for the following reason:

It is clear that the local authority are entrusted by Parliament with the decision on a matter which the knowledge and experience of that authority can best be trusted to deal with. The subject-matter with which the condition deals is one relevant for its consideration. They have considered it and come to a decision upon it. It is true to say that, if a decision on a competent matter is so unreasonable that no reasonable authority could ever have come to it, then the courts can interfere. That, I think, is quite right; but to prove a case of that kind would require something overwhelming, and, in this case, the facts do not come anywhere near anything of that kind. I think Mr Gallop [legal counsel for APPH] in the end agreed that his proposition that the decision of the local authority can be upset if it is proved to be unreasonable, really meant that it must be proved to be unreasonable in the sense that the court considers it to be a decision that no reasonable body could have come to. It is not what the court considers unreasonable, a different thing altogether.

So in the material extracted above, Lord Greene MR makes clear, first, that various grounds of review are interrelated and to an extent overlap. Indeed, it is perfectly possible to justifiably characterise certain legal mistakes in several ways, as illustrated with reference to the decision to dismiss a teacher because of their red hair: that is an ‘unreasonable’ (absurd) exercise of public power, it also evidences ‘bad faith’ and it is tainted by an ‘irrelevant consideration’. Second, Lord Greene makes clear that ‘unreasonableness’ is a test that is difficult to satisfy and that is concerned with determining what decision a reasonable public body would make; it is not to substitute the court’s view of reasonableness for that of a public body or official. Lord Greene concluded by stating:

The court is entitled to investigate the action of the local authority with a view to seeing whether they have taken into account matters which they ought not to take into account, or, conversely, have refused to take into account or neglected to take into account matters which they ought to take into account. Once that question is answered in favour of the local authority, it may be still possible to say that, although the local authority have kept within the four corners of the matters which they ought to consider, they have nevertheless come to a conclusion so unreasonable that no reasonable authority could ever have come to it. In such a case, again, I think the court can interfere. The power of the court to interfere in each case is not as an appellate authority to override a decision of the local authority, but as a judicial authority which is concerned, and concerned only, to see whether the local authority have contravened the law by acting in excess of the powers which Parliament has confided in them.

The ‘Wednesbury’ test has a close affinity with notions of ‘irrationality’ (recall Lord Diplock in CCSU v Minister for the Civil Service) and ‘illogicality’. These formulations are attempts to avoid the circularity in Lord Greene’s definition, and to identify parts of the underlying philosophy of judicial review and guide future developments. As French CJ observed in the case of Minister for Immigration v Li, “lawfulness, fairness and rationality ... lie at the heart of administrative justice.”

Satisfying the threshold referred to by Lord Greene MR in Wednesbury is difficult, and though often pleaded there are few reported cases where it has been successfully argued. Another reason why unreasonableness is applied cautiously by the courts in Australia is because of the backdrop of the legality/merits distinction, which is of course referable to the Australian Constitution.

One important case is Minister for Immigration v Li (2013). In outline, Li was an overseas student training to be a cook. She had applied for a skilled student visa, but her application was refused because she had not obtained an assessment that her skills met the required criteria for the visa grant.  She appealed (via ‘merits review’) the refusal of a visa to the Migration Review Tribunal (which at that time was a free-standing tribunal – today its functions are performed by the ART). Another skills assessment was unsuccessful in November 2009.

Li’s migration agent advised the MRT that the second assessment was flawed due to two fundamental errors, and that a review of that negative assessment had been sought. The agent requested that the MRT make no decision until that review of the skills assessment was concluded. Without waiting however, the MRT proceeded to affirm the initial (negative) decision, on the basis that Li had been given enough opportunities to present her case and had not provided evidence that she had sought a re-assessment.

Upon a judicial review to the Federal Circuit Court the MRT’s approach was found to be ‘unreasonable’.  This FCC decision was upheld by the Federal Court, and, subsequently, was appealed to the High Court (Minister for Immigration v Li (2013) 249 CLR 332), which also found the MRT’s approach to the matter to be legally flawed.

Judicial Review and Human Rights Protection

Australia, notoriously, lacks a federal bill of rights; notorious because unlike most other liberal democracies, Australia does not have either a constitutionally entrenched Bill of Rights or a national human rights statute. Instead, there are limited protections found in the Australian Constitution (either expressly or by implication) and legislation has been passed by Commonwealth, state and territory governments that outlaw, for example, discrimination on assorted grounds (race, age, sex and disability).

It is, therefore, not possible for people to directly challenge the validity of legislation on the grounds of its non-conformity with human rights norms. As a result, individuals and groups that wish to use the courts to vindicate what they regard as human rights abuses will need to find some way of couching their claims in a manner that is cognizable to the law. While human rights claims of one sort or another are sometimes made in the course of legal proceedings relating to the criminal law or tort law, they tend to be made with greater frequency in the administrative law (as well as constitutional law) context. Why might that be? Boughey offers one hypothesis when she notes that “[b]oth administrative law and human rights law are principally concerned with ensuring that public power is exercised fairly”.

Within the realm of administrative law, how might human rights norms be brought to bear? Duxbury, identifies three sources of human rights that might influence administrative decision-making and judicial review of such decision-making.

First, international human rights law might be held to condition the manner in which executive power is exercised, at least in some instances. So, for instance, where an administrative decision-maker is given discretion to make a certain decision, administrative law doctrine could ‘read in’ a requirement that that official not exercise his or her discretion in a manner inconsistent with the provisions of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, or Convention on the Rights of the Child, for instance.

Secondly, administrative law principles could be shaped by “general humanitarian values”, in addition to or in place of specific human rights provisions formally recognised in international law.

Thirdly, Duxbury asserts, human rights norms may emerge from the common law, including the notion of fundamental rights that the ‘principle of legality’ serves to protect.

In terms of the specific features of the administrative law landscape in Australia where human rights claims might be brought to bear, we should first consider the issue of statutory interpretation, and secondly the grounds of review, including procedural fairness and ‘relevant considerations’ in particular. So, for instance, we already know from cases such as Saeed (workshop four) and Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth (workshop two) that the ‘principle of legality’ can be understood as requiring the courts to interpret legislation, as far as is possible, in a manner that renders it consistent with fundamental individual rights. Recall the language from Gleeson CJ in Plaintiff S157: “... courts do not impute to the legislature an intention to abrogate or curtail fundamental rights or freedoms unless such an intention is clearly manifested by unmistakable and unambiguous language.”

That is, courts will not interpret a legislative provision as interfering with or derogating from fundamental common law rights unless the language of the provision clearly and plainly reveals that this was the intention of the legislature. We can regard this as one way in which Australia’s administrative law regime gives effect to human rights.

Consider a couple of other interesting cases that highlight the interaction between human rights and statutory interpretation in the administrative law context. First, in Evans v New South Wales the Federal Court dealt with Clause 7(1)(b) of the World Youth Day Regulation 2008 (NSW), which purported to “empower an authorised person to direct a person within a World Youth Day declared area to cease engaging in conduct that causes annoyance to participants in a World Youth Day event”.

The Federal Court in this case granted a declaration that the Clause was invalid, as the very broad language of the provision appeared to authorise an interference with the fundamental right to freedom of speech. Since the legislation did not clearly express an intention that Clause 7 was to authorise such an interference with a fundamental (common law) right, the application of the principle of legality entailed that the legislation did not authorise a clause to have that effect.

Second, in Coco v The Queen, the salient issue before the court was whether incriminating information obtained from a listening device installed on private property by police officers, posing as Telco workers, could be used to support a criminal conviction. The High Court found that the unauthorised entry onto private property, to install the device constituted trespass on its face. As such, and since there was no clear legislative language sufficient to rebut the presumption that Parliament did not intend to interfere with fundamental rights and freedoms, the High Court found that there was no relevant authority to enter private property to install the device.

In Coco the High Court approached the task of statutory interpretation by applying the common law ‘principle of legality’. Four members of the Court stated, in a joint judgment:

The insistence on express authorization of an abrogation or curtailment of a fundamental right, freedom or immunity must be understood as a requirement for some manifestation or indication that the legislature has not only directed its attention to the question of the abrogation or curtailment of such basic rights, freedoms or immunities but has also determined upon abrogation or curtailment of them. The courts should not impute to the legislature an intention to interfere with fundamental rights. Such an intention must be clearly manifested by unmistakable and unambiguous language.

In short, when interpreting statutory powers, the courts invoke common law rights and freedoms; where constructional choices are open to the courts, Acts are construed in a rights-respecting manner so that basic rights are not encroached upon.

Human rights Acts in Australia also include provisions requiring that all Acts of Parliament enacted in the relevant jurisdiction be interpreted where possible (i.e. consistent with the purposes of the Act being interpreted) in a manner that is compatible with the human rights set out in the relevant human rights legislation.  The relevant provision in Queensland is s 48(1) and (2) of the Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld), which is discussed further below.  The equivalent interpretative provision in the Victorian Charter of Human Rights and Responsibilities Act 2006 (Vic) (s 32) was linked to the principle of legality by members of the High Court in Momcilovic v The Queen.  According to French CJ:

Section 32(1) [of the Victorian Charter] … requires statutes to be construed against the background of human rights and freedoms set out in the Charter in the same way as the principle of legality requires the same statutes to be construed against the background of common law rights and freedoms. The human rights and freedoms set out in the Charter in significant measure incorporate or enhance rights and freedoms at common law. Section 32(1) applies to the interpretation of statutes in the same way as the principle of legality but with a wider field of application.

The way statutes are interpreted in administrative law proceedings can also serve to help give effect to international human rights obligations. There exists, for instance, a presumption that Parliament intends to legislate in accordance with international law, including international law regarding human rights. In particular, where a statue has been enacted pursuant to a treaty obligation, any ambiguities in the text should be given “a construction which accords with Australia’s obligations”.

So the courts presume that Parliament intends to give effect to Australia’s obligations under international law, but only where legislation is ambiguous and where constructional choices are open to the courts. It is, therefore, open to the legislature to deprive people of fundamental rights (such as, freedom from arbitrary detention) via express legislative terms (as in Chu Kheng Lim). As Kiefel J stated in the Malaysian Declaration Case (recall the case from workshop five):

In Polites v The Commonwealth it was accepted that a statute is to be interpreted and applied, so far as its language permits, so that it is in conformity, and not in conflict, with established rules of international law. In Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Teoh, Mason CJ and Deane J took the proposition to apply to favour the construction of a statute which is in conformity, and not in conflict, with Australia’s international obligations, at least so far as the language of the legislation permits. The ambiguity, to which such a construction was relevant, should not be viewed narrowly, in their Honours’ view. Their Honours went on to say:

“So expressed, the principle is no more than a canon of construction and does not import the terms of the treaty or convention into our municipal law as a source of individual rights and obligations.”

… However, if it is not possible to construe a statute conformably with international law rules, the provisions of the statute must be enforced even if they amount to a contravention of accepted principles of international law.

Given the presumption that legislation is intended to comply with Australia’s international human rights obligations, can we regard these obligations as ‘relevant considerations’ that must be considered by administrative decision-makers? The answer to this question appears to be ‘no’ (see Kioa v West).

Human rights norms might also find their way into administrative law proceedings via the notion of procedural fairness. The natural question to ask here is whether the violation of (at least certain) human rights norms necessarily entails a violation of procedural fairness. There does not appear to be good authority for this general proposition. In Minister of State for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Teoh, the High Court did, however, hold that “an unincorporated treaty could give rise to a legitimate expectation for the purposes of the law relating to procedural fairness”. Specifically, it was found that Australia’s ratification of an international convention was sufficient to give rise to a legitimate expectation that if a decision-maker intended to act inconsistently with the requirements of the Convention on the Rights of the Child, “procedural fairness requires that the person affected should be given notice and an adequate opportunity of presenting a case against the taking of such a course”.

This use of an unincorporated treaty to flesh out the content of procedural fairness has been heavily criticised, however, including, to a large extent, by the High Court itself in the subsequent case of Minister for Immigration and Multicultural Affairs; Ex parte Lam. One of the criticisms raised against the decision in Teoh is that there is, arguably, something fundamentally undemocratic about an international convention ratified by the executive branch, but never enacted via legislation from the legislative branch, determining the contours of what procedural fairness entails in a given case.

We should also take note of the fact that certain administrative law principles and doctrines can potentially be seen as undermining human rights protection. These include non-justiciability, standing requirements, and ‘privative clauses’.

The doctrine of non-justiciability, for instance, prevents certain decisions—those held to be inappropriate for judicial review—from being reviewed by the courts. Privative clauses (that seek to oust people’s capacity to access justice via judicial review) purport to do likewise. Standing requirements limit the range of individuals and organisations that can seek judicial review of administrative decisions. To the extent that we think human rights norms find protection through judges via judicial review proceedings, it is clear that elements of administrative law doctrine that reduce the availability of such review will naturally be seen to undermine rather than advance the cause of human rights protection.

By way of conclusion, we can note Duxbury’s observation that courts in Australia are “more comfortable defending fundamental or common law rights, but cautious when using international human rights law”. We can also step back and query whether good public administration implies respect for human rights in the first place. It may seem that this is obviously the case. Boughey, however, suggests that perhaps that is not so. What could motivate this view? Recall here that the principle of legality contemplates that human rights—in particular, ‘fundamental’ human rights recognised by the common law—may be abrogated or modified by legislation that expresses a clear and unmistakeable intention to cause that result. Would refusing to give effect to such a piece of legislation, on the grounds that it was inconsistent with some human rights norm, further the cause of good administration or undermine it? Boughey appears to take the latter view.

Further, there have been some warnings that were the courts to move too far in the direction of using administrative law principles in an attempt to secure respect for human rights norms of one kind or another, this would undermine the ‘integrity’ of administrative law including, potentially, by leading courts to violate the separation of powers and going beyond their constitutionally prescribed judicial review function. The fear, that is, appears to be that if the courts focus too much on human rights in conducting judicial review, this will lead them to stray into merits review, by interfering with lawful administrative decisions that judges find ultimately incorrect or unjust in light of the decisions violating certain human rights norms.

The Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld) and Government Accountability

Introduction

In 2019, the Queensland Parliament passed the Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld) (‘HRA’), making Queensland only the third jurisdiction in Australia (after the ACT and Victoria) to adopt human rights legislation. In this section the HRA is introduced and explained: what the legislation covers, how it will operate and what it will mean for human rights protection in Queensland. Although the HRA was passed by the Queensland Parliament in 2019, the substantive human rights, public entity obligations, complaint-handling and other operative provisions commenced from 1 January 2020.

Section 3 of the HRA lists the three main objects of the Act:

to protect and promote human rights;

to help build a culture in the Queensland public sector that respects and promotes human rights; and

to help promote a dialogue about the nature, meaning and scope of human rights.

The Bill’s main objectives were to:

establish and consolidate statutory protections for certain human rights;

ensure that public functions are exercised in a way that is compatible with human rights;

promote a dialogue about the nature, meaning and scope of human rights; and

rename and empower the Anti-Discrimination Commission Queensland as the Queensland Human Rights Commission to:

provide a dispute resolution process for dealing with human rights complaints; and

promote an understanding, acceptance and public discussion of human rights.

These are important aims, not least because of Queensland’s history of public corruption and maladministration leading up to the Fitzgerald Inquiry from 1987-1989.

Section 4 of the HRA provides that the main objects are to be achieved primarily by

identifying the human rights that Parliament specifically seeks to protect and promote;

requiring public entities to act and make decisions in a way that is compatible with human rights;

requiring statements of compatibility to be tabled for all Queensland Bills;

providing that Parliament may override inconsistencies between statutory provisions and human rights;

requiring courts and tribunals to interpret Queensland legislation in a way that is compatible (or most compatible) with human rights;

authorising the Supreme Court to issue a declaration of incompatibility if a statutory provision cannot be interpreted compatibly with human rights (and providing for reporting procedures about such declarations of incompatibility); and

expanding the functions of the Queensland Human Rights Commission to resolve human rights complaints and to promote an understanding and acceptance of human rights and the HRA in Queensland.

Application of Human Rights

The Queensland Parliament’s intention is that the Act be in accordance with the ‘dialogue model’ of human rights protection which promotes discussion (or ‘dialogue’) about human rights between the three branches of government.  Under this model, ‘each of the three arms of government – the executive, the legislature (parliament) and the courts – have a legitimate role to play, while the parliament maintains sovereignty.’  Accordingly, in addition to promoting human rights, the HRA places specific duties and obligations on all branches of government: the executive plays its part through administrative decision-making and policy development, the legislature (via its committees and parliamentary debates) scrutinises and passes legislation, and the judiciary strives to interpret laws in a way that is human rights compatible and adjudicate on whether public entities have administered the law in a way that promotes and respects human rights.

However, parliamentary sovereignty (legislative supremacy) is clearly preserved: when introducing the HRA into Parliament, the Attorney-General noted as follows:

Importantly, in this model parliament remains sovereign and may, if it wishes, intentionally pass legislation that is not compatible with the human rights in the bill.  In this sense, the bill draws on the tradition of legislative protection of human rights associated with the United Kingdom rather than the United States Bill of Rights.  This bill, once enacted, will be an ordinary act of parliament.

The HRA does not automatically override other laws that are inconsistent (incompatible) with those human rights that are enumerated in the HRA. Indeed, as an ordinary Act of Parliament the HRA itself is vulnerable to amendment or repeal by future Parliaments which have the capacity to enact (or amend or repeal) the HRA.

The HRA defines ‘human rights’ in s 7 as the 23 human rights that are listed in ss 15-37.  The rights are primarily civil and political, along with two economic, social and cultural rights. Section 11 clarifies that only individuals in Queensland (not corporations) have human rights.  A note in s 11(2) states explicitly: ‘A corporation does not have human rights.’

Limitations on human rights

It is important to be aware that some (but not all) international human rights norms may be subject to limitations (meaning they are not absolute and unqualified rights). For example freedom of expression, right of peaceful assembly and freedom of association can be qualified but any limitations must be ‘prescribed by law’ and relate to public safety, health and morals, public order or national security, and respect for the rights and freedoms of others.

Similarly, the human rights protected under the HRA are not absolute and may be subject under law to reasonable limits that can be demonstrably justified in a free and democratic society in order to protect the rights of others or important public policy issues.

Section 13(1) sets out information on how human rights may be limited, providing:

A human right may be subject under law only to reasonable limits that can be demonstrably justified in a free and democratic society based on human dignity, equality and freedom.

The words ‘subject under law’ mean that a law or regulation may allow a human right (or rights) to be limited.  If a law or regulation for this purpose cannot be identified, a human right may not be able to be limited.

If a relevant law or regulation can be identified, then a range of factors must be considered.  These factors are intended to align generally with the principle of proportionality to guide the determination of whether a limit on a right is justifiable.

Section 13(2)(a)-(b) sets out factors regarding the nature of the human right and the nature of the purpose of the limitation on the human right, which may be identified by answering the following questions:

Which human right(s) protected by the Act is/are/will be subject to limits?

What is the purpose of the human right(s)?

What are the values that underpin the right(s) and what is its (are their) scope?

What is the purpose of the limitation (for example, is it sufficiently important to justify limiting the human right)?

Some limitations on rights will need more substantial justifications than others.  For example, it would be easier and more acceptable to justify limitations to the rights to freedom of expression or freedom of movement than it would be to justify limiting freedom from torture, or cruel, inhuman and degrading treatment.

If the purpose of imposing a limitation on a human right is not consistent with the values of a free and democratic society, then the limitation may not be reasonable or justified.

Section 13(2)(c) requires consideration of the relationship between the limitation and its purpose – for example, does the limitation help to achieve the purpose?  Is there evidence to support this?  If the answers to the questions are ‘no’, then the limitation may not be reasonable and justified.  However, if a rational connection can be made between the limitation and the reason for which it is proposed, it may be reasonable and justified to limit the human right.

Next, is the limitation strictly necessary?  Or is there an alternative way to achieve the same purpose?  Which option has the least impact on human rights?  If there is another way to achieve the purpose of the limitation that is less restrictive and reasonably available, then limiting the human right may be unreasonable or unjustified: see s 13(2)(d).

Finally, s 13(2)(g) calls for a balancing exercise between the matters in paragraphs (e) and (f): is the importance of the purpose of the law limiting the human right greater than the importance of preserving the human right in this case, taking into account the nature and extent of the limitation?  If the answer to this question is yes, then the proposed limitation may be reasonable and demonstrably justified under s 13.

The more important the human right, and the greater the incursion on it imposed by the limitation, the more important the purpose of the limitation will have to be to show that limiting the human right is reasonable and demonstrably justified.

What follows below is particularly important for our studies because it relates to the context of government (executive) accountability for human rights promotion and protection in Queensland.

‘Public entity’ obligations:

One important human rights protection under the HRA is the obligations that it places on public entities to act and make decisions in a way that is compatible with human rights and, when making decisions, to give proper consideration to a human right or rights relevant to the decision: s 58(1).  This means that Queensland public officials and government departments and employees must observe the rights in the HRA when they formulate policies and provide services.  They must act in a way that is compatible with HRA rights and take relevant human rights into proper consideration when making decisions.  This was intended to be a ‘two-pronged’ approach that would embed human rights across all public entity functions.

So what is a public entity for the purposes of these obligations?

Section 9 defines the meaning of ‘public entity’.  The first category consists of ‘core’ public entities that are relatively easy to identify.  Section 9(1) lists examples of core public entities such as government entities within the meaning of s 24 of the Public Service Act 2008 (Qld), public service employees, members of the Queensland Police Service (including non-State police officers), local governments, local government councillors or employees, Ministers when not exercising functions connected with parliamentary proceedings, members of portfolio committees when the committee is acting in an administrative capacity, courts or tribunals when acting in an administrative capacity, staff members or executive officers of public entities, entities prescribed by regulation to be a public entity, or entities that have agreed to accept obligations under the HRA.  These entities fit within the definition of public entity at all times, no matter what functions they are performing.  An entity matching these examples or descriptions must comply with the public entity obligations imposed under the HRA.

The HRA expressly excludes the legislative and judicial branches of government (although contrast s 5(2)(a)).  Section 9(4) excludes the Parliament or a person performing functions in connection with parliamentary proceedings or a court or tribunal ‘except when acting in an administrative capacity’ from public entity obligations, which bind only the executive government in accordance with the separation of powers doctrine.

If an entity does not match the examples or descriptions of core public entities, the second category includes ‘functional’ public entities: entities that fall within the definition of public entity only when they are performing functions of a public nature.  This category reflects the modern operation of government in which many public services are delivered by non-governmental organisations on behalf of the State.  It includes entities established under an Act when they are performing functions of a public nature and entities whose functions include functions of a public nature when they are performing those functions for the State or for a public entity: s 9(1)(f) and (h).

The next question to be answered is: when is a function of a public nature?  The short answer is that a function is of a public nature when it is carried out in connection with a government responsibility.

Section 10 provides some guidance for determining whether an entity’s function is of a public nature, setting out a non-exhaustive list of matters to be considered when deciding whether an entity’s function is a function of a public nature for HRA purposes: see s 10(1).  They include whether the function is carried out because of a requirement under law, whether the function is connected to or generally identified with government functions, and whether the entity is publicly funded to perform the function.  Section 10(3) provides a non-exhaustive list of functions that are of a public nature, such as the operation of a corrective services facility or other detention centre and the provision of emergency services, public health services, public education, public transport and public housing.

There are some exceptions in s 58(2)-(4) to public entity obligations, such as where a public entity could not reasonably have acted differently or made a different decision because of a statutory provision or another law (in other words, the public entity is acting to give effect to a statutory provision that is not compatible with human rights), or where a public entity is a religious body whose act or decision is in accordance with the doctrine of the religion concerned, or where the act or decision is of a private nature.

The HRA requires public entities to act compatibly with human rights.  It is unlawful for a public entity to fail to comply with the obligations to act and make decisions in a way that is compatible with human rights.  Individuals are also able to make a complaint when a public entity acts or makes decisions inconsistently with the HRA.

Human Rights Complaints in Court: ‘Piggy-back’ action and unlawfulness on human rights grounds

The HRA does not create a stand-alone cause of action for an alleged breach of human rights.  However, if a complainant is entitled to pursue a separate cause of action seeking any relief or remedy in relation to a public entity’s act or decision (for example, judicial review), s 59 provides that the alleged human rights breach may operate as an additional ground of ‘unlawfulness’ that can be attached to the separate, independent cause of action.  This has been referred to as a ‘piggy-back’ action.

Section 59 further provides that even if the separate cause of action is unsuccessful, the complainant may obtain the relief or remedy that was available in the separate cause of action if the human rights complaint is upheld, unless the relief or remedy consists of damages.  Section 59(3) states that a human rights breach will not entitle the complainant to an award of damages.

Thus s 59 action is conditional because it arises only if a complainant has a separate entitlement to seek any relief or remedy because a public entity’s act or decision was unlawful based on another ground independently of the HRA (that is, other than because of the alleged human rights breach).  If that condition is satisfied, the complainant may seek the relief or remedy on a supplementary ground of unlawfulness (that is, unlawfulness based on the human rights breach) in respect of the same act or decision.

Turning to the new human rights obligations on the legislative branch of the State.

Statements of Compatibility:

Section 38 of the HRA provides that all Members of Parliament who propose to introduce a Bill into Parliament must prepare and table a statement of compatibility for the Bill.  This applies to all Bills, whether government or private members’ Bills.  The statement of compatibility must set out whether the Bill is compatible or incompatible with the human rights in the HRA, in the opinion of the Member who has introduced the Bill.  The statement of compatibility must provide reasons that explain how the Bill is compatible, and the nature and extent of any incompatibility. Statements of compatibility do not bind courts or tribunals in the future. The HRA further provides that a similar statement (called a human rights certificate) must be prepared for any subordinate legislation: s 41.

Override declarations:

The Queensland Parliament is also given the power to declare that legislation or a legislative provision is valid and operates despite being incompatible with the HRA, called an ‘override declaration’: see s 43.  The effect of an override declaration is that the HRA does not apply to the declared legislation (or provision) to the extent of the declaration while the declaration is in force: s 45.  There is a statutory ‘sunset clause’ – a provision that includes an override declaration expires 5 years after its commencement (or earlier if expressly stated), although an override declaration may be re-enacted by Parliament at any time: see ss 45(2) and 46.

The HRA makes it clear that override declarations should only be used in exceptional circumstances, such as war, a state of emergency or an exceptional crisis situation constituting a threat to public safety, health or order. In practice, however, the Parliament in Queensland has made a number of declarations in circumstances that are difficult to describe as exceptional.  If an override declaration is made, then a statement explaining the exceptional circumstances is required to justify to Parliament the need to override human rights: see s 44.

The HRA also mandates human rights scrutiny of new legislation to complement existing parliamentary portfolio committee requirements.  Under s 39, the parliamentary committee that is responsible for examining a Bill introduced into Parliament must (a) consider and report to Parliament about whether the Bill is compatible with human rights, and (b) consider the statement of compatibility and report to Parliament about it.

Turning now to the adjudicative institutions, the Queensland courts and tribunals: they also have obligations under the HRA, in particular when they are exercising certain functions, interpreting legislation and adjudicating legal disputes for human rights claims. The HRA binds all persons, including the State of Queensland.  It applies to a court or tribunal to the extent that the court or tribunal exercises functions under Part 2 and Part 3 Div 3: ss 5(1)-(2)(a).

The relevant functions of Queensland courts and tribunals under Part 2 (the human rights provisions) include those functions which involve applying or enforcing the human rights related to court and tribunal proceedings: for example, rights to recognition and equality before the law, the right to a fair hearing and rights in criminal proceedings.  Courts and tribunals are required to apply or enforce those rights.

Statutory interpretation:

Section 48 of the HRA requires Queensland courts and tribunals to interpret statutory provisions, to the extent possible that is consistent with their purpose, in a way that is compatible with human rights, or most compatible with human rights.  This obligation does not permit courts and tribunals to vary from Parliament’s intention when it enacted a particular law.  The interpretation by a court or tribunal must be consistent with the legislative purpose of the statutory provision.

The term ‘compatible with human rights’ is defined in s 8: An act, decision or statutory provision is compatible with human rights if it does not limit a human right or limits it only to the extent that is reasonable and demonstrably justifiable in accordance with s 13 (which sets out applicable limitations to human rights).

When interpreting a statutory provision, Queensland courts and tribunals may consider international law and the judgments of domestic, foreign and international courts and tribunals where these are relevant to a human right: s 48(3). However, the requirement to interpret legislation in a way that is compatible (or most compatible) with human rights does not authorise courts and tribunals to invalidate any laws.  It also does not apply to a statutory provision that is currently subject to an override declaration.

The Queensland Supreme Court or Court of Appeal may make a declaration of incompatibility to the effect that the court believes that a statutory provision cannot be interpreted in a way that is compatible with human rights: s 53.  If the court is considering making a declaration of incompatibility, it must formally notify the Attorney-General and the Queensland Human Rights Commission (‘QHRC’) and give them a reasonable opportunity to intervene in the proceeding or to make submissions about the proposed declaration.  The court cannot make a declaration of incompatibility about a statutory provision if an override declaration is in force in relation to the provision.

Queensland Human Rights Commission: Complaints procedure

Another important innovation brought about under the HRA is the complaints procedure.  Part 4 Div 2 contains provisions relating to human rights complaints that may be made to the QHRC.  This procedure allows individuals to complain to the QHRC about alleged breaches by public entities of their obligations to act and make decisions compatible with human rights.

Since 1 January 2020, individuals whose human rights have been limited by a public entity are able to complain about the alleged human rights breach to the QHRC.  Complaints may only be made with regard to acts or decisions that occur on or after 1 January 2020.

Under the HRA, the individual must first make the complaint to the relevant public entity, who has 45 business days to respond.  If the public entity fails to respond or gives a response that the individual considers to be an inadequate response to the human rights complaint, the individual may then bring the complaint to the QHRC: see s 65(1).

If the Commissioner decides to accept a human rights complaint, the Commissioner ‘may take the reasonable action [s/he] considers appropriate to try to resolve the complaint.’  This may include seeking information about the complaint from the complainant or the respondent, or another relevant entity, or discussing the complaint with the complainant and the respondent: ss 77-78.  The Commissioner may also seek to resolve the complaint ‘in a way that is informal, quick and efficient’ by conducting a conciliation conference: ss 77(2)(e) and 79-80.

If the Commissioner believes that a human rights complaint has not been resolved by conciliation or otherwise, there are no further steps under the HRA to resolve the complaint.

If a complaint has not been resolved by conciliation or otherwise, the Commissioner must prepare a report about that complaint which must be given to the complainant and the respondent, may publish information about the unresolved complaint, and must also report the unresolved complaint in the Commissioner’s annual report which must be given to the Attorney-General: ss 88 and 90-91; see also s 93.

The publication and reporting of human rights complaints allows them to be brought to the attention of the Attorney-General and, from there, the Queensland government and Parliament: see s 94.  This may shine an unwelcome light on respondent public entities that are identified in unresolved complaints.
