# Slide 4

The remedies in Part 3 of the JR Act

4

In addition to the Supreme Court’s power to quash decisions, the Court has the power back-date the quashing order to the date of the original decision, which is equivalent to that decision being void ab initio
The power to refer the matter for further consideration by the original decision-maker “subject to … directions” allows, for eg, the Supreme Court to direct that when a tribunal rehears a matter, the membership of the tribunal be different to avoid any apprehension of bias
The powers to declare and direct are limited by the general principles of legality review (as distinct from merits review).  There is also no power to direct the payment of damages as there is no general liability in damages under administrative law in Australia

# Slide 8

The prerogative (and constitutional) writs (and their statutory incarnations)

8

The prerogative writs were writs initially developed by the Court of King’s Bench (hence the reference to the writs as “Common Law” remedies) beginning in England in the 12th Century
The writs initially developed to control inferior courts (being issued by superior courts of record)
The main, but not exclusive, focus of the writs was on the jurisdictional error grounds of legality review
Prohibition – prohibits action from occurring that is tainted by jurisdictional error (including breaches of the rules of natural justice) and fraud by a party before an inferior court or tribunal.  Seeking prohibition is roughly equivalent to review of “conduct” via s6 of the ADJR Act and s21 of the JR Act (but prohibition is not restricted to “procedural action” of the kind considered in ABT v Bond).  There must remain something left to prohibit for the writ of prohibition to issue (ie the decision-maker must not be “functus officio”)
Certiorari - quashes decisions – on the grounds of jurisdictional error and on the ground of non-jurisdictional error of law apparent on the face of the record of the decision.  Certiorari is roughly equivalent to the review of a “decision” via s5 of the ADJR Act and s20 of the JR Act (but certiorari’s focus on a decision is not restricted to a “decision” of the kind discussed in ABT v Bond)
Mandamus – commands the performance of public duties.  Mandamus is roughly equivalent to the review in respect of a “failure to decide” via s7 of the ADJR Act and s22 of the JR Act
Note also habeas corpus and informations in the nature of quo warranto

Standing for the prerogative writs and under Part 5 of the JR Act

17

In Queensland, standing for all the remedies in Part 5 of the JR Act is governed by section 44

Declarations and injunctionsPart 5 of the JR Act and the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction

20

JR Act s43(2)(a) links the availability of declarations and injunctions to the matters and the persons to which and to whom the prerogative writs apply
JR Act s43(3) links the availability of declarations and injunctions under the JR Act Part 5 to the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction to issue declarations and injunctions

# Slide 21

Declarations and injunctions as administrative law legality review remedies

21

Important private law remedies that also have an important role in administrative law
A declaration is a statement of the legal position of the parties to a dispute made by a Superior Court - that is, Supreme Court and upwards.  Declaratory relief has been developed by courts exercising equitable jurisdiction and it is often considered together with the equitable remedy of an injunction.  Unlike an injunction, a declaration carries no immediate penalty if ignored.  A declaration is merely a statement of what the law is.  If a respondent ignores a declaration, the applicant will generally have to come back before the court, prove that the declaration is being ignored and seek an injunction.  In the public law use of these remedies, because the respondent is a public entity, the courts generally prefer to issue declaratory relief
An injunction is a court order enforceable by imprisonment for contempt of court, requiring a party to civil proceedings to do something or, more commonly, to refrain from doing something

# Slide 26

Advantages of declaratory reliefPart 5 of the JR Act and the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction

26

Available in respect of decisions etc, of an administrative, legislative and judicial character
All common law grounds of legality review can be raised using declaratory relief (including non-jurisdictional errors of law)
In the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction, declarations are available against non-governmental entities (recall Onus v Alcoa)
Provided that the issue is not hypothetical, declaratory relief can be used to declare rights or to interpret legislation – for a discussion of this “pre-emptive” use of declaratory relief, see Aronson et al, 1073-1081
The flexibility and simplicity of declarations make them administrative law’s most effective remedy

Injunctions as an administrative law remedy

An example of an interim injunction being issued by the High Court in an administrative law context is in the Matter of – A proposed application of Shah v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship [2011] HCATrans 195, per Hayne J. Injunctions are either prohibitory or mandatory

# Slide 30

Interlocutory injunctions in public law litigation

30

Australian Broadcasting Corporation v O’Neill (2006) 227 CLR 57, [65]-[71], per Gummow and Hayne JJ (with whom Gleeson CJ and Crennan J agreed, [19]):

[Interlocutory injunctions are functionally equivalent to stays under s29 of the JR Act

There are 2 inquiries for interlocutory injunctions: “… first … whether the plaintiff has made out a prima facie case, in the sense that if the evidence remains as it is there is a probability at trial of the action the plaintiff will be held entitled to relief … and … second … whether the inconvenience or injury which the plaintiff would be likely to suffer if an injunction were  refused outweighs or is outweighed by the injury which the defendant would suffer if an injunction were granted”]

[In commercial litigation, where a plaintiff seeks an interlocutory injunction, a third requirement is that the plaintiff must give an “undertaking as to damages” to compensate the defendant for the effects of the interlocutory injunction if the plaintiff’s case ultimately fails]

# Slide 33

Injunctions to enforce the criminal law

33

Onus v Alcoa of Australia Ltd (1981) 149 CLR 27 at 35, per Gibbs CJ, and 63, per Wilson J:

[“The case is … one in which two private citizens who cannot show that any right of their own has been infringed bring an action for the purpose of restraining another private citizen (Alcoa) from breaking the criminal law by acting in contravention of s. 21 of the Relics Act”

# Slide 34

Injunctions to enforce the criminal law

34

Gouriet v Union of Post Office Workers [1978] AC 435, 481, per Lord Wilberforce:

[The invocation of “… assistance of civil courts in aid of the criminal law … is an exceptional power confined, in practice, to cases where an offence is frequently repeated in disregard of a, usually, inadequate penalty … or to cases of emergency. … It is not without its difficulties … [T]his jurisdiction – though proved useful on occasions – is one of great delicacy and is one to be used with caution”

Why must this use of injunctions be “jealously guarded”?

“If Parliament has imposed a sanction (eg, a fine of £1), without an increase in severity for repeated offences, it may seem wrong that the courts-civil courts-should think fit, by granting injunctions, breaches of which may attract unlimited sanctions, including imprisonment, to do what Parliament has not done. Moreover, where Parliament has (as here in the Post Office Act 1953) provided for trial of offences by indictment before a jury, it may seem wrong that the courts, applying a civil standard of proof, should in effect convict a subject without the prescribed trial”]
