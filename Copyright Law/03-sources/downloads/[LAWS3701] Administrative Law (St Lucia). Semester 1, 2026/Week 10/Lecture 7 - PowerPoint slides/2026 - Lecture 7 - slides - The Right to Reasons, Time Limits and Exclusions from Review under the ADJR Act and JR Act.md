# Slide 2

Introduction to statutory rights to reasons for administrative decisions

2

There appears to be no general common law duty for administrative decision-makers to provide reasons for their decisions

Public Service Board of NSW v Osmond (1986) 159 CLR 656 at 662 and 667-670, per Gibbs CJ (with whom Wilson and Brennan JJ agreed):

“There is no general rule of the common law, or principle of natural justice, that requires reasons to be given for administrative decisions, even decisions which have been made in the exercise of a statutory discretion and which may adversely affect the interests … of other persons”

Although compare the observation in Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW (2010) 239 CLR 531 at [83], per French CJ, Gummow, Hayne, Crennan, Kiefel and Bell JJ, that:

“… in … some cases the failure to give reasons may constitute a failure to exercise jurisdiction”]

# Slide 4

The right to reasons under the ADJR Act and the JR Act

4

Features of the statutory right to reasons
The right to reasons requires showing a “decision to which … [the ADR Act/JR Act] applies”, but the right does not depend on showing that the decision is unlawful, ie the right to reasons applies to lawful, as well as unlawful, decisions.  A written statement of reasons will, however, potentially improve the prospects of successful legality review 

See, for example, RP Data Pty Ltd v Brisbane City Council (1995) 90 LGERA 42 at 45, per Davies JA, with whom McPherson JA and Dowsett J agreed, suggesting that reasons might have assisted an applicant prove its legality review challenge:

[“… the appellant must fail for want of proof.  It did not, as it was entitled to pursuant to s32(1) of the Judicial Review Act, seek reasons for the first respondent’s decision and no evidence has been adduced of those reasons or of what advice it sought or obtained before making that decision. … The appellant therefore failed on this basis of review”]

# Slide 7

The right to reasons under the JR Act

7

A “decision to which this part applies”?
A “person who is entitled to make an application … under s20”?
A “written statement in relation to the decision”?

An applicant for a written statement of reasons for a decision must be aggrieved and must basically show that the decision is one to which the JR Act applies but does not need to show that the decision is unlawful (ie the right to reasons does not depend on showing a ground of legality review)

# Slide 8

The right to reasons under the JR Act

8

The required content of a statement of “reasons”

Ansett Transport Industries (Operations) Pty Ltd v Wraith (1983) 48 ALR 500 at 507, per Woodward J:

[“... decision-maker[s] should set out ... [their] understanding of the relevant law, any findings of fact on which ... [their] conclusions depend (especially if those facts have been in dispute), and the reasoning processes which led ... [them] to those conclusions ... [They] should do so in clear and unambiguous language, not in vague generalities or the formal language of legislation.  The appropriate length of the statement covering such matters will depend upon considerations such as the nature and importance of the decision, its complexity and the time available to formulate the statement.  Often those factors may suggest a brief statement of one or two pages only”]

Court costs and the JR Act - Reasons

20

[Section 50 of the JR Act provides the Supreme Court with the power to make a special costs order if someone commences court proceedings to vindicate their statutory right to reasons.  Normally, in litigation, costs follow the event, ie the party that loses the litigation pays the court costs of the winner.  JR Act s50 varies that rule

The special costs provisions of the JR Act have no equivalent in the ADJR Act]

# Slide 21
