LETTER WRITING

Letter writing is often regarded as the poor cousin of document drafting in 
legal practice. While solicitors write letters far more often than they draft legal 
documents, not enough time is given to reflection about the three important 
considerations in letter writing: substance, language and layout.

You must pay attention to each of these considerations if your letter is to 
achieve its purpose.

Substance

Planning the letter

Planning is the what, when, where, why and how of letter writing. One of the 
questions to ask yourself in this context is whether the letter is necessary. Other 
forms of communication may better achieve the desired end. Most legal letters 
do one or more of four things. They are written to inform, persuade, make a 
request of the reader or record an action or fact (De Groot and Maxwell, 1994). 
Once you have decided the category into which your letter fits, the planning is 
more focused.

Categories of letters

To inform

Letters of advice most often fall into the information category. The criterion

is to provide the required information. To do so it may be necessary to give 
an overview of the facts on which the information is based. However, it is 
important to keep the letter as succinct as possible and, unless the letter of 
advice is to another legal professional, limit references to the law to those 
that are crucial to the advice. It is often worthwhile to set out a summary of 
the most important aspects of the advice at the beginning of the letter and 
elaborate on them at some later appropriate point: Instructions to a client are 
often a part of a letter providing advice. Make sure that the instructions are 
easy to understand, especially if the client must take some action.

To persuade 
If the letter is written to persuade the reader to change his or her views on a 
particular matter, you need to take care to gauge the strength of the opposition 
that may be encountered. If you anticipate only perfunctory resistance, then 
simply stating your case and supplying the supporting information may be all 
that is needed. In other circumstances, you may need to make out a forceful 
legal case or appeal to the reader's sense of duty or emotions. It is a good idea 
to make your case at the beginning of the letter and support it in whatever way 
you think appropriate. The circumstances in which such a letter is written are 
varied: for instance, you may be seeking assistance, or offering assistance, or

advising against a particular action.

To request

Keep letters of request as short as possible, giving only the supporting 
infonnation necessary to support the request. Always write in a courteous 
manner, unless the desire is to be confrontational, as perhaps in a letter of 
demand. Conversely, when replying to a request for information, give only the 
requested information. You may assume that if the requesting writer wanted 
more information he or she would have asked for it.

To record

Letters designed to record something are for that purpose alone- that is, to 
record in writing an event, an action, or a conversation. They should always 
be written to con:fim1 oral instructions, but are also important as a record of 
a meeting or an action. They have evidentiary value and _must be accurate in

every detail.

Your client's instructions

A client's instructions must be followed precisely and are of critical 
importance when writing to a third party on your client's behalf. Failure to

folt,)w instructions may lead to a claim of professional negligence against you 
and your :finn. Ask yourself whether the letter achieves what the client wants. 
If additional instructions are necessary, you must obtain them before taking

any action.

The facts and the law

Any facts stated in your letter must be accurate. Once the letter has been sent it 
cannot easily be recalled. Reflecting on and checking the facts before sending 
the letter may avoid future embarrassment. On the other hand, often it is not 
necessary to deal with the law to any significant extent. It is nevertheless 
important to make sure that the letter is an accurate reflection of the relevant law.

Say what you mean and mean what you say

Be clear about what you mean, and follow up with action

Do not use cliched or hackneyed expressions or metaphors in your letter, but 
state the issues in clear terms so there is no doubt about the meaning you seek 
to convey.

Similarly, if you have written that a particular consequence will follow, make 
sure that it does. If you become known for never following your words with 
actions, your letter writing will not be as authoritative as it might otherwise be.

Do not exaggerate or apologise unnecessarily

Do not exaggerate in your letter writing and do not apologise unnecessarily. 
However, good manners demand that you apologise when an apology is 
appropriate.

It is far better to be understated but accurate and firm in your letter than to 
use exaggeration or hyperbole. A letter is a permanent record of an action, an 
event, or a moment in time and exaggeration may lead to embarrassment or 
unintended consequences.

The same can be said of unnecessary apology. Simply state the facts. For

instance, if a phone call should have been made but was not, instead of writing 
Unfortunately, the phone call ... or We are sorry the phone call ... , write 
the facts: We did not make the phone call.

sequence (De Groot and Maxwell, 1994). It may be necessary to prepare

A number of different ranking sequences are possible, depending on the nature of 
the letter. For instance, the issues may be ranked in chronological order, in order 
of importance, from problem to solution, or from problem to results of research.

Stating issues and facts in chronological order gives clarity to letters of

Simple and appropriate words in context

A reminder about the principles of plain English: using plain English is just as 
important in letter writing as it is in document drafting.

Jargon

A particular sort of jargon is associated with lawyers' letter writing. It amounts 
to a pretentious use of outmoded words and phrases. An example is the way 
instant is used in the context of this month or this week as it is ip these 
sentences-

Following our telephone conversation of 13th instant ...

OR

Following our telephone conversation of Tuesday instant

Instant comes from the Latin instans meaning present. The Macquarie 
Dictionary says of instant in this context-

present, current (now used elliptically): the 10th instant (the 10th day of the

present month).

Instead of writing instant, use the clearer words this week or this month, or 
simply write 13th [month].

Another example of jargon is ultimo used in the context of last month-

Again, turning to the Macquarie Dictionary, the word ultimo is an 
abbreviation of the Latin phrase ultimo mense meaning in the last month. It 
is clearer to write on the 13th of last month or for greater certainty on 13th 
February or on 13 February.

On even date has no redeeming feature at all. Do not use it. Simply write 
today.

As for the body of your document, occasionally it is not possible to replace 
words and phrases that fall into the category of technical jargon with a concise 
and accurate plain language translation. If this happens, ensure that you 
explain the technical term simply and concisely the first time it appears (Carr, 
2010).

Sentence construction

Each sentence must be complete. It must have a verb. It must not be too long 
and it should be written in the active voice rather than the passive voice, 
although this is not always possible.

A sentence should only contain or deal with one idea. Sentences that deal with 
a number of different ideas are very confusing and unnecessarily long.

Use a style guide

If your firm has a style guide, use it. Style guides may contain, for instance, 
appropriate information about the use of particular fonts, footnoting, use of 
lists, notation and page layout.

Make the layout reader-friendly

A reader-friendly layout will include plenty of type-free space on the page. 
The same principles apply in typing letters as in drafting documents. Closely 
written type is difficult to read and digest and your reader may need to read it 
more than once to understand its contents. This is a waste of time. Well-spaced 
paragraphs (numbered if the letter is lengthy), typed in an easy-to-read type 
and font size all help to create a reader-friendly layout.

The rapid development of participatory models of government across all 
Australian jurisdictions over the last decade has meant, among other things, a 
concomitant development of tribunals for many different areas of government

responsibility. In addition to statutory tribunals, administrative decision makers 
also must now make written decisions and, in doing so, comply with statutory

rules.

At common law a judge must provide sufficient reasons for an appellate 
court to consider an appeal. However, administrators need not do so (Public 
Service Board of New South Wales v Osmond ( 1986) 159 CLR 656). Australian 
parliaments have attempted to remedy this deficiency in administrative decision 
making by statute. In Queensland, for example, the Right to Information Act 
2009 (Qld) section 110 imposes an obligation on the Information Commissioner 
to give written reasons for a decision made on an external review. The Acts 
interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) provides in section 27B that, if a statute imposes a

duty on a 'tribunal, authority, body or person making a decision' to give written 
reasons for a decision, the reasons must include findings of material fact and the 
evidence on which those findings are made.

Providing reasons for a decision allows the reasoning to be analysed,

improves accountability, engenders trust and public confidence and gives an 
opportunity for further review of the decision, if necessary (Thawley, 1996). 
However, when giving reasons for a decision, your primary task is to explain 
the reasoning in clear unambiguous terms that each party understands, rather 
than in tenns that are legalistically framed to withstand scrutiny by a higher 
authority. The target audience must be able to understand each part of the 
reasons. You should use specific and unambiguous language, avoiding esoteric 
terms and complex legalistic language .

These requirements are principles of plain English writing. A further principle 
of plain English legal writing requires the use of an appropriate document 
structure for the task in hand (Kimble, 1996/97).

Writing clear and precise decisions will be much easier if you follow guidelines 
similar to those for letter writing, as set out in the following checklist.

Checklist for letters outlining decisions

1. The subject line should simply be one line explaining what the letter is

about-

Your application for a licence under the Widget Conservation Act 
1999

2. Include the additional formal information as part of an introductory 
paragraph to the letter.

Communicating by email

The use of email is ubiquitous. It is also very dangerous. For lawyers it is 
a two-edged sword. The email trail may be both helpful and damaging to 
lawyers and their clients at the same time. It is helpful as it provides an 
indelible record of transactions, agreements, and discussions between parties 
and their legal representatives. It is damaging because using the 'reply' 
function without checking the contents of previous emails may lead to 
information being unwisely and widely distributed.

Email is increasingly important in legal practice, especially in litigation. As 
Asprey reminds us (2010)-

Our ability to communicate immediately and internationally by email and the

internet poses special problems such as:

■ 
the potential for mistakes that cannot be undone

■ 
the inability to limit or control who may receive our emails

■ 
international defamation.

Bearing these comments in mind, although it appears to be a more informal 
method of communication, remember that an email should be treated with the 
same caution, discretion and thought as an ordinary letter. In addition, never 
forget the danger of emails being misdirected. Before you send emails consider 
the following advice-

• 
Do not let the apparent informality of emails make you incautious about 
what you say and how you say it.

■ Do not use the 'reply' or 'forward' function for email until you are satisfied

about the contents of any email chain.

■ Do not write emails that you would be worried about seeing quoted or 
forwarded to others not authorised to receive them.

■ Do not communicate sensitive or confidential information by electronic 
means unless the information has been secured in some way.

■ Do not forward or distribute an email without permission from the author.

■ Do not insert an email address until you are satisfied with the contents 
of an email, especially for emails sent in a professional capacity. Many
