WORKSHOP FOUR: Grounds of Judicial Review

Introduction to judicial review

We have just considered merits review, concerning the reconsideration of the substantive fairness or justice of the merits of a decision. Merits review is about the re-making (de novo) of an administrative decision (by another body) looking at the applicable law/policy and facts of the matter. Judicial review (JR) of executive action is carried out by the superior courts in Australia is concerned with the legality of decision-making processes, not the substance of a decision made by the Executive (and inferior courts). JR ensures that the decisions and conduct of administrative officials (and inferior courts) is lawful, reasonable and procedurally fair. The discrete grounds of judicial review sourced in the common law, and codified in statute (AD(JR)A 1977 (Cth) and JRA 1991 (Qld)) are types of legal error, and it follows that judicial review is a more limited form of accountability than merits review. Additionally, the remedial powers of the courts when engaged in judicial review are more limited than in merits review proceedings.

Judicial (legality) review is ultimately protected by the Constitution (s 75) as we discussed in workshop two, and is also (based on common law) an inherent aspect of the powers of the State Supreme Courts. Conversely ‘appeals’ to the courts are only granted by statute (by leave or as of right). Such ‘statutory appeals’ can resemble judicial reviews in some instances (e.g. s 172 of the ART Act 2024 provides for an appeal to the Federal Court of Australia on ‘a question of law’) but other forms of statutory appeal are quite different: the nature and scope of a particular statutory appeal is determined by the legislation conferring the appeal right. As it is conferred by statute it can be removed by Parliament in a way that judicial review cannot be (recall Plaintiff S157 – entrenched minimum provision of JR under the Constitution - and see further below).

Another crucial difference between judicial and merits review is the law/fact distinction, which is related to the legality/merits distinction discussed in previous workshops. The various grounds of judicial review are largely (though not exclusively) concerned with different types of errors of law not errors of fact. The very limited circumstances in which errors of fact can be remedied and corrected via judicial review will be examined in later workshops. For present purposes it is sufficient to merely raise the few exceptions to the general rule.

Sources of jurisdiction for judicial review

There are three, inter-connected, sources of jurisdiction for the courts to entertain judicial review applications: common law, constitutional and statutory schemes. Rejoice, for these different sources make judicial review at the federal, state and territory levels more complicated than is warranted!

First, applicants must determine whether the government action they wish to review is state, territory or federal. In Queensland, applicants seeking to challenge Queensland government decisions look, primarily, to the Judicial Review Act 1991, and there is a historic and enduring common law basis for review that inheres in the power of the Supreme Court (as a superior court of record). The precise means by which applicants can seek judicial review in Queensland is explored in the second half of the course (‘access to justice’). Alternatively, at the Commonwealth level, regarding Commonwealth government decisions, there is the High Court’s ‘original jurisdiction’ under s 75(v) Constitution (which we discussed in workshop two), the Federal Court’s jurisdiction under s 39B Judiciary Act (Cth) and the Federal Circuit and Family Court’s and Federal Court’s jurisdiction under the ADJRA 1977 (Cth).

So, at common law the courts in England, and the State Supreme Courts in Australia, have enjoyed the inherent power to remedy particularly grave legal errors (called ‘jurisdictional errors’) and also what are called ‘non-jurisdictional errors of law on the face of the record’ made by inferior courts with limited jurisdiction.

The courts have also corrected legal errors made by executive officials exercising administrative power (on the basis of, broadly, ultra vires and procedural unfairness). These legal errors could be remedied by issuing writs against either inferior courts or government officials/bodies respectively. And so the rule of law was maintained. But in practice the law and procedures governing the availability of the writs was technical and arcane. This is why judicial review was modernised and codified in Australia in the 1970s (federally) and in several states and territories subsequently. In short, jurisdictional error (acting without, or in excess of, power) remains of central importance for common law based judicial review in Australia and it applies to correct errors made by inferior courts and administrative decision-makers. At common law the terms jurisdictional error and ultra vires are now used interchangeably and cover substantially (if not precisely) the same grounds.

The constitutional judicial review jurisdiction regarding Commonwealth government decisions is rooted in s 75(iii) and (v). Section 75(v) serves to provide an entrenched avenue through which individuals can redress jurisdictional errors via the constitutional writs: mandamus and prohibition. Section 75(v) also provides the basis for injunctive relief in addition to the writs. The nature of these remedies will be explored towards the end of the course.

Section 75(v) provides for judicial review against ‘officers of the Commonwealth’ (including federal Ministers and their delegates, statutory bodies and public servants). Whereas s 75(iii) is broader in scope, it makes no reference to particular remedies but rather matters ‘in which the Commonwealth, or a person suing or being sued on behalf of the Commonwealth, is a party’. The provision enables people to bring private law actions against the Commonwealth (eg tortious claims, such as false imprisonment; per the CPCF case). Moreover it may capture independent contractors employed to carry out public functions at the behest of the Commonwealth pursuant to a contractual arrangement.

The Federal Court enjoys concurrent jurisdiction with the High Court to judicially review matters where remedies are sought against officers of the Commonwealth. This jurisdiction is conferred by s 39B Judiciary Act and it essentially replicates s 75(v) of the Constitution and was introduced to avoid the High Court becoming overly congested with judicial reviews.

In summary, the content of constitutional (s 75) judicial review jurisdiction is drawn from the body of common law on judicial review.

By contrast with the common law and constitutional paths to judicial review, statutory judicial review is not as technical, it is less impenetrable, and it is not remedially oriented. Rather, the particular grounds of judicial review are neatly listed and the statutes purport to ‘codify’ the common law grounds of review. Statutory review was intended to be more accessible and transparent means of review compared with the historic processes based on the writs, or actions for declaratory and injunctive relief. The statutory schemes (where they are in force) operate alongside the common law; they do not strip the superior courts of their inherent power to grant remedies in the form of prerogative writs to correct legal errors.

What this means, at the federal level, is that if a person cannot review a type of decision under the AD(JR)A 1977 (because it is not a type of executive action to which the ADJRA applies) then, in the alternative, the person may be able to turn to judicial review under the Judiciary Act or the Constitution. In short, under the Judiciary Act and Constitution it is possible to review a wider range of federal executive action, than under the ADJRA 1977 pathway (which is limited to decisions of an administrative character made under an enactment). For instance non-statutory federal (executive) powers may be reviewed under either the Judiciary Act or Constitution, but they cannot be reviewed under the AD(JR)A 1977 for the reasons noted above.

JR Grounds: Introduction

Grounds of judicial (legality) review are the legal bases for arguing that a court should grant relief (meaning a remedy) to an ‘aggrieved person’. The grounds play a central role, arguably the central role, in judicial review. Talking about grounds for review is a way of describing how a decision maker (executive official, tribunal member or judge of an inferior court) can fall into legal error that will lead a superior court to grant a remedy to correct the legal mistake made.

Judicial review developed through the English common law in relation to: (i) serious legal errors (amounting to ‘jurisdictional errors’) made by inferior courts and tribunals, and less serious (non-jurisdictional) legal errors on the face of the decision-making record; and, (ii) subsequently, legal errors made by executive government officials exercising administrative powers. At common law (and in s 75(v) constitutional (writ) cases in Australia) one particular and very well-established ground of review is breach of the rules of ‘natural justice’ (or breach of ‘procedural fairness’ as it is often referred to today). Additionally, the legislation that has codified judicial review (at federal and state/territory level) also lists natural justice as one of eighteen grounds of judicial review.

The statutory grounds of review, essentially, mirror the common law grounds of review that were well accepted at the time the judicial review statutes were enacted in Australia. For most of the grounds of review, the arguments made and cases cited in the course of litigation will be the same whether or not a matter is proceeding by way of (1) common law, (2) constitutional jurisdiction or (3) statutory judicial review. However, while the concept of ‘jurisdictional error’ is central to judicial review at common law and via the Constitution, aggrieved applicants do not need to argue about the concept of ‘jurisdictional error’ under statutory review processes, instead they plead one (or more) of the eighteen enumerated grounds listed in the AD(JR)A 1977 or JRA 1991 (Qld) (or the equivalent legislation in other States and territories).

JR Grounds 1. Natural Justice (Procedural Fairness)

Breach of the rules of natural justice (or, ‘procedural fairness’ as it is more frequently referred to today) is commonly argued in judicial (legality) reviews. It is the very first of the grounds listed in the judicial review statutes and has deep historical roots in the common law. This ground of review is concerned with the manner in which decisions are made and it is comprised of two fundamental components, broadly the fair hearing rules, and the rules against bias. Plainly the rules are directed towards ensuring the people have an opportunity to be heard in respect of decisions that impact on their rights and interests, and that those conducting the hearing (or deciding) the matter are independent and impartial. The rules of procedural fairness were originally developed in the English common law in relation to the proceedings of inferior courts (eg magistrates) but were extended to condition the exercise of administrative powers by executive government officials and statutory bodies (eg tribunals).

So, the principles of natural justice spring from the common law. As Mason J explained, in the leading case of Kioa v West, the duty to observe the requirements of natural justice was “a common law duty, in the sense of according procedural fairness, in the making of administrative decisions which affect rights, interests and legitimate expectations”. Natural justice is deeply embedded in the common law and its critical importance informs judges’ approaches to interpreting statutory powers as the High Court of Australia (HCA) explained in Saeed’s case. In that case it was re-stated that statutes are construed against a background of common law notions of fairness and justice. Therefore, statutory construction “proceeds upon the assumption that the legislature, being aware of the common law principles, would have intended that they apply to the exercise of a power of the kind referred to in Annetts v McCann”. The power referred to by the HCA in Annetts case was a power to destroy or prejudice a person’s rights or interests.

It is important to recognise that natural justice is not only an implied condition of administrative decision-making by the executive under statute. Natural justice can also apply to, and is capable of conditioning the exercise of non-statutory executive powers (or ‘prerogative powers’). This was established in the leading English case of CCSU v Minister for the Civil Service.

In short, although natural justice is taken to apply to administrative decisions that affect people’s rights and interests, the fair hearing requirements may be excluded or modified by legislation, either expressly or by necessary implication. Additionally, the factual context in which the executive action is taken may mean that natural justice is not implied (as is customary). Before turning to the circumstances in which fair hearing principles may be excluded (or modified) there is an initial, threshold, question to address. Is natural justice only applicable when legal rights are affected by decision-makers who are under a duty to act judicially?

Threshold questions: When does the duty to afford natural justice arise?

The common law duty to accord procedural fairness does not attach to every decision of an administrative character. The question of whether the common law will fill the gap left by the legislature depends upon whether the exercise of the power will affect the rights or interests of the party.

Traditionally the courts focussed upon whether a party’s legal rights (typically property rights) were adversely affected in a ‘direct and immediate way’ by a decision taken by a body under a duty to act ‘judicially’, such as an inferior court or a public body with the trappings of a court, such as an adjudicative (merits review) tribunal, commission or board.

In Ridge v Baldwin, the House of Lords held that a police authority (called the watch committee) that was invested with statutory powers to discipline and dismiss police officers, in circumstances where their misconduct warranted dismissal, could not lawfully dismiss an officer without first telling them about the nature of the allegations against them and hearing their response. Lord Reid referenced a line of unbroken authority dating back as far as 1615, and stated,

that the power of dismissal in the Act of 1882 ... cannot now be exercised until the watch committee have informed the constable of the grounds on which they propose to proceed and have given him a proper opportunity to present his case in defence.

In Ridge v Baldwin the watch committee was an administrative body (not akin to the courts, or bodies with the trappings of a court) but it was held to be subject to natural justice requirements because its powers of dismissal were stated in the Municipal Corporations Act and because of the adverse effect on the legal rights of Mr Ridge. Specifically, Mr Ridge was deprived of his employment (after 33 years’ service) as a public office-holder, and was denied certain statutory pension rights. His reputation was, necessarily, tarnished too. Because the watch committee had breached natural justice principles the decision was legally invalid (null and void).

Subsequent cases made clear that the duty to afford natural justice applied to executive officials and bodies exercising administrative powers affecting the rights of person, and where the exercise of powers affects interests or privileges. Two important cases illustrate the point. The first is FAI v Winneke which concerned the important question of whether the Victorian Governor/executive were obliged to comply with natural justice when declining to approve a company’s application for renewal of an approval (a licence) to act as insurer for workers’ compensation.

FAI Insurances Ltd was an insurer approved by the Victorian government, holding a government issued licence to provide workers’ compensation insurance in Victoria. FAI had been approved/licensed to provide accident insurance for twenty years, but in 1981 they were denied licence renewal on the basis that the Victorian executive government doubted it was a fit and proper entity to act as a workers’ compensation insurer. They doubted that that FAI had the financial assets to meet potential insurance claims. Were FAI entitled to natural justice in respect of the decision taken by the Governor (on the advice of the executive) to not renew FAI’s approval?

In FAI v Winneke the High Court held that where a person (or corporation) has held an approval/licence (of one kind or another) and is then to be deprived of that approval/licence, procedural fairness is owed before the decision is taken. This is because the decision adversely affects the party and strips them of rights – in this case, the right to carry on business – held under the terms of the approval/licence. Mason J explained:

The fundamental rule is that a statutory authority having power to affect the rights of a person is bound to hear him before exercising the power. The application of the rules is not limited to cases where the exercise of the power affects rights in the strict sense. It extends to the exercise of a power which affects an interest or a privilege or which deprives a person of a "legitimate expectation", to borrow the expression of Lord Denning MR in Schmidt v Secretary of State for Home Affairs, in circumstances where it would not be fair to deprive him of that expectation without a hearing.

Mason J continued: “In these circumstances the appellant [FAI] had a legitimate expectation that its approval would be renewed or at the very least that it would not be refused without its having an opportunity of meeting objections raised against it.” Mason J also reasoned that the nature of the decision-maker did not preclude the application of natural justice principles: “the difference in the nature and character of the Governor in Council is not sufficient in my opinion to deny the existence of some duty to accord natural justice.” Thus, the fact that the statutory power to grant or refuse a renewal of approval to act as an insurer was vested in the Governor in Council was not a sufficient reason to deny the existence of the duty to comply with natural justice.

Having dealt with the threshold question, importantly, Mason J went on to stress that the content of the duty to afford natural justice will vary with the particular circumstances of the case at hand:

… the difference in the nature and character of the Governor in Council is not sufficient in my opinion to deny the existence of some duty to accord natural justice, though the difference will be reflected in the content of the duty and what is to be expected by way of discharge of the duty, it being accepted now that the content of the duty varies with the particular circumstances of the case. It is impossible to suppose that Parliament intended that the Governor in Council would conduct a hearing similar to a judicial hearing. But it is possible, indeed proper, to attribute to Parliament the intention that the Governor in Council will act in conformity with natural justice, by giving to the applicant an adequate opportunity to present its case, as, for example, by written submissions on matters alleged to be relevant.

In summary, decisions affecting the grant, refusal, renewal and revocation of a licence, are conditioned by procedural fairness principles, and this is so even in respect of initial grant applications (as contrasted with renewal decision-making) in circumstances where a party has no legal ‘right’ strictly, to a grant of the licence sought.

Three years after FAI v Winneke, the High Court handed down its decision in what is generally regarded as a seminal decision on natural justice in Australia: Kioa v West.

Jason Kioa and his wife were non-citizens with permission (permits) to remain in Australia as temporary entrants. They had a daughter who was an Australian citizen. The Immigration Minister cancelled Kioa’s visa after he stayed beyond the expiration period of the permit and worked illegally. This rendered him an illegal immigrant liable to deportation under s 18 Migration Act. Kioa’s application for a further entry permit was refused.

Kioa argued that the subsequent deportation decision had been reached in breach of natural justice principles contrary to s 5(1) ADJRA 1977 (Cth). The appellants contended that they were not given an opportunity to respond to adverse material relied upon by the Minister’s delegate in the immigration department when making the deportation decision. At the time of the decision in Kioa this was quite a bold submission because there was earlier authority supporting the argument that deportation decisions were not subject to natural justice requirements, although those cases were decided before the ADJRA came into force.

A majority of the court held that the Tongan citizens were entitled to natural justice (even though they had no legal right to remain in Australia) in respect of the decision to deport them. This was because the Minister’s delegate (the decision-maker) decided to reject Kioa’s application for another entry permit, on the basis of prejudicial information obtained by the Minister’s delegate from a third party, which was not put to Kioa for a response. Kioa was not given an opportunity to deal with the (adverse) grounds on which his further entry permit application was, in part, refused.

Mason J and Brennan J gave the leading judgments. Mason J introduced the threshold and content questions by observing that: “Under the general law [ie common law] it is always a question whether the rules apply and, if so, what rule or rules apply to the making of the particular decision.” His Honour continued, making the following important statements of principle:

It is a fundamental rule of the common law doctrine of natural justice expressed in traditional terms that, generally speaking, when an order is to be made which will deprive a person of some right or interest or the legitimate expectation of a benefit, he is entitled to know the case sought to be made against him and to be given an opportunity of replying to it:… The reference to "right or interest" in this formulation must be understood as relating to personal liberty, status, preservation of livelihood and reputation, as well as to proprietary rights and interests. … [T]he concept of "legitimate expectation" extends to expectations which go beyond enforceable legal rights provided that they are reasonably based: … the expectation may arise from the very nature of the application, as it did in the case of the application for a renewal of a licence in FAI or from the existence of a regular practice which the person affected can reasonably expect to continue: Council of Civil Service Unions v Minister for the Civil Service.

And further, that

The law has now developed to a point where it may be accepted that there is a common law duty to act fairly, in the sense of according procedural fairness, in the making of administrative decisions which affect rights, interests and legitimate expectations, subject only to the clear manifestation of a contrary statutory intention. … But the duty does not attach to every decision of an administrative character. Many such decisions do not affect the rights, interests and expectations of the individual citizen in a direct and immediate way. Thus a decision to impose a rate or a decision to impose a general charge for services rendered to ratepayers, each of which indirectly affects the rights, interests or expectations of citizens generally does not attract this duty to act fairly.

Here then, Mason J identifies that there is a presumption of procedural fairness conditioning administrative decision-making affecting rights, interests and legitimate expectations in a direct and immediate way; subject to a contrary statutory intent (ie legislation can abrogate or modify the principle’s application).

The interests that attract, and are protected by, procedural fairness principles are increasingly wide, and the High Court has on several recent occasions flatly rejected arguments that natural justice does not apply to official ‘discretionary’ decision-making affecting non-citizens where inquiries related to the protection of fundamental human rights. For example, in Plaintiff M61/2010E v Commonwealth, the issue was about the procedural fairness and legality of decision-making processes that affected the interests of asylum seekers (people seeking refugee protection visas) who were detained on Christmas Island.

The applicants in M61 were Sri Lankan asylum seekers who had sought entry to Australia, claiming refugee protection, invoking Australia’s treaty obligations under international human rights law (1951 Convention relating to the Status of Refugees and the 1967 protocol to the Convention). As they had arrived by boat, unauthorised (that is without a valid visa), they were deprived of their liberty and subjected to mandatory immigration detention under the Migration Act 1958. As ‘unlawful (offshore) entrants’ (meaning, asylum seekers arriving by boat) the applicants were subject to a statutory bar preventing them from formally applying for a protection visa unless and until the Immigration Minister exercised personal powers under the Migration Act 1958 (s 46A, or s 195A) ‘lifting the statutory bar’ to permit a visa application or grant a visa application.

There was a two-step process by which the Minister first considered and decided whether to lift the statutory bar and, secondly, decided whether to permit/grant a visa application. Informing the minister’s decision about whether to lift the bar was what purported to be a ‘non-statutory’ administrative process by which a person’s eligibility for refugee status was assessed. Officials would assess a person’s claim, permit a merits review of that claim if the initial assessment was negative, and then advise the Minister accordingly. The ‘non-statutory’ refugee status assessment process was designed to be highly discretionary – that is to say unconditioned by the requirements of the Migration Act and associated case-law bearing on the meaning of a ‘refugee’. In M61 the applicants alleged that their refugee status assessments were tainted; they claimed the process was procedurally unfair and that decision makers had also made other ‘errors of law’ when assessing their cases.

The Australian government countered by arguing that they were not obliged to afford the applicants procedural fairness in the context of a non-statutory, discretionary, decision-making process, a process that was (supposedly) divorced from and not governed by the Migration Act. The government maintained that they were not destroying, defeating or prejudicing a legal right.

The High Court rejected the government’s contentions that the administrative processes were unfettered by procedural fairness requirements. The Court found that the refugee status assessment processes were, in fact, undertaken in order to inform the Minister’s exercise of his statutory (dispensing) powers, they could not simply be divorced from the Act because they were steps taken under and for the purposes of the Act.

Moreover, and importantly, as the conduct of the refugee status assessment inquiries prolonged the applicant’s time in immigration detention (deprivation of liberty), the rights and interests of the applicants were directly affected by the refugee status assessment (RSA) inquiries and assessments. A unanimous High Court stated:

… the contrast which the Commonwealth and the Minister sought to draw between destruction, defeat or prejudice of a right, on the one hand, and a discretionary power to confer a right, on the other, proceeds from too narrow a conception of the circumstances in which an obligation to afford procedural fairness might arise. The more comprehensive statement of principle by Mason J in FAI Insurances Ltd v Winneke sufficiently answers the submissions by the Commonwealth and the Minister. His Honour said that the obligation to afford procedural fairness is not limited to cases where the exercise of the power affects rights in the strict sense, but extends to the exercise of a power which affects an interest or a privilege. It is then important, in the present matters, to identify the rights and interests affected.

… [O]nce it is decided that the assessment and review processes were undertaken for the purpose of the Minister considering whether to exercise power under either s 46A or s 195A, it follows from the consequence upon the claimant’s liberty that the assessment and review must be procedurally fair and must address the relevant legal question or questions. The right of a claimant to liberty from restraint at the behest of the Australian Executive is directly affected.

In short, the High Court found that the administrative processes directed to determining whether a person was a refugee were subject to natural justice principles. The administrative steps taken via the RSA processes informed the Minister’s consideration of whether to exercise statutory powers. And further, these ‘discretionary’ processes did not occur in a legal black hole; they had to be subject to the rule of law (legality and procedural fairness principles) and subject to judicial supervision. This was because the applicants were deprived of their liberty pending the outcome of those processes and because it would be pointless in having a refugee status determination process that was not guided by established legal principles and criteria governing refugee status.

On the facts it was found that the applicants had, in fact, been denied procedural fairness. The substance of country of origin information (about Sri Lanka), which bore adversely on the applicants’ case, was not put to the applicants to respond to. The Court issued a ‘declaration’ stating that there had been breaches of natural justice.

The concept of ‘legitimate expectations’ – No longer a touchstone of the requirement that a decision-maker accord procedural fairness

The notion of ‘legitimate expectations’ was, for a time, accepted in Australian administrative law as a touchstone for the application of natural justice, along with ‘rights and interests’ affected by administrative decisions. Its rise to prominence reached a high-water mark in the case of Teoh. Akin to ‘interests’, a ‘legitimate expectation’ went beyond legal rights and extended the reach and protection of natural justice. There were no fixed categories of legitimate expectation, but established classes included: where an expectation arose from a government undertaking or representation to the Australian public generally (Teoh’s case); an expectation that an existing licence will be renewed or advance notice of non-renewal will be given (FAI’s case); and an expectation that a beneficial (tribunal) recommendation will not be overturned (Haoucher v Minister for Immigration). Where expectations were raised and recognised by the courts a right to natural justice was established.

Difficulties with the meaning and application of the concept were, subsequently, raised by the High Court in the case of ex parte Lam, and the High Court has recently called it “an unfortunate expression”. The court emphatically rejected the utility of the concept in WZARH:

The “legitimate expectation” of a person affected by an administrative decision does not provide a basis for determining whether procedural fairness should be accorded to that person or for determining the content of such procedural fairness. It is sufficient to say that, in the absence of a clear, contrary legislative intention, administrative decision-makers must accord procedural fairness to those affected by their decisions. Recourse to the notion of legitimate expectation is both unnecessary and unhelpful. Indeed, reference to the concept of legitimate expectation may well distract from the real question; namely, what is required in order to ensure that the decision is made fairly in the circumstances having regard to the legal framework within which the decision is to be made.

In conclusion, the expansion of the types of interests that attracted natural justice occurred during the second half of the twentieth century. It can be safely stated that natural justice protects a wide range of individual’s interests beyond legal rights – that is, privileges, benefits, and advantages – within the power of government officials. However, the ambit of the interests sufficient for attracting procedural fairness remains unclear. In Plaintiff S10/2011 v Minister for Immigration, four members of the High Court agreed with Brennan J in Kioa:

The presumption that the principles of natural justice condition the exercise of a statutory power may apply to any statutory power which is apt to affect any interest possessed by an individual whether or not the interest amounts to a legal right or is a proprietary or financial interest or relates to reputation.

It is also worth noting, at this point, that in Plaintiff S10 the joint judgment linked the concept of ‘interest’ to the notion of interest that gives a person standing to bring an action for judicial review. It is not clear if the two concepts have the same contours.

The issue of what interests are ‘recognised’ and attract natural justice principles was, briefly, taken up by the High Court in Isbester v Knox City Council. The case concerned a local council’s decision to destroy an allegedly dangerous dog called ‘Izzy’. The decision to destroy the dog affected the owner’s interests, and the local council was obliged to accord procedural fairness in its decision-making processes, the plurality judgment stated:

Whether one describes an interest in a dog as a property right, or acknowledges the importance of a domestic pet to many people, the appellant is a person who may be affected by a decision which will require her interests to be subordinated to the public interest. It is therefore understandable that the council has accepted throughout these proceedings that a decision under s 84P(e) requires compliance with the requirements of natural justice.

Seemingly, provided people consider something to be ‘important’ that thing is sufficient to attract natural justice. That is potentially a difficult test to administer and apply coherently.

It should also be remembered that natural justice is, presumptively, applicable only where administrative decisions affect individual’s rights and interests in a direct and immediate way. So, this restriction on the application of natural justice means that where decisions affect people’s rights and interests indiscriminately, for example a government decision affecting the community at large, (eg about the frequency and timing of use of an airport by commercial aircraft) there is no presumption that natural justice applies. If the decision,

… affects the interests of the public, or a section of the public, at large rather than the interests of particular individuals it will, usually at least, be a decision in relation to which no particular individual or body can claim an entitlement to procedural fairness: particularly, an entitlement to be heard, in relation to a proposed decision, before it is made.

Additionally, if the exercise of statutory powers prejudices the interests of a particular party, but does so indirectly, the affected party does not have a right to be heard before the power is exercised.

So, in Saraceni v ASIC, applicant was a person who had been a company director of three companies that had gone into receivership. The Australian Securities and Investments Commission had exercised its statutory power to authorise a person (‘a Receiver’) to apply to a court for a summons for examination of a person (an investigation, effectively) who was a company officer or provisional liquidator of a company.

Mr Saraceni challenged the decision of ASIC to confer the status of eligible applicant upon the Receivers, on grounds of procedural unfairness. Saraceni argued that he was entitled to notice of the application by the Receivers to ASIC and an opportunity to be heard on the question of the grant of the status of eligible applicant.

The Federal Court held that Mr Saraceni was only indirectly affected by the exercise of ASIC’s statutory powers, so he had no right to a hearing before the powers were exercised and any investigation commenced. North J explained:

The decision of ASIC to authorise a person to apply for a [court] summons for examination has a consequence for the potential examinees [such as Saraceni]. It brings closer the chance of an examination of the potential examinee occurring. In ordinary parlance that consequence is properly described as a prejudice to the potential examinee because it is a disadvantage to that person. Further, it is a prejudice to an interest which the potential examinee has in remaining free of the intrusion which an examination entails, and free from the necessity to expend time and effort required to participate in such an examination. However, prejudice is not a concept with a fixed content. There are degrees of prejudice. Not all statutory powers which prejudice a person’s interest attract the obligations of procedural fairness. … The statutory power conferred by s 596A [Corporations Act 2001 (Cth)] prejudices the interests of potential examinees but does not do so in a sufficiently direct way as to require potential examinees to be heard in relation to the decision of ASIC.

So, in Saraceni the Federal Court held that the power of authorisation conferred on ASIC was not subject to a duty to accord natural justice to a potential examinee, at the beginning of an investigative process into the affairs of a company (stage one), and ahead of any court Order (stage two), and commencement of the investigative process of examining company officers (directors, secretaries etc). The rights of the potential examinee were either not affected at stage one or were merely indirectly affected.

Whether exercise of a statutory power is conditioned on an obligation of procedural fairness is determined by a process of statutory construction. Natural justice will not apply if it is excluded by statute expressly or by necessary implication.

Statutory exclusion or modification of natural justice principles

Procedural fairness is a flexible obligation that requires administrative decision-makers to adopt fair procedures that are appropriate and adapted to the particular task at hand. But it is pointless asking what the duty requires in a given circumstance (ie determine the content) if legislation has clearly excluded the application of the principles. The leading cases dealing with the statutory exclusion of the common law rules of natural justice are Miah and Saeed. Miah’s case is discussed below.

Legislation can trump the common law and exclude natural justice but only if the legislature employs “plain words of necessary intendment” that exclude the principles. When deciding whether legislation does exclude common law principles the courts apply the ‘principle of legality’. As the High Court affirmed in Saeed,

In Annetts v McCann Mason CJ, Deane and McHugh JJ said that the principles of natural justice could be excluded only by “plain words of necessary intendment”. And in Commissioner of Police v Tanos Dixon CJ and Webb J said that an intention to exclude was not to be assumed or spelled out from “indirect references, uncertain inferences or equivocal considerations”. Their Honours in Annetts v McCann added that such an intention was not to be inferred from the mere presence in the statute of rights consistent with some natural justice principles. The presumption that it is highly improbable that Parliament would overthrow fundamental principles or depart from the general system of law, without expressing its intention with irresistible clearness derives from the principle of legality.

In Coco v The Queen the High Court said, with respect to fundamental rights, that “[t]he courts should not impute to the legislature an intention to interfere with fundamental rights”. And, “… the same may be said as to the displacement of fundamental principles of the common law.” Accordingly, natural justice principles can only be excluded if the legislature evinces such an intention clearly, manifested by unmistakeable and unambiguous language.

Additionally, legislation can spell out what procedures are to be followed in certain administrative decision-making contexts (sometimes called statutory procedural codes). An issue arising is whether a given statutory procedure for making and reviewing executive decisions reveals a legislative intention to exhaustively provide for natural justice, or whether the common law can supplement the legislative scheme.

In Re Minister for Immigration, ex parte Miah, the High Court was not prepared to accept that a statutory procedural code for dealing fairly, efficiently and quickly with visa applications excluded the common law. By a slender majority the Court decided that the decision of the Minister’s delegate (an official in the Immigration Department) had denied Miah natural justice in the course of determining whether he was a refugee to whom Australia owed protection obligations under international law.

Specifically, after Miah applied for a protection visa there was a change in government in Bangladesh. By reason of that occurrence, the decision-maker determined, critically, that Miah had no well-founded fear of persecution by reason of his religion and could safely be repatriated. Miah was not invited to make submissions to the delegate with regard to the change in circumstances occasioned by the election of a new government. And as Gaudron J observed, there was a real question (if Miah’s claims were accepted) whether the change in government in Bangladesh was sufficient to preclude whatever fear of persecution he had developed.

The Australian government argued that the delegate was not obliged (under the terms of the statutory procedural code) to invite Miah to make submissions about the change of government. It was said that the delegate had complied with the statutory code, therefore there was no unfairness.

The subdivision of the Migration Act which contained the procedural code was entitled “dealing fairly, efficiently and quickly with visa applications”. In light of that, Gaudron J concluded that the decision-making powers were to be exercised to ensure procedural fairness, albeit in a manner that is quick and efficient; the obligation to accord procedural fairness was not excluded. Her Honour also held that the existence of a right of merits review before the Refugee Review Tribunal (which is now incorporated into the Administrative Review Tribunal) did not mean the original decision-maker (the immigration official) was relieved of the obligation to accord procedural fairness. It is worth fully setting out the views of Gaudron J:

… the delegate did not simply reject the claims made by Mr Miah. Indeed, he barely considered them. Rather, he had regard to the recent elections and change of government in Bangladesh and drew inferences from limited and, to some extent, equivocal information which he seemed to think rendered Mr Miah's claims virtually irrelevant. A question, thus, arose whether, as subdiv AB [of the Migration Act] contemplates, he should have invited further information or submissions from Mr Miah to ensure procedural fairness.

The basic principle with respect to procedural fairness is that a person should have an opportunity to put his or her case and to meet the case that is put against him or her. Mr Miah was not given the opportunity to put a case by reference to the change in government in Bangladesh or to answer the case made against him by reference to that change. Procedural fairness required that he be given that opportunity.

McHugh J concurred, stating:

The subdivision sets out various formal procedures which the Minister may or must follow to ensure fairness to applicants. But subdiv AB does not declare that they exhaustively define the content of fair procedure. The subject matter of the Act, the fact that it implements Australia's international obligations, and the omission of words unambiguously pointing to an intention to exclude all the common law rules of natural justice indicate that the exercise of power under subdiv AB is conditioned on the observance of those rules except where the provisions of the Act specifically supersede them.

For McHugh J, the ‘code’ argument was unsatisfactory, it did not exclude additional incidents of procedural fairness, use of the word ‘fairly’ in the subdivision’s heading made it difficult to extrapolate a clear intention to exclude rules of natural justice from applying to the procedures.

Although extrinsic materials (the explanatory memorandum) pointed to a legislative intention that the code was intended to replace the common law, McHugh J reasoned that the Minister’s views about the meaning of the statute were not determinative, if it was at odds with the text of the Act, adding: “[T]he need to act on the text of the enactment and not the Minister's statements is particularly important when the Minister's meaning has serious consequences for an individual.” Here his Honour makes an important point about statutory interpretation; it is the language (the text) of the Act that is controlling when determining meaning, not what politicians intended or understood the words in an Act to mean.

After the decision in Miah, parliament legislated to clearly provide for a code that exhaustively stated the procedural obligations owed to non-citizens, and ousted common law principles, via the Migration Legislation Amendment (Procedural Fairness) Act 2002 (Cth). This Act was intended to overcome the effect of the High Court’s decision in Miah. The issue re-emerged before the High Court in Saeed v Minister for Immigration, a case you should read carefully in preparation for workshop four (the case is accessible via Learn.UQ). Additionally, before workshop four please revisit CPCF v Minister for Immigration (which you first studied ahead of and during workshop two) and consider whether the rules of procedural fairness were to be implied (and so were applicable) in the context of the exercise of maritime powers under s 72(4) Maritime Powers Act.

Content questions: In law context is everything.

The content of natural justice turns, in part, on the statutory context and in part on the particular circumstances (context) of the particular case

Even in circumstances where fundamental rights are at issue, such as deprivation of liberty, there may be occasions, and circumstances, where the courts decide that they cannot imply natural justice (‘fair hearing’ requirements) or imply natural justice principles fully. O’Shea’s case illustrates the point.  O’Shea had been sentenced to a period of indefinite (‘preventive’) detention under South Australian criminal law; in SA there was a three tiered administrative process for determining whether offenders should be released on licence (‘parole’).

O’Shea was examined by two medical practitioners who decided he was a fit and proper person to be released, and the parole board also recommended that the person was fit to be released, and that detention should be terminated. O’Shea and his legal counsel appeared at a hearing, before the parole board made its recommendation to the South Australian government.

However, the parole board’s decision was not the operative (or, legally conclusive) determination; that responsibility was effectively vested in a political institution (the Cabinet) and the State Governor who had the power to decide if release was in the ‘public interest’.

The Cabinet considered the parole board’s recommendation but concluded that release was not in the ‘public interest’ and did not advise the Governor to exercise his statutory powers (under the Correctional Services Act 1982 (SA)) to release the prisoner. The Governor acted on the advice of Cabinet and O’Shea claimed that he should have been able to put his case directly to the Governor before the final determination was made. The High Court rejected that argument.

A majority of the Court determined that a hearing before the Parole Board provided a sufficient opportunity for a party to present his case. So the decision-making process, viewed in its entirety, provided procedural fairness. Wilson and Toohey JJ stated:

the duality of the legislative scheme embodied in s 77a of the Criminal Law Act characterizes the Governor’s decision as an expression of an unfettered discretion as to what the public interest requires in the instant case. It may be that a system of judicial review would ensure greater protection for the individual than the present scheme, but that is not what the legislature has provided. The Correctional Services Act expressly secures procedural fairness to Mr O'Shea in the course of the Board's consideration of his case. But beyond that he is in the hands of the Government, which must accept political responsibility for his release. Given the nature of this decision, it cannot be said that Mr O'Shea could have more than a hope that the Governor would be prepared to act on the recommendation of the Board. Hope, of itself, is not sufficient to ground an expectation that will attract legal consequences.

But if the final decision-maker intended to consider some new material, not appearing in the report of the parole board and the affected party had had no opportunity of dealing with that additional material, fairness would require that (s)he should be afforded that opportunity.

On Cabinet’s role in the parole process, Mason CJ observed that some Cabinet decisions relating to broad political, economic, and social concerns wouldn’t attract natural justice; whereas other matters are more closely related to justice to the individual, and in such individual cases the duty to accord natural justice would be applicable. Mason CJ also observed that the duty to accord natural justice might entail a requirement that written submissions of the individual affected (or a summary of submissions) be placed before Cabinet. This would not amount to an intrusion into Cabinet’s control over its own proceedings.

Mason CJ stated further that:

Although it is unrealistic and impractical to insist on a person having the opportunity to present submissions on matters of high level general policy, the same considerations do not apply to the impact of policy on the individual and to those aspects of policy which are closely related to the circumstances of the particular case and that is the case here.

Now, what if a government decision-maker’s original decision is subject to a review? Are the requirements of natural justice applicable at the initial (original) stage of administrative decision-making too? Subject to statutory exclusion or modification, generally, the answer is yes: there is no general rule that a right of appeal or review denies or limits the application of natural justice rules at the initial stage. However, the existence of appeal or review rights may affect the content of natural justice (the extent to which the natural justice principles apply) at the initial stage of decision-making.

You should study the leading case of ex parte Miah closely and examine what McHugh J stated about the assorted relevant factors that help determine whether a right of review or appeal excludes or limits the rules of natural justice at first instance.

The context for a fair hearing and the ‘materiality’ of procedural errors

In Minister for Immigration and Border Protection v WZARH, the High Court stated:

Where … the procedure adopted by an administrator can be shown itself to have failed to afford a fair opportunity to be heard, a denial of procedural fairness is established by nothing more than that failure, and the granting of curial relief is justified unless it can be shown that the failure did not deprive the person of the possibility of a successful outcome. The practical injustice in such a case lies in the denial of an opportunity which in fairness ought to have been given.

Subsequently, in Minister for Immigration and Border Protection v SZSSJ - the High Court stated, unanimously:

procedural fairness requires the repository of a statutory power to adopt a procedure that is reasonable in the circumstances to afford an opportunity to be heard to a person who has an interest apt to be affected by exercise of that power. The implied condition of procedural fairness is breached, and jurisdictional error thereby occurs, if the procedure adopted so constrains the opportunity of the person to propound his or her case for a favourable exercise of the power as to amount to a “practical injustice”.

In NBNB v Minister for Immigration, Allsop CJ and Katzman J of the Federal Court, stated that how unfairness (or “practical injustice”) is revealed or demonstrated in any particular case will depend on the circumstances. And further, their Honours stated that: “Generally speaking, if a person is not informed of an important aspect of a case he or she has to meet … practical injustice will have been demonstrated.”

More recently the High Court has linked the notion of ‘practical injustice’ to the concept of ‘materiality’ in the following way. In Minister for Immigration v SZMTA, Bell, Gageler and Keane JJ stated that for a breach of procedural fairness to constitute a jurisdictional error on the part of the administrative decision-maker (a Tribunal in this case) the breach must give rise to practical injustice. They continued by stating that practical injustice means: “the breach must result in a denial of an opportunity to make submissions and that denial must be material to the Tribunal’s decision.”.

How is materiality to be determined?

A question for the courts to resolve is, therefore, whether a breach of procedural fairness – denial of an opportunity that ought to have been given – is material or immaterial (in the sense that the breach did not deprive the person affected of the possibility of a successful outcome). So, in SZMTA, Bell, Gageler and Keane JJ said that a breach of the obligation of procedural fairness is ‘material’ if compliance could have resulted in a different outcome:

A breach is material to a decision only if compliance could realistically have resulted in a different decision. Where materiality is in issue in an application for judicial review, and except in a case where the decision made was the only decision legally available to be made, the question of the materiality of the breach is an ordinary question of fact in respect of which the applicant bears the onus of proof.

And on the particular facts of the case in SZMTA, the non-disclosure of the fact that some documents were subject to a ‘notification’ certificate under the Migration Act (s 438) (and so withheld from a visa applicant on ‘public interest’ grounds), did not constitute a material error. Therefore, the breach of procedural fairness (lack of notice to the applicant about the existence of the notification certificate) did not amount to a jurisdictional error. This was because the documents covered by the certificate had no bearing on the decision of the Tribunal. So, the visa applicants had not lost an opportunity to advance their case.

Departing from the majority view in SZMTA, Nettle and Gordon JJ concluded that an analysis about the ‘materiality of the error’ was only relevant for the court’s exercise of discretion about whether to grant relief (a remedy) after jurisdictional error was made out. Their Honours rejected materiality of error as a criterion of jurisdictional error.

In summary, and simply put, materiality requires that an error in the making of a decision only invalidates the decision if the error might have affected the outcome of that decision. A majority of the HCA in SZMTA decided that the applicant bears the onus of proving an error was material (not the challenged decision-maker).

Recent HCA authority has, however, emphasised that discharging this evidential onus will not be “demanding or onerous” for an applicant.  What the evidential onus requires in practice was considered by the HCA in the cases of Nathanson v Minister for Home Affairs (2022) and LPDT v Minister for Immigration, Citizenship, Migrant Services and Multicultural Affairs (2024).  In LPDT, Gageler CJ, Gordon, Edelman, Steward, Gleeson and Jagot JJ considered materiality in the following passages.  The complexity of the issues raised by materiality justifies extended quotation from this joint judgment of the HCA.

[6] In some cases, where an error is established, the error will be jurisdictional irrespective of any effect that the error might or might not have had on the decision that was made in fact. In other cases, the potential for an effect on the decision will be inherent in the nature of the error. An example of the former is apprehended or actual bias. … An example of the latter is unreasonableness in the final result. … In such cases, the error necessarily satisfies the requirement of materiality.

[7] In most cases, however, an error will only be jurisdictional if the error was material to the decision that was made in fact, … in the sense that there is a realistic possibility that the decision that was made in fact could have been different if the error had not occurred. … That is because it is now accepted that a statute which contains an express or implied condition to be observed in a decision-making process is ordinarily to be interpreted as incorporating such a “threshold of materiality” in the event of non-compliance. …

Two questions

[9] Where it is alleged in an application for judicial review that a decision is affected by jurisdictional error constituted by a breach of an express or implied condition of a conferral of decision-making authority by a statute … which incorporates a requirement of materiality, there are two questions: has an error occurred; and, if so, was that error material.

[10] The inquiry posited by each question is wholly backward-looking. … Both questions are to be answered by reference to the decision that was made and, depending on the nature of the error, how that decision was made. Those are facts in respect of which the applicant for judicial review bears the onus of proof on the balance of probabilities. … Proof of these facts ought to be neither difficult nor contentious.

[11] What must be proved to show what decision was made and how it was made will depend upon the nature of the error. In a common case … where the error alleged is breach of a condition governing the reasoning to be undertaken by the decision-maker, the applicant’s onus of proving the relevant facts is discharged by nothing more than the tender of the decision-maker’s statement of reasons.

[12] Where the jurisdictional error alleged is one concerned with the process of the decision making, such as a denial of procedural fairness, what must be proved by the applicant will depend upon the precise error alleged to have occurred in the decision-making process, having regard to any relevant statutory provisions within the applicable legislative framework. Examples of the types of evidence that have been sufficient for establishing the relevant facts in such cases include the appellate record, … and evidence of the content of a document or information that was required to be provided as part of the decision-making process. …

[13] The applicant must satisfy the court on the balance of probabilities that the alleged error in fact occurred. … Unless the error is of a type such as those identified at [6] above (where the error is always material and therefore jurisdictional), whether the error is, or is not, material is determined by inferences drawn from the evidence adduced on the application.

[14] The question in these cases is whether the decision that was in fact made could, not would, “realistically” have been different had there been no error. … “Realistic” is used to distinguish the assessment of the possibility of a different outcome from one where the possibility is fanciful or improbable. … Though the applicant must satisfy the court that the threshold of materiality is met in order to establish that the error is jurisdictional, meeting that threshold is not demanding or onerous. …

[15] What must be shown to demonstrate that an established error meets the threshold of materiality will depend upon the error. In some cases, it will be sufficient to show that there has been an error and that the outcome is consistent with the error having affected the decision. … Where the error is a denial of procedural fairness arising from a failure to put the applicant on notice of a fact or issue, the court may readily be able to infer that, if fairly put on notice of that fact or issue, the applicant might have addressed it by way of further evidence or submissions, and that the decision-maker would have approached the applicant’s further evidence or submissions with an open mind. … In those cases, it is “no easy task” for the court to be satisfied that the loss of such an opportunity did not deprive the person of the possibility of a successful outcome. … Importantly, a court called upon to determine whether the threshold has been met must be careful not to assume the function of the decision-maker: … the point at which the line between judicial review and merits review is crossed may not always be clear, but the line must be maintained. …

[16] In sum, unless there is identified a basis on which it can be affirmatively concluded that the outcome would inevitably have been the same had the error not been made, once an applicant establishes that there has been an error and demonstrates that there exists a realistic possibility that the outcome of the decision could have been different had that error not been made, the threshold of materiality will have been met (and curial relief will be justified subject to any issue of utility or discretion).

Fair hearing requirements

Prior Notice

As Lord Denning observed in Kanda, ‘if a right [or opportunity] to be heard is worth anything, it must carry with it the right to know the case to be met ...’. Notice of the case to be met has both temporal and substantive elements. The opportunity to be heard implies that a party is given adequate time to prepare. Additionally, it implies that a party has a proper opportunity to ascertain the nature of proceedings and the substance of legal and factual matters to be addressed; and that the nature and content of information (that is adverse to a party) is disclosed to them.

In Minister for Immigration v SZSSJ (2016) the High Court explained what adequate notice meant in substance:

Ordinarily, affording a reasonable opportunity to be heard in the exercise of a statutory power to conduct an inquiry requires that a person whose interest is apt to be affected be put on notice of: the nature and purpose of the inquiry; the issues to be considered in conducting the inquiry; and the nature and content of information that the repository of power undertaking the inquiry might take into account as a reason for coming to a conclusion adverse to the person.

In Lyons v Building Services Authority, (a matter before QCAT) Deputy President Judge Kingham and Member Howe commented on the importance of sufficient notice, they said:

Appropriate prior notice allows a party to prepare and present his case effectively. Inadequate notice both in respect of either time or substance prevents a party from being able to do so, and amounts to a denial of procedural fairness.

So, typically, a person must be given advance notice of a decision to be made that is going to affect their rights and interests, so that they can reasonably prepare to respond. But this is context-dependant and may turn on the statutory context and nature of the power being exercised. As Mason J explained in Kioa:

It would be going too far to say that fairness requires that in all cases in which a deportation order is to be made notice should be given to the prohibited immigrant of the intention to make such an order and of the grounds upon which it is to be made. The Migration Act plainly contemplates that in the ordinary course of events a deportation order will be made ex parte. And the prohibited immigrant may be a person who, intent upon remaining in Australia without lawful right or title, has evaded the authorities and will continue to do so. […] To insist that he be notified of the intention to make a deportation order would serve only to facilitate evasion and frustrate the objects of the statute. These considerations indicate that, in the case where the reason for the making of the order is that the person concerned is a prohibited immigrant, the dictates of natural justice and fairness do not require the giving of any advance notice of the proposed making of the order.

Exactly how much notice should be given, and in what form, is context dependant, but typically prior notice must be adequate in the circumstances; it must usually be in writing and afford a person a reasonable period of time to prepare, obtain and marshal material in order to respond to the case against them.

In Dunghutti Elders Council v Registrar of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Corporations, the Registrar had a statutory power to determine that an Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Corporation be placed under special administration (new management) on prescribed grounds in s 487-5 Corporations (ATSI) Act 2006 (Cth) (CATSI Act), including, for example, where a corporation’s officer was not acting the best interests of its members.

Before making such a determination the Registrar was required to give a corporation notice in writing, inviting the corporation to ‘show cause’, within a specified reasonable period, why a determination should not be made and to consider representations the corporation makes within that period (s 487-10). The content of the notice was not otherwise prescribed by statute.

The Registrar issued a notice stating that he considered particular grounds in s 487-5(1) might have existed. The appellant argued that there was a denial of procedural fairness relating to aspects of the notice and that a reasonable period to respond was not afforded.

The Full Court of the Federal Court held that the court’s role was to ensure the power was exercised within the legal framework erected in the Act, supplemented by the principles of procedural fairness operating within that framework.

The Court found that the issuing of the show cause notice under s 487-10(1) was the beginning of the process which engaged the appellant’s entitlement to be accorded procedural fairness. There was no procedural entitlement anterior to the process prescribed by statute: “in the present case the appellant’s invitation to introduce a level of process and procedural entitlements as precursors to the commencement of the show cause process ﬁnds no foothold in the CATSI Act.” Furthermore, the Court stated that:

The appellant is, as we have said, entitled to procedural fairness in relation to whether it should be put under special administration, but the practical content of procedural fairness in the present context is not the same as the procedural fairness to which a person accused of a crime is entitled. The appellant can reasonably be expected to engage responsively and responsibly to concerns as to its stewardship expressed by the ﬁrst respondent.

In terms of whether the affected corporation had been given a reasonable time period to respond to the notice, the Full Court agreed with the primary judge who had decided that what was reasonable (in the absence of a stipulated set period) was a period determined with reference to the tasks required to be undertaken.

On the facts a period of two weeks was reasonable given the number of issues raised in the show cause notice, factual complexity of those issues, the need to assemble material if representations were to be meaningful, the need for prompt action to be taken and the need or desirability of obtaining legal advice. The Full Court stated:

The CATSI Act requires that the show cause notice allow a reasonable time for a response. There can be no doubt that the concern as to the appellant’s stewardship of moneys held by it in trust was of such a nature as to make a demand for a prompt response reasonable.

Adequate disclosure of the nature of the case to be met.

There needs to be sufficient particularity in the notice given to a person so that they know the precise nature of the case made against them. As the Full Federal Court explained in Commissioner for ACT Revenue v Alphaone Pty Ltd (a licencing case):

It is a fundamental principle that where the rules of procedural fairness apply to a decision-making process, the party liable to be directly affected by the decision is to be given the opportunity of being heard. That would ordinarily require the party affected to be given the opportunity of ascertaining the relevant issues and to be informed of the nature and content of adverse material.

Contemporary case-law (from the decision in Kioa onwards) has placed emphasis upon supplying details of the particular matters that are critical, significant and adverse to a person’s rights or interests. As Brennan J explained in Kioa v West:

The person whose interests are likely to be affected does not have to be given an opportunity to comment on every adverse piece of information, irrespective of its credibility, relevance or significance. Administrative decision-making is not to be clogged by inquiries into allegations to which the repository of the power would not give credence, or which are not relevant to his decision or which are of little significance to the decision which is to be made. Administrative decisions are not necessarily to be held invalid because the procedures of adversary litigation are not fully observed. […] [A]n opportunity should be given to deal with adverse information that is credible, relevant and significant to the decision to be made.

Reverting to Dunghutti’s case, the appellants complained that the show cause notice was inadequate because, contrary to common law principles and the CATSI Act, they were not provided with the dates of letters of complaint (submitted by members of the corporation) or disputes, nor details of the number of complaints made, or whether the complaints were current. The Court in Dunghutti cited Baker v Gough, where Jacobs J said that:

… where a person is given the right to show cause or the opportunity to be heard before action is taken against him, he is entitled to know the ground upon which it is proposed to take that action so that he can have a true opportunity of showing cause against it. It seems to me that the degree of precision in relation to those grounds may vary according to the particular type of case and the particular allegation which is made, or matter which is to be determined …

And then the Federal Court (Full Court) cited the primary judge in Dunghutti’s case with approval, and said that

… whether the obligation to afford procedural fairness has been discharged is a “practical” matter that was “not to be evaluated minutely”: Habib v Director-General of Security (2009) 175 FCR 411 at 430 ... . Regardless of whether the disclosure requirement ﬁnds its content in the common law or in s 487-10 of the CATSI Act, his Honour was of the view at [34] that the requirements of procedural fairness are satisﬁed where “the substance of the legal and factual concern is disclosed”. In expressing that view, his Honour relied upon Plaintiff M61/2010E v Commonwealth (2010) 85 ALJR 133 at 150-151; 272 ALR 14 at 36 ... .

On the facts the Full Court was satisfied that the primary judge was correct to conclude that the appellants had been adequately notified of the substance of the adverse material – the complaints made against the corporation. There was no obligation on the Registrar to put before the corporation adverse material before the giving of the notice. The appellant’s entitlement to procedural fairness was measured by the need to ensure it had the opportunity to place such material before the Registrar as might deter him from deciding to put it under special administration.

Turning now to a different but related matter. What if relevant, adverse, material is not disclosed to a person because the decision-maker disregarded the prejudicial information, claiming that they did not take it into account or place any weight upon it in the course of deciding a matter? Does procedural fairness, nonetheless, require disclosure of that material, and, if so, why? These issues were addressed by Brennan J in Kioa v West, and taken up again more recently in VEAL v Minister for Immigration, which is listed for careful reading for workshop four.

Finally, it is unfair for a decision-maker to consider material that is prejudicial to the subject of a proposed decision, without having given that subject a fair opportunity to be heard in respect of that material. This means a right to rebut or qualify (adverse) material before a decision-maker. The High Court has affirmed that:

Procedural fairness requires a decision-maker to identify for the person affected any critical issue not apparent from the nature of the decision or the terms of the statutory power. The decision-maker must also advise of any adverse conclusion which would not obviously be open on the known material. However, a decision-maker is not otherwise required to expose his or her thought processes or provisional views for comment before making the decision.

In summary, unless accurate and adequate substantive notice is given, the right to be heard fairly may be illusory.

A reasonable opportunity to respond.

A person affected by a decision to which procedural fairness principles apply must be afforded a reasonable opportunity to answer the case against them, but this does not equate to an unlimited opportunity to put forward their case in any manner they wish; the form and scope of a hearing will vary, depending on the context.

In a foundational case, Heatley v Tasmanian Racing and Gaming Commission, Mr Heatley was given no prior notice of the Commission’s intention to issue a ‘warning off notice’ ordering him to stay away from racecourses for an indefinite period, and no opportunity to make representations about it prior to its service upon him. No reasons were given for the issue of the notice either.

The High Court held that the Racing Commission, when acting under statutory powers to bar a person from entering any racecourse or specified racecourses, was obliged to comply with natural justice principles: it was required to give notice (except in an emergency) of the proposed action, and afford a person who was to be subject to the decision an opportunity to make representations. This was because Mr Heatley was deprived of the expectation/opportunity (not a legally enforceable right) available to all other members of the public of going to a racecourse upon payment of the admission charge. In the course of giving the leading judgment, Aickin J stated:

The cases show clearly that the principles of natural justice do not comprise rigid rules, but the requirements of compliance with those principles will depend upon the particular circumstances. “Fairness” may require, or be satisfied by, different procedures even by the same statutory authority in different circumstances. …

Fairness requires that the person affected should, save in an emergency, be given notice by the Commission of its intention to issue a warning-off notice and of the grounds for that proposed action and should be afforded an opportunity to make representations to the Commission on his own behalf, which it must consider before taking action. … I do not think that fairness requires in this context an oral hearing, though in some circumstances the Commission may well find that it cannot resolve inconsistencies between its information and written submissions from the person concerned without such a hearing. It is, however, for the Commission itself to devise its own procedures in the light of its obligation to act fairly. It should however not act on information the general nature of which is not revealed to the person affected.

So, procedural fairness does not require an administrator or adjudicative body to afford an oral hearing to a person affected by a decision, as the High Court affirmed in WZARH:

In the absence of a clear, contrary legislative intention, administrative decision-makers must accord procedural fairness to those affected by their decisions. Whether an oral hearing is required in order to accord procedural fairness to a person affected by an administrative decision depends on the practical requirements of procedural fairness in the circumstances of the case. There is no general rule that procedural fairness requires an administrative decision-maker to afford a person affected by the decision an oral hearing in every case.

Statutory requirements pointing to informality and speed in decision-making may weigh against provision of an oral hearing, at least during initial stages of decision-making. A person will have to establish that an oral hearing is necessary in order to determine the case fairly. An oral hearing would be necessary in circumstances where evidential inconsistences are present (as noted in Heatley above), or where a person’s credibility is at issue, or potentially where the person cannot speak the English language sufficiently well.

Representation

In the oft-cited case of Cains v Jenkins Sweeney and St John JJ stated:

On the authorities there is no absolute right to representation even where livelihood is at stake. But that is not to say that in all cases a tribunal can refuse it with impunity. The seriousness of the matter and the complexity of the issues, factual or legal, may be such that refusal would offend natural justice principles.

Representation by a lawyer (or layperson) at an administrative hearing may be required, to ensure procedural fairness, if the matter is complex and the person cannot present their own case because they lack the capacity to comprehend proceedings. And while there is a right to cross-examine witnesses in civil litigation there is no corresponding right in administrative proceedings which are, ostensibly, more informal and ‘inquisitorial’. However, where credibility of a key witness is at issue, an administrative body ought to permit cross-examination of that witness in fairness to the person affected by that witnesses’ evidence.

Finally, a failure to adjourn an administrative hearing may breach procedural fairness principles (i.e. an opportunity to present the case) and amount to a legally unreasonable decision. This was the circumstance in a matter before the HCA in Minister for Immigration v Li, a case we will examine in a later workshop when we explore what is meant by the legal standard of unreasonableness in judicial review applications.

In summary, remember that compliance with an implied condition of procedural fairness requires the repository of a statutory power to adopt a procedure that is reasonable in the circumstances to afford an opportunity to be heard to a person who has an interest apt to be affected by exercise of that power.

Summerton and Veterans Review Tribunal (Cth)

Legislative Framework

The War Veterans’ Welfare Entitlements Act 1999 (Cth) – ‘An Act to provide for the payment of pensions and other benefits to, and to provide medical and other treatment for, war veterans and certain other persons, and for other purposes’ – regulates the provision of pensions, compensation and support for veterans and their families, for injury, disease or death which is related to service with the Australian Defence Force.

The 1999 Act provides that the Veterans’ Review Tribunal has power to undertake merits reviews (de novo) of the welfare entitlement decisions of the Department of War Veterans’ Affairs and the Tribunal may exercise all the powers and discretions that are conferred on any relevant person who made the original decision. The tribunal may affirm, vary or set aside the decision under review and either remit the matter to the original decision-maker or substitute a fresh decision (s.33).

Under the terms of section 2 of the Act the VRT is directed to provide a mechanism of review that is ‘economical, informal and quick.’ Further, section 30(1)(b) provides that:

the proceeding shall be conducted with as little formality and technicality, and with as much expedition, as the requirements of this Act and of every other relevant enactment and a proper consideration of the matters before the Tribunal permit.

Facts

On April 2 last year, Mr Summerton applied for a war veterans’ disability pension at the ‘special rate’ on the basis that he is a ‘veteran’ who has rendered ‘eligible wartime service’ (as defined in the 1999 Act) between October 1999 and February 2000 during the deployment of the International Force East Timor (INTERFET), and because his injuries and mental illness were ‘war caused’ (as required under the 1999 Act) and these have incapacitated him permanently.

The Department of Veterans’ Affairs (Cth) determined that Mr Summerton qualified for a disability pension at 100% of the ‘general rate’ because his hearing loss and emphysema were ‘war caused’. But the DVA decided that he did not qualify for an increased service pension, at the ‘special rate’, because his post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) was not ‘war-caused’. There is no dispute that Summerton suffers from PTSD, but the DVA did not accept Mr Summerton’s claim that a particular occurrence happened while he was rendering operational service in East Timor and that this occurrence caused his PTSD.

On 28 August last year, Mr Summerton was notified of the delegate’s decision in writing. The relevant provisions in the War Veterans’ Welfare Entitlements Act that provided the framework and principles regarding war-caused injury and disease were outlined in the decision letter, and the letter conveyed the gist of the adverse evidence/material on which the delegate’s conclusions were based. Summerton was advised of his statutory appeal rights and the relevant timeline and filing fee for submitting a valid appeal to the Veteran’s Review Tribunal (VRT).

Mr Summerton lodged an appeal application of that adverse decision with the Tribunal on 12 September last year: broadly, on the grounds that the decision was unfair and incorrect.

Merits Review Hearing

After an initial conference and then conciliation did not yield a mutually agreeable outcome, an oral hearing was scheduled on 15 February. Before the VRT, Mr Summerton, who was appearing as a self-represented litigant, spoke slowly and was evidently anxious about narrating his experiences in East Timor and details of the particular incident which he claimed had given rise to his PTSD. After one hour of the hearing and following a period of questioning by the Department’s legal representative, he became agitated and asked for a short rest to re-gather his composure and collect his thoughts. This request was refused by the presiding tribunal Member who cited the exhortation in s 30(1)(b) relating to quick resolution of matters and the need to balance fairness with efficiency.

Summerton continued, with some difficulty, presenting his case and, subsequently, asked for a brief adjournment after 90 minutes of the hearing on the basis he was finding it hard to concentrate and to fully understand the point of the questions being put to him. The VRT Member agreed to this request. After a 45-minute lunch break the hearing continued.

During the afternoon hearing session, the DVA’s solicitor presented new evidence that cast doubt on the validity and credibility of Summerton’s claims about witnessing firsthand the incident in East Timor alleged to have triggered his PTSD. A retired army officer’s sworn statement (affidavit) contradicted Mr Summerton’s claim that he had actually witnessed a drunken brawl, in which his friend died, between Australian army and naval officers while on recreational leave on a beach near the Beachside Hotel in Dili in January 2000.

The officer’s statement maintained that Mr Summerton was drinking at a nearby bar when the incident occurred and could not, therefore, have directly witnessed the alleged incident. The retired army officer also gave oral evidence, via video-link from Tasmania, and was asked several questions by the Tribunal member. Summerton, who was angered by the nature of the army officer’s evidence, was not permitted to cross-examine him by the tribunal. Official military records corroborate Summerton’s assertion that a brawl took place involving INTERFET personnel on a beach in Dili in January 2000 but provide no details as to any fatalities arising.

Aspects of Summerton’s own oral testimony before the Tribunal were unclear and unfocussed, and occasionally inconsistent with his own written submissions tendered prior to the oral hearing. A statutory declaration from his wife lent general support to his written claims that, upon his return home from East Timor, he became progressively more detached, withdrawn and isolated as time passed.

Before the end of the hearing the VRT Member pointed to material that her associate had unearthed upon making inquiries: a blog (‘memoirs from Timor Leste’) that provided hearsay evidence relating to, and supporting, the occurrence of an incident on a beach in Dili and the death of an INTERFET soldier. The blog speaks of a group of friends who were drinking in the Beachside Hotel at the time of the brawl, and Summerton is mentioned as being among the group of friends who were drinking at the pub. The Tribunal Member indicated she placed ‘only very little weight’ on this hearsay material and, consequently, did not invite either Summerton or the DVA solicitor to address the veracity of this material and respond.

Several weeks after hearing, the VRT, in deciding that Summerton was ineligible for a disability pension at the ‘special rate’, observed in its written published decision:

Having paid due regard to the relevant provisions in the 1999 Act (which is beneficial legislation) and to relevant policy which governs war-caused injuries and diseases and having regard to all the material before the Tribunal, I find that the applicant’s testimony is unreliable, even accounting for the passage of time.

I conclude that Mr Summerton’s PTSD is not war-caused, because there is no relationship to an occurrence arising during his period of operational service. The claim fails and the decision under review is affirmed.

A person who is aggrieved by a decision to which this Act applies that is made after the commencement of this Act may apply to the Federal Court … for an order of review in respect of the decision on any one or more of the following grounds: (a) that a breach of the rules of natural justice occurred in connection with the making of the decision.

Task Two: Understanding the bias rules.

The bias rules complement the right to a fair hearing. Bias connotes the absence of impartiality. The word ‘bias’ is “used to indicate some preponderating disposition or tendency, a ‘propensity; predisposition towards; predilection; prejudice’”.

Unfairness can spring from a denial of an opportunity to present a case and be heard, and also from the denial of an opportunity to have that case considered properly (independently and impartially). The failure to consider the case can arise from the decision maker lacking the necessary detachment and permitting bias to affect their judgment and administrative (or judicial) actions. The rules against bias support the fair administration of justice by removing the influence of interests (financial or otherwise) or other factors (e.g. familial association) that might serve to divert an administrative official or judge from discharging their role in an objective fashion.

In Australia the rules relating to bias have developed differently to English principles set down in the case of R v Bow Street Magistrate; Ex parte Pinochet (No 2), a case in which a judge’s association/activities with a party (Amnesty) involved in proceedings automatically gave rise to disqualifying apprehended bias.

In Australia there is no automatic disqualification for a judge or administrative official with a pecuniary interest in a matter or association with a party to a proceeding. Disqualification turns on the circumstances of each particular case. Context is the variable when an allegation of bias is raised by a party to a dispute in an adjudicative (administrative) or adversarial (court) setting. The context sensitive nature of the rule against bias was confirmed by the High Court in Minister for Immigration v Jia Legeng (2001). Thus, rules established for judicial bias will be modified for other types of decision-maker.
