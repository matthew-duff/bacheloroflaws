Administrative Law (Cth) — Judicial review — Jurisdictional error —
Procedural fairness — Requirement that error must be material —
Decision not to revoke mandatory cancellation of visa — Review of
non-revocation decision by Administrative Appeals Tribunal — Reliance
by Minister on incidents of domestic violence as relevant to best interests
of children — Failure by Tribunal to give visa holder opportunity to
address relevance of domestic violence incidents to protection of
Australian community — Whether denial of procedural fairness material
to decision of Tribunal — Whether applicant for judicial review required
to establish nature of evidence or submissions that might otherwise have
been presented — Migration Act 1958 (Cth), ss 499, 501, 501CA.

A citizen of New Zealand, N, arrived in Australia in 2010 and was
subsequently granted a Class TY Subclass 444 Special Category visa. In
2018, after N had been convicted of a number of offences for which he
was sentenced to a total effective period of imprisonment of two years and
six months, N’s visa was cancelled by a delegate of the Minister pursuant
to s 501(3A) of the Migration Act 1958 (Cth). Another delegate of the
Minister decided not to revoke the cancellation of N’s visa under
s 501CA(4) of the Act, having had regard to a range of “primary”
considerations and other considerations set out in a ministerial direction
made under s 499 of the Act (“Direction 65”). N applied to the
Administrative Appeals Tribunal for review of the non-revocation
decision. Prior to the hearing before the Tribunal, Direction 65 was
replaced by a new ministerial direction made under s 499 of the Act
(“Direction 79”), which included a new paragraph requiring decision-
makers, in assessing the nature and seriousness of the non-citizen’s
conduct, to have regard to “[t]he principle that crimes of a violent nature
against women or children are viewed very seriously, regardless of the
sentence imposed”. N had never been charged with or convicted of any
domestic violence offence, and the delegate had not mentioned domestic
or family violence in the statement of reasons for the non-revocation
decision.

have caused the Tribunal to reach a different conclusion about the
seriousness of his conduct and, consequently, a different outcome. On
appeal, a majority of the Full Court concluded that the primary judge was
correct to ﬁnd that the procedural unfairness was not material to the
Tribunal’s decision.

Held, allowing the appeal, that N had discharged his onus of
demonstrating that the Tribunal’s denial of procedural fairness deprived
him of a realistic possibility of a different outcome so as to give rise to
jurisdictional error. He was not required to articulate a speciﬁc course of
action which could realistically have changed the result, such as the nature
of any additional evidence or submissions that might have been presented
at the Tribunal hearing.

Per Kiefel CJ, Keane and Gleeson JJ. There will generally be a realistic
possibility that a decision-making process could have resulted in a
different outcome if a party was denied an opportunity to present evidence
or make submissions on an issue that required consideration. The standard
of “reasonable conjecture” is undemanding. Where a Tribunal errs by
denying a party a reasonable opportunity to present their case, “reasonable
conjecture” does not require demonstration of how that party might have
taken advantage of that lost opportunity.

Per Gageler J. The onus which the applicant bears to establish
materiality is no greater than to show that, as a matter of reasonable
conjecture within the parameters set by the historical facts established on
the balance of probabilities, the decision could have been different had a
fair opportunity to be heard been afforded. Establishing that threshold of
materiality is not onerous.

Per Gordon J. A serious denial of procedural fairness involving a denial
of an opportunity to be heard in relation to an important issue in the
context of an evaluative decision (as occurred in this case) is a category of
error that will be jurisdictional regardless of the effect the error may have
had on the conclusion of the decision-maker. Where such an error is
established by an applicant, or otherwise admitted, there is no additional
or separate onus on the applicant to demonstrate that the error “could
realistically have resulted in a different decision”.

Per Edelman J. The onus of proof of materiality, where it arises, is so
slight that it can be satisﬁed by an applicant establishing nothing more
than a “quadruple might” at a high level of generality: but for the denial
of procedural fairness, there might have been things that N or his wife
might have said at the hearing that might have assisted his case in a
manner that might have led to a different result.

entail a realistic possibility of a different decision, assessed as a matter

KIEFEL CJ, KEANE AND GLEESON JJ. The issue in this appeal is
whether procedural unfairness by the Administrative Appeals Tribunal
(“the Tribunal”) in the course of hearing the appellant’s application for
review of a decision to refuse to revoke the mandatory cancellation of
his visa involved jurisdictional error. Following a hearing conducted by
the Tribunal, the Tribunal affirmed the decision to refuse to revoke the
visa cancellation. As the Courts below recognised, the Tribunal’s error
in failing to afford the appellant procedural fairness will have involved
jurisdictional error only if that failure was material to the Tribunal’s
decision. Materiality is established if the error deprived the appellant of
a realistic possibility of a different outcome (12). The appellant bore
the onus of demonstrating that the denial of procedural fairness was
material in this sense (13).
Applying these principles, the appellant discharged his onus of
demonstrating that the Tribunal’s denial of procedural fairness
deprived him of a realistic possibility of a different outcome. That
realistic possibility was demonstrable from the record of the Tribunal’s
decision. Contrary to the conclusion of the majority of the Full Court
of the Federal Court of Australia, the appellant was not required to
articulate a speciﬁc course of action which could realistically have
changed the result (14). It follows that the appeal must be allowed and
the matter remitted to the Tribunal for determination according to law.

Content and proof of materiality of a denial of procedural
fairness
In Hossain v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection (51), a
majority comprising Kiefel CJ, Gageler and Keane JJ enunciated a
common law principle of statutory interpretation. That principle is that
a statute conferring decision-making authority is “ordinarily to be
interpreted as incorporating a threshold of materiality in the event of
non-compliance” (52). It is well recognised that, generally speaking,
legislation should be construed to discourage unnecessary litigation, to
reduce wasting time and cost and to preserve the dignity of the
law (53). And, in particular, in relation to the Act, this Court has
declined to attribute to the legislation the impractical intention that an
error in process, which cannot have affected the outcome of the
process, requires that the process be repeated (54).
In MZAPC, a majority comprising Kiefel CJ, Gageler, Keane and
Gleeson JJ explained the evolution of the contemporary understanding
of jurisdictional error that supported that principle of interpreta-
tion (55). Their Honours further explained (56):
“The principle accommodates determination of the limits of

denied an opportunity to present evidence or make submissions on an

conjecture” is undemanding. It recognises that a fundamental purpose

GAGELER J. In Minister for Immigration and Border Protection v
WZARH (66) a denial of procedural fairness was found to have been
established by a failure to afford an applicant for a refugee status
assessment a reasonable opportunity to be heard in light of a signiﬁcant
change in the decision-making procedure of which he was not
informed. Noting that denials of procedural fairness can take different
forms in different contexts, Gordon J and I said (67):
“There are cases in which conduct on the part of an administrator
in the course of a hearing can be demonstrated to have misled a
person into refraining from taking up an opportunity to be heard that
was available to that person in accordance with an applicable
procedure which was otherwise fair. To demonstrate that the person
would have taken some step if that conduct had not occurred is, in
such a case, part of establishing that the person has in fact been
denied a reasonable opportunity to be heard.
Where, however, the procedure adopted by an administrator can
be shown itself to have failed to afford a fair opportunity to be
heard, a denial of procedural fairness is established by nothing more
than that failure, and the granting of curial relief is justiﬁed unless it
can be shown that the failure did not deprive the person of the
possibility of a successful outcome. The practical injustice in such a
case lies in the denial of an opportunity which in fairness ought to
have been given.”

Procedural fairness and natural justice
Procedural fairness is based upon natural justice. One of the innate,
or natural, elements of justice is that a person should have a reasonable
opportunity to respond to adverse allegations. This basic requirement is
so fundamental, and is such a strong expectation of a reasonable person
to whom the relevant legislation applies, that it has repeatedly been
held that the implication of procedural fairness in an administrative
hearing can only be removed by Parliament using “plain words of

intendment” (131).

The legal rules concerning materiality, particularly the application of
