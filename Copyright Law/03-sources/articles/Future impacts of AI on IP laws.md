The future

"Machine learning allows us to build software solutions that exceed human understanding and shows us how AI can 
innervate every industry”, according to Steve Jurvetson, Board Member of SpaceX and Tesla.

Artificial Intelligence (AI) technology is developing rapidly and will have significant impact on many businesses. AI 
technology will impact the law in three ways:

•
existing laws will change to take into account this fundamentally new technology;

•
new laws will be introduced to regulate AI; and

•
the practice of law will change, with lawyers using AI tools to provide efficient and accurate legal services 
to clients.

Intellectual Property (IP) law is also impacted by AI, with lawmakers, judges and academics considering scenarios 
where AI is creating and inventing. IP law is developing, but perhaps not as rapidly as AI technology is advancing.

Historically, when dealing with new, fast-developing technology, the law developed slowly and often in hindsight as 
it grappled to keep up. See Swinson, J’s Artificial Artistry: The Legal Implications Of AI-Generated Art (or Legally, Is 
AI Art Really Art?) 
Art and Australia, 58, no 2, 2023.

There are policy proposals being considered by government in Australia and elsewhere. Outside of Australia, there 
are many pending court cases that are likely to have impact on Australian law.

AI policy

In Australia on 5 December 2023, the Federal Attorney-General announced 
 the establishment of a copyright and 
AI reference group (referred to as the Copyright and Artificial Intelligence Reference Group (CAIRG)) “to better 
prepare for future copyright challenges emerging from AI”.

The announcement of the reference group stated, “AI gives rise to a number of important copyright issues, including 
the material used to train AI models, transparency of inputs and outputs, the use of AI to create imitative works, and 
whether and when AI-generated works should receive copyright protection”.

CAIRG is said to be a standing mechanism for ongoing engagement with stakeholders across a wide range of 
sectors, including the creative, media and technology sectors, to consider AI and IP issues in a careful and 
consultative way.

CAIRG met in October 2025 
 to discuss three priority areas that the government asked CAIRG to explore:

•
encouraging fair, legal avenues for using copyright material in AI through examination of licensing 
arrangements;

•
improving certainty on the application of copyright law to material generated through the use of AI; and

•
exploring avenues for less costly enforcement in relation to AI outputs-related infringement, including 
through a potential small claims forum.

That Department also issued Guidance for AI Adoption 
 on 21 October 2025.  One recommendation when 
adopting AI is to understand “Training data sources and compliance details with relevant privacy, intellectual 
property and copyright laws”.

In November 2025, the Digital Transformation Agency 
 released an AI Plan for the Australian Public Service 2025 
. One of the aims is to provide a platform for making intellectual property discoverable and reusable across the 
public service, to reduce duplication, reduce costs, and accelerate knowledge sharing. However, the AI Plan has 
little detail on intellectual property issues and AI.

The Australian Government’s National AI Plan 
 released on 2 December 2025 confirms that Australia will take a 
light touch approach to regulating AI (relying on existing laws rather than introducing a specific AI Act or mandatory 
guardrails).

The Attorney-General’s Department also considered AI and automated decision making in respect of privacy law 
issues. See Privacy Act Review 2023, Ch 19 
. The Privacy Act was amended in November 2024 by the Privacy 
and Other Legislation Amendment Bill 2024 
, passed on 29 November 2024, because of this review. The 
amendments are said to be the first tranche of amendments. The amendments in December 2024 did not deal with 
AI.

The Australian Senate conducted an inquiry into the opportunities and impact for Australia arising out of the update 
of AI technologies 
. The Senate established the Senate Select Committee on Adopting Artificial Intelligence in 
March 2024 and six hearings were conducted in Canberra and Sydney, with the last hearing taking place in July 
2024. Over 240 public submissions were made to the Select Committee. The Select Committee tabled its Report 
 
in November 2024. The Report made 13 recommendations including that dedicated legislation be introduced to 
regulate high-risk AI. Three of the recommendations related to IP:

•
Recommendation 8: “That the Australian Government continue to consult with creative workers, 
rightsholders and their representative organisations through the CAIRG on appropriate solutions to the 
unprecedented theft of their work by multinational tech companies operating within Australia”.

•
Recommendation 9: “That the Australian Government require the developers of AI products to be 
transparent about the use of copyrighted works in their training datasets, and that the use of such works is 
appropriately licenced and paid for”.

•
Recommendation 10: “That the Australian Government urgently undertake further consultation with the 
creative industry to consider an appropriate mechanism to ensure fair remuneration is paid to creators for 
commercial AI-generated outputs based on copyrighted material used to train AI systems”.

The Australian Government’s Digital Transformation Agency introduced a mandatory policy, “Policy for the 
responsible use of AI in government” 
 regarding use of AI in federal government in September 2024 and released 
version 2 
 effective on 15 December 2025. The original policy required that agencies conduct a risk assessment 
for the use of AI. One of the risks that was to be considered is that the use of AI “results in intellectual property 
concerns due to the system manipulating, transforming or reproducing material for which a third party owns 
copyright.” However, version 2 of the Policy is more general and does not specifically refer to intellectual property. 
Also see the Agency’s AI transparency statement 
.

It is becoming common for contracts for the procurement of IT systems, SaaS services or software licenses to 
include an AI clause that deal with the IP issues relating to the use of AI. For example, the clause may prevent the 
use of customer data to train AI systems.

Other countries are also conducting reviews relating to AI and IP. See Guidance Note: International developments 
in IP laws and AI.

There is potential conflict between various policy settings. For example, the law of confidential information is 
designed to protect business trade secrets. How will this be impacted if AI laws require disclosure of how 
technology works to prove safety and suitability for use?

Australia is a signatory 
 to the Statement on Inclusive and Sustainable Artificial Intelligence for People and the 
Planet.

On 4 February 2025, the Attorney-General’s Department published, in respect of Copyright and AI, a summary of 
CAIRG responses to its transparency discussion paper. Transparency requirements, in relation to copyright, 
includes requirements that data used to train, fine-tune or test a model to be legally obtained, and for data sources 
to be disclosed. See Copyright and AI – Summary of CAIRG responses to transparency discussion paper and

Uncertainty

Will the law develop swiftly (by legislation) or slowly (via case law)? Some British academics recently opined that 
“machines have no legal personality in the US, EU and UK and cannot therefore be held responsible for 
infringement. While programmers/developers and end users could face liability where the AI infringes on another’s 
work, the question of who should be liable where the machine carries out tasks with a significant degree of 
autonomy (and alters its programming to such an extent that neither party can appreciate the risk) has not been 
fully explored yet and may inevitably require a case-by-case 
assessment 
.

At a high level, the questions and issues include:

•
How do the legal rules apply to AI developed works?

•
What are the relevant facts? How does AI really work?

•
What is the level of human creativity and effort when it appears that AI is doing some or all of the creating?

•
Who are the creators?

•
Who should be responsible for the creations of AI, eg for the purposes of determining who is a tortfeasor?

•
Does AI impact the rationale for IP law? IP law creates incentives to create and allows creators and 
inventors to reap the commercial benefits from their creative endeavours, and to enhance their reputation. 
Do these rationales apply where AI is the creator or inventor?

•
Who should share in the economic rewards from AI and AI creations?

•
Can AI create prior art that prevents a human inventor being awarded a patent?

•
Should an AI machine be given a legal persona and be able to own and control its creations?

•
Do we need an IP system at all when AI is doing the creating?

The answers to these questions may be different depending on what is being created and how AI is being use. For 
example, should the legal rules be the same for the creation of a work of art, the invention of a new drug, and a 
conversation with a machine that provides legal advice?

Consider the following three examples:

•
Several teams of scientists are developing AI technology to “read” your mind when you sleep and create a 
movie that is a reconstruction of your dreams. Dreams are truly imaginative and creative. Who should own

the copyright in the movie of your dreams? What if you dream about the Star Wars movies — is the movie 
of your dreams an IP infringement?

•
AI is very successful in image recognition applications. For example, AI can very successfully identify all 
objects in a photograph of a living room. Assume that the photograph is a famous copyright work. Is it 
copyright infringement to use AI to identify the objects in the photograph? Does the answer change if the AI 
technology is being used to help a blind person “view” the photograph?

•
AI is also enabling new forms of expression. An example is a deep fake video, often in the form of a 
mockumentary or comedy. A video work can be created that makes it appear that King Charles and Luke 
Skywalker are doing the most ridiculous things together, and that looks real. This could be art, or political 
commentary, or both. Is this an IP infringement or some other legal wrong?

There are also uncertainties regarding specific areas of IP law. For example:

•
Whether an AI system can be a co-inventor of a patented invention or can be used to assist creating a 
patentable invention.

•
Whether an AI system used by a human can create copyright protected works.

•
Who should be the owner of IP where an AI system is used to create the IP?

•
Can a business protect a logo developed by an AI machine for the business — as a registered trade mark 
or otherwise?

•
How to best protect the IP in an AI system.

•
If IP developers keep their technology secret, how do we learn and verify what is happening?

Predictions

It will happen faster than can be imagined. What is imagined will become real. Futurists have many similar 
predictions regarding AI.

There is often a debate whether the future will look like “The Terminator” or “The Jetsons”.

Here are four predictions regarding IP and AI:

•
IP law will change to deal with AI technology. This will likely include laws relating to protection, ownership 
and infringement.

•
Different countries will take different approaches at least in the short term, leading to a fragmentation of IP 
law in this area. However, as Australian barrister Dr Rothnie states: “Apart from the uncertainties about 
how our law will deal with these issues, it seems clear that careful consideration of how things are 
developing overseas is required and … development of uniform approaches”. See New Copyright and AI 
reference group 
. It is unlikely that such a uniform approach will develop in the short term. See Guidance 
Note: International developments in IP laws and AI.

•
The United States and Europe are heading in different directions. The United States is currently focusing 
on AI as a trade and economic dominance issue. The United States wants to use AI to improve the 
efficiency of government. Moreover, the United States is considering federal laws to discourage its States 
from regulating AI. Europe is more concerned about regulating AI so that it is safe and used responsibility. 
Australia appears more aligned with Europe, although it is early days to make a call on this.

•
There will be policy developments to enable the efficient use or licensing of existing works to train AI 
systems that take into account the interest of owners of existing copyright rights.

•
IP law will come to recognise a human-AI partnership in the creation of AI-generated works and inventions. 
One or more humans may contribute intellectual creativity in conjunction with the AI system. The human 
could provide detailed instructions to the AI system or could make modifications to what the AI system 
creates. This could be a recursive creative process, with various rounds of interaction and improvement, 
involving joint work between the AI system and the human. In this case, the law may develop to identify 
and recognise the human’s intellectual creativity. A motion picture (cinematograph film) is created by a

•

example, there is already competition between consumer-facing generative AI systems such as ChatGPT

general search services (September 2024) 
. Issues have been raised regarding the application of unfair 
contract terms to the terms that AI companies use in respect of use of copyright works.  See Treasury’s 
Review of AI and the Australian Consumer Law 
 Final report, November 2025.
