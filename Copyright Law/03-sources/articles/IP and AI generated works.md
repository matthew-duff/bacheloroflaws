Artificial Intelligence

Artificial intelligence (AI) is a branch of computer science concerned with creating computer systems and smart 
machines capable of performing tasks that require human-like intelligence.

AI is different to “traditional” computer programming. In a “traditional” computer program, all the possible outcomes 
are determined and written into the computer program. A typical program structure is “if X happens, then do Option 
1”. If something happens that the computer does not expect, then the program fails or crashes. A “traditional” 
computer program can mimic human decision-making and give the impression of being human but is not making 
independent intelligent decisions.

In contrast, an AI system is designed to learn, and to produce outputs even where the input is unexpected. For 
example, for an AI system that is designed to have a conversation, the AI system is not just mimicking human 
conversation, but simulates understanding through statical pattern recognition (rather than cognitive 
comprehension) and responds in a way that is indistinguishable from a human.

AI is a broad field. There are many different applications for AI, including:

•
healthcare — eg, diagnosis of diseases or developing new drugs;

•
business and finance — eg, making investment decisions or detecting fraud;

•
transport — eg, self-driving cars or optimising traffic flows;

•
customer service — eg, answering customer queries or recommending suitable products; and

•
legal — eg, undertaking legal reviews of cases or preparing due diligence reports.

Generative AI

A sub-field of AI is generative AI. A well-known example of generative AI is ChatGPT.

Generative AI focuses on creating new content, rather than simply analysing or classifying existing data. For 
example, generative AI can create a new work of art, write a report or a song, create a presentation or develop a 
new drug. ChatGPT generates responses to prompts and can chat with humans.

Generative AI is a subset of AI. ChatGPT is a subset of generative AI. There are many more types and uses of AI 
than ChatGPT. ChatGPT is just one example of how AI is used.

Generative AI can learn. One type of learning is called machine learning. This type of AI uses algorithms to learn 
from data without being explicitly programmed. For example, a machine learning algorithm can be trained on a set 
of contracts, and then be able to review or draft new contracts.

A good way to understand machine learning is to think about how young humans learn. If someone has never 
played Monopoly, but watch 50 games of Monopoly being played, they will learn the rules of the game without ever 
having to read the rule book or ask someone how to play. If a person watches 5,000 games of Monopoly, they will 
likely become a champion player. An AI machine has the time and patience to watch 500,000 games (provided it 
can get access to such data) and will then likely become the world expert Monopoly player.

In short, generative AI models are designed to learn patterns and characteristics from large datasets and use this 
knowledge to create new and original data that is similar to the training data.

Although AI has been a field of computer science for over 40 years, there have been significant advances in the 
past 5 years. In 2017, a paper called Attention Is All You Need 
 introduced something called a Transformer 
architecture. This is the underlying technology for ChatGPT and many other new AI systems. So much of what we 
are seeing today in relation to AI is very new and in early stages of development.

It is hard to make predictions as to what will happen next in AI, but the field is developing rapidly, and future AI 
systems are likely to be a significant advance on what is happening today.

Practice Tip: Thinking about AI.

Don’t think that ChatGPT is everything that AI can do. It is just one example. And it is very new. AI can do much 
more than ChatGPT now and will be able to do dramatically more in the future.

Copyright protection and AI

One of the most debated and litigated topics relating to AI is the impact and effect of copyright law on AI.

The below considers copyright protection and AI.

The Guidance Note: Copyright infringement and AI considers issues of copyright infringement.

Copyright subsistence in AI generated works

Generative AI can be used to generate works that are the kinds of works that are protectable by copyright. 
Examples include art, music, poems, plays, novels, meeting notes, instruction manuals and legal contracts.

If a work is created by AI, is it protected by copyright?

Many works are created by humans using computer technology (that may or may not involve AI). For example:

•
a digital photograph taken using a smart phone;

•
computer-generated imagery special effects in a movie;

•
music that is played on an electronic keyboard;

•
a house plan created using a computer-aided design system; and

•
a graphic design created using a computer drawing and design application.

Unlike in the UK, the Australian Copyright Act 1968 (Cth) (Copyright Act) does not have special provisions for 
computer-generated works. This issue has been considered by law reform bodies in Australia, but no changes to 
the law have been made to address computer-generated works.

If a work is solely created by an AI system, without any human intellectual effort, it is unlikely to be protected by 
copyright in Australia. Although there are no cases directly on point, cases such as IceTV Pty Ltd v Nine Network 
Australia Pty Ltd and Telstra Corp Ltd v Phone Directories Co Pty Ltd support this view. 
References: IceTV Pty Ltd v Nine Network Australia Pty Ltd (2009) 80 IPR 451;  [2009] HCA 14; BC200902942
Telstra Corp Ltd v Phone Directories Co Pty Ltd (2010) 194 FCR 142;  273 ALR 725;  90 IPR 1;  [2010] FCAFC 149

There are also hints in the Copyright Act that an author must be a person, not a computer. For example, the

definition of “author” in relation to a photograph “means the person who took the photograph”. As another example, 
for an unpublished work, s 32(1) refers to the “author” as a “qualified person”.

However, what is less clear is whether a human working with an AI system as a tool could be regarded as the 
author for copyright purposes. For example, it is not controversial that this guidance note is protected by copyright, 
even though the author wrote it using a computer program (a word processor) and used a computerised spell-
checker to correct mistakes. If a human’s intellectual effort is significant in the creation of the work, then the human 
may be the author for copyright purposes.

This view can find support in Data Access Corp v Powerflex Services Pty Ltd, where computer-generated data was 
protected because of the skill and judgment of a human author in the creation process.
References: Data Access Corp v Powerflex Services Pty Ltd (1999) 45 IPR 353

Practice Tip: Document the human’s effort.

If trying to obtain copyright protection in a work created using AI, document the human’s intellectual effort, skill and 
judgment in working with the AI system to create the work.

If copyright law develops further in this direction, it will be important to ascertain which human’s are most involved in 
the intellectual effort, skill and judgment in creating the work. For example, consider the effort of the following 
people:

•
the people who created the model;

•
the people who specified the data used to train the model;

•
the people who created the training data;

•
the people who developed the AI application; and

•
the people who worked with the AI application to create the work.

One or more of these people may be considered as an author or joint author of the output of the AI application.

If there is minimal or no human involvement in creation of the work, or if the human involvement in the creation of 
the work is merely administrative or mechanical rather than creative, the law in Australia does not support copyright 
protection for the work.

Thus, a photograph taken by a monkey is unlikely to be protected by copyright in Australia, but a photograph taken 
by a monkey that has been trained for years by a human to take photographs may possibly be protected by 
copyright.

Over time, a distinction may develop between AI generated works and AI assisted works.

Originality and authorship

The requirements of originality and authorship overlap with the discussion above.

In copyright law, a work must originate with an author. The requirement of originality is not that the work be novel or 
new, but that it must be created by the author. If the author merely copies another’s work, then there is no 
originality.

One issue is that in an infringement lawsuit, the person claiming copyright ownership must prove originality. If the 
work is created by an AI system, how can this be proven if we have no idea how the AI system created the work? 
For example, if the AI system is asked to write a speech about D-Day, one does not know whether the AI system 
simply found a great speech on the internet and simply copied it (ie this is not original) or if the AI system has learnt 
from thousands of speeches and hundreds of books about D-Day and created an original speech, or somewhere in-
between. This lack of transparency makes proof of originality somewhat problematic under law.

Moreover, as stated above, under the law in Australia, the work must originate from an author who is a human.

The requirement of “authorship” may preclude copyright protection to a work that is deserving of protection, simply 
because the extent to which a computer or AI was utilised in its creation exceeds a particular (uncertain) level and 
how the work was created is not transparent.

Ownership

Copyright is personal property.

Under Australian copyright law, only a legal person can own copyright. An AI system cannot own copyright.

If there is copyright ownership in an AI generated work, and the AI system is not the owner, the question is who is 
or should be the copyright owner. As discussed above, there could be more than one owner. Generally, it is the 
person or persons who are most connected to the intellectual effort in the creation of the work who should be the 
owner or owners of the copyright in the work.

When using a commercial AI system, one should check to see if the Terms of Use or other contract governing use 
of the AI system deals with copyright ownership or copyright licensing, as this may impact who owns the work 
generated by the AI system.

Berne Convention

The Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works 
 (Berne Convention) is a convention that 
assists in protection of copyright works outside of the country whether the author who created the work resides.

Australia is a party to the Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works of September 9, 1886 
(Berne Convention). For more information on the Berne Convention, see the Guidance Note: Copyright treaties. 
The Berne Convention does not define the ‘‘author’’ of a work, leaving this to the contracting parties, but its text and 
historical context strongly suggest that ‘‘author’’ and ‘‘authorship’’ for the purposes of the Berne Convention refer to 
the natural person who created the work.

For example, Art 7 of the Berne Convention prescribes the length of copyright protection granted to an “author” to 
be 50 years after “his” death. This strongly suggests that the Berne Convention only applies to works created by 
humans.

Copyright subsistence in AI systems

The AI system itself is a work (or is comprised of more than one work) and so can be protected by copyright.

For example, if the computer code for an AI system has been written by a human author, that computer code is 
clearly protected by copyright in Australia.

Copyright law protects the code, but not the functionality of the code. The same output can be produced by radically 
different code, but that does not mean that there is an infringement of the code.

Where an AI system has been trained on data, and the creator of the AI system spent time and effort selecting the 
appropriate data to use, the dataset may be protected by copyright as a compilation. However, there must be 
independent intellectual effort in selecting the data — if the creator of the AI system merely selects all the data 
available on Wikipedia as a training set, that is probably not sufficient to obtain compilation copyright rights. But if 
one is training an AI system to draft contracts, and the person training the system sorts through thousands of 
contracts to select the best contract examples, that selection is likely to be protected as a compilation copyright 
owned by the person doing the selection.

Where humans help train the AI system, eg by correcting errors, selecting the best answer or ranking output, then 
that human input may assist in showing that human effort is used in creating the AI system and thus copyright 
protection is warranted. The creator of the AI system should ensure contracts are obtained from each such trainer 
to transfer any copyright rights to the creator of the system, otherwise the copyright may be jointly owned by a 
range of authors.

Patents and AI

Patent law protects new inventions. To obtain patent protection, a patent application must be prepared describing 
the invention, filed, and examined and approved by government patent examiner.

The following issues arise regarding patent law and AI:

•
Can an AI system be an inventor under patent law?

•
Can an AI system that creates an invention own a patent for that invention?

•
Can something that is created by an AI system be used in commerce without infringing an existing patent?

•
Can an AI system be protected by patent?

In Australia, the law is clear on these points.

An AI system cannot be an inventor of a patentable invention in Australia.

In 2022, a five-member Full Federal Court decided that an AI system cannot be named as an inventor on a patent 
application. See Commissioner of Patents v Thaler (Thaler).
References: Commissioner of Patents v Thaler (2022) 166 IPR 215

The Thaler case concerned a patent application for a “Food container and devices and methods for attracting 
enhanced attention”. Dr Thaler nominated an AI system, called DABUS, as the inventor of the patent application 
and asserted that Dr Thaler was not an inventor.

In a unanimous decision, the Full Federal Court decided that an “inventor” must be a “natural person”. This decision 
was reached due to the historical centrality of the role of a human inventor in patent applications and a reading of 
various provisions of the Patents Act 1990 (Cth).

The High Court did not grant special leave on the basis that this case was not an appropriate vehicle to consider 
this case. Potentially, if a human and an AI system worked together, and a human was a co-inventor, the result may 
be different. But that issue was not before the courts in the Thaler case.

Applying similar logic, an AI system that creates an invention cannot own a patent for that invention in Australia. 
Only legal persons can own patents, and an AI system is not a legal person.

Many policy makers believe that the patent system was designed to incentivise human invention and that patent 
protection should not be granted to AI-generated inventions, at least at this stage.

If an AI system creates something, such as a product, a system, an algorithm or a formula, then the person or 
business using the AI system and wanting to exploit what is created must take care. The creation could infringe an 
existing patent. It appears that generative AI systems do not take into account Australian patents when generating, 
and so could create something that infringes an existing Australian patent.

One can infringe a patent even if unaware of the patent. Knowledge and intent are not elements necessary to 
succeed in a patent infringement claim. A generative AI system, even if not trained using existing patents, could 
generate output that, if used, is a patent infringement. That is, an “independent creation” by a generative AI system 
could infringe a patent.

Practice Tip: Avoid patent infringement by searching the Patents Register, AusPat 
.

If an AI system generates a product, method or system that needs to be used or exploited, it is wise to search 
AusPat 
 to make sure that it does not infringe an existing patent.

An AI system is a sophisticated technology system. If a person creates a new AI system, can it be 
protected by patent?

Trade marks and AI

Generative AI systems are being used by businesses to generate new logos, slogans, product names and business 
names. These are all examples of trade marks.

For example, a typical AI system that generates trade marks will ask the user for information about the type of 
product or service and characteristics of the business (eg friendly, authoritative, trustworthy) and styles preferred 
(trendy, classic, childish, elegant, retro). The AI system will then generate a collection of brand names and logos. 
The business can then select the preferred brand names and logos, and have the system undertake further 
refinements based on user feedback.

Legal issues that may arise from the use of such AI systems include:

•
Does the brand or logo infringe another’s trade mark?

•
Could the brand or logo infringe another’s copyright?

•
Can the brand or logo be protected?

The Australian trade mark system is a system of registration. Trade mark protection is obtained by filing a trade 
mark application, having it successfully examined, and then registered on the Trade Marks Register.

Trade mark infringement

A business can develop a new trade mark, but if someone already has registered the same or similar mark for the 
same or similar services, then the business cannot register its new trade mark and is likely to infringe the existing 
trade mark registration if the business uses its new trade mark. It is not a defence to trade mark infringement to 
claim independent creation of the trade mark.

If a business uses an AI system to generate a brand or logo for the business, that brand or logo may be the same 
or similar to an existing trade mark on the Australian Trade Marks Register. If so, that is a significant problem. Use 
of the AI generate brand or logo could be trade mark infringement, even if the AI system was unaware of the 
existing registered trade mark.

Practice Tip: Avoid trade mark infringement by searching the Trade Marks Register 
.

If using an AI brand or logo generating system, conduct a trade mark clearance search on at least the Australian

Generative AI are likely to present novel trade mark infringement claims. For example, in the United States, 
publishers such as The Wall Street Journal and The New York Times have brought lawsuits against AI developers 
alleging trade mark infringement, false designation of origin, and trade mark dilution. The case alleges that AI 
outputs mislead consumers when they reproduce their trade marks, particularly when they do so alongside AI 
hallucinations.

For example, in 2024, the owners of The Wall Street Journal and The New York Post sued AI developer Perplexity 
for trade mark dilution as well as copyright infringement. The claim was that Perplexity generated hallucinated text 
and attributed it these newspapers using their trade marks, violating their trade mark rights. The court denied 
Perplexity’s motion to dismiss in its entirety. See Dow Jones & Co., Inc. v. Perplexity AI, Inc., 797 F. Supp. 3d 305, 
315 (S.D.N.Y. 2025) 
. This is an interlocutory decision only, and a jury trial is likely in 2026.

See also Advance Local Media LLC, et al. v. Cohere, Inc., 2025 WL 3171892, No. 25-CV-01305 (CM) (S.D.N.Y. 
Nov. 13, 2025) 
.

The UK High Court’s ruling in Getty Images v Stability AI [2025] EWHC 2863 (Ch) 
, delivered on 4 November 
2025, found trade mark infringement and is considered in the Guidance Note: International developments in IP laws 
and AI.

These decisions serve as a warning to both AI developers and users of generative AI outputs to carefully evaluate 
whether generative AI outputs may be infringing others’ trade marks.

Practice Tip: Take care when using AI to generate advertising.

The increasing use of AI by businesses is likely to draft increasing scrutiny as courts and regulators scrutinise AI-
generated outputs for trade mark and false advertising issues.

Copyright infringement of a trade mark

Consideration must also be given to whether a brand, slogan or logo that is generated by an AI system infringes 
another’s copyright.

Most likely, a brand or slogan will not infringe copyright because brands and slogans are not typically protected by 
copyright. See Exxon Corp v Exxon Insurance Consultants International Ltd and Fairfax Media Publications Pty Ltd 
v Reed International Books Australia Pty Ltd.
References: Exxon Corp v Exxon Insurance Consultants International Ltd [1981] 3 All ER 241
Fairfax Media Publications Pty Ltd v Reed International Books Australia Pty Ltd (2010) 88 IPR 11

However, a logo can be protected by copyright. See AGL Energy Ltd v Greenpeace Australia Pacific Ltd. 
References: AGL Energy Ltd v Greenpeace Australia Pacific Ltd (2021) 159 IPR 336

If an AI system generates a logo for a business, and copies or adapts another’s logo or artwork in doing so, then 
the output potentially could be copyright infringement of that logo or artwork.

In contrast, if an AI system generates a logo for a business, and the AI system has no access to or use of the logo 
or artwork in question, then the output is unlikely to be copyright infringement of that logo or artwork. This is 
because copyright, unlikely trade mark, only protects copying. An independent creation without access to or use of

Trade mark protection

Broadly, s 27 of the Trade Marks Act 1995 (Cth) (Trade Marks Act) states that a person may apply for a trade mark 
if the person claims to be the owner of the trade mark and the person intends to use the trade mark, authorise 
another to person to use the trade mark or the person intends to assign the trade mark to a corporation about to be 
formed. Section 58 also states that the registration of a trade mark may be opposed on the ground that the 
applicant is not the owner of the trade mark.

There is no explicit requirement that the person applying for the trade mark created or authored the trade mark. The 
focus of s 27 is use, not creation or authorship. In fact, it is common for a business to engage an advertising agent 
or brand consultant to develop branding and trade marks for the business. Accordingly, it should not matter if the 
business uses an AI generator rather than an advertising agent to create a brand or logo for the business.

The waters were muddied by Pham Global Pty Ltd v Insight Clinical Imaging Pty Ltd, which also appears to require 
“authorship” of the trade mark, without really explaining “authorship”. Although it is uncertain, there does not appear 
to be a requirement in Australian trade mark law that “authorship” be authorship of a person.
References: Pham Global Pty Ltd v Insight Clinical Imaging Pty Ltd (2017) 141 IPR 1

It is likely however, that the applicant for a registered trade mark must be a legal person, and not an AI machine or 
program. The Trade Marks Act defines “Applicant” as a “person” and defines a “person” to be a body of persons, 
whether incorporated or not. The Trade Marks Act also defines “registered owner” as being a “person”.

Designs and AI

Designs in Australia are protected by the Designs Act 2003 (Cth).

A design right protects the overall visual appearance of new and distinctive products. To be protected, a design 
must be registered.

Section 13 of the Designs Act sets out who is entitled to be registered as the registered owner of a design. The 
section list various options, but all require that it is a “person” who is entitled to be the owner of the registered 
design. The Act also states that “the person who created the design” is the “designer”.

Thus, it is likely that a design that is created by an AI system cannot be registered as a design in Australia.

However, there may be an argument for protection if a human uses an AI system as a tool to assist in creating or 
improving a design. If so, the human would have control over the creative design process.

An AI system cannot own a registered design in Australia.

As stated above, to obtain design protection, the design must be new and distinctive. What will happen to the 
design system if an AI system creates 1 million designs of chairs and automatically publishes all the designs? It 
may be difficult for a human designer to obtain design protection of a human created design because the human’s 
design may not be new and distinctive in light of the published AI-generated designs.

As with other forms of IP discussed in this guidance note, there is a risk that a design created by an AI system, if 
used, will infringe an existing registered design. This could be the case even if the AI machine was not aware of and 
did not use the existing registered design in its training dataset. Accordingly, there is risk in using AI generated 
designs without first undertaking a design register clearance search, starting at the Australian Designs Register 
.

Trade secrets, confidentiality agreements and contracts

This section will consider the protection of AI algorithms as trade secrets and the commercialisation of AI 
technology.

Trade secret law is often called the law of confidential information. This section discusses these topics at a high 
level and does not distinguish between them.

Provided that the information is not already publicly known and can be kept secret, then trade secret protection is 
often a desirable form of protection. Trade secret protection is low cost and can last a long time — for as long as 
the information is kept confidential.

Trade secret law can protect many aspects of AI technology, such as raw data, training sets and computer code. 
See, eg, a case under NY law involving the protection of algorithms for a “smart chat” platform: LivePerson, Inc v 
24/7 Customer, Inc.
References: LivePerson, Inc v 24/7 Customer, Inc, 83 F. Supp. 3d 50 (SDNY 2015)

Many AI systems are only provided via a subscription service, where the AI developer maintains and controls the 
system, and a user has access to use features of the AI system for a usage fee. This assists the AI developer 
maintain control and secrecy over the AI technology. However, this raises questions from users as to how the AI 
system operates and whether it is reliable and non-discriminatory.

As laws develop regarding requirements for AI to be trustworthy and transparent, such as the European Union’s 
Artificial Intelligence Act 
 (AIA), it may be harder for AI developers to keep all aspects of an AI confidential. Thus, 
trade secret protection may have less application as AI laws come into force. It is noted that the AIA has provisions 
that try to maintain trade secret protection. For example, Art 70 requires those implementing the law to “put 
appropriate technical and organisational measures in place to ensure the confidentiality of information and data 
obtained in carrying out their tasks and activities in such a manner as to protect … confidential business information 
or trade secrets of a natural or legal person…”.

There is also a risk to AI developers where laws require disclosure, eg in relation to government decision-making or 
litigation discovery. This may require the AI developer to disclose their trade secrets to regulators or third parties 
who were impacted by the AI decision. For example, litigants challenging a government’s application of AI 
algorithms might be able to gain access to information about those algorithms, even when they are developed and 
deployed by a private business who claims trade secret protection. Compare Royal Brush Manufacturing, Inc v 
United States, Dixon Ticonderoga Company.
References: Royal Brush Manufacturing, Inc v United States, Dixon Ticonderoga Company, No. 22-1226 (Fed. 
Cir., 27 July 2023)

Trade secret law can also protect the output of an AI system, provided that the output is kept confidential. For 
example, AI systems can develop computer code or scientific formulas that are traditionally the subject matter of 
trade secret protection. However, if you are merely the user of the AI system rather than the owner of the AI 
system, then other users could (in theory) also use the AI system to develop the same output that you developed 
and publish that output, thus extinguishing your trade secret.

There also are confidentiality concerns regarding the use of AI systems. The input of a user and the output to a user 
may be used by the operator of the AI system to further improve the AI system, to create output for other users or to 
otherwise commercialise information that is input or output. It is important to review the terms of the agreement for 
use of the AI system to ensure that the user’s confidentiality is respected, and if not, then be aware and not input 
confidential information into the AI system.
