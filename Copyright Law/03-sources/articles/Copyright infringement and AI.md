Copyright infringement

In general terms, copyright infringement occurs where a person, without licence, does any of the acts that are 
comprised in the copyright owner’s exclusive rights. These exclusive rights depend on the type of copyright material 
in issue, but include reproduction, communication to the public, publication and authorisation of those activities.

For example, the owner of a copyright work has the exclusive rights to reproduce the work, publish the work and 
communicate the work to the public. In addition, for literary, dramatic or musical works, the copyright owner has the 
exclusive right to make an adaptation of the work and perform the work in public: s 31 of the Copyright Act 1968 
(Cth) (Copyright Act).

The owner of copyright in a sound recording has the exclusive right to make a copy of the sound recording, cause 
the sound recording to be heard in public, communicate the sound recording to the public and enter into a 
commercial rental agreement: s 85 of the Copyright Act.

For further details, see the Guidance Note: Key elements of copyright infringement.

However, there are also defences and exceptions to copyright infringement (such as fair dealing for the purpose of 
research or study) which in certain circumstances may mean that a particular action is not copyright infringement.

Copyright infringement and training an AI system

When training an Artificial Intelligence (AI) system, there will be better outputs if there are more inputs. The more 
high quality, relevant and up-to-date training data that is obtained, the better the AI outputs.

This training data is an input into the AI system and the system learns from this training data to produce the outputs 
to users.

Typically, the training data is collected and then copied into the AI system to train it.

This raises issues of potential copyright infringement. Because an exclusive right of the copyright owner is the right 
to reproduce the work (or a substantial part of the work) and to make an adaption of the work, then it is likely that 
copying the training data into the AI system to train it is a copyright infringement.

The copying of each work used as part of the training data is to be considered separately, and each time a copy is 
made that is a separate act of infringement. Thus, if 3 million songs are copied into the AI system to train it, that is 3 
million separate acts of copyright infringement.

It may not be copyright infringement of the work if:

•
Copyright in the work has expired (eg use of an old master painting from 1750).

•
The work is purely factual, such as a telephone directory.

•
Only an insubstantial part of the work is copied.

•
The work is in the public domain, eg certain US government works.

•
The person doing the copying owns the copyright in the works.

It appears that copying has been widespread to train AI systems.

For example, headlines from recent news articles state that “Firms like Meta and A16z admit having to pay billions 
for training data would ruin their generate-AI plans as they fight new copyright rules”.

OpenAI asserts that it does not copy the works that are used to train its ChatGPT LLM. They state (and noting this 
may be a simplification or a misleading statement):

ChatGPT does not copy or store training information in a database. Instead, it learns about associations between words 
and concepts, and those learnings help the model update its numbers/weights. The model then uses those weights to 
predict and generate new content in response to a user request. It does not “copy and paste” training information – much 
like a teacher who has learned from lots of prior study and can explain things because she has learned the relationships 
between concepts, but doesn’t store copies of the materials in her head.
The AI industry is relying on US doctrines such as the fair use defence (which does not apply in Australia) to justify 
such copying. This is a key undecided issue as to copyright infringement under US law. Part 3 of the United States 
Copyright Report on Copyright and Artificial Intelligence, titled Generative AI Training 
 was issued as a pre-
publication edition in May 2025. The report concludes:

In applying current law, we conclude that several stages in the development of generative AI involve using copyrighted 
works in ways that implicate the owners’ exclusive rights. The key question, as most commenters agreed, is whether those 
acts of prima facie infringement can be excused as fair use.
A final version has not been released. For more information, see our Latest Legal Update: US Copyright Office 
issues pre-publication version of part 3 Copyright & AI report, final version uncertain after director’s firing.

The Australian Attorney-General’s Department conducted a Ministerial Roundtable 
 on Copyright on 28 and 29 
August 2023 regarding copyright and AI, considering these issues, and released an Issues Summary Paper: 
Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Copyright 
 in September 2023.

In 2025, the Productivity Commission considered whether to introduce a Text and Data Mining (TDM) exception to 
the Australian Copyright Act. The Productivity Commission released an interim report on 5 August 2025 that 
proposed a TDM exception.

Text and data mining (TDM) involves the automated analysis of large volumes of text, images, audio, video, or 
datasets to identify patterns, trends, correlations, or other insights. Because TDM typically requires making copies 
of works (eg downloading, storing, or normalising content), it can otherwise infringe copyright unless an exception 
or licence applies.

Generally, a TDM exception permits certain acts of copying and analysis of copyrighted material for the purpose of 
computational analysis, without infringing copyright, provided specified conditions are met.

There was significant push-back 
 to the introduction of a TDM exception to Australian copyright law by Australian 
authors, creators and copyright lawyers.

In October 2025 
, the Australian Attorney-General definitively ruled out a TDM exception. The government 
confirmed that it will instead pursue a “licensing-first” approach to ensure creators are remunerated for the use of 
their works in AI training.

As a result, the Copyright and Artificial Intelligence Reference Group (CAIRG) met in October 2025 
 to discuss 
three priority areas that the government asked CAIRG to explore:

The Productivity Commission released its final report 
 on 19 December 2025.  It stated that it was difficult to 
design a policy response on the issues of AI and copyright for three reasons:

•
the scope and feasibility of AI-related copyright exceptions overseas;

•
the effect of AI training on incentives to create new Australian content; and

•
whether licensing markets for open web material will emerge without intervention.

As a result, the Productivity Commission recommended that it was premature to make wholesale changes to 
Australia’s copyright laws because of AI. Instead, it was recommended that the Australian Government monitor 
these uncertainties and the development of AI and its interaction with copyright holders over the next three years.

Similarly, the Australian Government’s National AI Plan 
 released on 2 December 2025 confirms that Australia 
will take a light touch approach to regulating AI (relying on existing laws rather than introducing a specific AI Act or 
mandatory guardrails).

Practice Tip: Don’t infringe when training an AI system.

If you are training an AI system using the works of others, there is a significant risk of copyright infringement. 
Ideally, obtain a licence from the copyright owners before using their works for training purposes.

Publishers and news organisations that own or control large portfolios of copyright works have granted licenses to

issues. These licensing deals started in 2024 with many being announced in 2025. The following are examples of

Copyright infringement and AI system output

When an AI system is trained on copyright works, it is probable that some of the output will reproduce or 
communicate a substantial part of a copyright work. For example, if a user asks an AI image generator to create an 
image of Batman eating a Big Mac, and if the AI system does so, this potentially is copyright infringement of 
copyright in the Batman drawings 
.

If the user then uses that image, the user is also likely infringing the Batman copyright.

Practice Tip: Don’t infringe when using the output of AI system.

Care should be taken when using works that are generated by AI, because doing so may be copyright infringement. 
It may be difficult to know if the work is an infringement or not. Read the user agreement from the AI system 
provider before using the output.

The issue of whether output generated by AI is a copyright infringement of one or more of the works used to train 
the AI model is both a difficult legal and technical question. Is an inexact statistical recreation of a work regarded as 
a copy of the work? One academic paper 
 on this topic states:
So, there can undoubtedly be some form of reproduction in outputs, but these tend to be inexact copies, and in the case of 
image models they can be dependent on how common they are in the training data, and would therefore be more about the 
analysis given to inputs. For the most part a system like ChatGPT is acting on a statistical likelihood of words following one 
another in a text, so it is not a verbatim copy of a work.
Whether there is infringement by communication to the public of the work that is output by the AI system also raises 
difficult issues as to the application of s 22(6) of the Copyright Act, which states that “a communication other than a 
broadcast is taken to have been made by the person responsible for determining the content of the 
communication”. This raises the question of who is responsible for determining the content of the communication. Is 
it the AI technology owner or the user who inputs the prompt or question into the AI technology?

It may also be difficult to prove copying. For example, an AI summary of a book may have been created from the 
original book or from a number of other summaries of the same book. Assuming there is similarity of expression, 
how does one prove what work or works the AI machine used to create the AI summary?

Attribution and moral rights

Strictly speaking, one does not need to cite an AI system as a creator or co-creator when using AI output. An AI 
system does not have moral rights under the Copyright Act.

Little consideration has been given to whether the use of works as training data in the machine learning process 
could amount to the infringement of moral rights of authors and, if so, whether law reform in the area is needed. 
See R Matulionyte, Can AI infringe moral rights of authors and should we do anything about it? an Australian 
perspective 
, Volume 15(1), Law, Innovation and Technology, 2003, pp 124–147.

However, it may be considered best practice to do so:

•
attributing AI sets out the true state of affairs;

•
it may be false attribution or misleading conduct to claim authorship of something not created by you;

•
putting people on notice that a non-human was involved informs readers and they may be more cautious 
relying upon the work. This may reduce other legal claims; and

•
doing so is consistent with Australia’s AI Ethics Framework 
 (7 November 2019) that recommends 
transparency and explainability: “There should be transparency and responsible disclosure so people can 
understand when they are being significantly impacted by AI and can find out when an AI system is 
engaging with them”.
