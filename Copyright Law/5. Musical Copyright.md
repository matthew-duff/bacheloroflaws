# 5. Musical Copyright

---

## What is a musical work?

> [!abstract] Key principle
> A musical work is an **"abstract concept"** — although it might be evidenced by a noted musical score or a sound recording, **neither of these is the work itself**.
>
> *EMI Songs Australia v Larrikin Music Publishing*; *Boomerang Investments v Padgett*

- **Lyrics** → separately protected as a **literary work**
- **Sound of lyrics being sung** → part of the **musical work** (*Boomerang*)

Perram J goes on at some length with this analysis.

---

## Sound recordings

> **"Sound recording"** = the aggregate of sounds embodied in a record.

### No requirement

When a record label wants to make a sound recording of a song:

1. It needs to obtain the **copyright permission** of the owner of the copyright in the relevant work (and any lyrics).
2. When the record label creates a record by recording an artist, they typically have **contractual rights** that bar the artist from creating more recordings.

## Kookaburra case

At first instance, **Jacobson J** found infringement.

### Three steps for infringement [60]

| Step | Task |
|------|------|
| **1** | Identify the work in which copyright subsists |
| **2** | Identify the allegedly infringing work and the part said to have been derived or taken from the copyright work |
| **3** | Determine whether the part taken is a **substantial part** of the copyrighted work |

> [!idea] Causal connection & objective similarity
> There is perhaps a counter-intuitive link between the requirements **'causal connection'** and **'objective similarity'**.

### Expert recognition on *Spicks and Specks*

- The difficulty the panel members had **is not sufficient** to overcome the conclusion that the relevant degree of objective similarity is made out.
- What *Spick & Specks* does show is that there are difficulties recognising the work, but a **sensitised listener** can detect the aural resemblance.

**On appeal:** Jagot J considered that the primary judge applied the correct test.

**Emmett J** found infringement but expressed some *"disquiet"* about the conclusion in the circumstances of the case, noting that *"some of the underlying concepts of modern copyright may require rethinking"*.

Perram J’s analysis of the infringement question:

1. **Identification of the work in suit**
2. **Identification of the parts said to have been taken:** *Warm*, *France*
