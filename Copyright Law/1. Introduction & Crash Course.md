## Copyright in 6 Easy Steps

1. Identify the copyright material
2. Identify the owners (authors and assignees) and licensees
3. Identify the exclusive rights of the owner
4. Identify any infringement
5. Identify any defences or exceptions
6. Consider available remedies

---

## Intellectual property overview

- Intellectual property is generally thought of as a **bundle of rights**.
- It includes rights in relation to:
  - **Ideas and inventions** — patents, confidential information
  - **Literary, artistic, dramatic and musical works** — copyright
  - **Computer programs, films, sound recordings, television broadcasts** — copyright
  - **Trade indicia / brands** — trade marks, tort of passing off, consumer protection legislation
  - **Designs** — designs legislation
  - **New plant varieties** — special legislation
  - **Circuit layout design** — special legislation

---

## What is copyright?

Copyright is:

- A **bundle of exclusive rights**
- Related to **particular work or subject matter**
- The right to do (or authorise others to do) **particular acts** in relation to that subject matter — e.g. reproduce, publish, communicate to the public

**Key points:**

- Conferred by **statute**. There is no common law copyright.
- **Territorial** — the *Copyright Act 1968* only confers exclusive rights in respect of Australia.
- Copyright is **property** ([[Copyright Act s 196]]), but:
  1. Creature of statute
  2. Long but finite duration
  3. A bundle of exclusive rights in relation to the particular *non-physical* subject matter — not ownership of the physical thing

### What kind of subject matter does copyright protect?

[[3. Part III - Works]]

1. Literary works (novels, poems, short stories; also *computer programs*)
2. Artistic works — **only** paintings, sculptures, drawings, photographs and etchings (nothing else)
3. Musical works (not the recording)
4. Dramatic works (including plays, choreography, “other dumb shows”)

[[4. Part IV - Subject matter other than works]]

1. Sound recordings
2. Films
3. TV and sound broadcasts
4. Published editions of works

---

## Who gets the exclusive rights?

The initial owner of the copyright typically **subsists** in:

- The **author** for Part III works ([[Copyright Act s 35]] (2))
- Depends on the type of subject matter (see [[Copyright Act s 97]]-[[Copyright Act s 100]])

---

## How long does copyright last?

- Copyright is granted only for a **limited term** and eventually expires.
- Term length depends on the nature of the particular subject matter.
- Generally, for Part III works: **70 years after the author’s death**.
- Once it expires, the work enters the **public domain**.

---

## Does copyright need to be registered?

- **No** registration required.
- There is no Australian copyright register.
- Compare: patents, trade marks, designs (which do require registration).
- Copyright comes into existence **automatically** upon creation of the subject matter.

---

## Prerequisites for copyright protection

1. **Originality** (Part III) — not copied  
   The relevant subject matter must originate from the author or creator.

2. **Expression vs fact/ideas**  
   Copyright does not protect pure information, facts or ideas; only the *particular expression* of that information or those ideas.

3. **Connection to Australia** — by direct connection or via a treaty.

4. **Material form**  
   Copyright does **not** protect subject matter unless it has been reduced to material form.

5. **Substantiality** (not in the Act but arises in the cases)  
   Copyright does not protect subject matter that is too insubstantial (e.g. single words, simple phrases).

---

## What rights does copyright confer?

1. **Economic rights** — allow the owner to exploit the subject matter.
2. **Moral rights** — protect the non-economic interests of the author.

---

## What is copyright infringement?

**Definition:** The exercise, without the owner’s permission, of any of the exclusive rights.

- Part III: [[Copyright Act s 36]]-[[Copyright Act s 39B]]  
- Part IV: [[Copyright Act s 110A]]-[[Copyright Act s 112B]]  

**Requirements:**

- The taking of a **substantial part** of the relevant work or subject matter
- The part taken is **objectively similar** to the copyright work
- **Actual copying** (direct or indirect; conscious or unconscious)

**AND** - there are also lots of exceptions and defences to copyright infringement. These include:
- Fair dealing (see [[Copyright Act s 40]]-[[Copyright Act s 42]] (Part II) and [[Copyright Act s 103A]]-[[Copyright Act s 103C]] (Part IV))
- Where there is a corresponding design (under certain circumstances) - see [[Copyright Act s 74]]-[[Copyright Act s 77A]]
- And lots of miscellaneous exceptions

### What remedies exist?
- Injunctive release
- Damages (including additional damages) or an account of profits.
- Declaratory relief
- Delivery up

See also [[Copyright Act s 115]].
