[[AI Tools and Licensing: Who Owns the Output? - TermsFeed]]

**Citation / Reference:** Leah Hudson, "AI Tools and Licensing: Who Owns the Output?", TermsFeed (blog), 3 December 2025, https://www.termsfeed.com/blog/ai-output-licensing/

**Overview / Key points**
- Defines AI-generated works (text, images, code, etc.) and explains that IP law is built on human originality, so ownership of AI output is contested.
- **IP and AI by jurisdiction:** (1) **EU** — CJEU and 2025 EP study: copyright requires "author's own intellectual creation" and "meaningful human input"; fully autonomous AI outputs are excluded; "meaningful human input" and human creative control can make works protectable. EPO rejected AI (DABUS) as inventor. (2) **US** — Copyright Office (2025): copyright protects human-authored expression including AI-assisted material, but not purely AI-generated material; *Thaler v Perlmutter* (2023): human authorship required; USPTO likewise rejected DABUS. (3) **UK** — CDPA s 9(3): author of computer-generated work is the person who undertakes "arrangements necessary for the creation" (user vs developer still untested); IPO 2025 guidance on AI and patents (technical contribution).
- **New/upcoming law:** EU AI Act (Art 53): GPAI providers must comply with copyright and publish a summary of training content; US Generative AI Copyright Disclosure Act (bill): disclosure of copyrighted works in training datasets. Neither addresses ownership of outputs.
- **Input ownership:** Users generally keep copyright in their own inputs; ToS of providers (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grok) vary (opt-out for training, paid vs unpaid data use, broad grants to provider). Training on scraped/copyrighted data is disputed (e.g. NYT v OpenAI, Getty v Stability AI).
- **Output ownership:** Evolving and fact-specific; many ToS assign output to the user, but legal protectability depends on human authorship and meaningful creative contribution in the jurisdiction.
- **Practical tips:** Review ToS; avoid uploading third-party content; opt out of training use where possible; document human creative steps; consider paid tiers for better data/IP protections.

**Significance for Copyright Law**
- Central reference for how copyright and related rights apply to AI inputs and outputs across major jurisdictions (EU, US, UK), and for the policy tension between human authorship and AI-assisted creation.
- Illustrates the importance of "meaningful human input" and creative control for subsistence and ownership, and the role of provider ToS alongside statutory and judicial rules.
