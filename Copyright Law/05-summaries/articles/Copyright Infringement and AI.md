[[Copyright Law/05-summaries/articles/Copyright Infringement and AI]]

**Citation/Reference:** Swinson, John V, *Copyright infringement and AI*, LexisNexis Guidance Note (maintained), The University of Queensland.

**Overview / Key points**

- **Copyright infringement generally:** Infringement occurs when someone, without licence, does an act within the copyright owner’s exclusive rights (reproduction, communication to the public, publication, adaptation, etc., depending on subject matter: s 31, s 85 *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth)). Defences such as fair dealing may apply.
- **Training an AI system:** Training typically involves copying works into the system; that copying can be a reproduction (or adaptation) of each work and thus separate acts of infringement (e.g. 3 million songs → 3 million potential infringements). Non-infringing scenarios: expired copyright; purely factual works; insubstantial part; public domain; ownership or licence; or a narrow defence (e.g. fair dealing for research/study). Most training occurs overseas, so Australian law may not apply; many cases are brought in other jurisdictions (see *Getty Images v Stability AI* [2025] EWHC 2863 (Ch) and international developments). Industry often relies on US “fair use”; that does not apply in Australia. US Copyright Office Part 3 report (May 2025) treats training as implicating exclusive rights, with fair use the key issue. Attorney-General’s 2023 roundtable and Issues Summary addressed AI and copyright. Productivity Commission (interim Aug 2025) proposed a TDM exception; after pushback, government ruled out TDM (Oct 2025) in favour of a licensing-first approach. CAIRG is considering licensing, certainty for AI-generated material, and enforcement (e.g. small claims). Final Productivity Commission report (Dec 2025) recommended against wholesale changes and suggested monitoring for three years. Practice: obtain licences before using others’ works to train; many publishers and news organisations have granted licences to AI companies (examples: Financial Times/OpenAI, Taylor & Francis/Microsoft, News Corp/OpenAI, NYT/Amazon, Microsoft Publisher Content Marketplace, Meta multi-year deals, Google partnerships).
- **AI system output:** Output may reproduce or communicate a substantial part of a work used in training (e.g. AI-generated image of Batman eating a Big Mac). Users of that output may also infringe. Whether inexact statistical recreations count as “copies” is legally and technically contested. Section 22(6) (person “responsible for determining the content of the communication”) raises who is liable for communication—AI operator or user. Proving which work(s) the AI used for a given output can be difficult.
- **Attribution and moral rights:** There is no obligation to cite the AI as creator; AI has no moral rights. Whether using works as training data infringes authors’ moral rights is underconsidered; reform may be needed. Best practice supports attributing AI: transparency, avoiding false attribution, informing readers, and aligning with Australia’s AI Ethics Framework (transparency and explainability).

**Significance for Copyright Law**

Directly addresses when training AI on copyright works and when using AI output can infringe, and the limits of Australian defences (no fair use; narrow fair dealing). It explains the shift to licensing-first and the role of CAIRG and Productivity Commission, and flags liability (reproduction, communication, s 22(6)) and proof issues. Essential for advising on training datasets, licensing, output use and attribution in an Australian copyright framework.
