[[Copyright Law/05-summaries/articles/Future Impacts of AI on IP Laws]]

**Citation/Reference:** Swinson, John V, *Future impacts of AI on IP laws*, LexisNexis Guidance Note (maintained), The University of Queensland.

**Overview / Key points**

- **The future:** AI will affect law in three ways: (1) existing laws will adapt to the technology; (2) new laws will regulate AI; (3) legal practice will use AI tools. IP law is evolving in response to AI creating and inventing, but often more slowly than the technology. Policy and litigation overseas will influence Australian law.
- **AI policy in Australia:** Copyright and AI Reference Group (CAIRG) established Dec 2023 to consider copyright challenges (training material, transparency, imitative works, protection of AI-generated works). CAIRG priorities (Oct 2025): fair legal use of copyright in AI (including licensing); certainty for AI-generated material; lower-cost enforcement (e.g. small claims). National AI Plan (Dec 2025): light-touch regulation using existing laws. Senate Select Committee on Adopting AI (Report Nov 2024) recommended inter alia: continued CAIRG consultation; transparency and licensing of copyrighted works in training; fair remuneration for creators. Government focus on responsible/ethical AI; Guidance for AI Adoption (Oct 2025) and DTA “Policy for the responsible use of AI in government” (v2 Dec 2025). Attorney-General’s transparency discussion (Copyright and AI) emphasises legally obtained training data and disclosure of sources. TDM exception ruled out (Oct 2025); “licensing-first” approach preferred. Procurement contracts increasingly address AI and IP (e.g. limits on using customer data for training).
- **Uncertainty:** Key open questions: how existing rules apply to AI-generated works; level of human creativity required; who counts as creator and who is liable (e.g. tort); whether IP rationales (incentives, rewards) apply when AI creates; who shares economic rewards; whether AI can create prior art; whether AI should have legal personality; whether the IP system is still needed. Illustrative scenarios: ownership of “dream reconstruction” films; whether AI image-recognition of a photograph infringes; deep fakes and political/artistic expression. Specific uncertainties: AI as co-inventor or assistor; copyright in human–AI works; ownership of AI-generated IP; registrability of AI-generated trade marks; protecting IP in AI systems; balancing trade secrets with safety/transparency demands.
- **Predictions:** IP law will change (protection, ownership, infringement). Approaches will differ internationally in the short term. US emphasises trade and efficiency; EU emphasises safety and responsibility; Australia closer to EU. Policy will develop to support licensing of works for AI training. Law may recognise human–AI partnership (e.g. identifying the human’s creative contribution, analogous to “maker” of a film under s 98). Competition and consumer law (e.g. unfair contract terms, digital platforms) will interact with IP.

**Significance for Copyright Law**

Sets out the policy and institutional context (CAIRG, Senate inquiry, National AI Plan, transparency and licensing-first stance) that will shape how Australian copyright treats AI training, AI-generated works and enforcement. Highlights doctrinal and policy uncertainties (authorship, liability, incentives) and predicts evolution toward human–AI partnership and licensing. Important for understanding likely legislative and policy direction and for preparing arguments on subsistence, ownership and infringement in an AI context.
