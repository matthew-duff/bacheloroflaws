[[IP and AI generated works]]

**Citation/Reference:** Swinson, John V, *IP and AI generated works*, LexisNexis Guidance Note (maintained), The University of Queensland.

**Overview / Key points**

- **AI and generative AI:** AI learns and produces outputs from unexpected inputs; generative AI (e.g. ChatGPT) creates new content. Machine learning uses data to learn patterns and produce new, similar outputs. Transformer architecture (from 2017) underpins many current systems.
- **Copyright and AI-generated works:** The Australian *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth) has no special regime for computer-generated works (unlike the UK). Works *solely* created by AI, with no human intellectual effort, are unlikely to attract copyright (*IceTV*, *Telstra v Phone Directories*). The Act implies an “author” is a person (e.g. “person who took the photograph”). Where a human uses AI as a tool and contributes significant intellectual effort, skill and judgment, the human may be the author (*Data Access Corp v Powerflex*). Documenting human creative input is critical; ownership may turn on who contributed most (model creators, data selectors, trainers, app developers, or end users). Minimal or purely mechanical human involvement does not support copyright. Originality and authorship overlap: the work must originate from a human author; opacity of how AI produces output complicates proving originality.
- **Ownership:** Only a legal person can own copyright; AI cannot. Check commercial AI terms for ownership/licence of outputs.
- **Berne Convention:** Text and history suggest “author” means a natural person (e.g. Art 7: protection 50 years after “his” death).
- **Copyright in AI systems:** Code written by humans is protected; compilations of training data may be protected where there is independent intellectual effort in selection. Human involvement in training (e.g. correcting errors, ranking) can support copyright in the system; obtain assignments from such contributors.
- **Patents and AI:** *Commissioner of Patents v Thaler*: an AI cannot be an “inventor” in Australia; only natural persons. AI cannot own patents. AI-generated products/methods may infringe existing patents (no intent required); search AusPat before commercialising. AI systems and their components (training methods, model, use cases) may be patentable subject to existing rules on computer-implemented inventions.
- **Trade marks and AI:** AI-generated brands/logos may infringe existing registrations (no defence of independent creation). Clearance search on the Trade Marks Register is essential. US/UK developments (e.g. *Dow Jones v Perplexity*, *Getty v Stability AI*) show liability for dilution and misuse of marks in AI outputs. Logos can attract copyright; AI output that copies or adapts another’s logo may infringe; ownership for registration focuses on use under s 27, not creation—AI-generated marks may be registrable by a legal person.
- **Designs and AI:** Designs Act requires a “person” as designer/owner; purely AI-created designs likely not registrable. Human use of AI as a tool may support protection. AI-generated designs can invalidate novelty for human designers and may infringe existing designs—clearance search recommended.
- **Trade secrets and contracts:** Algorithms, data and code can be protected as confidential information. Subscription models help maintain secrecy. EU AI Act and transparency/safety laws may limit confidentiality. Users must check terms so inputs/outputs are not used to train or commercialise without consent; data-providers should address secrecy and ownership in training agreements.

**Significance for Copyright Law**

Clarifies that Australian copyright does not protect purely AI-generated works and that authorship and originality require human intellectual effort. It frames how human–AI collaboration might attract protection, the importance of documenting human contribution, and the overlap with ownership, infringement (including in training and outputs), and other IP (patents, trade marks, designs). Essential for understanding subsistence, ownership and risk when creating or using AI-generated content and AI systems.
