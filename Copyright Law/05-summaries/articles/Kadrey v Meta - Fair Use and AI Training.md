**Citation / Reference:** *Kadrey et al v Meta Platforms, Inc.*, Order Denying Plaintiffs' Motion for Partial Summary Judgment and Granting Meta's Cross-Motion for Partial Summary Judgment, No 23-cv-03417-VC (ND Cal, 25 June 2025) (Chhabria J).

**Overview / Key points**
- Thirteen authors sued Meta for copying their books from shadow libraries (e.g. LibGen, Books3, Anna's Archive) and using them to train the Llama LLMs. Cross-motions on fair use (reproduction only; distribution claim remains).
- **Court’s general view (obiter):** Copyright aims to protect the incentive to create. Copying protected works to train generative AI that can flood the market with competing works often undermines that incentive; fair use does not automatically follow from "transformative" use, and market harm (fourth factor) is central. In many scenarios, training on copyrighted works without permission will not be fair use; developers may need to license.
- **Outcome in this case:** Summary judgment for Meta on fair use. (1) **Factor 1 (purpose/character):** Meta’s use was highly transformative (training an LLM for diverse tasks vs. reading books); commercial nature and use of shadow libraries did not outweigh that. (2) **Factor 2 (nature of work):** Favoured plaintiffs (expressive books). (3) **Factor 3 (amount used):** Favoured Meta—whole books were reasonably necessary for training. (4) **Factor 4 (market effect):** Plaintiffs’ theories failed: Llama could not regurgitate meaningful portions of the books; there is no cognisable "market" for licensing works as AI training data under the fourth factor; and although *market dilution* (flood of AI-generated competing works) could be a valid theory and might often decide fair use in AI-training cases, plaintiffs did not plead or evidence it—no analysis of Llama’s capacity to produce competing books, relevant markets, or actual/likely harm. With no empirical or sufficient inferential case on market harm, factor four favoured Meta.
- **Takeaway:** The ruling is limited to these 13 plaintiffs and their arguments; it does not hold that training on copyrighted books is lawful in general. It illustrates that plaintiffs in similar cases should plead and support market-dilution (indirect substitution) with evidence.

**Significance for Copyright Law**
- Important US authority on fair use and AI training: emphasises that transformativeness does not trump market harm and that market dilution from AI-generated competing works is a legitimate fourth-factor concern.
- Demonstrates that success for defendants can turn on plaintiffs’ failure to plead and prove market effects (especially dilution), rather than on a broad holding that all LLM training is fair use.
