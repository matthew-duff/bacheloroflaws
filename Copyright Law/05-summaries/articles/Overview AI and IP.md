[[Overview _ Artificial intelligence and IP]]

**Citation/Reference:** LexisNexis, *Overview — Artificial intelligence and IP* (maintained guidance note, 2026).

**Overview / Key points**

- **Generative AI and IP:** Generative AI (e.g. ChatGPT) creates new content by learning from existing data and raises questions about ownership, inventorship/authorship, infringement by training and output, and protection of AI systems.
- **Australian position — relatively certain:** (1) AI cannot own IP. (2) AI cannot be sole inventor (patent), sole author (copyright), or sole designer (registered design). (3) Training an AI system on existing copyright works is **likely copyright infringement** unless licensed. (4) AI-generated output may infringe patent, trade mark or design even if that right was not used in training.
- **Australian position — less certain:** Trade marks and AI; AI as co-inventor or assistive tool for patentable inventions; copyright in works created by a human using AI; ownership of IP when AI is used; responsibility and infringement when AI output is substantially similar to another’s work; how to protect IP in the AI system itself.
- **Copyright infringement and AI:** Three main issues: (1) **Input** – is using copyright works to train AI infringement? Copying into training data/memory likely infringes; developers often rely on defences such as “fair use” (US only; Australia has no general fair use). (2) **Output** – can AI output infringe works used in training? Yes in some cases (e.g. output depicting Superman). (3) **Liability** – who is responsible when AI output is substantially similar to a protected work? Contractual “AI clauses” in IT/SaaS contracts often address use of customer data for training.
- **Future impacts:** Policy and litigation (including overseas) will shape the law; key themes include **human–AI collaboration** (what level of human creativity is required for protection) and **efficient licensing** of works for AI training.

**Significance for Copyright Law**

- Summarises how Australian copyright and related IP doctrines apply to **AI training** (input) and **AI-generated works** (output), and where the law is clear vs uncertain.
- Highlights that **training on copyright works** is likely to involve **reproduction** and thus infringement absent licence or exception—relevant to infringement analysis and defences (e.g. fair dealing).
- Distinguishes **Australia** (no general fair use) from **US** (fair use defences and pending cases), which is important for comparative and policy discussion.
- Frames **human authorship** and **ownership** where AI is used as central ongoing issues for copyright subsistence and ownership in Australia.
