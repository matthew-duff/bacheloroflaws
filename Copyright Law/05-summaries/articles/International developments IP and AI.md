[[International developments in IP laws and AI]]

**Citation/Reference:** LexisNexis, *International developments in IP laws and AI* (maintained guidance note, authored and updated by Professor John V Swinson, The University of Queensland, 2026).

**Overview / Key points**

- **Jurisdictional variation:** IP is territorial (country-by-country). Patent rules on AI inventorship are broadly aligned; copyright subsistence/ownership and **defences to infringement** (e.g. fair use, TDM exceptions) differ; numerous US and UK lawsuits are pending.
- **United States:** (1) **Authorship** – human author required; *Thaler v Perlmutter* (D.C. Cir. 2025) holds a machine cannot be an author; mixed human/AI works (e.g. *Zarya of the Dawn*, Shupe’s book) get limited protection (e.g. compilation or human-authored elements). (2) **Training as infringement** – many cases (e.g. *Andersen v Stability AI*, *Authors Guild v OpenAI*, *NYT v OpenAI/Microsoft*, *In re OpenAI Copyright Infringement Litigation*); courts addressing fair use, “regurgitation,” and discovery of training data. (3) **Notable rulings** – *Andy Warhol Foundation v Goldsmith* (fair use) and *Thomas Reuters v ROSS Intelligence* (training use not transformative) affect AI training arguments; Anthropic settled after court found fair use for some training but liability for use of pirated books. (4) **Policy** – US Copyright Office multi-part report on AI; legislative proposals (e.g. Generative AI Copyright Disclosure Act); Trump Executive Order and industry submissions on copyright and AI.
- **United Kingdom:** Computer-generated works protected; “author” is person who made arrangements (CDPA 1988 s 178). TDM for AI currently limited to non-commercial research; government consulted on copyright and AI (e.g. licensing, code of practice). *Getty Images v Stability AI* [2025] EWHC 2863 (Ch): Stable Diffusion model not an “infringing copy” of training works; Getty succeeded on trademark/watermark claim.
- **European Union:** Thaler/DABUS: only natural persons as inventors. EU TDM exception under scrutiny for generative AI training; EU study (July 2025) on generative AI and copyright. French regulator fined Google over training on publishers’ content (neighbouring rights). AI Act (in force from 2024) regulates deep fakes and transparency, not core copyright rules.
- **China:** Some courts have protected AI-generated works where human set parameters and made creative choices (“originality” and “personalised expression”); conditional protection for AI-generated works.
- **New Zealand:** Mirrors UK on computer-generated works; *Thaler* – AI cannot be inventor.
- **Global:** Industry and intergovernmental principles (e.g. OECD AI Principles, G7 Hiroshima Process, News Media Alliance Global AI Principles) address IP, transparency and governance.

**Significance for Copyright Law**

- Provides a **comparative map** of how key jurisdictions treat **AI training** (infringement vs exceptions like fair use/TDM), **AI output** (subsistence, ownership, infringement), and **human authorship**—essential for understanding Australian law in context and potential reform.
- **US litigation** (fair use, regurgitation, discovery) and **US Copyright Office** guidance inform debates on training, transformative use and human contribution in Australia.
- **UK/EU** approaches (computer-generated works, TDM, licensing) show alternatives to a US-style fair use model and relevance of **licensing and remuneration** for training.
- **Territoriality** is emphasised: same conduct (e.g. training) may be infringing in one country and permitted in another, affecting where AI is trained and how Australian rights are enforced.
