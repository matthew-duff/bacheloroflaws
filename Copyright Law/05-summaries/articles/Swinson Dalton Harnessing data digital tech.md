[[Swinson Dalton Harnessing data and digital technology]]

**Citation/Reference:** John Swinson and Trent Dalton, *Harnessing data and digital technology – Interim Report* (Submission to Productivity Commission, 14 September 2025).

**Overview / Key points**

- **Context:** Joint submission by law professor John Swinson and author Trent Dalton to the Productivity Commission’s Interim Report on Harnessing Data and Digital Technology, responding to use of copyrighted materials to train AI models.
- **Three Cs (Compensation, Credit, Control):** Writers rely on fair payment, attribution/moral rights, and control over when and how works are used. Unregulated AI is framed as undermining all three and weakening human creativity.
- **Training vs output:** The submission argues the main harm is not only use of works as training data but **AI-generated outputs** that compete with human creators (e.g. books, articles) and undercut markets and incentives.
- **Creativity and humanity:** AI is characterised as lacking embodied experience, genuine consciousness, moral agency, and true originality; human creativity is tied to lived experience. Flooding markets with AI works is said to commoditise creativity and reduce incentive to create.
- **Censorship:** Concentration of AI tools in a few tech companies is said to allow censorship of certain views in AI outputs, unlike “neutral” tools (e.g. a typewriter).
- **Recommendations:** (1) **Compensation** – creators compensated when works are used to train AI, including a share of AI tool revenue. (2) **Control** – creators decide who may use their works for training and for which works. (3) **Credit** – prohibit AI outputs “in the style of” or “as sequel to” specific creators; require citation when works are used as basis for AI content. (4) **Jurisdiction** – Australian copyright should apply to AI models trained overseas but made available in Australia (anti-avoidance / product-by-process style approach). (5) **Censorship** – AI providers should publish explanations of override rules. (6) **No TDM exception** – oppose a broad text and data mining exception; prefer compensation or opt-out over blanket exceptions.
- **US authority:** Relies on *Kadrey v Meta* (N.D. Cal. 2025) for the proposition that copyright aims to preserve incentives for human creation and that training generative AI on copyrighted works can undermine those incentives and markets.

**Significance for Copyright Law**

- Directly addresses **training on copyrighted works** and **AI-generated outputs** as central policy issues for Australian copyright.
- Connects **moral rights** (credit, integrity) and **economic rights** to the debate over AI training and output.
- Proposes **extraterritorial / anti-avoidance** application of Australian copyright to overseas training when models are used in Australia—relevant to subsistence, infringement and remedies.
- Opposes a **TDM exception** and argues for **creator compensation and control** instead, informing reform options (exceptions vs licensing/remuneration).
- Illustrates **fair use / fair dealing** and **human authorship** in the context of AI (with reference to US authority), useful for comparing Australian doctrine and policy.
