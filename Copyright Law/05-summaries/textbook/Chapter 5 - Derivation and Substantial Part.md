# Chapter 5 - Derivation and Substantial Part

## Overview / Key Points

This chapter examines the two critical linkages required to establish copyright infringement in Australia: **derivation** and **substantial part**.

### 1. Derivation (Causal Connection)
Derivation refers to the factual "but for" connection between the plaintiff's work and the defendant's alleged infringing act.
*   **Nature**: It is a binary requirement (it either exists or it doesn't).
*   **Part III vs Part IV**: While essential for all copyright, it is primarily litigated in Part III works (literary, dramatic, musical, artistic). Part IV subject matter (films, broadcasts, etc.) usually involves mechanical exactitude, making derivation obvious.
*   **Inferences**: Since direct proof of copying is rare, courts use a two-part test to infer derivation:
    1.  **Objective similarity** between the works.
    2.  **Access**: The defendant had a reasonable opportunity to see/hear the plaintiff's work.
*   **Rebuttal**: A defendant can rebut the inference by proving **independent creation**, use of a common third-party source, or functional necessity.
*   **Indirect Copying**: Derivation can be satisfied via intermediate steps (e.g., copying a 3D widget derived from a 2D blueprint).
*   **Prior Information**: Following *Purefoy Engineering v Sykes Boxal*, derivation is not established if the defendant copies underlying facts/information that the plaintiff's work merely reports, rather than the plaintiff's original expression.

### 2. Substantial Part
Even if derivation is proven, infringement only occurs if the defendant has taken a "substantial part" of the work (s 14(1) *Copyright Act 1968*).
*   **Quality over Quantity**: The test is primarily qualitative. The focus is on whether the *originality* of the work has been taken.
*   **Part III Works (Modern Approach)**:
    *   **Originality as the Yardstick**: Following *Autodesk v Dyason (No 2)* and *Data Access v Powerflex*, the "substantiality" of a part is determined by its originality.
    *   **IceTV v Nine Network**: The High Court emphasised that taking "slivers of information" (like TV program times and titles) does not constitute a substantial part if those facts are inseparable from their expression or lack authorial mental effort.
*   **Part IV Subject Matter**:
    *   **Published Editions**: *Nationwide News v CAL* held that copying individual articles from a newspaper is not a substantial part of the "published edition" copyright, as the protected interest is the layout of the *whole* edition.
    *   **Broadcasts**: *TCN Nine v Network 10* (The Panel case) established that while even short segments can be "substantial" if they are "highlights" or "potent," the court must apply the s 14 test rather than assuming any taking is an infringement.

## Significance
Chapter 5 is fundamental to understanding the boundaries of copyright protection. It confirms that copyright does not protect ideas or information in the abstract, but rather the specific expression originated by the author. The shift towards a strictly qualitative, originality-based assessment of "substantial part" (especially after *IceTV*) ensures that "thin" works (like compilations of facts) receive limited protection, preventing copyright from being used to monopolize underlying data or facts.
