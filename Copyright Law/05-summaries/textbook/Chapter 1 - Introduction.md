## Overview
This chapter outlines the origins, justifications, and legislative evolution of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth). It traces the transition from British imperial law to a modern, technologically-neutral Australian regime heavily influenced by international treaties.

### Key Points
*   **Justifications**: Australian copyright law is driven by three main pillars:
    *   **Incentive**: Providing property rights to encourage authorship (Anglo tradition).
    *   **Natural Justice**: Rights as a consequence of human expression (Continental tradition/Berne Convention).
    *   **International Trade**: Integration of IP into trade law (TRIPS/FTAs) to protect comparative advantages in culture and technology.
*   **Historical Evolution**:
    *   **1905 Act**: Australia's first national legislation, replacing State Acts; required registration.
    *   **1912 Act**: Adopted the UK *Copyright Act 1911*, removing formalities (like registration) and establishing a "life + 50 years" term.
    *   **1968 Act**: The current regime, based on the UK *Copyright Act 1956*. It introduced a sharp distinction between **Part III "Works"** (original human authorship) and **Part IV "Subject Matter Other Than Works"** (recordings, films, broadcasts).
*   **Major Reform Tranches**:
    *   **1980 (Franki)**: Response to *UNSW v Moorhouse*; introduced statutory licenses for education and reprographic reproduction.
    *   **1984/1999/2001**: Progressive inclusion and refinement of **computer programs** as literary works.
    *   **1994 (TRIPS)**: Aligned Australian law with WTO trade norms, including rental rights and 50-year terms for performers.
    *   **2000 (Moral Rights)**: Introduced Part IX rights of attribution and integrity for authors and film creators.
    *   **2001 (Digital Agenda)**: Introduced the "communication to the public" right and anti-circumvention laws for technological protection measures (TPMs).
    *   **2004 (US-Australia FTA)**: Massive expansion including 20-year term extensions (to "life + 70"), safe harbour for ISPs, and stronger TPM laws.
    *   **2015/2018**: Introduced site-blocking injunctions (s 115A) to combat offshore piracy.
*   **Current Framework**: The *Copyright Act 1968* is a complex "collection of regimes" covering proprietary copyright, moral rights, performers' rights, TPM liability, and criminal sanctions.

## Significance
Chapter 1 provides the essential context for understanding why Australian copyright law is structured as it is. It highlights the shift from a purely domestic incentive-based system to one that is globally harmonized and increasingly complex due to technological change and international trade obligations. Understanding the distinction between Part III and Part IV subject matter is foundational for all subsequent study of the Act.
