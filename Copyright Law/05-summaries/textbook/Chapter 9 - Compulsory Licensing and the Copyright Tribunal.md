## Overview
Chapter 9 examines the Australian copyright law's reliance on **compulsory (statutory) licensing** and the regulatory role of the **Copyright Tribunal of Australia**. It distinguishes between licences administered directly and those managed by central collecting societies, while also covering Crown exploitation and the oversight of voluntary licence schemes.

### Key Points
*   **Nature of Compulsory Licences**: A public law mandatory offer that converts an exclusive right into a right to remuneration, facilitating socially beneficial activities while ensuring compensation for rights holders.
*   **Licences without a Central Body**:
    *   **Recording Musical Works (Part III, Div 6)**: A "vintage" licence dating back to the 1911 Act, allowing the manufacture of records of previously recorded musical works (excluding digital downloads/ringtones) subject to a prescribed royalty (currently 6.25% of retail price).
    *   **Secondary Uses of Sound Recordings (ss 108, 109)**: Covers public performance and broadcasting of sound recordings. Section 109 is notable for its controversial price caps (e.g., 1% of gross earnings for commercial radio).
*   **Licences with a Central Administering Body**:
    *   **Education (Part IVA, Div 4)**: Rationalised in 2017, this allows educational institutions to copy and communicate works and broadcasts, managed by declared collecting societies (Copyright Agency and Screenrights).
    *   **Retransmission (Part VC)**: Allows retransmission of free-to-air broadcasts (excluding internet retransmission) subject to equitable remuneration.
    *   **Satellite Re-broadcasting (Part VD)**: A recent, largely unused licence for remote/regional areas.
*   **Crown Exploitation (Part VII, Div 2)**:
    *   **Section 183**: Provides an "eminent domain" power for the Commonwealth or States to use copyright material for government services without infringement, subject to paying terms fixed by the Tribunal.
    *   **Section 183A**: Streamlines government copying through collective administration for specific classes of works.
*   **Licence Scheme Oversight**:
    *   The Copyright Tribunal regulates **voluntary collective licensing** (e.g., APRA, PPCA) to curb monopoly power.
    *   The Tribunal can confirm, vary, or substitute licence schemes but, per *Phonographic Performance Company of Australia v Foxtel (2019)*, it cannot create "compulsory" licences beyond the scope of what the licensor is authorised to grant.

## Significance
This chapter is critical for understanding the **balance between private property rights and public interest** in Australian copyright law. It highlights how the law manages market failures and monopoly power through the Copyright Tribunal. The discussion of price caps (s 152) and the distinction between broadcasting and internet streaming (*PPCA v Commercial Radio Australia*) illustrates the ongoing tension between legacy legislative frameworks and modern digital exploitation. It also clarifies the unique "eminent domain" status of the Crown, which operates outside standard infringement frameworks.
