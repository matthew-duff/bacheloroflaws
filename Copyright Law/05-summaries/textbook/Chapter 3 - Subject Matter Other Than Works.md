# Chapter 3 - Subject Matter Other Than Works

**Reference:** [Author], *Copyright Law*, Chapter 3: Subject Matter Other Than Works.

## Overview
Chapter 3 examines "Subject Matter Other Than Works" protected under Part IV of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth). Unlike Part III works, Part IV subject matter consists of non-authorial productions made by producers (e.g., wineries vs. vignerons). Key points include:

*   **Nature of Protection**: Part IV copyright is a "producers' copyright" intended to protect and encourage investment in industries supporting authorship. It requires no authorial originality and exists independently of any Part III copyright in underlying works.
*   **Sound Recordings (s 10(1))**: Defined as the "aggregate of the sounds embodied in a record." The "maker" is generally the owner of the record (media) at the time it was produced. Protection is narrow, confined to the actual sounds recorded (*CBS Records v Telmak*).
*   **Cinematograph Films (s 10(1))**: Defined as the "aggregate of the visual images embodied in an article or thing" capable of being shown as a moving picture. The "maker" is the person who made the arrangements for the production (producer).
    *   **Restricted Rights**: Like sound recordings, protection is limited to the exact visual images and sounds; thematic imitation does not infringe film copyright (*Zeccola v Universal City Studios*; *Telmak v Bond International*).
    *   **Interactive Media**: Video games can be films despite player-driven variability (*Sega v Galaxy Electronics*), but simple animations like spinning reels may not be (*Aristocrat v Pacific Gaming*).
    *   **Soundtracks**: Sounds in a soundtrack are deemed part of the film (s 23(1)), but this does not extinguish separate rights in pre-existing sound recordings dubbed into the film (*PPCA v FACTS*).
*   **Broadcasts (Television and Sound)**: Protection for the broadcast signal itself, distinct from the content.
    *   **Definition**: Straddles the *Copyright Act* and *Broadcasting Services Act 1992*. It excludes "over-the-top" (OTT) internet streaming and point-to-point services (*PPCA v Commercial Radio Australia*).
    *   **Metes and Bounds**: The "broadcast" in which copyright subsists is the discrete program (e.g., *The Today Show*), not every individual frame or second (*Channel Nine v Network Ten*).
*   **Published Editions**: Protects the typographical arrangement and layout of a published work.
    *   **Scope**: Only the "facsimile copy" right is granted. It is the only Part IV subject matter without a "communication to the public" right.
    *   **Identity**: Generally refers to the whole edition (e.g., the entire newspaper) rather than individual articles (*Nationwide News v Copyright Agency*; *NLA v Marks & Spencer*).

## Significance
This chapter is critical for understanding the **dual-layered nature of copyright** in Australia. It establishes that technical productions (recordings, films, broadcasts) receive a narrower, more "mechanical" form of protection than authorial works. The "what you see/hear is what you get" principle (*CBS v Telmak*) is a fundamental limitation on Part IV rights, ensuring that copyright in the "container" does not overreach into the "content" (ideas or styles) which remains the domain of Part III or the public domain.
