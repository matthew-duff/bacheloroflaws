# Chapter 8 - Assignment and Licensing

## Overview
This chapter examines the legal framework for the transfer and licensing of copyright in Australia under the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth). It covers the formal requirements for assignments and exclusive licences, the role of equity in informal transfers, and the principles governing voluntary licensing.

### Key Points
- **Nature of Copyright**: Copyright is personal property and is alienable by voluntary assignment, will, or operation of law (s 196(1)). It can be partially assigned by right, territory, or time (s 196(2)).
- **Formalities (s 196(3))**: An assignment of copyright is not effective at law unless it is in **writing signed by or on behalf of the assignor**. This requirement also applies to **exclusive licences** (s 10(1)) and **future copyright** (s 197).
- **Judicial Approach to Formalities**:
    - In **business-to-business** settings, courts may accept irregular forms (e.g., sale of business agreements) if the intent to assign is clear (*Greenfield Products*; *Murray v King*).
    - In **independent contractor** settings, courts are stricter. A commissioning party generally only acquires a licence unless there is an express signed assignment (*University of Sydney v ObjectiVision*).
- **Equitable Interests**: Where formalities fail but value has been given, equity may recognize an equitable interest (resulting trust), which can be perfected by a court order for legal assignment (*Gold Peg International v Kovan Engineering*).
- **Future Copyright (s 197)**: Allows for the legal assignment of copyright before it exists. It must be signed by the person who *would* have been the first owner.
- **Exclusive Licences**: Grant the licensee the right to sue for infringement (Part V Div 3), effectively "filling the shoes" of the owner.
- **Voluntary Licensing**:
    - **Bare Licences**: Gratuitous permissions that are generally revocable at will or on reasonable notice. However, **promissory estoppel** may prevent revocation if the licensee has acted to their detriment (*Chhabra v McPherson*).
    - **Contractual Licences**: Governed by contract law; breach can lead to both contractual and copyright infringement liability.
    - **Onus of Proof**: The plaintiff bears the burden of proving the absence of a licence. A "tolerated use" (where the owner is neutral or ambiguous) may be found to be non-infringing (*Avel v Multicoin Amusements*).
- **Successors in Title (s 196(4))**: A licence binds every successor in title to the grantor, regardless of notice. This differs from the UK position and emphasizes the need for due diligence during copyright acquisitions.

## Significance
Chapter 8 is critical for understanding how copyright moves from the first owner to commercial exploiters. The strictness of the signed writing requirement serves as a safeguard for authors (especially independent contractors) but necessitates careful drafting in commercial transactions. The Australian position on s 196(4) makes licences particularly robust, as they "run with the property" against subsequent assignees, creating a unique priority regime in Australian intellectual property law.
