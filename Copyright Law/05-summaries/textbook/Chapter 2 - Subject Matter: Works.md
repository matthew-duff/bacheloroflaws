## Overview / Key Points

This chapter examines the first of the two main regimes of proprietary copyright under the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth): **Works** (literary, dramatic, musical, and artistic). It details the threshold requirements for subsistence and the specific categories of works.

### 1. Threshold Requirements for Subsistence
For copyright to subsist in a "work", three key requirements must be met:
*   **Originality (s 32):** In Australia, "original" means the work must originate from the "independent intellectual effort" of a human author. It does not require aesthetic merit or inventiveness but must not be a mere copy.
    *   *Authorship:* The requirement for a human author is strict (*Telstra v Phone Directories*). Computer-generated material lacking a human author does not attract copyright as a "work".
    *   *IceTV v Nine Network (2009):* Shifted the focus from "industrious collection" (the "sweat of the brow" or *Walter v Lane* approach) to the particular form of expression originating from a human author.
*   **Material Form (s 22):** Copyright does not protect ideas, only their expression. Subsistence begins when a work is first reduced to "writing or some other material form" (defined in s 10(1) to include any form of storage, visible or not).
*   **Substantiality:** A non-statutory threshold. Trivial or de minimis works (e.g., short titles, single words, or simple geometric shapes) may be excluded as being too insubstantial to qualify as a "work" (*Exxon Corp v Exxon Insurance*).

### 2. Categories of Works
*   **Literary Works (s 10(1)):** Defined broadly to include anything in writing/print.
    *   *Compilations & Tables:* Protected if the selection or arrangement involves sufficient human intellectual effort (*IceTV*).
    *   *Computer Programs:* Defined as a set of statements or instructions used in a computer to bring about a result. Includes source and object code.
*   **Dramatic Works:** Includes choreography, dumb shows, and scenarios/scripts for films. It requires a "unity" capable of performance. Television formats are often difficult to protect unless sufficiently specific (*Green v Broadcasting Corp of NZ*).
*   **Musical Works:** Not defined by statute. Refers to the combination of melody and harmony. Lyrics are typically treated as separate literary works, though some debate exists regarding their integration into the musical work (*Boomerang Investments v Padgett*).
*   **Artistic Works (s 10(1)):** A closed list including:
    *   *Category (a):* Paintings, sculptures, drawings, engravings, and photographs (protected regardless of "artistic quality").
    *   *Category (b):* Works of architecture (buildings and models).
    *   *Category (c):* Works of artistic craftsmanship (requires a higher degree of aesthetic intent and "artist-craftsman" effort; *Burge v Swarbrick*).

### 3. Joint Authorship
Defined as a work produced by the **collaboration** of two or more authors where their contributions are not separate.
*   Requires a "common design" and significant authorial contribution to the expression, not just the ideas or preparatory work (*Kogan v Martin* (UK) contrasted with the stricter Australian approach in *IceTV*).

## Significance
Chapter 2 is fundamental to Australian copyright law as it defines the "copyright nouns"—the subject matter that the Act protects. It marks the critical transition in Australian jurisprudence from protecting "investment and labour" to protecting "authorial expression" following the High Court's decision in *IceTV*. Understanding these definitions is the first step in any copyright analysis, as different categories of works have different rules for ownership, duration, and infringement.
