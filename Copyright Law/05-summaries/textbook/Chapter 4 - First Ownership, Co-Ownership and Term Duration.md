# Chapter 4 - First Ownership, Co-Ownership and Term Duration

## Overview / Key Points

This chapter examines the threshold legal questions of copyright ownership and duration under the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth).

### 1. First Ownership of Works (Part III)
*   **Basic Rule (s 35(2)):** The author of a literary, dramatic, musical, or artistic work is the first owner of copyright. Authors must be natural persons.
*   **Employees (s 35(6)):** Copyright in works (excluding musical works) made by an employee in the course of employment under a contract of service vests in the employer, subject to agreement.
*   **Journalists (s 35(4)):** A "split" ownership rule applies. Journalists retain copyright for book publication and hard-copy facsimiles; the employer owns all other rights (e.g., digital/online).
*   **Commissioned Works (s 35(5)):** For specific commissions (photographs for private/domestic purposes, portraits, engravings), the commissioner owns the copyright. However, the author has a right to restrain use for purposes other than those communicated at the time of the contract.

### 2. First Ownership of Subject Matter Other Than Works (Part IV)
*   **Sound Recordings:** Generally owned by the "maker" (the owner of the master copy). Since 2005, performers are joint makers of recordings of live performances.
*   **Cinematograph Films:** Owned by the "maker" (the person who made the necessary arrangements, typically the producer).
*   **Broadcasts:** Owned by the person providing the broadcasting service.
*   **Published Editions:** Owned by the publisher.

### 3. Crown Ownership and International Organizations
*   **Crown Ownership (ss 176–178):** The Crown owns copyright in works, recordings, or films made "under its direction or control" or first published by it.
*   **Prerogative Rights:** The Act preserves the Crown's historical prerogative rights (e.g., over statutes), which are indefinite in duration.
*   **International Organizations:** Similar rules apply to declared organizations like the UN or WIPO.

### 4. Co-Ownership
*   **Tenancy in Common:** Co-owners generally hold copyright as tenants in common in equal shares (unless agreed otherwise).
*   **Consent for Use:** One co-owner cannot exploit the work or grant a licence without the consent of all other co-owners.
*   **Right to Sue:** Any co-owner can sue for infringement without joining others, but remedies (damages/account of profits) are awarded pro-rata.

### 5. Term Duration
*   **Standard Term:** For most works, the term is the life of the author plus 70 years.
*   **2017 Reforms (commenced 1 Jan 2019):** Abolished indefinite copyright for unpublished works.
    *   **Works made public:** 70 years from the year it was first made public.
    *   **Works not made public:** 70 years from the year of the author's death (or 70 years from making if the author is unknown).
*   **Part IV Subject Matter:**
    *   **Sound Recordings/Films:** 70 years from making public (if within 50 years of making), otherwise 70 years from making.
    *   **Broadcasts:** 50 years from the year of the broadcast.
    *   **Published Editions:** 25 years from the year of first publication.
*   **Crown Copyright:** Generally 50 years from making (a more restrictive term than standard copyright).

## Significance
Chapter 4 is critical for identifying who has the standing to sue for infringement and determining whether a work has entered the public domain. It highlights the shift from author-centric ownership to employer/commissioner-centric ownership in commercial contexts and the significant 2019 reforms that ended perpetual copyright for unpublished materials in Australia.
