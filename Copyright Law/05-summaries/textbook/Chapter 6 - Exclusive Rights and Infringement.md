# Chapter 6 - Exclusive Rights and Infringement

**Reference:** Swinson, J., & Swinson, D. (2023). *Copyright Law*.

## Overview
This chapter details the exclusive rights conferred by the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth), which function as the "verbs" of copyright law. These rights define the proprietary boundaries of copyright; any activity not falling within an exclusive right (e.g., private reading) is outside the scope of protection.

### Key Exclusive Rights
1.  **To Reproduce / To Copy (s 31, ss 85–88)**
    *   **Works (Part III):** The right is "to reproduce in a material form" (s 31(1)(a)(i)). This requires less exactitude than Part IV rights and can include non-mechanistic taking (e.g., plot architecture in *Allen v Rowling*).
    *   **Other Subject Matter (Part IV):** The right is "to make a copy" (ss 85, 86) or "facsimile copy" (s 88). This generally requires exact technical taking of the actual sounds or images (*CBS v Telmak*).
    *   **Material Form & RAM:** Following *Sony v Stevens* and 2004 reforms, "material form" includes temporary storage in RAM, even if the work cannot be further reproduced from it (s 10(1)).

2.  **To Publish (s 31(1)(a)(ii))**
    *   Applies only to Part III works.
    *   Defined as making a work public in Australia for the first time (*Avel v Multicoin*). It is an exhaustive right that can only be exercised once per territory.

3.  **To Perform in Public / Cause to be Seen or Heard in Public (s 31, ss 85–86)**
    *   Applies to works (except artistic) and Part IV films/recordings.
    *   **"In Public":** The touchstone is the commercial nature of the setting rather than audience size. Performances in motels (*Rank Film v Dodds*) or for employees (*APRA v CBA*) are considered "in public".

4.  **To Communicate to the Public (s 31, ss 85–87)**
    *   A technology-neutral right introduced in 2001 to cover "push" (broadcasting) and "pull" (on-demand/internet) transmissions.
    *   **Liability:** Primary liability rests with the person responsible for "determining the content" (s 22(6)). ISPs and users clicking links are generally excluded (s 22(6A)).
    *   **"To the Public":** Includes transmissions to a geographically dispersed audience, even if received individually (e.g., music-on-hold in *APRA v Telstra*).

5.  **To Make an Adaptation (s 31(1)(a)(vi))**
    *   Applies to literary, dramatic, and musical works.
    *   Includes translations, dramatizations, and musical arrangements. For computer programs, it includes "versions" that are not reproductions (e.g., decompilation).

6.  **Commercial Rental Right (ss 30A, 31(1)(c), 85(1)(d))**
    *   Applies to sound recordings and computer programs.
    *   A "dead-letter" right in the internet age, primarily intended to prevent commercial lending for the purpose of home copying.

### Key Principles of Infringement
*   **Direct Infringement:** Requires a coincidence of legally recognized subject matter, ownership, and the exercise of an exclusive right without permission.
*   **Substantial Part (s 14):** Any exercise of an exclusive right must relate to a "substantial part" of the subject matter, assessed qualitatively and quantitatively (*IceTV*).
*   **Joint Exercise:** In cloud/digital settings, rights may be exercised jointly by the service provider and the user (*NRL v Singtel Optus*).

## Significance
Chapter 6 is critical for understanding the scope of copyright protection in Australia. It shifts the focus from *what* is protected (subject matter) to *what actions* are prohibited. The chapter highlights how the law has evolved to remain technology-neutral (e.g., the communication right) while maintaining historical distinctions between Part III works (authorial) and Part IV subject matter (entrepreneurial). It also underscores the importance of the "substantial part" test as the primary filter for infringement across all exclusive rights.
