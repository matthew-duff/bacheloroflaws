[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/Galaxy Electronics Pty Ltd v Sega Enterprises Ltd (1997) 75 FCR 8]]

**Citation**: (1997) 75 FCR 8

### Facts
Sega (manufacturer) and Avel (exclusive Australian licensee) held rights in two video games, "Virtua Cop" and "Daytona USA Twin". The games presented on-screen moving images resembling a traditional movie (e.g., a police story or car racing). Each game required player interaction via input devices so that events varied according to player responses, though within a limited range of scripted variants. Galaxy and Gottlieb imported machines containing copies of the computer programs that generated these games and displayed them for sale or hire. The images were generated in real time from stored mathematical coordinates and 3D models rather than pre-recorded frames. Section 24(2) of the *Circuit Layouts Act 1989* (Cth) precluded protection of the games as computer programs under Pt III of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth), so the question was whether they qualified as cinematograph films under Pt IV.

### Issue
Whether the series of computer-generated visual images produced by video games constitute "cinematograph films" within the meaning of s 10 of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth); specifically: (1) whether "embodied" requires pre-existing frames in a film-like form; (2) whether the Act requires a choice between classification as cinematograph film or computer program; and (3) whether variable player-dependent sequences satisfy the requirement of "publication".

### Held
Yes. The Full Court dismissed the appeals and held that the video games constitute cinematograph films. The appellants' conduct amounted to infringement of the respondents' copyright in the games.

### Reasoning
- **Wide construction of "cinematograph film"**: The definition in ss 10 and 24 should be construed widely to cover new technologies; there is no implied requirement for "frames" like traditional film.
- **No mutually exclusive classification**: The Act does not require choosing between cinematograph film and computer program. The visual images are the *product* of the program; the program is a set of instructions, while the film is the aggregate of images. Different subject matter can attract different forms of copyright.
- **Meaning of "embodied"**: "Embodied" means giving material or discernible form to an abstract principle or concept. The abstraction must pre-exist the material manifestation.
- **Pre-existence of images**: The images existed before play—in the minds of the creators and in their drawings and models. They were embodied in the computer program (in digital form, e.g., 3D vertices) so as to be capable of being shown as a moving picture. It is irrelevant that the images appear in a different form (e.g., digital data) or are computed immediately before display. US authority (*Stern Electronics Inc v Kaufman*; *Midway Mfg Co v Artie International Inc*) supported treating interactive video game displays as audiovisual works.
- **s 24 requirement**: The article must have been "treated in relation to" specific sounds or visual images. A computer program is so treated; a piano capable of playing Beethoven is not, because the keyboard is not designed "in relation to" those particular sounds.
- **Publication**: All potential visual images are embodied in the integrated circuits; there is a single time of publication for all of them.

### Significance
This case establishes that **computer-generated video game images can be protected as cinematograph films** under Australian copyright law, even where: (1) images are generated in real time rather than stored as pre-recorded frames; (2) the sequence varies according to player input within predetermined limits; and (3) the images exist in digital form (e.g., 3D coordinates) rather than traditional film or video. It provides a pathway for Pt IV protection where Pt III protection for computer programs is excluded by the *Circuit Layouts Act*. The decision reflects a purposive, technology-neutral approach to the definition of "cinematograph film".
