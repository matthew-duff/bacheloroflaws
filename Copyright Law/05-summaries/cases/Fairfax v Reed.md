[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/Fairfax Media Publications Pty Ltd v Reed International Books Australia Pty Ltd (2010) 189 FCR 109]]

**Citation**: (2010) 189 FCR 109; [2010] FCA 984

### Facts
The applicant (Fairfax) publishes *The Australian Financial Review* (AFR). The respondent (Reed) operates "ABIX", a subscription service providing abstracts of newspaper articles—comprising the article headline, by-line, and a short summary written by Reed employees. Reed typically provided abstracts of 40–60% of AFR articles each day, often without altering the headline. Fairfax alleged Reed infringed its copyright in several works: (1) each individual headline; (2) each article including its headline and by-line (Article/Headline Combination); (3) the compilation of all articles in an edition (Article Compilation); and (4) each entire edition (Edition Work). Reed denied infringement and contended, among other things, that headlines were too insubstantial for copyright protection and that Fairfax had not established originality. Fairfax sought to rely on the presumption of originality in s 129(2) of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth) on the basis that the authors of the headlines were anonymous and could not be ascertained by reasonable inquiry.

### Issue
Whether copyright subsists in newspaper headlines, Article/Headline Combinations, compilations, and edition works; whether Reed takes a substantial part of any such works; and whether the presumption of originality in s 129(2) applies where an employer has chosen not to identify the authors.

### Held
Application dismissed. (1) Individual headlines are generally too insubstantial and too short to qualify as literary works in which copyright subsists. (2) Fairfax failed to establish that Article/Headline Combinations were discrete works of joint authorship. (3) Copyright subsists in the Article Compilation and Edition Work, but Reed did not take a substantial part of those works by reproducing headlines in its abstracts. (4) The onus under s 129(2) to establish that the author's identity cannot be ascertained by reasonable inquiry lies on the party alleging anonymity; Fairfax could not invoke the presumption where it had chosen not to identify authors who were ascertainable. (5) Reed's conduct would have been fair dealing for the purpose of reporting news under s 42(1)(b).

### Reasoning
- **Headlines**: Bennett J held that headlines function as titles of articles. Following *Francis, Day & Hunter Ltd v Twentieth Century Fox Corporation Ltd* [1940] AC 112 and other authorities, titles (and analogously headlines) are generally too insubstantial and too short to qualify as literary works, regardless of skill or labour expended. Public policy favours freedom to refer to articles by their headlines for bibliographic, citation and news-reporting purposes. Even clever or punning headlines did not overcome the established practice of denying copyright protection to titles.
- **s 129(2) presumption**: The onus of establishing that the author's identity is not generally known or cannot be ascertained by reasonable inquiry rests on the party claiming anonymity. The test is not confined to inquiry "external to the author or their employer". Where an employer has chosen not to identify employee-authors who could be ascertained from its own records, the presumption is not available.
- **Article/Headline Combination**: Joint authorship could not be established without identifying the authors; the presumption under s 129(2) did not apply.
- **Substantial part**: Even though copyright subsisted in the Article Compilation and Edition Work, reproducing headlines in the abstracts did not amount to taking a substantial part of those compilations.
- **Fair dealing**: Reed's use of headlines in abstracts for reporting news would constitute fair dealing under s 42(1)(b).

### Significance
**Fairfax v Reed** confirms that **newspaper headlines are not protected by copyright** as a class, being analogous to titles and too insubstantial to qualify as literary works under Australian law. It reinforces the public interest in being able to identify and refer to articles by their headlines. The case also clarifies that the onus under s 129(2) for the presumption of originality in anonymous works lies on the party alleging anonymity, and that an employer cannot invoke the presumption when it has simply chosen not to identify ascertainable employee-authors.
