[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/Sands and McDougall Pty Ltd v Robinson (1917) 23 CLR 4 9]]

**Citation**: (1917) 23 CLR 49

### Facts
The respondent (Robinson) produced a map of Europe by taking the common stock of geographical information in Australia and applying personal, independent intellectual effort—judgment and discrimination—to it. The map was new in the sense that, in size, outlines, contents, arrangement, and general appearance, it presented distinct differences from other existing maps, both in its totality and in specific parts. The appellants (Sands & McDougall) produced a map that was not a mere copy in the ordinary sense but was clearly a reproduction of a substantial part of Robinson's map. They had made changes (e.g., colours, corrected Balkan boundaries, some updated places) but the appellants' map still manifested a wholesale adoption of Robinson's individual work in size, draftsmanship, style, printing type, geographical selection, and general appearance.

### Issue
Whether "original" in s 1(1) of the Copyright Act 1911 (Schedule to the Copyright Act 1912) requires inventive originality (novelty as in patent law), or merely the kind of authorship that originates the work; and whether Sands & McDougall had infringed Robinson's copyright. A secondary issue was whether s 8 limited Robinson's remedy to an injunction only where the defendant proves it had no reasonable ground for suspecting copyright subsisted.

### Held
The respondent's map was an original literary work within s 1(1) and entitled to copyright. The appellants had infringed that copyright. The appellants had not proved they were unaware and had no reasonable ground for suspecting copyright subsisted, so the respondent's remedy was not limited to an injunction under s 8.

### Reasoning
- **"Original" does not mean inventive originality**: Isaacs J held that the word "original" in s 1(1) does not imply inventive originality. It has the meaning connoted by "author" in s 5—the author "originates or gives existence" to the work.
- **Correlative terms**: "Author" and "original work" are correlative; the one connotes the other. The Act did not introduce a separate patent-law style novelty requirement.
- **Walter v Lane explained**: That case did not require inventive originality; the reporter was an "author" because he produced something that did not exist before through his skill, labour, and expense. The work was "original" in the sense of being originated by him.
- **Application to Robinson's map**: Robinson applied personal intellectual effort to common information and produced a map with distinct characteristics and individuality—sufficient for originality.
- **Infringement**: The appellants' map was a reproduction of a substantial part of Robinson's map in a material form; the differences did not negate the wholesale adoption of Robinson's individual work.

### Significance
This case is a foundational authority on the meaning of **"original" in Australian copyright law**. It establishes that originality does **not** require novelty, invention, or patent-law style distinctiveness. Originality is satisfied when the author originates the work through their own skill, labour, and judgment applied to existing materials. The decision is critical for maps, compilations, directories, and other works based on pre-existing information.
