[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/Phonographic Performance Co of Australia Ltd v Federation of Australian Commercial Television Stations (1998) 195 CLR 158]]

**Citation**: (1998) 195 CLR 158

### Facts
Copyright owners in sound recordings (including record companies such as BMG Australia Ltd) had licensed filmmakers to embody their recordings in the sound-tracks of cinematograph films. Examples included "Dancing in the Storm" by Boom Crash Opera in *The Big Steal*, and "Everybody Wants to Rule the World" by Tears for Fears in *Peter's Friends*. Commercial television stations broadcast these films with the licence of the film copyright owners, but not with the licence of the sound recording copyright owners. PPCA (representing sound recording copyright owners) applied to the Copyright Tribunal under s 152 for a determination of the amount payable for broadcasting those sound recordings. FACTS (representing the television broadcasters) contended that broadcasting a film did not amount to broadcasting the sound recordings, because s 23(1) deems sounds embodied in a film sound-track "not to be a sound recording" for the purposes of the Act.

### Issue
When a cinematograph film incorporating pre-existing sound recordings in its sound-track is broadcast, does that constitute a "broadcast" of those sound recordings for the purposes of s 85(1)(c) of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth)? Or does s 23(1), which deems sounds in a film sound-track not to be a sound recording, mean that broadcasting the film does not broadcast the sound recordings and thus does not attract obligations to the sound recording copyright owners?

### Held
Yes (3:2—Gaudron, Gummow and Hayne JJ; McHugh and Kirby JJ dissenting). Broadcasting a cinematograph film that contains sound recordings in its sound-track **does** constitute a broadcast of those sound recordings for the purposes of s 85(1)(c). The television broadcasters must therefore pay (or comply with a Tribunal determination under s 152) in respect of the sound recordings embodied in the films they broadcast.

### Reasoning
- **Limited purpose of s 23(1)**: Section 23(1) prevents a filmmaker from acquiring a *separate* copyright in the sound-track as a sound recording (i.e., avoids dual copyright in the same sounds). It does not, however, mean that pre-existing sound recordings cease to exist or that the copyright owner's rights are extinguished when sounds are embodied in a film sound-track.
- **Focus on sounds, not medium**: The exclusive right in s 85(1)(c) relates to broadcasting the *sounds* embodied in a record, not the physical record itself. Section 23(1) addresses only a particular *form* of record (the sound-track). When a film is broadcast, the aggregate of sounds constituting each sound recording is broadcast.
- **Distinction**: What is deemed "not to be a sound recording" is the aggregate of sounds as recorded in that particular form (the sound-track). That does not deny that the same aggregate of sounds, which constitutes a pre-existing sound recording, is broadcast when the film is transmitted.
- **Policy**: To hold otherwise would significantly impair the rights of sound recording copyright owners and require them to rely on film copyright owners to enforce their rights, reducing the scope of s 85.
- **s 110(3)**: That provision (which says using a record not derived from the film sound-track does not infringe film copyright) does not support the broadcasters' construction; it addresses film copyright infringement, not sound recording copyright.

### Significance
Establishes that **sound recording copyright owners retain broadcast rights** when their recordings are incorporated into film sound-tracks. Television broadcasters (and, by analogy, others who broadcast films) must obtain licences or pay statutory royalties in respect of sound recordings embodied in films, not merely in respect of the film copyright. The case clarifies the limited scope of s 23(1): it prevents dual copyright in sound-tracks but does **not** extinguish the original sound recording copyright owner's exclusive right to broadcast under s 85(1)(c).
