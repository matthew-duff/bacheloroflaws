[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/Telstra Corporation Ltd v Phone Directories Company Pty Ltd (2010) 194 FCR 142]]

**Citation**: (2010) 194 FCR 142

### Facts
Telstra Corporation Ltd and Sensis Pty Ltd published White Pages Directories (WPD) and Yellow Pages Directories (YPD) listing subscribers' names, addresses, telephone numbers and related information. They alleged that Phone Directories Company Pty Ltd had infringed their copyright in these directories. The production process involved the Genesis Computer System and its predecessors. Human contributions included gathering listing information (from various sources, with ~15% manually entered); maintaining the database; and some manual checking, pagination and editing. However, the compilation of each directory—the selection, ordering and arrangement of listings in the form in which they appeared in the published directory—occurred at the automated "book extract" and "book production" stages. The "fixing" of the work in material form (the galley file) first occurred through these automated processes. Telstra claimed copyright in each directory as an "original literary work" first published in Australia under s 32(2)(c) of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth).

### Issue
Whether copyright subsists in the White Pages and Yellow Pages directories as original literary works where they are largely computer-generated, with human contributions being anterior to the directories being reduced to material form.

### Held
No. Appeal dismissed. Copyright does not subsist in the directories. They are not original literary works because they were not compiled by human authors—the compilation was essentially automated.

### Reasoning
- **Human authorship required**: Copyright subsists only in works that originate from one or more human authors. The Act protects authorship; skill and labour alone do not attract protection (*IceTV Pty Ltd v Nine Network Australia Pty Ltd* (2009) 239 CLR 458 applied).
- **Focus on fixation**: Much skill and "sweat of the brow" may attend preparatory steps, but copyright protects the fixation of the work in material form. The relevant authorial contribution must be directed at the reduction of the work to that form.
- **Anterior contributions insufficient**: Human contributions in gathering data, operating systems and checking were anterior to the directories being reduced to material form. The compilation—selection, arrangement and presentation—was performed by the computer system at the book extract stage. None of the individuals who contributed had any conception of the final form of the directories.
- **Desktop Marketing distinguished**: *Desktop Marketing Systems Pty Ltd v Telstra Corporation Ltd* (2002) 119 FCR 491, which upheld copyright in telephone directories on a "sweat of the brow" basis, was distinguished. IceTV established that productive effort must be directed to the particular form of expression; Desktop Marketing did not address that requirement.
- **s 128 presumption**: The presumption of copyright subsistence under s 128 does not operate where evidence adduced establishes that copyright does not subsist in the work.

### Significance
This case **overturns the Desktop Marketing approach** to copyright in compilations. It establishes that **computer-generated compilations are not protected by copyright** in Australia and that the "sweat of the brow" doctrine does not apply. Copyright requires human authorship directed at the production of the work in its material form. The decision is critical for understanding subsistence of copyright in databases, directories and other compilations produced by automated processes.
