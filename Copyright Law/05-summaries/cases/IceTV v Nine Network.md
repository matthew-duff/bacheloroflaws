[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/IceTV Pty Ltd v Nine Network Australia Pty Ltd (2009) 239 CLR 458]]

**Citation**: (2009) 239 CLR 458

### Facts
Nine Network produced weekly television programme schedules from its database, including programme times, titles, synopses, classification, and other information. These schedules were supplied to aggregators who combined them with other broadcasters' data to produce aggregated guides. IceTV operated a subscription-based electronic programme guide (IceGuide) that displayed programme details for free-to-air TV. When preparing the IceGuide, IceTV employees predicted date, time and title based on previous weeks' broadcasts and used the aggregated guides to check and correct discrepancies. Nine alleged that IceTV indirectly reproduced a substantial part of its weekly schedules by taking the time and title information from the aggregated guides. IceTV conceded copyright subsisted in the weekly schedules but denied infringement.

### Issue
Whether IceTV's use of time and title information in the IceGuide constituted reproduction of a "substantial part" of Nine's weekly schedules (compilations) within the meaning of s 14(1)(b) of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth).

### Held
No. The High Court allowed IceTV's appeal. The reproduction of time and title information was **not** a reproduction of a substantial part of the weekly schedules. No copyright infringement occurred.

### Reasoning
- **Copyright protects form of expression, not facts**: Copyright in a compilation protects the particular form of expression—the words, figures and symbols used and the selection and arrangement of information—not the underlying facts or information themselves.
- **Qualitative assessment of substantiality**: In assessing whether a substantial part has been reproduced, the whole work must be considered and the quality of what was taken assessed by reference to originality in the expression of that part.
- **Time and title information is factual**: The time and title information was dictated by its factual nature. The form of expression for such information was minimal and essentially dictated by the nature of the information itself.
- **Composite whole**: Each weekly schedule was a composite whole including synopses, additional programme information and the arrangement of elements. Divorcing the factual time and title information from the synopses and other creative elements, the taking of such information alone was not a reproduction of a substantial part in the qualitative sense.
- **Limits on "skill and labour" analysis**: The Court cautioned against over-reliance on "misappropriation of skill and labour" and the emphasis in *Desktop Marketing Systems Pty Ltd v Telstra Corporation Ltd* (2002) 119 FCR 491 on "labour and expense" per se. Notions of skill and labour are not the statutory test; the Act does not protect misappropriation of skill and labour alone.

### Significance
IceTV significantly clarifies the scope of copyright protection for **compilations** in Australian law. It establishes that copyright protects the **form of expression** of compilations—not the underlying facts or information—and that taking factual information (such as programme times and titles) does not necessarily amount to reproducing a substantial part. The decision casts doubt on the broad "sweat of the brow" approach in *Desktop Marketing*, suggesting that reasoning in that case may be "out of line" with copyright law's traditional focus on originality in expression. IceTV is critical for understanding the balance between protecting compilers' investment and allowing legitimate use of factual information, particularly for electronic programme guides and similar compilations of scheduling data.
