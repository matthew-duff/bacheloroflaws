[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/Burge v Swarbrick (2007) 232 CLR 336]]

**Citation**: *Burge v Swarbrick* (2007) 232 CLR 336

**Facts**: 
The respondent (Swarbrick), a naval architect, designed a racing yacht (JS 9000). In the process, he hand-crafted a full-scale model of the hull and deck known as "the Plug". The appellants used mouldings from the Plug to manufacture their own yacht. Swarbrick claimed copyright infringement, asserting that the Plug and the resulting mouldings were "works of artistic craftsmanship" under s 10 of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth). He had not registered the design under the *Designs Act 1906* (Cth).

**Issue**: 
Whether the Plug or the hull and deck mouldings constituted a "work of artistic craftsmanship", thereby exempting them from the "design-copyright overlap" provisions in s 77 which would otherwise strip them of copyright protection due to industrial application.

**Held**: 
The High Court held that the Plug and mouldings were **not** works of artistic craftsmanship. Consequently, because the design had been applied industrially without registration, s 77 applied to deny copyright protection against three-dimensional reproduction.

**Reasoning**: 
1. **Composite Phrase**: "Work of artistic craftsmanship" is a composite phrase to be construed as a whole, focusing on the extent to which artistic expression is unconstrained by functional considerations.
2. **Functional Constraints**: The court established that the more a work's form is dictated by functional or utilitarian requirements (the "design brief"), the less scope there is for the "real or substantial artistic effort" required for artistic craftsmanship.
3. **Objective Test**: Determination is an objective assessment by the court, not solely dependent on the author's subjective intent or the aesthetic appeal/beauty of the work.
4. **Application**: In this case, the design of a "sports boat" was heavily subordinated to engineering and functional requirements (speed, stability, etc.). The visual appeal was secondary to these functional constraints, meaning the designer was acting as an engineer/stylist rather than an artist-craftsman.

**Significance**: 
This is the leading Australian authority on the definition of "works of artistic craftsmanship". it clarifies the "design-copyright overlap" by providing a test centered on **functional constraint**: where function dictates form, the work is likely a design (requiring registration) rather than a work of artistic craftsmanship (protected by copyright).
