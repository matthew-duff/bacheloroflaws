[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/Burge v Swarbrick (2007) 232 CLR 336]]

_Burge v Swarbrick_ [2007] HCA 17; (2007) 232 CLR 336

---

## Facts:

- **The Parties:** John Swarbrick, a naval architect, designed the JS 9000, a high-performance racing yacht. The appellants were former employees and a company (Boldgold) that produced a yacht using mouldings derived from Swarbrick’s design.
    
- **The Work:** During the design process, Swarbrick hand-crafted "the Plug"—a full-scale model of the hull and deck. This Plug was used to create moulds, which in turn produced the hull and deck mouldings.
    
- **The Dispute:** Swarbrick alleged copyright infringement. He did not have a registered design under the _Designs Act 1906_ (Cth). He argued the Plug and mouldings were "works of artistic craftsmanship."
    
- **Design Constraints:** The JS 9000 was marketed as a "sports boat." Evidence showed the design was heavily dictated by functional requirements: speed, ease of handling for a small crew, and transportability. Swarbrick, however, testified that he intended to create a vessel of "great aesthetic appeal."
    

---

## Procedural History:

- **Trial Court (Federal Court of Australia):** Carr J found in favor of Swarbrick. The court held that the Plug and mouldings were both "sculptures" and "works of artistic craftsmanship," thus exempt from the "anti-overlap" provisions of s 77 of the _Copyright Act_.
    
- **Full Court of the Federal Court:** The Full Court dismissed the appeal, largely upholding the trial judge's findings that the aesthetic intentions of the creator were paramount.
    
- **High Court of Australia:** The appellants were granted special leave to appeal to the High Court.
    

---

## Issue:

Whether a work (specifically a yacht hull prototype) qualifies as a "work of artistic craftsmanship" under s 10(c) of the _Copyright Act 1968_ (Cth) when its form is significantly dictated by functional and utilitarian considerations.

---

## Holding:

**No.** The High Court allowed the appeal, holding that the Plug was not a work of artistic craftsmanship. Consequently, the s 77 defense applied, and Swarbrick’s copyright claim failed because he had not registered the design.

---

## Rule of Law:

The determination of whether a work is a "work of artistic craftsmanship" does not depend on a judicial assessment of "beauty" or the author's stated "aesthetic intent." Instead, it turns on **the extent to which the work’s artistic expression is unconstrained by functional considerations.**

---

## Reasoning:

The High Court’s logic focused on the objective intersection of art and utility:

- **Composite Phrase:** "Work of artistic craftsmanship" must be construed as a single, composite phrase. It requires more than just "craftsmanship"; it requires a level of "artistic" effort that is not merely incidental to a functional goal.
    
- **The "Discrimen" (The Test):** The Court established that where design choices are "necessarily subordinated" to functional requirements (like hydrodynamics in a racing yacht), there is less scope for the "real or substantial artistic effort" required by the Act.
    
- **Rejection of Subjective Intent:** While the author's intent is admissible, it is not determinative. The Court favored an objective test. In this case, the design brief for the JS 9000 was dominated by engineering and market requirements (speed, cost, crew size).
    
- **Statutory Policy (The Overlap):** Sections 74–77 of the _Copyright Act_ aim to prevent "dual protection." Generally, industrial designs must be registered under the _Designs Act_ (offering shorter protection). The exception for "artistic craftsmanship" exists to protect "true" artist-craftsmen (like potters or silversmiths) whose work is not primary dictated by utility.
    
- **Application:** The Court found the Plug’s form was a result of naval architecture (engineering) rather than "artistic" expression. Because the functional constraints were substantial, the work fell into the category of industrial design rather than artistic craftsmanship.
