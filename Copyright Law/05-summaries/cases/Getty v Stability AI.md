[[Getty-Images-v-Stability-AI]]

**Citation:** *Getty Images (US) Inc & Ors v Stability AI Ltd* [2025] EWHC 2863 (Ch) (Mrs Justice Joanna Smith DBE, 4 November 2025).

**Facts:** Getty Images and related claimants sued Stability AI in the English High Court for primary and secondary copyright infringement, database rights, trade mark infringement, and passing off in relation to Stability’s generative AI image model “Stable Diffusion.” Getty’s case was that Stability had scraped and used millions of Getty’s images (and those of iStock and other group companies) from the internet to train Stable Diffusion without consent, and that the model could generate synthetic images bearing Getty’s and iStock’s watermarks. Before trial, Getty abandoned: (i) the “Training and Development Claim” (no evidence training occurred in the UK), (ii) the “Outputs Claim” (primary infringement by generated images, after Stability blocked the relevant prompts), and (iii) the database rights claim. The claim that remained included: (a) trade mark infringement (s 10(1), (2) and (3) TMA) and passing off based on synthetic images bearing Getty/iStock marks; and (b) secondary infringement of copyright (ss 22 and 23 CDPA) on the basis that Stable Diffusion, when imported/possessed/distributed in the UK, was an “infringing copy” because its making would have infringed copyright had it occurred in the UK (s 27(3) CDPA).

**Issue:** (1) Whether Stable Diffusion was an “infringing copy” for the purposes of secondary infringement (ss 22, 23, 27 CDPA). (2) Whether Stability was liable for trade mark infringement (and passing off) when users in the UK generated images containing Getty/iStock watermarks. (3) Subsidiary issues on copyright subsistence/ownership in sample works and whether certain contributor licences were exclusive under s 92 CDPA.

**Held:** (1) **Secondary infringement:** The claim failed. An “article” for ss 22 and 23 CDPA can be intangible (e.g. an electronic copy in the cloud), but an “infringing copy” must still be a *copy*—i.e. a reproduction of the copyright work. The model weights of Stable Diffusion do not store or contain the copyright works; they are the product of learned patterns and features. Therefore Stable Diffusion is not an “infringing copy” and there was no infringement under ss 22 or 23. (2) **Trade marks:** Getty succeeded only in part, on a narrow and largely historic basis: (a) s 10(1) TMA: infringement established only in respect of *iStock* watermarks generated by users of v1.x (via DreamStudio/Developer Platform), based on specific example images; claim in respect of Getty Images watermarks dismissed. (b) s 10(2) TMA: infringement established for iStock watermarks (v1.x, same access routes) and for Getty Images watermarks generated by v2.x (example: “First Japanese Temple Garden” image). (c) s 10(3) TMA: dismissed. No finding on passing off. No liability for SD XL or v1.6 (no evidence of UK users generating the relevant watermarks). (3) **Other:** Getty failed to make out title to copyright in two sample works (SOCI A3, A4); established title in SOCI A9, A10, A11. Some sample licences held not exclusive (e.g. #2, 3, 10, 11, 13, 30, 32); others held exclusive (#17, 19, 34–38). No finding on number of works used in training; claim for additional damages dismissed.

**Reasoning:** On secondary infringement, the court held that “infringing copy” takes its meaning from the Act’s concept of “copy” in s 17 (reproduction in a material form, including storage by electronic means). *Sony v Ball* showed that an article is an infringing copy only when it *contains* a copy (even transiently); once it no longer contains the copy, it ceases to be an infringing copy. Here the model never contained or stored the works—only learned parameters derived from training—so it was not a “copy” and could not be an “infringing copy.” On trade marks, the court found a “threshold issue” of incidence of synthetically generated watermarks was satisfied only for v1.x (and for Getty watermarks on v2.x) in the ways described; for later models (SD XL, v1.6) the evidence was contrived or unrepresentative. Liability under s 10(1) and 10(2) was then assessed by reference to identity/similarity of sign and mark, average consumer, and context; s 10(3) failed on the facts (e.g. no sufficient detriment or unfair advantage). Stability was not directly liable for release of v1.x via third-party GitHub/Hugging Face pages.

**Significance:** The case is a leading English authority on AI and copyright: it holds that a trained generative AI model whose weights do not store or reproduce copyright works is **not** an “infringing copy” under the CDPA, so secondary infringement (importation, possession, distribution, etc.) does not apply even if training used infringing copies elsewhere. It confirms that “article” can be intangible but that “copy” remains central. It also illustrates the limits of trade mark claims where AI outputs occasionally resemble a claimant’s marks (watermarks): liability was found only in narrow, version-specific circumstances and did not extend to later model versions or to all types of watermark. The judgment leaves open the possibility of primary infringement claims in the jurisdiction where training actually occurred.
