[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/Dais Studio Pty Ltd v Bullet Creative Pty Ltd (2007) 165 FCR 92]]

**Citation**: *Dais Studio Pty Ltd v Bullet Creative Pty Ltd* (2007) 165 FCR 92; [2007] FCA 2054

**Facts**:
The first applicant (Dais) designed websites and developed a Content Management System (CMS) called "WebStable" for its clients. The second respondent (Mr Petro) was a former employee of Dais. Dais alleged that after leaving his employment, Mr Petro downloaded two specific files from the CMS—a 'table file' (for table appearance) and an 'editor file' (for text formatting)—and used them to build websites for his own clients. Dais sued for copyright infringement, breach of confidence, and breach of the *Corporations Act*.

**Issue**:
1. Were the individual files ("table file" and "editor file") "computer programs" under s 10 of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth)?
2. If the CMS as a whole was the copyright work, did the reproduction of these two files constitute a "substantial part" of that work under s 14?

**Held**:
1. **Yes**. Each file was a "computer program" and thus a literary work. The fact that they required other software (like an internet browser) to function did not disqualify them from the definition in s 10.
2. **No**. The reproduction of the two files did not constitute a "substantial part" of the CMS. The application was dismissed.

**Reasoning**:
*   **Computer Program Definition**: The court distinguished *Data Access v Powerflex*, holding that a set of instructions is a "computer program" even if it requires the participation of other software components to bring about a result.
*   **Substantial Part**: The court applied both qualitative and quantitative tests. Qualitatively, Dais failed to show that the files involved a high degree of skill, labour, or judgment that made them "substantial" relative to the whole CMS. Quantitatively, the files represented less than 1% of the total source code of the CMS, which supported the finding that they were not a substantial part.
*   **Confidential Information/Corporations Act**: These claims failed because the information was not considered confidential in the industry context, and there was no breach of standards expected of a former subordinate employee.

**Significance**:
The case clarifies that individual files or modules within a larger software system can qualify as "computer programs" in their own right. However, it highlights the difficulty of proving copyright infringement when only small, functionally common components of a larger system are copied, emphasizing that "substantiality" is determined by the quality and originality of the specific code taken relative to the entire work.
