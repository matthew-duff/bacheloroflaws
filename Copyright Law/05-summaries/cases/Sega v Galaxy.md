[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/Sega Enterprises Ltd v Galaxy Electronics Pty Ltd (1996) 69 FCR 268]]

**Citation**: (1996) 69 FCR 268

### Facts
Sega Enterprises Ltd sued Galaxy Electronics Pty Ltd (and Gottlieb Electronics) for copyright infringement. The applicants' video games "Virtua Cop" and "Daytona USA" displayed computer-generated visual images as moving pictures. The images were **not** stored as traditional film frames; instead, mathematical co-ordinates of 3D models, animation data, and texture mapping data were stored digitally. The controlling program used these to generate images on screen in real-time in response to player inputs. Different game plays were possible within the overall limits of objects and scenes the program could generate. The respondents argued the visual images were synthesised "on the fly" and were not "embodied" in any article or thing. Integrated circuits were used in the games.

### Issue
Whether the video games fall within the definition of "cinematograph film" in s 10(1) of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth), as amplified by s 24. This was vital because if not, the applicants' case would fail (per *Avel Pty Ltd v Wells* (1992) 36 FCR 340).

### Held
Yes. The games were cinematograph films within the definition. The Circuit Layouts Act 1989 (Cth) did not exclude the application of the Copyright Act to the games.

### Reasoning
- **"Embodied"**: Burchett J gave "embodied" a broad meaning—giving material or discernible form to something abstract. Lord Cranworth in *Jefferys v Boosey* used it to mean an author giving a creation a form for continued existence and use. Writing is a code the brain interprets; the computer similarly interprets coded co-ordinates and input data as visual images on the screen.
- **Object of copyright**: The focus must be on the product (the visual images shown as a moving picture), not the means of production. The manuscript or medium is not the work—the work is the product of the mind (Lord Mansfield in *Millar v Taylor*).
- **Liberal construction**: Courts have taken a broad view of copyright legislation since *Bach v Longman*. Parliament intended cinematograph film not to be limited to any particular technology.
- **Legislative history**: The Gregory Report (1952) stated regard should be had to the "end-product" (sound or visual representation) rather than the "means whereby these effects are produced". The Attorney-General's second reading speech assimilated videotape to cinematograph film. The definition is expressed in terms of the result achieved, not the means employed.
- **s 24**: Visual images are taken to be "embodied" if they are "capable, with or without the aid of some other device, of being reproduced from the article or thing". The apparatus (with the player's input device) is capable of reproducing the aggregate of visual images as a moving picture. Variations from player input are within predetermined limits.
- **Frames not essential**: Confusing means with ends. Original technology used frames; computer graphics may achieve motion differently (e.g., pixels). "Moving pictures were once achieved only frame by frame; but now or in the future the motion may pour across the video screen in pixels."
- **Circuit Layouts Act**: The fact that integrated circuits attract that Act does not subtract from the further fact that their use brings the visual images to the screen as a moving picture—the Copyright Act still applies.

### Significance
Establishes that **computer-generated video games** (where images are generated in real-time from stored data in response to player input, rather than pre-stored as a "digital movie") can qualify as **cinematograph films** under the *Copyright Act 1968*. Critical for the copyright protection of video games. Adopts a technology-neutral, liberal approach focussing on the visual result (moving picture) rather than the storage or production technology used.
