[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/Federal Commissioner of Taxation v Seven Network Ltd (2016) 241 FCR 1]]

**Citation**: (2016) 241 FCR 1

### Facts
Between March 2006 and August 2008, Seven Network Ltd (Seven) paid the International Olympic Committee (IOC), a Swiss resident, approximately $97.7 million for exclusive Australian broadcasting rights to the Olympic Games. The payments were consideration for access to, and use of, a live digital signal (ITVR Signal) produced by host broadcasters and transmitted to Seven for television broadcast in Australia. The Commissioner of Taxation contended these payments were "royalties" subject to withholding tax under s 128B of the *Income Tax Assessment Act 1936* (Cth). Under s 17A(5) of the *International Tax Agreements Act 1953* (Cth), withholding tax did not apply unless the payments were royalties within the meaning of Art 12(3) of the Australia–Switzerland Double Taxation Agreement (Swiss Treaty). At the relevant time, Art 12(3) did not contain an equivalent to s 6(1)(db) of the ITAA 1936, which expressly included payments for use of visual images or sounds transmitted by satellite or cable in connection with television broadcasting.

### Issue
Whether payments for the use of a live digital television signal for broadcasting were "royalties" within Art 12(3) of the Swiss Treaty—specifically:
1. Whether copyright subsisted in a cinematograph film in the live digital signal;
2. Whether the payments were for use of "copyright ... or other like property or right"; and
3. Whether payment for use of a digital signal was a royalty because the payer might later acquire broadcast copyright in Australia.

### Held
Appeal dismissed. The payments were **not** royalties within Art 12(3) of the Swiss Treaty. Seven was not liable to withhold tax or pay penalties.

### Reasoning
- **Cinematograph film**: The digital signal was **not** a cinematograph film in which copyright subsisted. Under ss 10(1) and 24 of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth), a cinematograph film requires fixation and a material embodiment of an aggregation of visual images and sounds capable of being shown as a moving picture and of being reproduced. The ITVR Signal was transmitted virtually instantaneously; at any moment it carried only a tiny fraction of images and sounds. There was no recording or permanent storage in the signal or cable; images and sounds could not be *reproduced* from the signal (only *produced* by a receiving device). Following *Network Ten Pty Ltd v TCN Channel Nine Pty Ltd* (2004) 218 CLR 273 (*The Panel*), the definition turns on fixation and material embodiment—not ephemeral transmission. *Galaxy Electronics* and *Stevens* required storage in fixed, material form.
- **Other like property or right**: A "like right" in Art 12(3) means an **intellectual property right** recognised as such by the intellectual property laws of a Contracting State. The phrase "copyright, patent, design or model, plan, secret formula or process, trade-mark, or other like property or right" enumerates a class of intellectual property rights. It does not extend to commercially valuable monopoly rights or freedoms that are merely "functionally equivalent" to copyright but are not recognised IP rights under domestic law. The 2013 amendment to the Swiss Treaty to add a paragraph equivalent to s 6(1)(db) confirmed that such payments were not previously covered.
- **Future copyright**: A payment for use of a digital signal does **not** become a royalty because the recipient may later acquire copyright in broadcasts it makes. A royalty is payment for a licence or permission granted by the owner of a monopoly or property right in respect of that property. Seven acquired broadcast copyright by its own broadcasting activities under the *Copyright Act*; the payment was not for an act comprised in copyright owned by another.
- **Forbearance**: The Commissioner's alternative argument that the payment was for forbearance of third-party copyright rights was rejected: only Seven (as a licensed broadcaster) had the legal right to broadcast in Australia; the host broadcaster and IOC did not hold broadcasting licences and had no relevant copyright to forbear.

### Significance
This case is significant for Copyright Law because it **clarifies the scope of "cinematograph film"** and the requirement of **fixation and material embodiment**: a live, ephemeral digital television signal does **not** constitute a cinematograph film. It affirms that copyright subject matter (such as cinematograph films) requires fixation in a material, tangible form, not transitory electromagnetic transmission. It also confirms that "other like property or right" in double taxation treaty contexts refers to **intellectually property rights recognised by domestic law**, not merely commercially analogous monopolies. The decision illustrates the interaction between copyright law and tax law in characterising payments for access to broadcast signals.
