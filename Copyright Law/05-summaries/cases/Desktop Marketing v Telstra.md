[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/Desktop Marketing Systems Pty Ltd v Telstra Corp Ltd (2002) 119 FCR 491]]

**Citation**: [2002] FCAFC 112, (2002) 119 FCR 491

### Facts
Telstra claimed copyright in its White Pages and Yellow Pages telephone directories (as published compilations under s 32(2) of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth)) and in its headings books (as unpublished compilations under s 32(1)). Desktop produced three CD-ROM products containing subscriber listing data (names, addresses, telephone numbers) taken largely from Telstra’s directories. Desktop’s products were electronic databases with different order, layout, and presentation from the printed directories. At first instance, the judge found for Telstra on both subsistence and infringement. Desktop appealed.

### Issue
Whether copyright subsists in factual compilations (telephone directories and headings books) when the compiler has incurred substantial labour or expense in gathering data, even without appreciable skill or intellectual effort in selection or arrangement; whether “whole-of-universe” compilations can attract copyright; and whether Desktop infringed despite the different format and presentation of its products.

### Held
Appeal dismissed. Copyright subsisted in Telstra’s White Pages, Yellow Pages, and headings books. Desktop infringed that copyright.

### Reasoning
- **Originality**: Originality does not require novelty, inventiveness, creativity, or literary merit. A factual compilation may be an original literary work if the compiler has either (a) exercised skill, judgment or knowledge in selecting, presenting or arranging material, or (b) undertaken substantial labour or incurred substantial expense in collecting the information. The test is one of fact and degree.
- **Labour and expense**: Labour and expense in collecting information can establish originality even when directed at gathering data rather than presenting or arranging it. Telstra’s “collecting/receiving” of subscriber data, given its systems and obligations, was sufficient. The fact that Telstra receives data under statutory obligations does not deny copyright.
- **Whole-of-universe compilations**: Compilations that list all items in a given class (e.g. all subscribers) can attract copyright; *Feist Publications Inc v Rural Telephone Service Co Inc* (1991) 499 US 340 was distinguished.
- **Infringement**: Infringement turns on taking a “substantial part” of the work, assessed by reference to the originality of what is taken. Where originality lies in labour or expense in collecting data, formal or visual similarity is not required. Desktop took a substantial part by copying names, addresses and telephone numbers; its different layout and format did not avoid infringement.

### Significance
The case establishes the **“industrious collection”** or **“sweat of the brow”** doctrine in Australian copyright law: factual compilations can attract copyright where there is substantial labour or expense in collecting data, without a requirement for creativity or intellectual effort in selection or arrangement. It diverges from the US *Feist* approach and is central to the protection of databases, directories, and other factual compilations in Australia.
