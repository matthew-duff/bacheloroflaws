[[Copyright Law/03-sources/cases/Krueger Transport Equipment Pty Ltd v Glen Cameron Storage and Distribution Pty Ltd [2008] FCA 803]]

**Citation**: [2008] FCA 803

### Facts
Krueger (a trailer manufacturer) designed a load restraint system for curtain-sided trailers carrying double-stacked pallets—the "Slide-A-Gate" concept featuring sliding side posts with lightweight gates attached to each post. Krueger disclosed this design concept to Glen Cameron Storage & Distribution ("Camerons") at a meeting on 2 December 2002, when both were responding to Amcor's tender for distribution services. Krueger contended that Camerons agreed to keep the design confidential. Camerons subsequently disclosed the concept to Vawdrey (a competitor manufacturer), and Vawdrey produced drawings incorporating the same design. Camerons won the Amcor tender using Krueger's drawings but awarded the trailer supply contract to Vawdrey, who built 22 trailers according to engineering drawings substantially similar to Krueger's Subsequent Krueger Drawings (seven posts with gates of nearly identical size and shape).

### Issue
1. Whether Camerons breached an equitable or contractual obligation of confidence by disclosing Krueger's design to Vawdrey.
2. Whether Krueger held copyright in the Krueger Sketch and Krueger Drawings, and whether Vawdrey (and Camerons as authoriser) infringed that copyright by reproducing a substantial part.
3. Whether Vawdrey's cross-claim for unjustified threats under s 202 of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth) should succeed.

### Held
- **Breach of confidence**: Yes. Camerons breached both an equitable and contractual obligation of confidence. Krueger would have won the contract but for the wrongful disclosure.
- **Copyright**: Copyright subsisted in both the Krueger Sketch and Krueger Drawings as original artistic works. However, infringement of the Krueger Sketch was not made out (only the unprotected concept was copied in similar form). Vawdrey did infringe copyright in the Subsequent Krueger Drawings by producing the Vawdrey Engineering Drawings, which reproduced a substantial part. Camerons authorised that infringement and was also liable under s 36.
- **Cross-claim**: Dismissed.

### Reasoning
- **Breach of confidence (Coco elements)**:
  - *Quality of confidence*: The Krueger Concept (gates hanging off sliding posts, overlapping/stackable, rigidly attached) was not routine or in the public domain; confidentiality does not require patent-level novelty.
  - *Obligation*: The circumstances imported an equitable obligation; the court also found an express oral agreement to keep quotations and technical information confidential (and that it was a reasonable restraint of trade).
  - *Unauthorised use and detriment*: The court inferred from objective evidence (timing of calls, drawings, annotations, and requests to amend Vawdrey's design to match Krueger's) that Tindal disclosed the Krueger Concept to Paul Vawdrey during a phone call on 4 December 2002, and that but for this disclosure Krueger would have won the contract.
- **Copyright (idea vs expression)**:
  - Copyright protects the *form of expression*, not ideas or concepts: *Baker v Selden*, *Desktop Marketing*.
  - The Krueger Sketch expressed little more than the concept; the Vawdrey drawings were detailed technical works in a different form—no substantial reproduction of protected expression.
  - The Krueger Drawings contained a distinct expression of the concept. Vawdrey had *indirect access* through Camerons' disclosure and subsequent oral instructions to align Vawdrey's design with Krueger's (analogous to *LED Builders*). The choice of seven posts and gate size was not dictated by the concept; the Vawdrey Engineering Drawings used substantially the same form of expression as the Subsequent Krueger Drawings and were objectively similar.
- **Authorisation**: Camerons authorised Vawdrey's infringing acts within the meaning of s 36: *Barrett Property v Metricon Homes*.

### Significance
This case illustrates the **idea–expression dichotomy** in Australian copyright law: copyright subsisted in the engineering drawings but not in the hand sketch because the sketch conveyed mainly the unprotected concept. It also demonstrates **indirect copyright infringement** based on inferred access and causal connection—the court found copying where an intermediary (Camerons) disclosed the work and gave instructions to bring the defendant's design into line with the copyright holder's. The case reinforces that breach of confidence can protect commercially sensitive design information even where the design is not novel in a patent sense, and that liability for breach of confidence and copyright infringement may arise on overlapping facts.
