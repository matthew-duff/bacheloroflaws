[[Copyright Law/05-summaries/cases/Feist v Rural Telephone]]

**Citation**: *Feist Publications, Inc. v Rural Telephone Service Co.*, 499 U.S. 340 (1991)

### Facts
Rural was a telephone utility in Kansas, required by state regulation to publish an annual directory (white pages and yellow pages). The white pages listed subscribers' names, towns, and telephone numbers in alphabetical order. Feist published area-wide directories covering multiple service areas and sought licences from 11 telephone companies; Rural refused. Feist used Rural's white pages without consent, verifying and adding data (e.g., street addresses). 1,309 listings in Feist's directory were identical to Rural's, including four fictitious "trap" listings Rural had inserted to detect copying. Rural sued for copyright infringement. The District Court and Tenth Circuit held the directory copyrightable and ruled for Rural; the Supreme Court granted certiorari.

### Issue
Are telephone directory white pages (names, towns, telephone numbers) copyrightable? Does Feist's use of that information constitute infringement?

### Held
Rural's white pages are not entitled to copyright. Feist's use of the listings does not constitute infringement. Reversed (Court of Appeals).

### Reasoning
- **Originality is constitutionally required**: Under Art I § 8 cl 8, "authors" and "writings" presuppose independent creation plus a modicum of creativity. Facts do not owe their origin to authorship—they are discovered, not created.
- **Compilations**: Compilations of facts may be copyrightable only if the selection, coordination, or arrangement of the facts is original (independently made and minimally creative) (*Copyright Act 1976*, §§ 101, 102).
- **Rejection of "sweat of the brow"**: Copyright rewards originality, not labour. Effort or "industrious collection" alone does not create copyright.
- **Application to Rural**: Rural's raw data (names, towns, numbers) are uncopyrightable facts. The "selection" (all subscribers, as required by law) and "arrangement" (alphabetical by surname) lacked any minimal creative spark—they were obvious and routine. Therefore the white pages did not meet constitutional or statutory requirements for copyright.

### Significance
Key US authority establishing that **originality** (independent creation + minimal creativity) is constitutionally required for copyright. **Facts are never copyrightable**; compilations are protected only to the extent of **original selection, coordination, or arrangement**. Alphabetical listing of raw factual data does not meet that standard. The rejection of the "sweat of the brow" doctrine—that copyright rewards originality, not effort—is often cited in Australian discussions of *IceTV v Nine Network* and originality in compilations.
