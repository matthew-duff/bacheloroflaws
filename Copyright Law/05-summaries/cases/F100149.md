[[Telstra Corporation Limited v Phone Directories Company Pty Ltd [2010] FCAFC 149]]

**Citation**: [2010] FCAFC 149

### Facts
Telstra and Sensis published the White Pages Directory (WPD) and Yellow Pages Directory (YPD) for regional areas across Australia. Phone Directories Company and related respondents used or reproduced directory information. Telstra and Sensis claimed copyright in the directories and alleged infringement. The directories were produced through a heavily automated process using the Genesis Computer System: listing information was collected from subscribers and entered into a database (about 15% manually, 85% automatically); a "rollover" process carried forward data from the previous year; the "Book Extract" and "Book Production" programs extracted, sorted, arranged and paginated listings according to prescriptive Rules; human workers performed data entry, error-checking, and pagination, but were constrained by Rules and automated systems. The compilations (the particular form and arrangement of listings) first took material form at the "book extract" stage—an automated process. Numerous Sensis employees contributed in various roles, but the trial judge found that authors could not be identified, human contributions were largely mechanical or anterior to the work taking material form, and the system was designed to limit originality. Telstra and Sensis relied on *Desktop Marketing Systems v Telstra* (2002), which had held that telephone directories attracted copyright by virtue of substantial labour and expense.

### Issue
Whether copyright subsists in the White Pages and Yellow Pages directories under the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth) when those directories were compiled primarily by automated computerised processes rather than by the intellectual effort of identifiable human authors.

### Held
No. The Full Federal Court (Keane CJ, Perram and Yates JJ) dismissed the appeal. Copyright does not subsist in the directories.

### Reasoning
- **Authorship and originality**: Copyright in a literary work subsists only where the work originates from an individual author or authors. Under *IceTV Pty Ltd v Nine Network Australia Pty Ltd* (2009) 239 CLR 458, originality requires that the work originates with the author and involves "independent intellectual effort" or "sufficient effort of a literary nature". The Act protects the particular form of expression of information, not facts or skill and labour per se.
- **Compilation by automation**: The compilations (the selection, ordering and arrangement of listings in their published form) were brought into existence by the Genesis Computer System, not by individuals. The actual fixing in material form occurred at the Book Extract and Book Production stages—automated processes. No individual had any conception of the final form of each directory.
- **Human contributions insufficient**: Human input (data entry, rule-application, error-checking, pagination) was either anterior to the work taking material form, mechanical, or constrained by the Rules. Those activities did not constitute selecting, ordering or arranging the compilation in material form. Authorship of a compilation requires gathering or organising material *and* selecting, ordering or arranging its fixation in material form—read conjunctively.
- **Desktop Marketing distinguished**: *Desktop Marketing* was not concerned with automated compilation. *IceTV* requires focus on authorship and the particular form of expression, not on preventing appropriation of skill and labour. *Desktop Marketing* cannot govern where the compilation is produced by an automated process.
- **Joint authorship rejected**: The directories were not works of joint authorship—contributions were not collaborative and were not directed at the actual compilation.
- **Section 128 presumption**: The presumption of subsistence under s 128 was displaced where the evidence positively established that copyright did not subsist.

### Significance
Establishes that **copyright does not subsist in compilations produced by automated processes** absent identifiable human authorship directed at the particular form of expression of the work. *IceTV* is applied to hold that "industrious collection" or "sweat of the brow" alone does not suffice; the work must originate from an author's intellectual effort directed at creating the material form. Significantly limits *Desktop Marketing*, which had upheld copyright in telephone directories on labour and expense grounds. Highlights that automation may preclude copyright subsistence where the compilation is effected by a computer system rather than individuals. Has implications for databases, directories and other compilations produced by automated systems.
