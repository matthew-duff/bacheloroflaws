[[Universal Music Publishing Pty Ltd v Palmer (No 2) [2021] FCA 434]]

**Citation**: [2021] FCA 434

### Facts
Universal Music Publishing held Australian copyright in Twisted Sister's song "We're Not Gonna Take It" (composed by Dee Snider, 1984). Clive Palmer (United Australia Party) authorised creation and use of "Aussies Not Gonna Cop It" in UAP campaign advertisements in 2018. The music and lyrics were noticeably similar to the original, especially the chorus. Palmer's team sought a licence from Universal, were quoted $150,000 plus approval from Snider, declined, and used the song anyway. Universal sued for copyright infringement, alleging reproduction of a substantial part. Palmer argued the chorus was not original because it copied "O Come, All Ye Faithful," and that parody/satire defences applied.

### Issue
Whether "Aussies Not Gonna Cop It" reproduced a substantial part of "We're Not Gonna Take It"; whether the chorus was original; whether parody or satire defences applied; and what remedies (including additional damages for flagrancy) were appropriate.

### Held
Copyright was infringed. Palmer's originality argument was rejected. Parody/satire defence was rejected. Damages: $500,000 compensatory; $1,000,000 additional damages for flagrant infringement ($1.5m total).

### Reasoning
- **Substantial part**: Assessed qualitatively, not by counting bars or notes. The court considered structure, melody, harmony and prominence from the perspective of an ordinary reasonably experienced listener. The chorus was the most prominent feature; the two songs were strikingly similar.
- **Originality**: Palmer's argument that the WNGTI chorus copied "O Come, All Ye Faithful" was rejected. The chorus was indisputably original and qualitatively significant.
- **Lyrical changes**: Replacing "we're not gonna take it" with "Aussies not gonna cop it" was immaterial and did not avoid infringement.
- **Parody/satire**: The statutory defence did not apply on the facts; the use was not accepted as parody or satire.
- **Additional damages**: Palmer's deliberate conduct—seeking a licence, being refused on clear terms, and using the work anyway—supported a finding of flagrant infringement and justified $1m additional damages under s 115(4) of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth).

### Significance
Important on substantial part in musical works (qualitative test, ordinary reasonably experienced listener; quality not quantity), originality as a bar to infringement, the limits of parody/satire defences, and additional damages for flagrant infringement where the defendant knowingly infringes after being refused a licence.
