[[2026 Session 2 - LAWS5206 Copyright Law - current]]

**Citation**: 2026 Session 2 – LAWS5206 Copyright Law (28 February 2026)

### Facts / Background
Session Two of LAWS5206 Copyright Law introduces copyright subsistence (when and how copyright arises) and the framework for analysing copyright disputes. It covers requirements for protection, key distinctions (copyright vs physical object; ideas vs expression), illustrative cases on originality and compilations, and limits on what can be protected.

### Issue
- What is protectable under copyright (subsistence)?
- What are the four requirements for copyright protection?
- How do originality, material form, substantiality, and connection to Australia operate?
- What is the distinction between copyright and the physical object, and between ideas/facts and expression?
- Are there public policy or moral limits on copyright protection?

### Held / Key Takeaways
1. **Copyright in 6 Easy Steps**: (1) Identify copyright material; (2) Identify owners/licensees; (3) Identify exclusive rights; (4) Identify infringement; (5) Identify defences; (6) Consider remedies.
2. **Protectable subject matter**: Part III (works: literary, artistic, dramatic, musical) and Part IV (sound recordings, films, broadcasts, published editions). Performers’ rights exist separately.
3. **Four requirements for protection**: Connection to Australia; originality (Part III) or not copied (Part IV); material form; substantiality.
4. **Originality** = originates from the author + some independent intellectual effort; no novelty or inventiveness required; author must be human; “industrious collection” alone is no longer sufficient post-*IceTV*.
5. **Copyright ≠ physical object**; copyright ≠ ownership of the thing embodying the work.
6. **Ideas/facts not protected**; only expression is protected.
7. **Public policy limits**: No general bar to copyright in obscene or immoral works in Australia (*Venus Adult Shops v Fraserside*); discretion may affect remedies, but rarely subsistence.

### Reasoning / Legal Principles
- **Originality**: *IceTV* (2009) 239 CLR 458 – requires independent intellectual effort; labour/expense alone (per *Desktop Marketing*) is doubtful. *Telstra v Phone Directories* (2010) – computer-generated works without human author lack originality.
- **Material form**: Work must be reduced to material form (s 22(1)); no need to be permanent; excludes extemporaneous speeches, conversations, unrecorded performances.
- **Substantiality**: Quantity and complexity; headlines, titles, short slogans usually too insubstantial (*Fairfax v Reed* (2010)).
- **Connection to Australia**: Qualified person, first publication in Australia, or Berne/UCC reciprocity.
- **Compilations**: *Feist* (US) – “sweat of the brow” rejected; *Desktop Marketing* – accepted “industrious collection” in Australia; *IceTV* – cast doubt on *Desktop Marketing*; *Telstra v PDC* – directories held not original (automated creation, no human author).

### Significance
Provides the structural framework for subsistence under the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth) and the doctrinal shift from “industrious collection” toward “independent intellectual effort” (*IceTV*). Essential for understanding what can and cannot attract copyright and how subsistence feeds into infringement analysis.
