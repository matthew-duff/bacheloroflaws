**Citation**: LAWS5206 Copyright Law, Session 1 (28 February 2026). Course materials presented by Professor John Swinson and Ben Gardiner KC.

### Facts / Background
Session 1 provides an introductory overview of copyright law and sets up the framework for the course. It covers the nature of intellectual property, the statutory basis of copyright, and a crash course in copyright fundamentals.

### Issue / Key Topics
- What is intellectual property and copyright?
- What subject matter does copyright protect?
- Who owns copyright, for how long, and is registration required?
- What rights does copyright confer (economic and moral)?
- What constitutes infringement and what remedies are available?

### Held / Main Conclusions
- Copyright is a bundle of statutory exclusive rights conferred by the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth). There is no common law copyright; it is territorial (Australia only) and property (s 196).
- Protection is limited to subject matter in Part III (works) and Part IV (subject matter other than works). If it is not in Part III or IV, it is not protected.
- Copyright arises automatically on creation—no registration is required.
- Copyright generally lasts 70 years after the author’s death for Part III works; once expired, works enter the public domain.
- Infringement requires taking a substantial part, objective similarity, and actual copying; defences and exceptions (e.g. fair dealing) apply.

### Reasoning / Legal Principles
- **Part III works**: Literary, artistic, musical, and dramatic works (including computer programs per s 10).
- **Part IV subject matter**: Sound recordings, films, broadcasts, published editions.
- **Ownership**: First owner is generally the author for Part III works (s 35(2)); Part IV ownership varies (ss 97–100).
- **Prerequisites for protection**: Originality (Part III) / not copied (Part IV); expression vs facts/ideas; connection to Australia; material form; substantiality.
- **Economic rights** (ss 31, 85–88): reproduction, public performance, recording, broadcasting, translation, adaptation.
- **Moral rights** (Part IX): attribution, integrity.
- **Remedies** (s 115): injunctions, damages (including additional damages), account of profits, declaratory relief, delivery up.

### Significance
Session 1 establishes the foundational framework used throughout the course: **Copyright in 6 Easy Steps**—(1) identify the copyright material, (2) identify the owners and licensees, (3) identify the exclusive rights, (4) identify any infringement, (5) identify defences or exceptions, (6) consider remedies. This structure organises analysis of copyright issues and is essential for approaching problems systematically.
