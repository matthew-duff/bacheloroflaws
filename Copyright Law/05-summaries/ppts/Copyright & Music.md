[[Copyright & music - v5]]

**Source**: LAWS5206 Copyright Law lecture presentation – Copyright & Music (v5)

### Facts / Background
Lecture presentation on copyright protection of music, covering: (1) how songs are protected (musical works, literary works, sound recordings); (2) sampling vs interpolation and licensing; (3) the Taylor Swift master recordings dispute as a practical example; and (4) three leading Australian music copyright cases (EMI Songs v Larrikin, Boomerang Investments v Padgett, Universal v Palmer), plus the UK *Shape of You* case.

### Issue
How does copyright protect music? What subject matter is protected? What is the scope of Part III vs Part IV protection? What are the requirements for infringement in “sound-alike” cases (objective similarity, causal connection, substantial part)?

### Held / Key Takeaways
- **Musical work** (Part III) is an abstract concept evidenced by score or recording; it can include instrumentation and sung lyrics, but not the performance itself (*EMI Songs v Larrikin*; *Boomerang Investments v Padgett*).
- **Sound recording** (Part IV) requires no originality; it protects the actual recording only. Deliberate imitation does *not* amount to copying the sound recording (*CBS v Telmak*).
- **Infringement in sound-alikes** is usually pleaded under Part III (musical work, literary work), not Part IV, because Part IV requires exact (mechanical) copying.
- **Sampling** = use of actual parts of another recording; **interpolation** = re-recording a melody. Both need clearance; sampling can infringe Part III and Part IV; interpolation risks Part III only.

### Reasoning
- **EMI Songs v Larrikin (Kookaburra/Down Under)**: Men at Work’s flute riff in *Down Under* reproduced two bars of *Kookaburra*. Causal connection conceded; objective similarity found by the ordinary experienced listener despite differences in style/context. What was taken was a substantial part of the work. Jagot J: bars “can be heard once attention is directed to them”.
- **Boomerang Investments v Padgett (Love is in the Air)**: Glass Candy’s *Warm in the Winter* and Air France’s *France is in the Air* copied the sung line “love is in the air” and melody. Perram J: musical work includes sung lyrics; objective similarity established; causal connection found (copying deliberate); substantial part established—the phrase is “the essential air of the song”. Literary work claim failed (words alone not sufficiently original).
- **Universal v Palmer (We’re Not Gonna Take It)**: Clive Palmer’s UAP ad song was objectively similar to Twisted Sister’s *We’re Not Gonna Take It*; causal connection found (Palmer’s evidence dishonest); substantial part taken. Defences and remedies dealt with separately.
- **Taylor Swift**: Re-recorded her first six albums when masters were sold to Scooter Braun. Re-recordings do not infringe the originals because they are not copies; she retained rights in music and lyrics. Demonstrates the narrow scope of Part IV and the value of retaining Part III rights.

### Significance
Clarifies how **musical works** (melody, harmony, rhythm, lyrics-as-sung) and **sound recordings** are separately protected under the Act, and why “sound-alike” cases turn on Part III. Illustrates the three-step infringement analysis (identify work, identify taken part, assess substantial part) and the link between causal connection and objective similarity. Shows the practical importance of understanding Part III vs Part IV in contract and licensing negotiations (e.g., master ownership vs composition rights).
