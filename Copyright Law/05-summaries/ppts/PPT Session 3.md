**Citation**: LAWS5206 Copyright Law, Session 3 (28 February 2026).

### Facts
Session 3 continues subsistence under Parts III and IV of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth). It covers subsistence in particular forms of subject matter and revisits the "Copyright in 6 Easy Steps" framework (identify material, owners, rights, infringement, defences, remedies).

### Issue
What subject matter attracts copyright under Part III (works) and Part IV (subject matter other than works), and what subsistence requirements and case law govern each category?

### Held
Copyright subsists in Part III works (literary, dramatic, musical, artistic) and Part IV subject matter (films, sound recordings, broadcasts, published editions). Each category has distinct subsistence requirements and leading authorities. Precise identification of the relevant work or subject matter is critical before considering ownership and infringement.

### Reasoning

**Part III — Works**
- Literary, dramatic, musical, and artistic works. Computer programs are literary works: s 10.
- Literary works: no threshold of literary merit; ideas/facts not protected. Selecting the work matters (too small = no subsistence; too large = may not take substantial part). Tables/compilations require human authorship and more than “sweat of the brow” (IceTV, Telstra v PDC, Tonnex).
- Computer programs: utilitarian; functionality not protected; UI not protected as literary work. Source code and object code both covered by s 10 after 1984 amendments. Apple v Computer Edge (1986) 161 CLR 171 — source code literary work; object code initially controversial. Data Access v Powerflex — data alone not a program. Campaigntrack (2021) — subset of statements can be a “computer program”; dropdown menus not literary works. Motorola v Hytera (2022–24) — iterative revisions can be original; substantial part assessed as a whole.
- Dramatic works: must be capable of performance. Aristocrat v Pacific — electronic slot machine game not a dramatic work (no plot, choreography). Green v Broadcasting Corp of NZ — TV format not dramatic work (insufficient unity). Seven v Endemol — format subsistence left open; interlocutory injunction refused.
- Artistic works: paintings, sculptures, drawings, engravings, photographs, buildings, works of artistic craftsmanship. No artistic merit required generally. Lucasfilm v Ainsworth — Stormtrooper helmet not sculpture (utilitarian, production element). Mann J: “sculpture” = three-dimensional work made by artist’s hand. Drawings include diagrams, maps, plans; s 21(3) protects 3D production from 2D. Works of artistic craftsmanship: Burge v Swarbrick — artistic expression must be “unconstrained by functional considerations.” State of Escape — Escape Bag not work of artistic craftsmanship (design constrained by function).

**Part IV — Subject matter other than works**
- Films: cinematograph films; scripts/screenplays are Part III. Sounds in film not separate sound recordings: s 23. Computer games may be films (Sega v Galaxy); electronic gaming machines not (Aristocrat). Live transmission without storage not a film (Seven Network).
- Broadcasts: TV/sound broadcasts from Australia by licensed or national broadcasters. Narrow protection; infringement requires copying/re-broadcast of actual broadcast (TCN v Ten). Excludes streaming, podcasts. The Panel litigation: program-level subsistence; probably not individual segments.
- Published editions: typographical layout; narrow protection against facsimile copying.

### Significance
Session 3 lays out the taxonomy of copyright subject matter and the subsistence rules for Part III and Part IV. It emphasises the need to identify the precise work or subject matter and its requirements (connection to Australia, originality/material form, substantiality) before turning to ownership, infringement, and remedies. The cases covered provide the foundation for applying subsistence to computer programs, dramatic works, artistic works (including sculptures and works of artistic craftsmanship), films, and broadcasts.
