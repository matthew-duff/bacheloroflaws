Copyright — Literary work — Compilation — Infringement — Whether
substantial part reproduced — Qualitative assessment — Originality —
Copyright Act 1968 (Cth), ss 10(1) “joint authorship”, “literary work”,
14(1), 29(1), 31(1), 32, 36.

Section 31(1)(a) of the Copyright Act 1968 (Cth) provided that
copyright in a literary work included the exclusive right to reproduce the
work in a material form. Section 10(1) deﬁned “literary work” to include
a compilation expressed in words, ﬁgures or symbols. Section 14(1)(a)
provided that the exclusive reproduction right included the right to
reproduce a substantial part of the work. Section 32(1)(a) provided that
copyright subsisted in an original literary work that was unpublished and
of which the author was a qualiﬁed person at the time the work was made.
Section 32(2) provided that where an original literary work had been
published, copyright subsisted in the work if the ﬁrst publication took
place in Australia and the author of the work was a qualiﬁed person at the
time the work was ﬁrst published. In relation to published literary works,
s 29(1)(a) and (2) provided that the act of publication was to be of the
whole work and not only a substantial part of it. Section 35(2) provided
that, subject to s 35, the author of a literary work was the owner of any
copyright subsisting in the work. By force of ss 78-83, the Act gave
protection to a work of “joint authorship”, which was deﬁned by s 10(1)
to mean a work that had been produced by the collaboration of two or
more authors in which the contribution of each author was not separate
from that of the other(s). Section 36 provided that the copyright in a
literary work was infringed by a person who, not being the owner of the
copyright and without the owner’s licence, did, or authorised the doing, in
Australia of any act comprised in the copyright. Section 14(1)(b) provided
that in the Act, a reference to reproduction of a work included a
reproduction of a substantial part of the work.

reproduction involved the use of the words, ﬁgures and symbols, and the

about the authorship of the weekly schedules but found that Nine’s skill

Per French CJ, Crennan and Kiefel JJ. (1) For copyright to subsist in a
literary work, it must be original in the sense that the work originated with
the author. The creation of the work must have required some independent
intellectual effort, by neither literary merit nor novelty nor inventiveness.
(2) In considering whether copyright subsists in a work, “industrious
collection” or “sweat of the brow”, on the one hand, and “creativity”, on
the other, are kindred aspects of a mental process which produces an
object, a literary work, a particular form of expression which copyright
protects.
(3) Whether there has been an “appropriation” of the author’s skill and
labour in a work depents on the nature of the skill and labour and, in
particular, whether it is directed to the originality of the particular form of
expression.

Per Gummow, Hayne and Heydon JJ. (1) The essential source of
original works remains the activities of authors. The exclusive rights
comprised in the copyright in an original work subsist by reason of the
relevant ﬁxation of the original work of the author in a material form and
do not extend to the ideas or information contained in the work. In an
infringement proceeding the work, the author or authors of the work and
the relevant time of making or ﬁrst publication should be identiﬁed.
(2) The statutory requirement that the part of the work taken must be
substantial assumes that there may be some measure of legitimate
appropriation of the investment in the work of the skill and labour of the
author.
(3) Notions of the “skill and labour” of the author are irrelevant to the
existence of the author’s title to the copyright and to the assessment of
what constitutes a “substantial part”. The Copyright Act does not deal
with misappropriation of skill and labour alone.

central to the statutory protection given by copyright legislation,
including the Act.

collaborative effort and an evolutionary process of development,

The EU Database Directive
In 1996 the Directive of the European Parliament and of the Council
on the Legal Protection of Databases (the Directive) (177) was adopted
by the European Union (EU). It is signiﬁcant for the issues on the
present appeal that the Australian legislation has no counterpart.
Of the genesis of the Directive, Professor Cornish has written (178):
“At once excited and alarmed by the capacity of digitization to
store massive ﬁles of information and of the internet to deliver it in
individually requested packages, the publishing industry, and, by its
side, music and ﬁlms, secured a Database Directive from the EU.”
Whilst “traditional copyright” respecting compilations was carefully
conﬁned and ﬁxed upon the effort that went into the selection and
arrangement in a compilation, the new right was “accorded directly to
the investor in a database” (179).
The
Directive
deﬁnes
“database”
to
mean
“a
collection
of
independent works, data or other materials arranged in a systematic or
methodical way and individually accessible by electronic or other
means” (Ch I, Art 1(2)). Chapter III of the Directive relevantly
provides for the implementation of a “sui generis” right for the
“maker” of a database who shows there has been a “substantial
investment” in either the obtaining, veriﬁcation or presentation of the
contents of the database and for that right to extend to prevention of
the extraction and/or re-utilisation of the whole or of a substantial part
of the database (Ch III, Art 7(1)), subject to certain exceptions (Ch III,
Art 9) and lawful uses (Ch III, Art 8).
The Directive also provides in Ch II for Member States to afford
protection to databases which, “by reason of the selection or
arrangement of their contents, constitute the author’s own intellectual
creation” (Ch II, Art 3(1)) (emphasis added). However, the position of
“the author” in Ch II may be contrasted with that of “the maker” in
Ch III. In explanation of the latter, recital 39 states:
“Whereas, in addition to aiming to protect the copyright in the
original selection or arrangement of the contents of a database, this
Directive seeks to safeguard the position of makers of databases
against misappropriation of the results of the ﬁnancial and
professional investment made in obtaining and collection [of] the
contents by protecting the whole or substantial parts of a database
against certain acts by a user or competitor.”
In the absence of implementation of laws analogous to the kind
described in the Directive, the matters now in issue cannot be resolved
by concluding, as did the Full Court (180), that Ice appropriated “the
fruits of Nine’s skill and labour”.

Conclusions
When the issue of substantiality is approached in the manner
indicated in these reasons, it is apparent that the primary judge reached
the correct result and that this should not have been disturbed.
Ice also emphasised that the time and title information appearing in
the Weekly Schedule was “decompiled” into the very different form of
the Aggregated Guides, and that IceTV derived the time and title
information through that medium, rather than directly from the Weekly
Schedule. Given the above conclusion respecting the issue of
substantiality, it is unnecessary to determine whether the fact of
so-called “indirect copying” by IceTV prevented there being a
“reproduction” of the Weekly Schedule.
One ﬁnal point should be made. This concerns the submission by the
Digital Alliance that this Court consider the Full Court’s decision in
Desktop Marketing (214) and, to the contrary of Desktop Marketing,
affirm that there must be some “creative spark” (215) or exercise of
“skill and judgment” (216) before a work is sufficiently “original” for
the subsistence of copyright.
It is by no means apparent that the law even before the 1911 Act was
to any different effect to that for which the Digital Alliance contends. It
may be that the reasoning in Desktop Marketing with respect to
compilations is out of line with the understanding of copyright law
over many years. These reasons explain the need to treat with some
caution the emphasis in Desktop Marketing upon “labour and expense”
per se and upon misappropriation. However, in the light of the
admission of Ice that the Weekly Schedule was an original literary
work, this is not an appropriate occasion to take any further the subject
of originality in copyright works.
