## Technology


Diagram of the latent diffusion architecture used by Stable Diffusion


The [denoising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denoising "Denoising") process used by Stable Diffusion. The model generates images by iteratively denoising [random noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_noise "Random noise") until a configured number of steps have been reached, guided by the CLIP text encoder pretrained on [concepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concept "Concept") along with the attention mechanism, resulting in the desired image depicting a representation of the trained concept.

### Architecture

Main article: [Latent diffusion model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latent_diffusion_model "Latent diffusion model")

[Diffusion models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion_model "Diffusion model"), introduced in 2015, are trained with the objective of removing successive applications of [Gaussian noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_noise "Gaussian noise") on training images, which can be thought of as a sequence of [denoising autoencoders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denoising_autoencoder "Denoising autoencoder"). The name _diffusion_ is from the [thermodynamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic "Thermodynamic")[diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion "Diffusion"), since they were first developed with inspiration from thermodynamics.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-15)

Models in Stable Diffusion series before SD 3 all used a variant of diffusion models, called [latent diffusion model (LDM)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latent_diffusion_model "Latent diffusion model"), developed in 2021 by the CompVis (Computer Vision & Learning)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-16) group at [LMU Munich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LMU_Munich "LMU Munich").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-paper-17)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-stable-diffusion-github-8)

Stable Diffusion consists of 3 parts: the [variational autoencoder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_autoencoder "Variational autoencoder") (VAE), [U-Net](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U-Net "U-Net"), and an optional text encoder.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-:02-18) The VAE encoder compresses the image from pixel space to a smaller dimensional [latent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latent_space "Latent space"), capturing a more fundamental semantic meaning of the image.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-paper-17) Gaussian noise is iteratively applied to the compressed latent representation during forward diffusion.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-:02-18) The U-Net block, composed of a [ResNet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residual_neural_network "Residual neural network") backbone, [denoises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noise_reduction "Noise reduction") the output from forward diffusion backwards to obtain a latent representation. Finally, the VAE decoder generates the final image by converting the representation back into pixel space.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-:02-18)

The denoising step can be flexibly conditioned on a string of text, an image, or another modality. The encoded conditioning data is exposed to denoising U-Nets via a [cross-attention mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attention_\(machine_learning\) "Attention (machine learning)").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-:02-18) For conditioning on text, the fixed, pretrained [CLIP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contrastive_Language-Image_Pre-training "Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training") ViT-L/14 text encoder is used to transform text prompts to an embedding space.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-stable-diffusion-github-8)Researchers point to increased computational efficiency for training and generation as an advantage of LDMs.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-stable-diffusion-launch-7)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-paper-17)

With 860 million parameters in the U-Net and 123 million in the text encoder, Stable Diffusion is considered relatively lightweight by 2022 standards, and unlike other diffusion models, it can run on [consumer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumer_electronics "Consumer electronics") GPUs,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-19) and even [CPU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CPU "CPU")-only if using the [OpenVINO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenVINO "OpenVINO") version of Stable Diffusion.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-20)

#### SD XL

The XL version uses the same LDM architecture as previous versions,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-:4-21) except larger: larger UNet backbone, larger cross-attention context, two text encoders instead of one, and trained on multiple aspect ratios (not just the square aspect ratio like previous versions).

The SD XL Refiner, released at the same time, has the same architecture as SD XL, but it was trained for adding fine details to preexisting images via text-conditional img2img.

#### SD 3.0

Main article: [Diffusion model § Rectified flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion_model#Rectified_flow "Diffusion model")

The 3.0 version[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-:6-22) completely changes the backbone. Not a UNet, but a _Rectified Flow Transformer_, which implements the rectified flow method[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-:7-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-:8-24) with a [Transformer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformer_\(deep_learning_architecture\) "Transformer (deep learning architecture)").

The Transformer architecture used for SD 3.0 has three "tracks", for original text encoding, transformed text encoding, and image encoding (in latent space). The transformed text encoding and image encoding are mixed during each transformer block.

The architecture is named "multimodal diffusion transformer (MMDiT), where the "multimodal" means that it mixes text and image encodings inside its operations. This differs from previous versions of DiT, where the text encoding affects the image encoding, but not vice versa.

### Training data

Stable Diffusion was trained on pairs of images and captions taken from LAION-5B, a publicly available dataset derived from [Common Crawl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Crawl "Common Crawl") data scraped from the web, where 5 billion image-text pairs were classified based on language and filtered into separate datasets by resolution, a predicted likelihood of containing a watermark, and predicted "aesthetic" score (e.g. subjective visual quality).[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-Waxy-25) The dataset was created by [LAION](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LAION "LAION"), a German non-profit which receives funding from Stability AI.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-Waxy-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-26) The Stable Diffusion model was trained on three subsets of LAION-5B: laion2B-en, laion-high-resolution, and laion-aesthetics v2 5+.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-Waxy-25) A third-party analysis of the model's training data identified that out of a smaller subset of 12 million images taken from the original wider dataset used, approximately 47% of the sample size of images came from 100 different domains, with [Pinterest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinterest "Pinterest") taking up 8.5% of the subset, followed by websites such as [WordPress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WordPress "WordPress"), [Blogspot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blogspot "Blogspot"), [Flickr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flickr "Flickr"), [DeviantArt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DeviantArt "DeviantArt") and [Wikimedia Commons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikimedia_Commons "Wikimedia Commons").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] An investigation by Bayerischer Rundfunk showed that LAION's datasets, hosted on Hugging Face, contain large amounts of private and sensitive data.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-:2-27)

### Training procedures

The model was initially trained on the laion2B-en and laion-high-resolution subsets, with the last few rounds of training done on LAION-Aesthetics v2 5+, a subset of 600 million captioned images which the LAION-Aesthetics Predictor V2 predicted that humans would, on average, give a score of at least 5 out of 10 when asked to rate how much they liked them.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-28)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-Waxy-25)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-LAION-Aesthetics-29) The LAION-Aesthetics v2 5+ subset also excluded low-resolution images and images which LAION-5B-WatermarkDetection identified as carrying a [watermark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watermark "Watermark") with greater than 80% probability.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-Waxy-25) Final rounds of training additionally dropped 10% of text conditioning to improve Classifier-Free Diffusion Guidance.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-:5-30)

The model was trained using 256 [Nvidia A100](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ampere_\(microarchitecture\) "Ampere (microarchitecture)") GPUs on [Amazon Web Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazon_Web_Services "Amazon Web Services") for a total of 150,000 GPU-hours, at a cost of $600,000.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-stable-diffusion-model-card-1-4-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-33)

### Limitations

Stable Diffusion has issues with degradation and inaccuracies in certain scenarios. Initial releases of the model were trained on a dataset that consists of 512×512 resolution images, meaning that the quality of generated images noticeably degrades when user specifications deviate from its "expected" 512×512 resolution;[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-diffusers-34) the version 2.0 update of the Stable Diffusion model later introduced the ability to natively generate images at 768×768 resolution.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-release2.0-35) Another challenge is in generating human limbs due to poor data quality of limbs in the LAION database.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-36) The model is insufficiently trained to replicate human limbs and faces due to the lack of representative features in the database, and prompting the model to generate images of such type can confound the model.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-37) In addition to human limbs, Stable Diffusion is unable to generate legible ambigrams and some other forms of text and typography. Stable Diffusion XL (SDXL) version 1.0, released in July 2023, introduced native 1024x1024 resolution and improved generation for limbs and text.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-38)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-39)

Accessibility for individual developers can also be a problem. In order to customize the model for new use cases that are not included in the dataset, such as generating [anime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anime "Anime") characters ("waifu diffusion"),[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-40) new data and further training are required. [Fine-tuned](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine-tuning_\(machine_learning\) "Fine-tuning (machine learning)") adaptations of Stable Diffusion created through additional retraining have been used for a variety of different use-cases, from medical imaging[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-41) to [algorithmically generated music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riffusion "Riffusion").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-42) However, this fine-tuning process is sensitive to the quality of new data; low resolution images or different resolutions from the original data can not only fail to learn the new task but degrade the overall performance of the model. Even when the model is additionally trained on high quality images, it is difficult for individuals to run models in consumer electronics. For example, the training process for waifu-diffusion requires a minimum 30 GB of [VRAM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VRAM "VRAM"),[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-43) which exceeds the usual resource provided in such consumer GPUs as [Nvidia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nvidia "Nvidia")'s [GeForce 30 series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GeForce_30_series "GeForce 30 series"), which has only about 12 GB.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-44)

The creators of Stable Diffusion acknowledge the potential for [algorithmic bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithmic_bias "Algorithmic bias"), as the model was primarily trained on images with English descriptions.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-stable-diffusion-model-card-1-4-32) As a result, generated images reinforce social biases and are from a western perspective, as the creators note that the model lacks data from other communities and cultures. The model gives more accurate results for prompts that are written in English in comparison to those written in other languages, with western or white cultures often being the default representation.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-stable-diffusion-model-card-1-4-32)

### End-user fine-tuning

To address the limitations of the model's initial training, end-users may opt to implement additional training to [fine-tune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine-tuning_\(machine_learning\) "Fine-tuning (machine learning)")generation outputs to match more specific use-cases, a process also referred to as [personalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Text-to-image_personalization "Text-to-image personalization"). There are three methods in which user-accessible fine-tuning can be applied to a Stable Diffusion model checkpoint:

- An "embedding" can be trained from a collection of user-provided images, and allows the model to generate visually similar images whenever the name of the embedding is used within a generation prompt.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-45) Embeddings are based on the "textual inversion" concept developed by researchers from [Tel Aviv University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tel_Aviv_University "Tel Aviv University") in 2022 with support from [Nvidia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nvidia "Nvidia"), where vector representations for specific tokens used by the model's text encoder are linked to new pseudo-words. Embeddings can be used to reduce biases within the original model, or mimic visual styles.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-46)
- A "hypernetwork" is a small pretrained neural network that is applied to various points within a larger neural network, and refers to the technique created by [NovelAI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NovelAI "NovelAI") developer Kurumuz in 2021, originally intended for text-generation [transformer models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformer_\(machine_learning_model\) "Transformer (machine learning model)"). Hypernetworks steer results towards a particular direction, allowing Stable Diffusion-based models to imitate the art style of specific artists, even if the artist is not recognised by the original model; they process the image by finding key areas of importance such as hair and eyes, and then patch these areas in secondary latent space.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-47)
- [DreamBooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DreamBooth "DreamBooth") is a deep learning generation model developed by researchers from [Google Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google "Google") and [Boston University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_University "Boston University") in 2022 which can fine-tune the model to generate precise, personalised outputs that depict a specific subject, following training via a set of images which depict the subject.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_Diffusion#cite_note-48)
