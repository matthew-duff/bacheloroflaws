	\22## What is subsistence?

**Subsistence** means that copyright exists in the relevant subject matter.

> [!IMPORTANT] Key points
> - **Presumption:** Subsistence and ownership are presumed unless contested ([[Copyright Act s 126]]).
> - **No registration:** Unlike some other jurisdictions, there is no need to register copyright in Australia — copyright exists from the time of creation of the relevant subject matter.
> - **Proprietary Copyright:** The "bedrock" regime. Involves a divide between **subject matter** (the "copyright nouns") and **exclusive rights** (the "copyright verbs"), joined by the concept of a **substantial part**.

Where subject matter is protected, that protection has:

| Aspect       | Meaning                                                                                      |
| :----------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Scope**    | The extent of the exclusive rights — what can the copyright owner prevent others from doing? |
| **Duration** | How long the copyright lasts.                                                                |
|              |                                                                                              |

Both depend on the nature of the subject matter.

## Threshold Requirements for Works (Part III)

Before outlining the categories of works, three threshold requirements must be met: **originality**, **material form**, and (most probably) **substantiality**.

---

### 1. Connection to Australia

There must be a relevant connection to Australia:

- **Qualified person** — see [[Copyright Act s 32]], [[Copyright Act s 89]], [[Copyright Act s 90]], [[Copyright Act s 92]], [[Copyright Act s 189]], [[Copyright Act s 191B]].
- **First publication in Australia** — for works, see [[Copyright Act s 89]]–[[Copyright Act s 92]].
- **Made in Australia** — in the case of sound recordings, films and broadcasts.

---

### 2. Originality (Part III works)

- [[Copyright Act s 32]] requires the work to be **"original"**.
- The terms **"original"** and **"author"** as used in the Act are **correlative**: [[Sands & McDougall v Robinson]].
- **Definition:** "Original" is shorthand for "that which originates from the mind of a human author".
- **Human Authorship:** The author must be a **human being**: [[Telstra v Phone Directories]].
  - This is a matter of "fact and degree" ([[Data Access v Powerflex]]).
  - Computer-generated material lacking a human author generally fails this requirement ([[Telstra v Phone Directories]]; [[Acohs v Ucorp]]).
- **Merit:** Originality does not require merit, inventiveness, or "freshness" ([[University of London Press]]). It merely requires human origination of expression.
- **Expression vs Idea:** Originality is tied to the particular expression, not the background work, ideas, or facts.
  - *Example:* A photographer discovering a location (idea/fact) vs the artistry of the specific photo (expression).

> [!quote]- [[Copyright Act s 32]] — *Copyright Act 1968*
> **(1)** Subject to this Act, copyright subsists in an original literary, dramatic, musical or artistic work that is unpublished and of which the author:
>
> (a) was a qualified person at the time when the work was made; or  
> (b) if the making of the work extended over a period — was a qualified person for a substantial part of that period.
>
> **(2)** Subject to this Act, where an original literary, dramatic, musical or artistic work has been published:
>
> (a) copyright subsists in the work; or  
> (b) if copyright in the work subsisted immediately before its first publication — copyright continues to subsist in the work.

---

### 3. Compilations and "Industry"

- Copyright protects **forms of expression**, not facts or ideas.
- **Shift in Law (2009):** Prior to [[IceTV v Nine Network]], "industry" (sweat of the brow) was often enough for protection ([[Walter v Lane]]; [[Desktop Marketing v Telstra]]).
- **Current Position ([[IceTV v Nine Network]]):** Mere industry or investment is unlikely to qualify as originality.
  - Originality resides in the **intellectual work of evaluative selection or conceptual arrangement** by a human author.
  - [[Tonnex v Dynamic]]: Upheld copyright in a compatibility chart because the selection and arrangement involved personal assessment and discretion.
- **Thin Originality:** Factual compilations have "thin" originality; protection only extends to the specific form of organization, not the facts themselves.

---

### 4. Material form

- Copyright in **works** (Part III subject matter) subsists from the time the work is **"made"** (unpublished: [[Copyright Act s 32]] (1)) or **published** (published: [[Copyright Act s 32]] (2), [[Copyright Act s 29]]).
- **Definition ([[Copyright Act s 10]] (1)):** Includes any form (visible or not) of storage, whether or not the work can be reproduced therefrom.
- **"Whether visible or not":** Refers to whether it is in a human-readable form (e.g., digital storage like a USB) (*1984 Reforms*).
- **Fixation vs Work:** The work is the *intangible* property (chose in action), not the physical artefact.
  - *Example:* Graham Sutherland's portrait of Churchill was destroyed, but the copyright (intangible property) subsisted until 70 years after his death.

Copyright does not extend to things that are not expressed in material form:
- Extemporaneous speeches
- Conversations
- A "spectacle" ([[Victoria Park Racing v Taylor]])
- ? A fireworks display (though a "script" for one might be a dramatic work).

---

### 5. Substantiality

- **De minimis non curat lex:** The law does not concern itself with trifles.
- A non-statutory threshold requirement filtering out trivial matter.
- **Factors:** Complexity of expression and quantum of originality.

**Examples held to be insufficiently substantial:**
- **Adam Ant's face paint:** "Two straight lines... could not possibly attract copyright" ([[Merchandising Corp v Harpbond]]).
- **Simple logos:** Drawn in "about four minutes" ([[Sullivan v FNH Investments]]).
- **Headlines:** Generally too short/insubstantial ([[Fairfax v Reed]]).
- **Titles:** "The Man Who Broke the Bank at Monte Carlo".
- **Short phrases:** "HELP-HELP-DRIVER-IN-DANGER..." ([[Data Access v Powerflex]]).

## Categories of Works (Part III)

The Copyright Act is a "metaphorical filing cabinet"; a work must fit into one of these four categories:

### i. Literary Works ([[Copyright Act s 10]] (1))
- **Broad definition:** Anything readable, irrespective of quality ([[University of London Press]]).
- **Includes:** Tables, compilations, and computer programs.
- **Computer Programs:** "A set of statements or instructions to be used directly or indirectly in a computer in order to bring about a certain result."
  - Must express an **algorithmic or logical relationship** ([[Data Access v Powerflex]]).
  - Data files (PDF, MP3) are generally *not* computer programs ([[Australian Video Retailers v Warner]]).
  - Sub-programs (macros, apps) can be independent works ([[Dais Studio v Bullet Creative]]).

### ii. Dramatic Works ([[Copyright Act s 10]] (1))
- **Includes:** Choreography, dumb shows, scenarios/scripts for films.
- **Excludes:** The film itself (which is Part IV subject matter).
- **Requirement:** Must have "sufficient unity to be capable of performance" ([[Green v Broadcasting Corp]]).
- **Formats:** TV formats (e.g., *Opportunity Knocks*) often fail for lack of certainty/unity.

### iii. Musical Works
- **No statutory definition.**
- **Description:** Product of a human mind intended to be performed as a combination of sounds appreciated by the ear (other than linguistic content) ([[Sawkins v Hyperion Records]]).
- **Songs:** Usually consist of two works: the **musical work** (the score/melody) and the **literary work** (the lyrics).
- **Lyrics as Music?** Perram J in [[Boomerang v Padgett]] suggested lyrics are "instructions to the singer" and part of the musical work, but this is contested.

### iv. Artistic Works ([[Copyright Act s 10]] (1))
- **Exhaustive definition** in three categories:
  1. **Visual Art:** Paintings, sculptures, drawings, engravings, photographs (irrespective of artistic quality).
  2. **Architecture:** Buildings or models (irrespective of artistic quality).
  3. **Artistic Craftsmanship:** Requires a "work of art" element.
- **Sculpture:** "A three-dimensional work made by an artist's hand" ([[Metix v Maughan]]).
  - Focus is on the **aesthetic aspiration** of the maker ([[Lucasfilm v Ainsworth]]).
- **Drawings:** Includes maps, charts, and plans.
  - Technical drawings are protected regardless of artistic merit ([[Interlego v Croner Trading]]).

## 6. Are there limits on what can be protected?

### Public policy / moral grounds
- Historically, protection could be refused for "obscene" or "blasphemous" works.
- **Current view:** Refusing protection on public policy grounds is likely "constitutionally untenable" (*Venus Adult Shops v Fraserside Holdings*). Might affect damages, but not subsistence.

### No general public interest defence
- There is **no "public interest defence"** to copyright infringement (*Collier Constructions v Foskett*).
- **Political Communication:** Implied freedom might affect fair dealing or injunctions (*AGL v Greenpeace*).
