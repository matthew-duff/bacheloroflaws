# ⚖️ Part III Works: Subsistence and Categories

This document outlines the categories of "works" protected under **Part III** of the *Copyright Act 1968* (Cth). 

> [!IMPORTANT] Subsistence Requirements
> For copyright to subsist, a work must be:
> 1. **Original** ([[s 32]])
> 2. Reduced to **Material Form** ([[s 10(1)]])

---

## 📚 1. Literary Works
The category of "literary works" is broad and not confined to works of high "literary quality" (*University of London Press*). It covers work expressed in print or writing, irrespective of style (*Petersen J*).

### 🔍 General Principles
- **Identification is Critical:** Defining the "work" is the first step.
    - ❌ **Too Small:** Copyright may not subsist (e.g., headlines in *Fairfax*).
    - ⚠️ **Too Large:** May fail the "substantial part" test for infringement.
- **Excludes Ideas/Methods:** Protection extends only to the **expression**, not underlying methods or schemes (*Baker v Selden*).
- **Utilitarian Works:** Standard form contracts (*Real Estate Institute of NSW v Wood*), exam papers, and instruction manuals are protected.

### 📊 Tables and Compilations
Defined in **s 10(1)** to include *"a table, or compilation, expressed in words, figures or symbols."*

> [!IMPORTANT] The Modern Shift
> Historically, Australian law protected the "sweat of the brow"—labour, capital, and skill (*Walter v Lane*; *Desktop Marketing v Telstra*).
> 
> **Since *[[2009-icetv-v-nine-hca-14]] (2009):**
> - Retracted the "industry-protecting" role.
> - Insists on **human authorship** (rejecting purely computer-generated compilations).
> - Focuses on the **selection and arrangement** of data rather than just the effort of collection.

### 💻 Computer Programs
Protected as a sub-category of literary works (**s 10(1)**).

- **Evolution of Protection:**
    - *[[Apple Computer v Computer Edge]]* (1986): HCA originally held source code was a literary work, but object code (machine code) was not.
    - **1984 Amendments:** Prompted by the trial decision in *Apple*, the Act was amended to expressly include computer programs (both source and object code).
- **Key Case Law:**
    - 🔒 *Autodesk v Dyason*: Dealt with a hardware "lock" and look-up tables. Established that even small, functional parts (like a look-up table) could be protected if they were essential to the program's function.
    - 🔤 *Data Access v Powerflex*: HCA held that a single word (e.g., a command) is generally not a computer program, but a "compilation of words" might be.
    - 📂 *Campaigntrack v Real Estate Tool Box*: Copyright subsists in source code as a whole and in specific files/databases. Dropdown menus were held *not* to be literary works.
    - 🛠️ *Motorola v Hytera*: Involved "industrial scale copying" of source code. Perram J noted that "review and revision of code" constitutes original expression.

## 🎭 2. Dramatic Works
Includes choreographic shows, dumb shows, and scenarios/scripts for films (**s 10(1)**). 

> [!WARNING] Note
> It does *not* include the cinematograph film itself (which is a Part IV "subject matter other than works").

### 🎭 Key Requirements
- **Unity and Performance:** A dramatic work must have sufficient unity to be capable of performance (*Green v Broadcasting Corp of NZ*).
- **Marginal Subject Matter:**
    - 📺 **TV Formats:** Generally not protected if they lack a coherent framework or are just a "collection of ideas" (*Green*; *Seven v Endemol*).
    - 🖼️ **Static Scenes:** A static arrangement of people/objects is NOT a dramatic work (*Creation Records v News Group*).
    - 🎮 **Video Games:** Specifications for games lacking "performance by characters" or being too "random/unpredictable" may fail (*Aristocrat v Pacific Gaming*).
    - 🎆 **Fireworks:** Likely protected if there is a "script" or predetermined sequence.

## 🎵 3. Musical Works
The *Copyright Act* does **not** define a "musical work." It is generally understood as a *"combination of melody and harmony"* (*Sawkins v Hyperion Records*).

### 🎤 Lyrics vs. Music
- **Dual Authorship:** A song typically consists of two works:
    1. **Literary Work:** The lyrics.
    2. **Musical Work:** The music (melody, harmony, orchestration).
- **The "Phonetic" Debate:**
    - ⚖️ *Hayes v Phonogram*: Words are literary; music is musical. They are separate.
    - ⚖️ *Boomerang Investments v Padgett* (2020): Perram J held that lyrics, when viewed as "instructions to the singer on what sounds to make," can be part of the musical work. This is a controversial and evolving area.

## 🎨 4. Artistic Works
Exhaustively defined in **s 10(1)** into three categories:

### (a) Visual Art (Irrespective of Artistic Quality)
Includes paintings, sculptures, drawings, engravings, and photographs.
- 🖌️ **Paintings:** Requires an inanimate surface (*Adam Ant* case—face paint is NOT a painting).
- 🗿 **Sculptures:**
    - *Wham-O v Lincoln*: Wooden models for Frisbees were sculptures; the plastic Frisbees were not.
    - *Lucasfilm v Ainsworth*: Stormtrooper helmets were NOT sculptures because they were "utilitarian" props for a film, not created for their own aesthetic appeal.
- 📐 **Drawings:** Includes diagrams, maps, charts, or plans. Note: 2D drawings can be infringed by 3D objects.

### (b) Works of Architecture
Includes buildings and models of buildings. "Building" includes structures like half-court tennis courts.

### (c) Works of Artistic Craftsmanship
Works where a degree of **skill/craftsmanship** is required AND they must be **artistic**.

> [!QUOTE] Functional Constraint Test (*Burge v Swarbrick*)
> Does the work's form follow its function? If the design is "constrained by functional considerations" (e.g., a yacht hull for speed), it is likely NOT a work of artistic craftsmanship.

- 👜 **Case Example:** *State of Escape v Schwartz*: The "Escape Bag" was held NOT to be a work of artistic craftsmanship because most design decisions were primarily functional.
