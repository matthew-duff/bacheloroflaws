# Copyright Law: Study Strategy and Notes Protocol

## 🎯 Objectives
This document outlines the strategy for processing materials, taking effective notes, and preparing for the unique assessment structure in Copyright Law:
1. **Research Paper (30%)**
2. **Written Outline (10%)**
3. **Group Presentation (40%)**
4. **Reflection (10%)**
*(Note: No end-of-semester exam!)*

---

## 📂 Directory Structure & Material Utilization
To keep everything organized, we should establish a clear directory structure:

*   `/sources/` - Store all raw PDFs, cases, and reading materials here.
*   `/summaries/` - Contains your parsed, finalized notes for each topic/week.
*   `/research_paper/` - Contains drafts, sources, and your written outline.
*   `/group_presentation/` - Collaborative materials, slide drafts, and meeting notes.
*   `/reflection/` - A living document to track your thoughts throughout the semester.

### How to Utilize Materials
1.  **Copyright Act 1968**: Memorize the structure of the Act (Part III works vs Part IV subject-matter other than works). 
2.  **Case Law**: Focus on how courts apply statutory definitions to new technologies (e.g., AI, internet framing).
3.  **International Treaties**: Keep the Berne Convention and TRIPS in mind for broader context, especially for the research paper.

---

## 📝 Note-Taking Strategy

### Specialized Note System
Because there is no exam, notes should be geared towards research depth and presentation prep rather than rapid issue-spotting scaffolds.

1.  **Weekly Deep-Dives**
    *   Create a markdown file for each topic (e.g., `Subsistence of Copyright.md`).
    *   Instead of just rules, write down *open questions* and *ambiguities* in the law. These are gold for your research paper.

2.  **The "Reflection Log"**
    *   After each week's readings or lectures, jot down a 3-4 sentence reaction.
    *   *Why?* Doing this continuously makes the 10% Reflection assignment at the end of the semester effortless.

3.  **Presentation Brainstorms**
    *   Keep a running list of interesting real-world copyright disputes (e.g., music sampling, AI art generation) that could serve as a strong basis for the group presentation.

---

## ✍️ Preparation for Research Paper & Outline (30% + 10%)
This is a major independent project testing your legal research and argumentation skills.

*   **Focus Areas:** Niche or emerging areas of copyright law lacking clear precedent.
*   **Preparation Method:**
    *   **Early Topic Selection:** Use the early weeks of the course to gauge what interests you most. The written outline is due May 1, so the topic must be solid by mid-April.
    *   **Deep Research:** Go beyond the reading list. Look at international jurisdictions (US, EU) for comparative analysis on your chosen issue.

---

## 🗣️ Preparation for Group Presentation (40%)
A highly weighted assessment requiring teamwork and clear communication of complex legal concepts.

*   **Focus Areas:** Engaging the audience, clear breakdown of law, balanced arguments.
*   **Preparation Method:**
    *   **Early Organization:** Establish group roles early. Who researches? Who drafts? Who designs slides?
    *   **Mock Presentations:** Practice timing heavily. Group presentations often lose marks for going over time or having jagged transitions between speakers.
