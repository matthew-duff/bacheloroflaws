Submission to Productivity Commission

by

Professor John Swinson and Trent Dalton

Background

We make this submission in response to the Harnessing Data and Digital Technology 
interim report (“the Interim Report”), particularly Information Request 1.1 where the PC 
seeks feedback on the issue of copyrighted materials being used to train AI models.

This submission is jointly made by best-selling Australian author Trent Dalton and law 
professor John Swinson.  This submission represents our opinion.  It does not 
necessarily represent an official position of HarperCollins, The University of 
Queensland or any other entity.

John Swinson is a professor of law at The University of Queensland, where he teaches 
copyright law and privacy law as undergraduate subjects, and is course coordinator for 
Internet and AI Law, a post-graduate course work subject.  John has a degree in 
computer science (he studied AI in the 1980s) and a Master of Law from Harvard Law 
School.  He was a partner at an international law firm for over 20 years where he 
specialised in technology law including copyright litigation.

Trent Dalton is a two-time Walkley Award-winning journalist and the international 
bestselling author of Gravity Let Me Go, Lola in the Mirror, Love Stories and All Our 
Shimmering Skies.  His books have sold over two million copies in Australia alone.  The 
adaptation of his debut novel, Boy Swallows Universe, became Australia’s most 
watched Netﬂix TV show within three days of release and broke Top 10 lists in 60 
countries across the world.

Introduction

When a writer negotiates a publishing contract with a publisher, the writer will focus on 
three key issues, often called the Three Cs:  Compensation, Credit and Control.  
These support creativity.  Left unregulated, AI will destroy the Three Cs, lessening 
human creativity.

2

Writers need fair payment for their work.  Without compensation, we will return to the 
wild west of the United States in the 19th century.  The United States originally had little 
copyright protection for writers.  English books were pirated by United States 
publishers, and there was little incentive for U.S. writers to write.  The United States 
changed its attitude to copyright protection in the early part of the 20th century and 
became a creative powerhouse.  Currently in Australia, most professional writers are 
struggling to make a living.

The writer is credited for their work.  For example, the writer’s name is prominent on the 
cover of books.  It is illegal to publish a writer’s work without giving the writer credit, 
unless the writer consents.  It is also illegal for a person to claim another’s work as their 
own.  These moral rights exist to protect the personal relationship between a creator 
and their work, a concept separate from the economic rights of copyright.  Moral rights 
are a personal, non-transferable set of rights that remain with the writer, even if they 
have sold or transferred the copyright.  The core reasons for having moral rights are 
rooted in the idea that a work is an extension of the author’s personality, reputation, and 
honour.  There are also economic reasons for moral rights – to allow writers to build up 
their “brand” to generate repeat sales and to prevent consumer’s being misled.

Writers require control over their works.  This is a simple as deciding when a work is 
ready for publication, selecting which publisher will distribute their work, and approving 
(or rejecting) edits recommended by the publisher.  It’s the writer’s work.

As discussed below, the Interim Report fails to recognise the impact of AI on 
compensation, credit and control.  AI will have a material adverse impact on the 
creative process and the publishing business.  The impact will be more than a negative 
economic impact; it will degrade what makes us Australian and what it means to be 
human.

Writers and AI

The focus of Interim Report is on using existing works to train AI.  This focus misses the 
big picture.

AI companies in the United States, England and China are using Australian writers’ 
works to train AI to create AI-generated works in competition to the writers on whose 
work they were trained.   AI generated outputs are the bigger concern, not the writers’ 
input to train AI.  The output of AI will be works of ﬁction and non-ﬁction that can be 
churned out at low cost and that will compete for reader’s attention with works written 
by human writers.

In e`ect, AI companies are stealing writers’ works to develop AI tools to put writers out 
of business.

3

This was recognised in a recent United States copyright judgment that considered AI 
and fair use:1

“What copyright law cares about, above all else, is preserving the 
incentive for human beings to create artistic and scientiﬁc works. Therefore, it is 
generally illegal to copy protected works without permission. And the doctrine of 
“fair use,” which provides a defense to certain claims of copyright infringement, 
typically doesn’t apply to copying that will signiﬁcantly diminish the ability of 
copyright holders to make money from their works (thus signiﬁcantly diminishing 
the incentive to create in the future). Generative AI has the potential to ﬂood the 
market with endless amounts of images, songs, articles, books, and more. 
People can prompt generative AI models to produce these outputs using a tiny 
fraction of the time and creativity that would otherwise be required. So by 
training generative AI models with copyrighted works, companies are creating 
something that often will dramatically undermine the market for those works, 
and thus dramatically undermine the incentive for human beings to create things 
the old fashioned way.

Take, for example, biographies. If a company uses copyrighted 
biographies to train a model, and if the model is thus capable of generating 
endless amounts of biographies, the market for many of the copied biographies 
could be severely harmed. Perhaps not the market for Robert Caro’s Master of 
the Senate, because that book is at the top of so many people’s lists of 
biographies to read. But you can bet that the market for lesser-known 
biographies of Lyndon B. Johnson will be a`ected. And this, in turn, will diminish 
the incentive to write biographies in the future.

Or take magazine articles. If a company uses copyrighted magazine 
articles to train a model capable of generating similar articles, it’s easy to 
imagine the market for the copied articles diminishing substantially. Especially if 
the AI-generated articles are made available for free. And again, how will this 
a`ect the incentive for human beings to put in the e`ort necessary to produce 
high-quality magazine articles?”

The AI industry responds that AI learning is similar to human learning, and that AI merely 
reads and learns from existing works but does not store them.  This is a 
misrepresentation.

The AI process is fundamentally di`erent to the work of a writer who develops her skills 
by studying the works of others.  That writer will put her own spin on what she writes,

Creativity

A key issue for the PC to consider is how the ﬂood of AI generated works will impact the 
creativity of human authors and the resulting impact on the Australian economy and 
society.

AI learns from patterns and produces outputs based on a statistical analysis of these 
patterns.  AI is not human intelligence.  By deﬁnition is it artiﬁcial.  There is no humanity 
in AI.

• There is a lack of embodied experience in AI.  AI has no skin in the game.  It can’t 
feel the sting of a bluebottle, the warmth of a hug, or the pang of loss.  Humanity 
is not a thought; it's a feeling, and AI has no body to feel with.

6

• AI has no genuine consciousness or self-awareness.  AI's “consciousness” is an 
illusion.  It is a sophisticated mirror reﬂecting the data it has been trained on.  AI 
can mimic self-awareness, but it's not truly aware.  It doesn't have an inner life, a 
soul, or an existential dread. 
• AI lacks moral compass or empathy.  AI can learn the rules of right and wrong, 
but it will never feel the weight of a moral choice.  It can’t feel shame, guilt, or the 
messy, beautiful pain of empathy. 
• AI has an absence of a life-and-death experience.  AI doesn't fear death, so it 
can’t truly understand life. 
• AI has no human purpose.  Humans and AI can think, but only humans can think 
and not make thoughts their aim. 
• AI has incapacity for originality, creativity and intent.  Human creativity is not just 
pattern recognition; it’s a messy process born from a unique life experience.  A 
human artist's work is infused with their struggles, their passions, their broken 
heart, and their triumphs.  AI can paint like Van Gogh, but it can't su`er like him.

You may fall in love with your computer, but your computer will not fall in love with you.

Writers bring humanity to their works, which AI lacks.  If AI kills the market for books 
created by humans, humanity decreases.  Books that are created by AI will ﬂood the 
market, but for society as a whole, we will be worse o`.  We will enter an economically 
e`icient dark age.

But it is worse than that.  The industrial revolution was the death of many trades.  In 
Australia, we once had cabinetmakers, joiners and woodworkers who made and 
repaired quality furniture.  We had local skills.  Now, with Ikea and Amart, we have cost 
e`icient furniture, but we lost a range of technical skills.  Now all our furniture looks 
similar; the Temu look.  AI will cause the same to happen with other human skills.  
Readers will buy cost e`icient AI-created books, and we will have lost skills to create 
stories.  We will lose the skills to dream and imagine.

There is a di`erence between watching a football match played by human teams and a 
football match played by robot teams.  Humans can be injured, may break the rules, 
may get away with breaking the rules, and will express triumph and disaster in one 
game.  In the future, robots may play a technical superior game with interesting clever 
moves.  But would you prefer to watch robot football over human football?  If there are 
no humans with the skills to play football, you may have no choice.

This impacts not just writers but all creators.  For example, in relation to artists:

Left unregulated, AI will kill our imagination.   This will have a broader impact on human

Copyright Reform

Copyright reform is urgently needed, but not as suggested by the Interim Report.  We 
make the following suggestions for consideration by the PC:

Recommendation One - Compensation

Writers and other creators should be compensated if their work is or has been used to 
train AI.  This compensation should be on the basic of the output produced by the AI 
tool.

For example, if an AI tool produces revenue of $1 billion a year in Australia, then a 
generous percentage of that revenue should be paid to Australian writers and other 
creators whose work was used to create the AI tool.  The percentage should take into 
account not only the value of the work used to train the AI tool, but also the loss of 
revenue due to competition from AI generated works.

Recommendation Two – Control

Writers and other creators should have control of when and who uses their works to 
train AI.  A writer may be comfortable with one AI company using her works, but ﬁnd the

10

tools, practices and ethics of another AI company to be unacceptable.  A writer may 
allow some works to be used to train AI, but not other works.

Recommendation Three – Credit

It should be illegal for AI tools to create AI generated works “in the style of” or “as a 
sequel to” or “as a translation of” a writer’s or other creator’s work.

Further, if an AI tool uses a writer’s work as the basis for AI generated content, then the 
writer should be properly cited and credited.

Recommendation Four - Jurisdiction

AI models are trained outside of Australia.  Hence, the copying undertaken by AI 
companies to train their models takes place outside of Australia and Australian law 
does not apply to that training.

Australian law should make it clear that AI models that are trained outside of Australia 
but are made available to Australian users are subject to Australian copyright law, 
including in respect of training.

This reﬂects doctrines of United States patent law, known as the product-by-process 
doctrine.  If a patented process is undertaken outside of the United States (and thus 
outside of the reach of U.S. patent law), but the product of the process is imported into 
or used in the United States, then this is infringement of the U.S. process patent.  See 35 
U.S.C. § 271(g).

Using this same anti-avoidance concept, if an AI model is trained in the United States, 
and the use of the AI model is by Australian users, then Australian copyright law should 
apply to the training of the AI model in the United States.  This is particularly so for works 
of Australian authors that are used to train the AI models.

United States AI companies say that only one law should apply to them, and that is 
United States law.  Without real evidence, they threaten that if Australia does not adopt 
United States law, then AI development will move from Australia to the United States.  
Adopting a mirror of the anti-avoidance concept discussed above (that is part of United 
States law) will minimise this threatened harm.

Recommendation Five – Censorship

Where AI models have been programmed to promote, demote or exclude certain views, 
the AI provider should publish detailed explanations of such override rules.

Recommendation Six – No TDM exemption

We do not support a text and data mining exception to copyright law in Australia.

11

A TDM exception would mean less compensation, less control, and fewer protections 
for Australian creators, while advantaging large platforms and AI developers.

While TDM can support innovation and research, a broad exception that allows copying 
and mining of works without permission or compensation would disadvantage 
Australian creators.

For Australian writers and creators, the downsides of a TDM exemption include:

1. Loss of revenue opportunities

• 
Writers and creators may miss out on new revenue streams from licensing their 
works for TDM. 
• 
Without a right to control or be paid for mining, publishers and rights 
organisations lose bargaining power.

2. Erosion of control over works

• 
Creators lose the ability to decide how their work is used, including uses they 
may object to (e.g., training AI that competes with them). 
• 
Unlike fair dealing in Australia (which is purpose-specific and narrow), a broad 
TDM exception could remove consent entirely.

3. Undermining of incentives

• 
If works can be mined for free, the incentive for writers to invest in producing 
high-quality creative content is weakened. 
• 
This is particularly acute in smaller markets like Australia, where writers rely on 
limited income streams.

4. International competitiveness issues

• 
Some jurisdictions (e.g. the EU) allow creators to opt out of TDM.  If Australia 
introduced a broad exception without an opt-out, local writers would be 
disadvantaged compared to overseas peers.

5. Risk of market concentration

• 
Large tech companies, not individual creators, are the main beneficiaries of free 
access to creative works for AI training. 
• 
Writers bear the costs of creation, but corporations reap the profits from mining.

12

6. Possible moral rights concerns

• 
Mining may lead to derivative uses that conflict with a writer’s right of integrity 
(e.g., AI-generated works in their style), with little recourse under current law.

(These six points and reasons were produced by Google Gemini AI, so they must be 
true!) 
 
A TDM exception risks shifting value away from Australian creators and towards global 
technology companies.  Any reform should carefully balance innovation with the rights 
and livelihoods of creators.  Options such as compensation frameworks (see 
Recommendation One) or opt-out mechanisms (See Recommendation Two), rather 
than blanket exceptions, warrant close consideration.

Conclusion

Copyright protects creativity.  AI harms creativity.  Copyright law should be 
strengthened, not weakened, to protect writers and creators.  Otherwise, creativity as 
we know it will fade, leaving our culture poorer, our stories fewer, and our society 
dominated by imitation rather than original thought.
