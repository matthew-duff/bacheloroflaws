Trade practices — False and misleading statements — Goods delivered FOB —
Rejection of goods by buyer — Measure of damages — Trade Practices Act 1974
(Cth) ss 4, 6(2){a) and (h), 52.

Sale of goods — Consumer sale — Goods not of merchantable quality nor
reasonably fit for their purpose — Goods delivered FOB Sydney — Rejection of
goods by buyer in Norfolk Island — Measure of damages — Sale of Goods Act 1923
(NSW) ss 19, 53.

Consumer protection — Consumer sale — Goods not of merchantable quality nor
reasonably fit for their purpose — Measure of damages ~ Trade Practices Act 1974
(Cth) ss 4p, 71 — Sale of Goods Act 1923 (NSW) ss 19, 53,

Companies ~ Corporate veil — Whether second company liable as principal for
misrepresentation made by first company.

A contract for the sale of a brickmaking machine and shredder to pulverise soil
was entered into following advertisements and a demonstration. The machine failed
to perform as promised.

Held: (i) Against the first and second respondent the applicants were entitled to
recover for breaches of ss 52, 72(1) and (2) of the Trade Practices Act 1974 (Cth)
and s 19 of the Sale of Goods Act 1923 (NSW).

(ii) The second respondent’s liability was based on the finding that the first
respondent was carrying on business for the second respondent and that the first
respondent was in the position of an agent acting for an undisclosed principal.

Smith, Stone and Knight Lid v City of Birmingham [1939] 4 AN ER 116, applied.
Dennis Willcox Pty Lid v FCT (1988) 79 ALR 267; Sharrment Pty Lid v Official
Trustee in Bankruptcy (1988) 18 FCR 449; 82 ALR 530; Industrial Equity Lid
Blackburn (1977) 137 CLR 567; 17 ALR 575, distinguished.

(iii) Against the third, fourth and seventh respondents the applicants were
entitled to recover for breach of s 52 of the Trade Practices Act.

(iv) The measure of damages for breach of s $2 is the tortious measure.

Gould v Vaggelas (1984) 157 CLR 215; 56 ALR 31; Gates v City Mutual Life
Assurance Society Lid (1986) 160 CLR 1; 63 ALR 600, applied.

(v) A representation need not be the sole inducement under $ 52; it is sufficient if

the representation plays only a minor part in the formation of the contract.
Gould v Vaggelas (1984) 157 CLR 215; 56 ALR 31, applied.

(vi) Although the terms of the contract were for delivery FOB Sydney as the goods
were not in accordance with the contract they could be lawfully rejected after
delivery in Norfolk Island.

Vigers Brothers v Sanderson Brothers [1901] 1 KB 608, applied,

(vii) Thus, the measure of damages was for non-delivery pursuant to ss 53 and 71

of the Trade Practices Act.

Ihave earlier referred to the report made by Mr Duvall about the nature
of the soil on Norfolk Island from the point of view of its suitability for

Called in the Spreags’ case was Dr W R Lawson who is acting head of the
Department of Industrial Arts of the University of New South Wales. Dr
Lawson holds the degrees of Bachelor of Science (with first class honours)
from the University of Sydney and Doctor of Philosophy from the University
of New South Wales, Dr Lawson said that his field was ceramics which, he
said, was a broad term that covered everything from cement through to
pottery, bricks and tiles and then to ceramics used in engineering. He said
that ceramics was a very broad field. Some objection was taken to his
qualifications to speak about the issues in this matter, but in my opinion he
was qualified to do so.

Dr Lawson dealt first with the suitability of Norfolk Island soil as a
primary ingredient in the manufacture of bricks. He said that, although it
was variable, it contained between 5 and 15 per cent of a fine
montmorilionitic clay/silt formed from the weathering of basalt. He said
that the majority was a medium grained sand and that this composition was
very close to the ideal for making soil/cement building materials, namely, 75
per cent sand, 25 per cent silt and clay, with the clay not more than 10 per
cent,

Dr Lawson visited Norfolk Island in November or early December 1987.
The brickmaker and the shredder were produced to him by Mr Spreag and
he attempted to make bricks with the local soil. Two mixes were tried. One
contained 80 per cent of Norfolk Island soil, 10 per cent of basalt dust and
10 per cent of Portland cement. The second mix consisted of 35 per cent
Norfolk Island soil, 60 per cent basalt dust and 5 per cent Portland cement.

Dr Lawson said that, apart from some minor edge chipping, the bricks
showed good green strength by which he meant handling characteristics.
Compression tests later carried out at the University of New South Wales
were satisfactory. Dr Lawson concluded that, with further systematic testing
and using appropriate methods to accurately control water content, particle
size distribution, evenness of clay and cement coating on the non-plastic
materials, and proper drying and curing, it was apparent that the Norfolk
Island soil was suitable as a primary ingredient in the manufacture of
cement stabilised mud bricks. .

A test was carried out under Dr Lawson’s supervision to see whether 500
bricks per hour could be produced. The trial was conducted with the second
mix described above. The trial, not allowing time for careful preparation

bricks at the rate of 210 per hour. But, despite careful cleaning and oiling of
all the mould surfaces, after less than five minutes and the production of 10
bricks, deterioration in the quality of the top face of the bricks became
apparent. This was due to the build up of mix material on the ram mould.
After seven minutes the bricks began to stick to the base of the moulds,
eventually necessitating stopping to remove them. Further build up on the
ram mould was apparent and the quality of the bricks produced
deteriorated further After 10 mimutes one hopper full of mix had been used

Conclusions arising from the expert evidence

Dr Lawson and Mr White, who were both called to give oral evidence,
were of the opinion, notwithstanding what Mr Duvall said in his report, that
the Norfolk Island soil was suitable for cement stabilised brickmaking.'I
think Mr Duvall’s views, notwithstanding that he was not called, are entitled
to substantial weight. Reinforcing him is the plain fact that bricks have
never been successfully made on Norfolk Island. But I find it difficult to put
aside the evidence of two witnesses as well qualified as Dr Lawson and Mr
White are. Their evidence establishes that the problem was not the soil but
the brickmaker itself. Accordingly I accept Dr Lawson’s evidence to the
effect that the brickmaker in question was not suitable for brickmaking
wherever it might have been used unless it were used to make no more than
40 to 50 bricks per hour.

I do think that it emerges that the Norfolk Island material gave rise to an
additional problem especially if it was wet. Dr Lawson emphasised that no
more than 3 per cent moisture should have been added to dry material and
that material which was not dried out after rain was quite unsatisfactory. All
these findings lead me to the conclusion that the brickmaker was valueless
not only to the Spreags but to anyone except, perhaps, for the purpose of
scrap.

So far as the shredder is concerned, it may be that it could have been
made to work satisfactorily, but the starting problems encountered by Mr
David Foster were never rectified. Whatever the problem with it was, it was
never diagnosed. Furthermore, the shredder had the problems referred to
by Dr Lawson. It was simply not adequate for the task even if it had worked
satisfactorily. Finally, the shredder was of absolutely no use or value to the
Spreags unless they had a brickmaking machine which would work
satisfactorily

Liability of the respondents — Paeson and Componere

On 9 September 1986 Mr Foster was acting as agent for Paeson. It was
then that the Spreags were induced to purchase both the brickmaker and
the shredder. The contract of sale which subsequently resulted was induced
by misrepresentations made by Mr Foster on Paeson’s behalf that:

(a) the brickmaker would make bricks from any cement stabilised soil
including soil from the site where the brickmaker was installed;

(b) the brickmaker could be operated by one man;

(c) the brickmaker would produce bricks at the rate of 500 per hour.

In relation to the brickmaker which was the subject of the sale, each of
these representations was false. The machine supplied could make bricks,
but at a rate of no more than 40 to 50 per hour, and then not without
substantial trouble occasioned by the moist and sticky nature of the Norfolk
Island soil especially when wet, As the machine produced bricks, some of its
important working parts were damaged. It seems unlikely that, if the
Spreags had operated the machine for long, it would have maintained even
the meagre production rate which Dr Lawson found it to have. The Spreags
were induced to-buy the shredder in effect as an adjunct or accessory to the
brickmaker. They would not have bought it had they thought that the
brickmaker would not operate satisfactorily. In any event it was defective
because it could not be started without much difficulty and, once started,
broke down repeatedly. Furthermore, for the reasons given by Dr Lawson, it
would not have been suitable for its intended task even if it had worked
satisfactorily.

‘These findings lead to the conclusion that Paeson, by its agent Mr Foster,
engaged in misleading and deceptive conduct which caused the Spreags
damage. The liability of Paeson under s 52 of the Trade Practices Act is
therefore established.

‘The same findings lead to its liability for breach of the terms implied in
the contract of sale by s 71(2) of the Trade Practices Act and s 19(1) of the
Sale of Goods Act. The terms are not expressed in precisely the same

Act. L should mention in passing s 4B of that Act which provides, so far as

goods did not exceed “the prescribed amount”. Section 4B(2) provides that
the prescribed amount is $40,000. Each of the two machines in question, the
brickmaker and the shredder, was sold for a price less than $40,000 so that
s 71 of the Act applies to the transaction.

references in the Act to trade or commerce were by express provision

In summary then, I have decided that the Spreags are entitled to recover

Sawyer or Natural Power. The case against Mr Smith was withdrawn.
Accordingly, there should be judgment for each of those respondents. The
causes of action upon which the Spreags are entitled to succeed against
Paeson and Componere are for breaches of s 52 of the Trade Practices Act
and of the terms implied into the contract of sale by s 71(2) and (2) thereof
and s 19(1) and (2) of the Sale of Goods Act. As against Bergood, Mr Foster
and Mr MacGrory, the Spreags are entitled to succeed only under 5 52 of
the Trade Practices Act. The measure of damages which applies in actions
for breach of that section is the tortious measure: Gould v Vaggelas, supra,
and Gates v City Mutual Life Assurance Society Ltd (1986) 160 CLR 1; 63
ALR 600. In contract the measure of damages is for breach of a condition
going to the root of the contract on the basis that the Spreags did not accept
the two machines because of their inability to make bricks, whether on
Norfolk Island or elsewhere. 1 am satisfied that the Spreags never accepted
the machines but rejected them. This is clear from the entirety of the
circumstances of the case, including Mr Spreag’s letter earlier set out. The
Spreags still have possession of the machines but, apart from making them
available to Dr Lawson to conduct his tests, they have not been used and
they have been stored carefully to await the day, if it ever arrives, when
Paeson or Componere takes them back.

I should mention at this point discussion which took place during the
argument concerning the question whether a buyer, who has taken delivery
of goods, can subsequently reject them for breach of a condition of the
contract. The terms of the contract here provided for delivery FOB Sydney.
Thus the goods were delivered no later than the time when they passed over
the ship’s rail. This matter was the subject of consideration in the judgment
of Bigham J, in Vigers Brothers v Sanderson Brothers [1901] 1 KB 608, In that
case a contract for the sale of laths to be shipped and to be “about the
specification stated below”, provided that the property in the goods was.to
be deemed to have passed to the buyers when the goods were put on board,
and contained a clause that, if any dispute arose under the stipulations of
the contract, the buyers were not to reject any of the goods, but the dispute
was to be referred to arbitration. The sellers put on board laths which were
not of the specified lengths. It was held that the purchasers were entitled to
reject the laths on their arrival and were not bound by the terms of the
contract to accept them subject to an allowance to be fixed by arbitration.
Relevantly for this case Bigham J said (at 612):

“It was said that by the terms of the contracts the property in the goods
had passed on shipment, or, in the alternative, that the property had passed
716 AUSTRALIAN LAW REPORTS Fed CofA

by reason of the defendants having accepted the goods by receiving them on
board their ship, and that, therefore, the defendants must keep the goods
and be satisfied with an allowance in respect of their inferiority. I think
there is nothing in either of these points. The provision in the contracts as to
the passing of the property only applies to a shipment of goods which come
within the meaning of the contract; it cannot apply to any others; and the
receipt of the goods by the captain is no acceptance at all of the goods as a
delivery under the contract. The captain of the ship is merely an agent to
receive the goods for the purpose of carriage; he knows nothing of the
contract of purchase, and is not an agent to accept delivery under it.”

Similar considerations apply here. The goods which passed over the ship’s
rail were not in accordance’ with the contract of sale. Neither the
brickmaker nor the shredder was of merchantable quality. The brickmaker
was not capable of making bricks from Norfolk Island soil except at a rate of
40 to 50 per hour and then only with the greatest difficulty. The shredder
was defective and, in any event, could not, even if it had worked
satisfactorily, have reduced the size of the granules of soil to particles no

The measure of damages in a case such as this is stated in McGregor on
Damages (14th ed, para 629) as follows:

“If the buyer has lawfully rejected the goods the case becomes in effect
one of non-delivery and the measure of damages is therefore the same as
that applicable to non-delivery, with the one addition that where the seller
wrongfully refuses to take back the goods the buyer may recover expenses
incurred in keeping them cither until the seller does receive them or until
they can be resold on the market by the buyer.”

The measure of damages for non-delivery is provided for in s 53 of the
Sale of Goods Act and is the estimated loss directly and naturally resulting,
in the ordinary course of events, from the seller’s breach of contract. By
analogy, the measure of damages for breaches of the terms of contracts
implied by s 71 of the Trade Practices Act is the same.

In my opinion this is a case where there has been a total failure of
consideration: The Spreags have lost the entirety of the moneys which they
paid for the machines. Additionally they have been put to not insubstantial
expense and they should recover the amount of this. There should also be
an award of interest under s 51A of the Federal Court of Australia Act 1976
(Cth). The amounts paid by the Spreags to Paeson for the brickmaker, the
shredder and two sets of moulds totalled $48,350. Freight amounted to
$1679.48 and hydraulic fluid and air freight for it amounted to $416.25. The
wasted expenditure incurred by the Spreags amounted to $11,178.61. These
included the cost of soil brought to the Spreags’ premises, insurance of the
brickmaker and shredder, amounts incurred for the supply of cement and
lime, amounts paid for Customs duty and amounts incurred in wasted visits
to Sydney. There are some other incidental items to which it is unnecessary
to refer. The total of all these amounts is $61,624.34. The bulk of the
amounts was paid in September 1986, but some of the wasted expenditure
was incurred later. For ease of calculation I have selected a rate of interest
of 15 per cent per annum. I have done this to avoid the necessity of

The amount which I have indicated the Spreags should recover from
Paeson and Componere is based on the measure of damages which applies
in a case involving a breach of contract. No more could be recovered by
them in a claim for damages under s 52 of the Trade Practices Act so that it
is unnecessary to reach a conclusion about the measure which would apply
in such a case so far as the two companies are concerned. But it is necessary
to reach a conclusion on that matter in relation to the amount to be
recovered from Bergood, Mr Foster and Mr MacGrory.

Ordinarily the measure is the difference between what was paid for an
article and its actual value. Additionally, expenses may be recovered in
appropriate cases. In the present case it was submitted that there was no
evidence of the value of the brickmaker or the shredder so that the evidence
does not permit me to make any finding on the amount of damages which
should be recovered. In the light of Dr Lawson’s evidence about the
machines and the Fosters’ evidence about their experience with them, I am
satisfied that the two pieces of equipment have no value except for scrap,
There is no evidence of their scrap value, but I think that I may infer that it
would not be great. I may also infer that there would be no scrap metal
market on Norfolk Island so that the machines would have to be returned to
Sydney before they could be sold. Freight, using the figure claimed by the
Spreags for freight as a guide, would be at least $1700 and probably more,
bearing in mind increases in prices which have no doubt occurred since
1986. I am confident that this amount would exceed any scrap value which
the two machines might have. This leads me to the conclusion that the
amount which the Spreags are entitled to recover for damages for breach of
8 52 of the Trade Practices Act is the same as the amount they are entitled
to recover for breach of contract. There will need, however, to be a
reduction in the amount which the Spreags are entitled to recover against

with the consequence that the amount of $61,624.34 earlier referred to
needs to be reduced by $5250. So far as Bergood is concerned, it is on that
reduced sum that interest will need to be calculated,
