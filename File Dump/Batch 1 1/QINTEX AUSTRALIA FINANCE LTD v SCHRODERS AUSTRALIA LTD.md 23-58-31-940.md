Corporations — Debt — Corporate veil — Difficulties in identifying the contracting
party within group arrangements — Law reform — Whether companies in
insolvency should be treated the same way in which they acted prior to insolvency.

The contest in the present proceedings was to identify the member of the Qintex
group of companies on whose behalf a futures exchange contract had been
purchased by the defendant.

On 1 August 1989 the defendant, on behalf of a member of the Qintex group, sold
Y1.2b which realised $11,560,693.64. The defendant drew a cheque in favour of
Qintex Television Ltd (QTL). It paid the cheque into the account of the plaintiff.

At the same time as receiving directions to sell the yen, the defendant received
instructions to purchase a forward contract for Y1.2b for delivery on 4 December
1989. The exercise price for the contract was $12,054,244.12.

On 4 December 1989 the defendant closed out the forward exchange contract at a
loss. In the result, an amount of $1,377,137.60 became payable to the defendant by
the member of the Qintex group on whose behalf the forward exchange contract had
been obtained. The candidates were the plaintiff, QTL or perhaps some other
members of the group.

Held, nominating QTL as the party to the contract:

(i) The parties agreed that the decision as to which company in the group was the
contracting party has to be made in the light of the objective facts and surrounding
circumstances known to both of the contracting parties.

Reardon Smith Line Lid v Yngvar Hansen-Tangen (tas HE Hansen-Tangen)
{1976] 1 WLR 989, applied.

(ii) The laxity of the defendant in conducting its business and maintaining its

-records provided substantial problems and gave the plaintiff some basis for

argument. There was no evidence that anyone on behalf of the Qintex group
designated any of its member companies as the entity entering into the transactions
in question.

(iii) Where there is a departure from standard settlement instructions it is at least
indicative of the fact that there may be something different in the transaction. In
such a case past history may not be a reliable guide.

(iv) Acknowledging that the result was purely artificial, the facts and
documentation that existed point to QTL as the party to the contract rather than the
plaintiff.

(v) Consideration of the need for law reform where a number of companies in a
group conduct business in a manner that does not clearly maintain their separate
legal character.

Industrial Equity Ltd v Blackburn (1977) 137 CLR 567, applied.

As I see it, there is today a tension between the realities of commercial
life and the applicable law in circumstances such as those in this case. In the
everyday rush and bustle of commercial life in the last decade it was seldom
that participants to transactions involving conglomerates with a large
number of subsidiaries paused to consider which of the subsidiaries should
become the contracting party. A graphic example of such an attitude
appears in the evidence of Ms Ferreira, a dealer in the treasury operations
department of the defendant. In her written statement (Ex 3 para 26) she
said:

“In my discussion with either Craig Pratt or Paul Lewis when I confirmed
deals undertaken for Qintex, it was not my practice to ask which of the
Qintex companies was responsible for the deal. I always treated the client as
Qintex and did not differentiate between companies in the group. Paul
Lewis and Craig Pratt always talked as being from ‘Qintex’ without
reference to any specific company. To record foreign exchange transactions
Schroders needed a client code. 1 was only aware of one client coding for
Qintex which was the coding ‘QTLBRI’.”

Mr Craig Pratt and Mr Paul Lewis were respectively assistant treasurer

On the other hand, as late as 1977. In Industrial Equity Ltd v Blackbur
(1977) 137 CLR 567, the High Court of Australia confirmed the need to
preserve, as a matter of law, a rigid demarcation between wholly owned
subsidiaries in the same group of companies, as well as their holding
company. At much the same time, the House of Lords did Ekewise. That
decision was not even thought worthy of being reported but is referred to by
Palmer.

It may be desirable for parliament to consider whether this distinction
between the law and commercial practice should be maintained. This is
especially the case today when the many collapses of conglomerates
occasion may disputes. Regularly, liquidators of subsidiaries, or of the
holding company, come to court to argue as to which of their charges bears
the liability, If an illustration were needed of the futility and
inappropriateness of arguments of this kind, it may be found in the facts
which ground my decision in Impact Datascape Pty Lid v Mcintosh GB
October 1990, unreported). As well, creditors of failed companies
encounter difficulty when they have to select from among the moving targets
the company with which they consider they concluded a contract. The result
has been unproductive expenditure on legal costs, a reduction in the
amount available to creditors, a windfall for some, and an unfair loss to
others. Fairness or equity seems to have little role to play. This may be
illustrated by the argument in the present case. The plaintiff had received
the proceeds of the sale of Y1.2b into its account. Its submission was that
the liability, for the forward contract for ¥1.2b fell on OTL, which it said
was the contracting party and that accordingly, the defendant was not

- entitled to make the set off against moneys standing to the plaintiff’s credit

in the books of the defendant. Attractively as the plaintiff's case was
presented, its intrinsic merits were not readily apparent. If I may venture
the observation, there is a great deal to be said for the suggestion advanced
by those in charge of the demised Hooker group of companies that assets
and liabilities of the parent and the subsidiaries should be aggregated. It
may be argued that there is justification for preserving the same attitude in
relation to the demised companies as was displayed during their active
commercial life.

The parties, and in particular the defendant, have adduced a great deal of
background material which it is claimed, illuminates the answer to the
question earlier posed.

The parties agree that the decision as to which company in the Qintex
group was the contracting party has to be made in the light of the objective
facts and surrounding circumstances known to both of the contracting
parties. It is, however, important to bear in mind the words of Lord
Wilberforce in Reardon Smith Line Lid v Yngvar Hansen-Tangen [1976] 1
WLR 989. He said at 995:

“No contracts are made in a vacuum: there is always a setting in which
they have to be placed. The nature of what is legitimate to have regard to is
usually described as ‘the surrounding circumstances’ but this phrase is
imprecise: it can be illustrated but hardly defined. In a commercial context
it is certainly right that the court should know the commercial purpose of
the contract and this in turn presupposes knowledge of the genesis of the
transaction, the background, the context, the market in which the parties
are operating.”

And later he added at 996:

“... It is often said that, in order to be admissible in aid of construction,
these extrinsic facts must be within the knowledge of both parties to the
contract, but this requirement should not be stated in too narrow a sense.
When one speaks of the intention of the parties to the contract, one is
speaking objectively —- the parties cannot themselves give direct evidence of
what their intention was —- and what must be ascertained is what is to be
taken as the intention which reasonable people would have had if placed in
the situation of the parties. Similarly when one is speaking of aim, or object,
or commercial purpose, one is speaking objectively of what reasonable
person would have had if placed in the situation of the parties. It is in this
sense and not in the sense of constructive notice or of estopping fact that
judges are found using words like ‘knew or must be taken to have
known’ ...”

departure from standard settlement instructions, it is at least indicative of

was thereafter accepted by the Qintex group as an accurate statement of the
position which obtained.

to recover from the principal by making the appropriation from the
defendant’s accounts in December 1989.

However, the defendant is unable to point to anything in the evidence
which amounted to an appointment, either express or implied, by the
plaintiff of QTL as its agent. One very good reason for this was because no
one in Qintex gave a second thought to the fact that the transaction had
been booked to QTL. Furthermore, it was never intended by anyone that
QTL should act as agent for any other company in the Qintex group in this
transaction. As Professor Treitel points out in his Law of Contract 7th ed p
529: “the rule that an agent must intend to act on behalf of his principal
distinguishes agency from other analogous relationships, and is helpful in
cases where it is clear that a person acted as agent, but not clear whose
agent he was.” As well, in the September transactions, QTL appeared to be
the contracting party for the acquisition of the necessary yen to pay the
interest to QUKK. The plaintiff appeared to have absolutely no role to play
in that transaction.

As an alternative, the defendant contended that whichever company the
defendant dealt with, that company was acting as agent for the Qintex group
in the sense of all of those companies of which the ultimate holding
company was QAL. It was argued that each company in the group
thereupon became jointly and severally liable to the defendant. This
submission finds no foothold either in principle or in authority.
Additionally, it fails for the same reason as the other submission as to

agency.

Finally the defendant submitted that the plaintiff entered into the
contract itself. The matters relied on were in many instances not known to
the other party. At the end of the day I find the arguments advanced by the
plaintiff more compelling.

It must be acknowledged that whatever the decision on the competing
submissions, the result will be wholly artificial. The realities were these:

1. The forward exchange contract was taken out to cover the payment
required to be made in December by QJKK to Shinpan.

2. Capp instructed Pratt to purchase the hedge contract but, if possible,
without mentioning either QJKK or Shinpan (Ex 2 para 13). So far as the
evidence goes, Pratt was successful in this regard.

3. Therefore whichever Qintex company was the contracting party, it
acted as agent for an undisclosed principal, not the plaintiff, but OFKK.

4, All the documentation produced by the defendant identified the
contracting party as QTL. That was due to errors within the defendant's
organisation of which the plaintiff knew nothing and cared less.

5. The only objective fact to connect the plaintiff with the transaction was
the payment into its bank account. That in turn was an error on the part of
the Qintex group of which the defendant was unaware.

Doing the best I can with the facts thrown up by the compounded errors I
consider that, objectively, the facts and documentation point to QTL as the
party to the contract rather than the plaintiff. More neutrally, the facts do
not identify the plaintiff as the contracting party, whether as principal or
agent. That determines the issue thrown up by the summons in favour of the
plaintiff. ‘

In the course of final addresses, the plaintiff submitted, for the first time,
that it should be permitted to argue that, even if the contracting party was
the plaintiff, the defendant was not entitled to set off the amounts standing
to its credit. The amounts standing to the credit of the plaintiff at the date of
appointment of the receivers, were pre-receivership debts. The defendant
purported to set off those debts against the loss arising out of the
cancellation of the forward exchange contract. That loss did not crystallise
until after the appointment of the receivers. It was submitted that set off will
not be permitted between debts arising before a receivership and
afterwards.

The defendant submitted that the plaintiff should not be permitted to rely
upon a point which had not theretofore been advanced, and indeed,
according to the defendant, specifically disclaimed. The plaintiff submitted
that, with the exception of one question of fact, that is the date of
appointment of the receiver, the submission turned entirely on a point of
law, and in accordance with authority, | should allow the point to be argued.

Receivers were appointed, inter alia, to the plaintiff by order of the
Supreme Court of Victoria made on 21 November 1989: (Ex A para 1). The
order was made on the application of QAL. From the form of Order, it
would seem that many other interested entities were represented at the
hearing. I am sure that there would have been a number of secured
creditors. I do not know what rights any secured creditors may claim to the
moneys which had been standing to the credit of the plaintiff in the books of
the defendant. It seems to me that I would have to allow any secured
creditors to be represented at any hearing at which the question of set-off
was to be argued.
