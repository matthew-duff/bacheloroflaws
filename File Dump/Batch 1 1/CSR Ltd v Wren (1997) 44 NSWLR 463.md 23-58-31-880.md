    Negligence — Duty of care — Holding company and subsidiary's employee —
         Whether duty of care owed — Management staff of subsidiary being
B        employees of holding company — Sufficient relationship for duty of care.
           A plaintiff was awarded damages in the Dust Diseases Tribunal for
        mesothelioma which was found to be caused by having inhaled asbestos fibres
        whilst employed by Asbestos Products Pty Ltd during the period 1950 to 1951.
        Asbestos Products Pty Ltd was a wholly owned subsidiary of CSR Ltd and was
        described as part of its Building Materials Division. Asbestos Products Pty Ltd's
        board of directors were all staff members of CSR Ltd together with all non-factory
C       personnel working at Asbestos Products Pty Ltd.
           The essential question in the case was whether the relationship between CSR
        Ltd and the plaintiff was such as to give rise to a duty of care in CSR Ltd to
        protect him from the risk of foreseeable injury.
           Held: The fact that the operations of the subsidiary company, Asbestos Products
        Pty Ltd, were subject to direction, control or involvement by or from CSR and the
        fact that the management staff at the subsidiary company were all CSR Ltd staff
        were sufficient to establish a relationship between the plaintiff and CSR Ltd so as
D       to give rise to a duty of care by CSR Ltd to him and that duty was co-extensive
        with that owed by an employer to an employee. (465F, 483G-485E)
           Stevens v Brodribb Sawmilling Co Pty Ltd (1986) 160 CLR 16, referred to.

      POWELL JA. The essential facts which have given rise to this appeal and
    cross-appeal have been set out by Beazley JA and Stein JA in the judgment
    which their Honours have prepared, which judgment I have read in draft.
    I agree with the orders which Beazley JA and Stein JA have proposed for the
    disposition of the appeal, and generally with the reasons which their Honours
    give for proposing those orders, but I wish to make a short comment as to the
    basis for the duty of care which CSR owed to Mr Wren. I am, however, unable
D   to share their Honours' view that the cross-appeal should be upheld and I will
    record shortly my reasons for the view which I have reached on that question.
       CSR's duty of care:
       Although it is true that, at all relevant times, the manufacturing processes in
    which Mr Wren was engaged were carried out by employees of Asbestos
    Products Pty Ltd, which was CSR's subsidiary, and that it was Asbestos
E   Products Pty Ltd, and not CSR, which employed Mr Wren, it seems to me that,
    if one concentrates one's attention upon the legal relationship of master and
    servant, one is diverted from the crucial questions which were, first, were the
    facts such as to impose upon CSR a duty of care towards Mr Wren; and,
    secondly, if so, what was the nature and extent of that duty.
       As the facts which Beazley JA and Stein JA have recorded in their judgment
    make clear, despite the legal relationship of master and servant which existed
F   between Asbestos Products Pty Ltd and Mr Wren, it was not Asbestos Products
    Pty Ltd, through its officers or employees, but CSR, through those of its
    employees — including the factory foreman — which in fact controlled and
    supervised the factory, the manufacturing processes carried out within it, and
    the activities of Asbestos Products Pty Ltd employees — including Mr Wren —
    who were engaged in carrying out those processes. This being so, it seems to
    me that CSR ought to be regarded as having assumed pro hac vice the mantle
    of employer of those employees of Asbestos Products Pty Ltd who were
G   engaged in the manufacturing processes being carried out in the factory, and
    thus became subject to a duty, owed to those employees, to provide for them
    (inter alia) a safe place of work and to establish and maintain a safe system of
    work.
       Although, in such cases, the facts are the reverse of those with which the
    Court is now concerned to deal, a similar approach to that which I suggest is
466                             SUPREME COURT                              [(1997)

appropriate in this case can be seen in cases in which courts have to determine      A
what, if any, are the duties owed by a third party to a person whose services
have been “lent” by that person's employer to the third party: see, eg, Garrard
v AE Southey & Co Ltd and Standard Telephones and Cables Ltd [1952] 2 QB
174.
  As the facts which Beazley JA and Stein JA have recorded in their judgment
make clear, despite the fact that, at the relevant time, officers of not only
Asbestos Products Pty Ltd but of CSR knew or ought to have known, that               B
asbestos was a hazardous substance, both Asbestos Products Pty Ltd and the
relevant employees of CSR failed to ensure that the factory was a reasonably
safe place in which employees such as Mr Wren could work within the factory.

   BEAZLEY JA and STEIN JA. This is an appeal from a decision of the
Dust Diseases Tribunal in which the appellant (CSR), the holding company of
the respondent's employer, Asbestos Products Pty Ltd, was held tortiously            D
liable for the respondent's (Mr Wren's) asbestos caused mesothelioma. CSR
appeals against the determination of liability and also appeals against the
quantum of damages awarded to Mr Wren.
   Mr Wren, for his part, cross-appeals against the trial judge's determination
that Midalco Pty Ltd (formerly known as Australian Blue Asbestos and to
which we will refer as Midalco) was not liable in negligence for failing to warn
Mr Wren of the dangers associated with the inhalation of asbestos fibre.             E
   The issues on the appeal are whether CSR owed a duty of care to Mr Wren,
and if so whether it breached that duty, so as to be liable for his mesothelioma,
which was caused by inhalation of asbestos fibre during the period he was
employed by Asbestos Products Pty Ltd. The same issues in relation to Midalco
arise on the cross-appeal.
   Before proceeding, it should be noted that senior counsel for Mr Wren
accepted that, notwithstanding passages in the trial judge's reasons, no case was    F
made at trial that the circumstances were such that the corporate veil be lifted
or that Asbestos Products Pty Ltd was CSR's agent.
  Background facts:
  Prior to March 1944, Asbestos Products Ltd, a company unassociated with
CSR, had conducted an asbestos cement manufacturing business at premises at
Alexandria. On 24 March 1944, CSR's board of directors approved CSR's
entry into a tripartite agreement for the purchase of Asbestos Products Ltd's        G
business. On 28 June 1944, CSR signed, sealed and delivered various
documents relating to the purchase. Subsequently, on 13 October 1944, CSR
caused Asbestos Products Pty Ltd to be incorporated for the purposes of
carrying on that business, which it did until it was liquidated in 1956.
  Asbestos Products Pty Ltd manufactured essentially two types of asbestos

A   cement products, a flat board and a corrugated sheet, both for use in the
    building industry. Three types of asbestos were used in its process: chrysotile or
    white asbestos; amosite or brown asbestos; and crocidolite or blue asbestos.
    Asbestos Products Pty Ltd obtained its white asbestos from South Africa. From
    about 1946 it commenced using blue asbestos from Midalco's Wittenoom mine
    in Western Australia. The use of blue asbestos increased as production at
    Wittenoom increased. Asbestos Products Pty Ltd also obtained asbestos from
B   the United States of America. The asbestos from South Africa and Wittenoom

      Conditions in which the respondent worked:
      Asbestos Products Pty Ltd's Alexandria factory consisted of a two-storey
    building, described by the trial judge as being a large area at ground level with
    a loft above. The ground floor comprised some office accommodation, shower
    and change rooms, the fitters' room, the hammer mill, and, in a large open area,
    the places where asbestos cement products were moulded, stored, cured and
D   cut. On the upper level were located the beater room, two blower rooms and
    office accommodation for more senior management.
      The factory premises were poorly ventilated. There were no windows. The
    only natural light that came into the premises was through the doors of the
    building which, for the most part, were left open to provide both light and
    ventilation. This had the consequence that on a windy day any dust in the
    factory was blown around. There was no dust extraction equipment installed in
E   the premises. Nor were masks supplied to the employees.

       The manufacturing process:
       For the most part, the asbestos used by Asbestos Products Pty Ltd was
    supplied in hessian bags. Certainly this was the case in respect of the blue
    asbestos supplied by Midalco. Prior to use, Asbestos Products Pty Ltd stored
    the asbestos in a warehouse near its manufacturing factory (the factory). Once
    delivered into the factory premises, the asbestos was emptied out of the hessian
F
    bags and put through the hammer mill where the asbestos fibre was liberated
    from the rock in which it was contained. The rock, or at least that from
    Wittenoom, had, probably, already undergone a crushing process. The process
    in the hammer mill was described as one where the asbestos was “fluffed up”.
    The asbestos fibre was then conveyed upstairs under air pressure through
    ducting and delivered into the beater rooms. There the fibre was mixed with
    water and cement. It was re-transported under gravity to the ground floor where
G   it was placed upon conveyor belts and processed to the thickness required for
    the asbestos product into which it was to be finally converted. The processed
    product was taken from the rollers and placed between oiled steel sheets, which
    were either flat or corrugated, depending upon the product being manufactured,
    for the purposes of curing. During the curing process, the steel sheets were
    covered with an old felt roller which Mr Wren described as “dusty”. When

partly dry, the sheets were cut to size, the flat sheets being guillotined and the    A
corrugated sheets being cut by a bandsaw.

       Knowledge of dangers of asbestos:
       In the years prior to Mr Wren's employment, there had been a number of
    reports which dealt with the dangers of asbestos. Some of these reports were
    tendered in evidence. The first to which the Court was referred was the 1930
    Merewether and Price Report on the effects of asbestos dust on the lungs and
    dust suppression in the asbestos industry. Merewether and Price based their
D   report upon data obtained in an investigation during the years 1928 and 1929.
    The inquiry had been initiated by the Factory Department of the Home Office
    (UK) after the hospitalisation of an asbestos worker.
       The report explored the occurrence of pulmonary fibrosis and other
    pulmonary afflictions in asbestos workers. The study was restricted to the
    examination of workers employed in processes in which there was exposure to
    pure asbestos, or asbestos mixed with a very small percentage of cotton or other
E   vegetable fibre. The authors concluded that the general incidence rate of
    fibrosis of the lungs was rather less than one in eight, or, excluding those
    employed under five years, rather less than one in three. They also concluded
    that the incidence of fibrosis was unaffected by age or sex. After five years'
    exposure, the incidence rate mounted rapidly, and after ten years increased in
    geometrical progression. The authors also considered the effect of exposure
    within different processes, stating:
         “It seems clear that fibrosis of the lungs is a definite occupational risk
F        amongst asbestos workers as a class. Furthermore, it appears that the risk
         falls most heavily on those longest employed and on those engaged in the
         more dusty processes.”
    They concluded:
         “In order to prevent the full development of the disease amongst asbestos
         workers within the space of an average working lifetime, it is necessary to
         reduce the concentration of dust in the air of the workrooms to a figure
G        below that pertaining to spinning at the time over which these cases were
         exposed.”
       The authors expressed the view that it appeared probable that concentration
    of dust and length of exposure, as factors in the production of fibrosis, were
    interdependent within certain limits. They stated that if that hypothesis was
    correct, the practical inferences that followed were that:
472                              SUPREME COURT                              [(1997)

     “…the application of measures resulting in the reduction of the                  A
     concentration of dust in the air in the neighbourhood of dusty asbestos
     processes will cause, firstly a great increase in the length of time before
     workers develop a disabling fibrosis, and secondly, in the almost total
     disappearance of the disease, as the measures for the suppression of dust
     are perfected.”
   Merewether also published a detailed article in 1930 reviewing the results of
the investigation which was the subject of the Merewether and Price Report. He        B
summarised his findings as follows:
        “1. There is a definite risk of the development of a diffuse pulmonary
     fibrosis in persons exposed to the inhalation of asbestos dust.
        2. This risk varies directly as the length of employment (and hence
     length of exposure to dust), and is unaffected by the age of the worker.
        3. There is a considerable difference between the least dusty and the
     most dusty processes investigated, and there is a corresponding variation
     in the relative risk of the development of fibrosis in the workers in these      C
     processes.

  Did CSR owe Mr Wren a duty of care:
  The trial judge found that “CSR's influence over [Asbestos Products Pty

Donoghue v Stevenson [1932] AC 562 at 580. The gospellian stature of these
words in the English common law is such that one is rarely taken directly to the
report of the case or to any authority which precedes it. This case was an              C
exception. The Court was also taken through the development of the law of
negligence in Australia, ending with the High Court's most recent exposition of
the principles which determine the existence of a duty of care in Hill v Van Erp

not injure your neighbour”. As to who is one's “neighbour”, Lord Atkin said

     I am directing my mind to the acts or omissions which are called in

   Foreseeability:
   Lord Atkin thus identified two elements in determining the existence of a
duty of care: foreseeability and proximity. The notion of foreseeability is well-
settled. In Wyong Shire Council v Shirt (1980) 146 CLR 40 at 47, Mason J
stated:
                                                                                        F
     “A risk of injury which is quite unlikely to occur … may nevertheless be
     plainly foreseeable. Consequently, when we speak of a risk of injury as
     being ‘foreseeable’ we are not making any statement as to the probability
     or improbability of its occurrence, save that we are implicitly asserting that
     the risk is not one that is far-fetched or fanciful. Although it is true to say
     that in many cases the greater the degree of probability of the occurrence
     of the risk the more readily it will be perceived to be a risk, it certainly       G
     does not follow that a risk which is unlikely to occur is not foreseeable.”
Mason J summarised the test (at 48) as follows:
     “… a risk of injury which is remote in the sense that it is extremely
     unlikely to occur may nevertheless constitute a foreseeable risk. A risk
     which is not far-fetched or fanciful is real and therefore foreseeable.”

A   See also Nagle v Rottnest Island Authority (1993) 177 CLR 423 at 431.

         “Foreseeability does not mean foresight of the particular course of events

       Proximity and the role of policy:
       The role of proximity in determining the existence of a duty of care has
    proved somewhat vexed. Lord Atkin in Donoghue v Stevenson (at 580-581)
    traced the notion of proximity to Le Lievre v Gould [1893] 1 QB 491, noting
    that proximity was not confined to mere physical proximity, but extended:
F
         “… to such close and direct relations that the act complained of directly
         affects a person whom the person alleged to be bound to take care would
         know would be directly affected by his careless act.”
    See also Heaven v Pender (1883) 11 QBD 503, per Lord Esher.
       Lord Atkin recognised that, as a matter of policy, the “duty net” must not be
    cast too widely and that the notion of proximity was the method whereby
    reasonable constraints are placed upon the operation of the law of negligence so
G   as to satisfy policy considerations. His Lordship gave further recognition to
    policy later in his judgment. In reviewing Earl v Lubbock [1905] 1 KB 253, his
    Lordship agreed with Mathew LJ (at 592) that the duty could not be cast so
    widely that trade could not be carried on. It was thus established early that the
    notion of proximity operated as a control upon the test of reasonable
    foreseeability of injury.

       In Hill v Van Erp, the High Court held that a duty of care was owed by a
    solicitor to an intended beneficiary where the solicitor had acted negligently so
    that the will of a client was invalid, notwithstanding that there was neither
F   reliance on the part of the intended beneficiary nor the assumption of
    responsibility by the solicitor: see Brennan CJ (at 493) and Dawson J (at 501).

    law of negligence by rejecting the notion that proximity is a necessary
    determinant of the existence of a duty of care unaffected by other
    considerations. Certainly, Brennan CJ, Dawson J, McHugh J and Gummow J
    expressed disquiet that the notion of proximity should be so viewed. Dawson J

    would give rise to a duty of care where mere foreseeability of harm was

    of proximity remained “… a useful means of expressing the proposition that in

    establish a duty of care. Something more is required and it is described as

proximity”. His Honour concluded that “proximity in that sense expresses the          A
