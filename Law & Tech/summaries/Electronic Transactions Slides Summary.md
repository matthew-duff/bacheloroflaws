# Electronic Contracting Summary

**Reference:** Davidson, Alan, *Electronic Contracting LAWS5151*, 2026.

## 1. Introduction to Electronic Contracting
Electronic contracts are legally enforceable provided they satisfy standard common law elements and relevant statutory requirements.
- **Key Case:** *Walsh v Ionides* [1853] (Telegraph contract).
- **Key Case:** *Ford v La Forrest* [2001] QSC 261: First Qld case on the *Electronic Transactions Act* (ETA); affirmed that email acceptance is capable of creating legal relations.
- **Key Case:** *Peter Smythe v Vincent Thomas* [2007] NSWSC 844: eBay "click" contracts for high-value items (e.g., vintage aircraft) are binding.

## 2. Formal Requirements & "In Writing"
Generally, contracts do not need to be in writing unless required by statute (e.g., *Statute of Frauds 1677*, *Property Law Act 2023* (Qld) ss 7-8 for land dispositions).

### Common Law vs ETA on "Writing"
- **Common Law:** *Islamic Council of South Australia Inc v Australian Federation of Islamic Councils Inc* [2009] NSWSC 211: "Writing" includes data entered into a computer and appearing on a screen.
- **ETA Application:** The ETA applies where a signature is "required". If not required (e.g., standard email), common law principles may apply to determine if a name at the end of an email constitutes a signature (*Stuart v Hishon* [2013] NSWSC 766).

## 3. Electronic Transactions Acts (ETA)
Australia has nine ETAs (Cth, States, and Territories) designed to remove legal impediments to electronic commerce.

### Core Principles
1.  **Functional Equivalence:** Paper-based and electronic commerce should be treated equally.
2.  **Technology Neutrality:** The law should not discriminate between different forms of technology.

### International Basis
- *UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Commerce 1996*.
- *UNCITRAL Convention on the Use of Electronic Communications in International Contracts 2005* (ECC).

## 4. Statutory Requirements for Validity (Qld ETA)
- **General Validity (s 8):** A transaction is not invalid merely because it took place wholly or partly by electronic communications.
- **Writing (s 11):** Requirement met if information is:
    - Readily accessible for subsequent reference; and
    - The recipient consents to electronic communication.
- **Signature (s 14):** Requirement met if:
    - A method is used to identify the person and indicate their approval/intention;
    - The method is as reliable as appropriate (or proven in fact to fulfill the function); and
    - The recipient consents.
- **Production (s 17):** Documents can be produced electronically if integrity is maintained, they are accessible for reference, and the recipient consents.

### Notable Case Law on Writing/Production
- **Dropbox/Links:** *Conveyor & General Engineering v Basetec Services* [2014] QSC 30 and *McCarthy v TKM Builders* [2020] QSC 301: Sending a link to a Dropbox folder does **not** constitute sending/producing the documents themselves.
- **Accessibility:** *Grand Pacific Resort* [2010] QBCCMCmr 255: Information must be "capable of being retrieved".

## 5. Consent
Consent is a critical prerequisite for the ETA to override paper requirements (except in specific contexts like the *Corporations Act*).
- **Inferred Consent:** Can be inferred from conduct (e.g., *Stellard Pty Ltd v North Queensland Fuel Pty Ltd* [2015] QSC 119; *Singh v Legal Aid NSW* [2014]).
- **Insufficient Evidence:** Merely providing an email address on a form does not necessarily constitute consent to receive formal legal notices electronically (*Bedwell v Wantirna Park Estate* [2018] VCAT 34; *State of Tasmania v Herlihy* [2019] TASSC 5).

## 6. Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) Amendments (2022)
The *Corporations Act* now features a "global communications regime" that is largely technology-neutral.
- **s 110A:** Permits electronic signing of documents (identifying the person and indicating intention).
- **s 105A:** Defines time of sending and receipt.
- **s 127:** Documents (including deeds) can be executed electronically; common seals can be witnessed via electronic means.
- **No Consent Required:** Unlike the general ETA, many *Corporations Act* provisions do not require recipient consent for electronic sending/signing.

## 7. Time and Place of Dispatch/Receipt
### Dispatch (s 23 Qld ETA)
- When the communication leaves an information system under the originator's control.

### Receipt (s 24 Qld ETA)
- **Designated Address:** When the communication becomes "capable of being retrieved" by the addressee.
- **Non-Designated Address:** When it is capable of being retrieved **and** the addressee becomes aware it was sent.
- **Presumption:** It is assumed capable of being retrieved when it reaches the address.

### Key Receipt Cases
- **Spam Filters:** *Bauen Constructions v Sky General Services* [2012] NSWSC 1123: A document is received even if it goes to a spam folder.
- **Onus of Proof:** *Demex Pty Ltd v John Holland Pty Ltd* [2022] QSC 259: The sender bears the onus of proving the time of receipt on the balance of probabilities.

## 8. Postal Acceptance Rule (PAR)
The PAR (acceptance valid upon posting) is generally **not** applied to instantaneous communications (like telex or phone). Its application to email is debated:
- **Argument for PAR:** *Chwee Kin Keong v Digilandmall.com* [2004] SLR 594: Email is not strictly instantaneous as the sender loses control over delivery.
- **Argument against PAR:** *Olivaylle Pty Ltd v Flottweg GMBH* [2009] FCA 522: Email is "near instantaneous"; analogies should be drawn with telex (where PAR does not apply).
- **Current Status:** PAR is likely confined to situations where the offeror clearly intended acceptance to be communicated upon dispatch.

## 9. Automated Systems & Errors
- **Automated Message Systems (s 26C Qld ETA):** Contracts formed by automated systems (without human intervention) are valid.
- **Input Errors (s 26D Qld ETA):** Individuals have a right to withdraw portions of a communication containing an input error if:
    - The system provided no opportunity to correct;
    - They notify the other party as soon as possible; and
    - No material benefit has been received.

## 10. Incorporation of Terms & Jurisdiction
Terms are incorporated via signature, reasonable notice, or course of dealing.
- **"Click" Incorporation:** *Gonzalez v Agoda Company Pte Ltd* [2017] NSWSC 1133: Clicking "Book Now" can incorporate terms and conditions, including exclusive jurisdiction clauses.
- **Reasonable Notice:** *Dialogue Consulting v Instagram* [2020] FCA 1846: A website provides reasonable notice if it gives a "legitimate opportunity" to learn the terms.
- **Jurisdiction:** Courts generally respect jurisdiction clauses (e.g., "This contract is governed by the laws of Washington") unless there are strong reasons to override them.
