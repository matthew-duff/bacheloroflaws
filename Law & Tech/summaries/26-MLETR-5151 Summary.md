# Electronic Transferable Records - MLETR Summary

**Reference:** Davidson, Alan, *Electronic Transferable Records*, LAWS5151, 2026. Primary instrument discussed: **UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Transferable Records (2017)**.

> [!abstract] Core point
> `MLETR` solves the problem that some commercial documents matter not just because of their contents, but because they are **unique transferable originals** capable of being **possessed** and **delivered**. In the electronic environment, the law replaces those paper concepts with **control**, **exclusive control**, **integrity**, and a **reliable method**.

> [!important] Exam formula
> **Paper world:** possession + delivery + singular original  
> **Electronic world:** control + transfer of control + identified operative record

## Overview

This lecture is about the legal problem that arises when commerce moves from paper to digital form but the law still treats certain documents as valuable precisely because they are **singular physical originals** that can be **possessed**, **delivered**, and **transferred**. The UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Transferable Records (`MLETR`) was developed to solve that problem. Its purpose is to permit the legal use of **electronic transferable records** (`ETRs`) in a way that is functionally equivalent to paper transferable documents and instruments, both in domestic and cross-border trade.

The central challenge is not merely proving that an electronic message exists. The deeper challenge is replicating the legal functions of a paper document whose legal force depends on its uniqueness and transferability. A paper bill of lading, warehouse receipt, bill of exchange, or promissory note can often operate because one person can hold the original and transfer rights by physically delivering or indorsing it. Digital data, by contrast, is naturally copyable. MLETR therefore addresses the problem of **uniqueness**, **singularity**, **control**, and **integrity**.

The lecture presents MLETR as the next step in UNCITRAL's electronic commerce framework, building on earlier instruments that recognized electronic communications generally but deliberately excluded transferable instruments because the law had not yet developed a satisfactory solution for the "original/possession/duplication" problem.

> [!tip] Short answer
> `MLETR` matters because general electronic transactions law solved writing and signature problems, but not the harder problem of how an electronic record can stand in for a **unique transferable paper original**.

## 1. Why electronic transferable records required a separate legal instrument

Earlier electronic commerce instruments established broad recognition of electronic communications, but they did **not** fully solve the special problem posed by transferable documents and instruments.

### Earlier UNCITRAL framework

The lecture situates MLETR in the broader UNCITRAL sequence:

- **UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Commerce 1996**
- **UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Signatures 2001**
- **UN Convention on the Use of Electronic Communications in International Contracts 2005** (`Electronic Communications Convention` or `ECC`)
- **UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Transferable Records 2017**

These earlier instruments promoted legal recognition of electronic communications, electronic writing, and electronic signatures, but transferable documents remained a difficult category.

### ECC exclusion of transferable instruments

The lecture highlights **Article 2(2) of the Electronic Communications Convention**, which excludes:

- bills of exchange
- promissory notes
- consignment notes
- bills of lading
- warehouse receipts
- other transferable documents or instruments entitling the bearer or beneficiary to claim delivery of goods or payment of money

This exclusion matters because it shows that UNCITRAL had already accepted electronic commerce in general, but did not yet believe that electronic law had fully resolved the legal characteristics of negotiable or transferable paper instruments.

The lecture notes that the excluded category may extend to payment instruments such as:

- **letters of credit**
- **bank payment obligations**

So the issue is not limited to shipping documents. It also concerns financing structures in international trade where documentary possession and transfer perform core legal functions.

### The underlying difficulty

The lecture emphasizes that UNCITRAL recognized the need for a combined:

- **legal**
- **technological**
- **business**

solution.

That point is important. The problem of replacing paper originals cannot be solved by legal drafting alone. Law must recognize an electronic equivalent, but the relevant system must also be commercially workable and technically capable of ensuring that only one operative record exists for legal purposes at a time.

## 2. Development of the MLETR project

### UNCITRAL's concern with originality and duplication

The lecture repeatedly returns to the concepts of **singularity** and **uniqueness**. In paper systems, the legal market often relies on the assumption that there is one operative original. In digital systems, identical copies can proliferate instantly, which threatens the logic of negotiability and documentary control.

UNCITRAL acknowledged that solving this required more than a simple rule saying "electronic is fine." The law needed a framework that could answer:

- What counts as the electronic equivalent of the one original document?
- How can the system ensure no competing originals exist?
- How can rights be transferred in a way equivalent to delivery or possession?
- How can the relevant record be identified as the legally operative one?

### 2011 colloquium

The lecture notes that in **2011** UNCITRAL held a colloquium to identify a roadmap for future work in electronic commerce, with particular regard to legal issues relating to electronic transferable records.

This is significant because it shows MLETR emerged from a deliberate international effort to address a known gap in electronic commerce law rather than from ad hoc national experimentation alone.

### Final outcome: MLETR 2017

The result was the **UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Transferable Records 2017**, intended to facilitate the legal use of ETRs domestically and internationally.

The lecture frames MLETR as a model law based on the familiar UNCITRAL themes of:

- non-discrimination against electronic means
- functional equivalence
- technology neutrality

But its contribution is much more specific: it explains how electronic records can perform the legal roles traditionally performed by transferable paper documents or instruments.

## 3. MLETR's purpose and field of operation

### Core purpose

The lecture states that MLETR's purpose is to facilitate the legal use of electronic transferable records both:

- **domestically**
- **internationally**

This dual purpose is essential. Trade documents commonly operate across borders. A legal regime confined to purely internal transactions would not adequately address real commercial practice.

### Functional equivalence focus

MLETR applies to ETRs that are **functionally equivalent** to transferable documents or instruments. That means the law is not trying to reproduce paper form for its own sake. Instead, it asks whether an electronic record can perform the same legal functions as paper.

Those functions include:

- embodying rights or claims
- identifying the operative record
- allowing one person to exercise control
- enabling transfer of rights by transfer of control
- maintaining confidence in integrity and authenticity

MLETR therefore does not abandon the traditional legal logic of transferable instruments. It translates that logic into the electronic environment.

## 4. Key definitions

> [!note] Definitions that do the real work
> The most exam-important definitional move is this: not every electronic file is an `ETR`. It only becomes an `electronic transferable record` if it satisfies the `MLETR` requirements, especially under `Article 10`.

### Transferable document or instrument

The lecture quotes the MLETR definition:

> "Transferable document or instrument" means a document or instrument issued on paper that entitles the holder to claim the performance of the obligation indicated in the document or instrument and to transfer the right to performance of the obligation indicated in the document or instrument through the transfer of that document or instrument.

This definition captures the central paper-based category MLETR is trying to replicate electronically. Two features matter:

- the holder can **claim performance** of the obligation
- the right to that performance can be **transferred through transfer of the document or instrument itself**

In other words, the document is not merely evidence. It is legally bound up with the right it represents.

### Electronic record

The lecture also quotes the definition of "electronic record":

> information generated, communicated, received or stored by electronic means, including all information logically associated with or linked together so as to become part of the record, whether generated contemporaneously or not.

This is a deliberately broad definition. It allows the law to capture not only a single data file in isolation, but also logically associated information forming part of the relevant record.

### Electronic transferable record

The lecture indicates that an **electronic transferable record** is an electronic record that complies with **Article 10**. This is critical: not every electronic record is an ETR. It becomes an ETR only if it satisfies the statutory requirements concerning:

- content
- identity
- control
- integrity

So the legal status of an ETR is not automatic. It depends on compliance with the MLETR framework.

## 5. Foundational principles of MLETR

### Non-discrimination against electronic means

The lecture lists as a basic principle that the law should not deny legal effect merely because a record is electronic. This is an extension of the familiar electronic commerce principle already seen in ETA-style legislation.

### Functional equivalence

This is the most important doctrinal principle in the lecture. The paper concepts of:

- writing
- signature
- possession
- transfer

are not abandoned. Instead, MLETR asks what electronic features can perform those same legal functions.

The most important equivalence in the lecture is:

- **possession in the paper world = control in the electronic world**

Likewise:

- **delivery/transfer of possession in the paper world = transfer of control in the electronic world**

### Technology neutrality

MLETR does not mandate one technology. The lecture explicitly mentions that the relevant systems may include:

- registries
- tokens
- distributed ledgers

This is important because MLETR is designed as enabling legislation. It provides legal criteria, not a command to adopt a particular software architecture.

## 6. Article 7: no denial of legal effect because of electronic form

The lecture quotes **Article 7**:

> An electronic transferable record shall not be denied legal effect, validity or enforceability on the sole ground that it is in electronic form.

This is the anti-discrimination rule for ETRs. It performs a foundational but limited function. It prevents invalidation merely because a record is electronic, but it does **not** on its own make every electronic record a valid substitute for a paper transferable instrument. The record must still satisfy the specific requirements of the MLETR scheme.

## 7. Articles 8 and 9: writing and signature

The lecture notes that **Articles 8 and 9** provide functional equivalence for:

- electronic writing
- electronic signatures

These provisions reflect continuity with earlier UNCITRAL electronic commerce instruments. But in the MLETR context, writing and signature are not the only issues. The more distinctive challenge is how to deal with:

- originality
- exclusivity
- control
- transferability

Thus Articles 8 and 9 are necessary but not sufficient to solve the transferable-records problem.

## 8. Article 10: when an electronic record satisfies a requirement for a transferable document or instrument

Article 10 is the doctrinal centre of the lecture.

> [!important] Article 10 in one line
> An electronic record can stand in for a paper transferable document only if it contains the necessary information and a **reliable method** is used to establish **identity**, **control**, and **integrity**.

### Article 10(1): threshold requirements

Where the law requires a transferable document or instrument, that requirement is met by an electronic record if:

1. the electronic record contains the information that would be required in the paper document or instrument; and
2. a **reliable method** is used:
   - to identify that record as the electronic transferable record
   - to render it capable of being subject to control from creation until it ceases to have effect or validity
   - to retain its integrity

### Significance of Article 10

The lecture emphasizes that Article 10:

- preserves the need to comply with the **substantive law** governing the underlying document or instrument
- introduces the electronic-law elements of **identity**, **control**, and **integrity**

This means MLETR does not rewrite the substantive commercial law of bills of lading, promissory notes, or similar instruments. Instead, it provides the electronic-law mechanism by which those paper-law requirements can be fulfilled in digital form.

| Article 10 element | What it asks |
| --- | --- |
| Content | Does the record contain what the paper document would need to contain? |
| Identity | Can the system identify this record as **the** operative ETR? |
| Control | Can the record be subject to control throughout its legal life? |
| Integrity | Has the record remained complete and unaltered except for authorized/ordinary changes? |

### Identity

The record must be identifiable as **the** electronic transferable record. This responds directly to the duplication problem. In a digital environment there may be countless copies, screenshots, backups, and transmissions. The law therefore requires a reliable method to identify which record is the legally operative one.

### Control

The record must be capable of being subject to control from its creation until it ceases to have legal effect. This is the electronic analogue of the paper system's dependence on possession and delivery.

### Integrity

The record must maintain integrity. If the content of the record can be altered without authorization, confidence in the legal and commercial function of the instrument collapses.

## 9. Article 10(2): integrity

The lecture sets out the integrity criterion:

> Information contained in the electronic transferable record, including any authorized change from creation until the record ceases to have effect or validity, must remain complete and unaltered apart from normal changes arising in communication, storage, and display.

This criterion is carefully framed:

- it allows **authorized changes**
- it focuses on the information remaining **complete and unaltered**
- it disregards ordinary technical changes arising in communication, storage, or display

So integrity is not absolute immutability in a metaphysical sense. It is legal integrity: the record remains complete and unchanged except to the extent that authorized or technically ordinary changes occur.

## 10. Identity, duplication, and singularity

The lecture separately emphasizes the concepts of:

- identity
- risk of duplication
- uniqueness
- singularity

These are central to understanding why MLETR exists at all.

### Why paper was easier

In the paper world, the "original" transferable document often functions because:

- only one original is treated as operative
- physical custody can usually be identified
- transfer can occur by physical delivery

### Why digital records create difficulty

Digital records are inherently reproducible. If ten identical copies exist, the law must still determine:

- which one is the operative record
- who has the legal power to exercise rights tied to that record
- how to prevent multiple parties asserting conflicting claims from duplicate copies

### The lecture's emphasis on wording

The lecture uses terms such as:

- "operative"
- "authorised"
- "the"

This highlights that MLETR is concerned with identifying **the one authoritative record** for legal purposes, even in a medium where many copies may exist technically.

## 11. Article 11: control as the electronic equivalent of possession

This is one of the most important concepts in the entire lecture.

> [!important] High-value proposition
> If you remember only one doctrinal equation from this topic, remember: **possession in the paper world = control in the electronic world**.

### Core equivalence

The lecture states:

- **Possession = Control**

This is the functional equivalence move at the heart of MLETR. Since an electronic record cannot literally be physically possessed in the same way as paper, the law substitutes **control** as the concept that performs the same legal role.

### Article 11(1)

Where the law requires or permits possession of a transferable document or instrument, that requirement is met for an ETR if a reliable method is used:

- to establish **exclusive control** of that ETR by a person
- to identify that person as the person in control

This provision has several key implications:

- control must be **exclusive**
- the system must identify **who** is in control
- the method used must be **reliable**

The requirement of exclusivity is crucial because a transferable instrument loses much of its legal function if multiple parties can simultaneously claim control.

### Article 11(2): transfer of control

Where the law requires or permits transfer of possession of a paper transferable document or instrument, that requirement is met electronically by **transfer of control** over the ETR.

The lecture therefore states:

- **Transfer of possession (delivery) = Transfer of control**

This is the digital replacement for physical delivery. In the paper world, delivery may be simple handover. In the electronic world, there must be a reliable change in control that performs the same legal function.

### Importance

This is not merely semantic. It is what allows electronic transferable records to participate in commercial practices built on documentary transfer, such as financing, carriage of goods, and documentary title arrangements.

> [!tip] Exam wording
> A strong answer should say that `Article 11` does not literally recreate physical possession. It creates a **functional equivalent** by requiring a reliable method for **exclusive control** and identification of the controller.

## 12. Article 12: reliable method

The lecture repeatedly emphasizes the concept of a **reliable method**. This is the operative legal standard used throughout MLETR.

> [!note] Why Article 12 matters so much
> `MLETR` is technology-neutral because it does **not** prescribe one system. Instead, it repeatedly asks whether the chosen method is **as reliable as appropriate** or **proven in fact** to perform the required function.

### Where the reliable method standard appears

The lecture notes that reliability is used in relation to:

- **Article 10**: creating a valid ETR
- **Article 11**: control
- **Article 9**: signature
- **Article 13**: indication of time and place
- **Article 16**: amendment
- **Article 17**: replacement of paper with electronic
- **Article 18**: replacement of electronic with paper

So reliability is not an incidental concept. It is the general standard that allows MLETR to remain flexible and technology neutral.

### Article 12(a): reliability as appropriate

The method must be as reliable as appropriate for the function it is used to perform, in light of all relevant circumstances. The lecture lists factors that may be considered, including:

- operational rules relevant to reliability
- assurance of data integrity
- ability to prevent unauthorized access and use
- security of hardware and software
- regularity and extent of independent audit
- declarations or accreditation by supervisory or voluntary bodies
- applicable industry standards

This is a contextual standard, not a one-size-fits-all formula. Reliability depends on:

- the function being performed
- the practical setting
- the commercial context
- the safeguards surrounding the system

### Article 12(b): reliability proven in fact

A method can also satisfy the requirement if it is proven in fact to have fulfilled the relevant function by itself or together with further evidence.

This is important because it avoids rigid over-formalism. Even if a method does not tick every theoretical box in advance, it may still satisfy the law if evidence shows it actually achieved the required function.

## 13. Explanatory Note and the meaning of "reliable as appropriate"

The lecture supplements Article 12 with factors from the Explanatory Note to the Electronic Communications Convention. These include:

- sophistication of the parties' equipment
- nature of the trade activity
- frequency of transactions
- kind and size of transaction
- function of signature requirements in the relevant statutory environment
- capability of communication systems
- compliance with intermediary authentication procedures
- range of authentication procedures available
- trade customs and practice
- insurance coverage against unauthorized communications
- importance and value of the information
- availability and cost of alternative methods
- degree of industry acceptance of the identification method
- any other relevant factor

### Why this matters

This list demonstrates that reliability is a mixed legal-commercial-technical inquiry. Courts and lawmakers are not asked to focus solely on cryptography or system architecture. They are invited to consider:

- market practice
- transaction risk
- industrial acceptance
- commercial context
- evidentiary sufficiency

That makes the MLETR framework more adaptable to real trade environments.

## 14. Technology and technology neutrality

### No prescribed technological solution

The lecture says the working group barely discussed the technology in detail and used technology-neutral language. That is entirely consistent with UNCITRAL drafting style. MLETR is not a blockchain statute, a registry statute, or a token statute. It is a legal framework capable of accommodating different architectures.

### Technologies mentioned in the lecture

The lecture refers to:

- **registries**
- **tokens**
- **distributed ledgers**
- **blockchain** (via the Explanatory Note's reference to distributed systems)

### Significance of the limited technology discussion

The lecture suggests there was an "unspoken assurance" that experts regarded the technical problems of originality and duplication as sufficiently solvable for legal work to proceed. That is important. UNCITRAL did not insist on solving every engineering issue within the legal text. Instead, it drafted legal standards broad enough to support systems capable of meeting the necessary functions.

### Exam insight

A likely exam point is that **technology neutrality is deliberate**. The legal issue is not whether blockchain is fashionable or whether one platform is superior. The question is whether the method used is sufficiently reliable to establish identity, exclusive control, transfer of control, and integrity.

## 15. Replacement and change of form

The lecture briefly identifies MLETR provisions dealing with:

- **Article 16**: amendment
- **Article 17**: replacement of paper by electronic
- **Article 18**: replacement of electronic by paper

This is significant because commerce may not always remain fully paperless or fully digital throughout a transaction chain. A workable legal regime must permit changes of form while preserving legal continuity and avoiding duplication or parallel operative records.

The lecture also links this idea to the **UK Electronic Trade Documents Act 2023**, which includes a provision on **change of form**. This shows that one practical implementation issue for MLETR-style reform is ensuring a document can move between paper and electronic form without creating conflicting titles or rights.

## 17. The United Kingdom: Electronic Trade Documents Act 2023

The lecture gives special attention to the **Electronic Trade Documents Act 2023 (UK)**.

### Policy rationale

The UK reform is presented as significant because:

- UK trade volumes are immense
- English law underpins a very large share of global trade documentation
- paper-heavy trade processes create delay, cost, and environmental burden

The lecture notes claims that:

- the Act could significantly benefit UK businesses over the next decade
- global container shipping still generates enormous quantities of paper documents
- digitization promises financial, efficiency, and environmental gains

### Problem under old law

The lecture explains that the underlying law was based on historic merchant practices and did not allow an electronic document to be "possessed."

This mirrors the MLETR problem exactly: if possession is legally central, an electronic document cannot simply be treated as equivalent unless the law accepts an electronic functional substitute.

### Key conceptual move

The lecture highlights the Act's use of ideas such as:

- **control**
- **reliable system**
- **electronic trade document**
- capability of being possessed

This strongly resembles the MLETR framework. The UK statute is therefore an example of MLETR-style thinking translated into national legislation.

### Commercial significance

The lecture quotes strong endorsements from ICC representatives and Lord Holmes, stressing that even a relatively small conceptual legal change could have massive practical consequences by reducing document transfer times from days to minutes.

This reinforces the lecture's broader message: documentary trade law can be a bottleneck for digital commerce, and reform of possession/control rules may unlock very large gains.

## 18. The United States and Germany

### United States

The lecture notes that the US has adopted piecemeal legislation that inspired MLETR:

- **UCC Article 7**: electronic bills of lading and warehouse receipts
- **UCC Article 9**: security interests in electronic promissory notes
- **UETA** and **ESIGN**: provisions touching electronic transferable records

This shows that some legal systems moved incrementally before the MLETR framework was finalized. The US example suggests MLETR did not appear in a vacuum; it was informed by existing legislative experiments and commercial practice.

### Germany

The lecture notes that Germany has had legislation since 2014 for electronic bills of lading and other trade-related documents, inspired by the Rotterdam Rules and broadly compatible with MLETR. Germany is also described as considering fuller MLETR adoption.

This is significant because it shows compatibility can arise through different legislative pathways, not only by direct enactment of the model law.

## 19. Asia-Pacific and developing adoption

The lecture mentions interest or movement in:

- **Canada**, as part of G7 movement toward an MLETR-compatible framework
- **Indonesia**, which regarded the model law as timely guidance
- **Japan**, which has a working or study group considering MLETR for bills of lading
- **Thailand**, which approved inclusion of MLETR principles in electronic transactions legislation
- **Spain** and **India**, as jurisdictions showing interest

The lecture also points to an **APEC workshop** on paperless trade analyzing legal gaps and economic benefits of adopting or maintaining an MLETR-compatible framework.

### Importance of the APEC reference

This indicates that MLETR is not merely an abstract legal project. It is part of a regional trade modernization agenda focused on:

- legal interoperability
- reduction of friction in cross-border trade
- measurable economic benefits

## 20. Australia

The lecture does not present Australia as having enacted MLETR, but it notes Australia's relevance, including the **Digital Economy Agreement signed with Singapore**.

This suggests Australia is operating in a regional environment where MLETR-compatible trade law may become commercially important even if domestic reform is still developing.

For Australian legal analysis, this raises questions about:

- competitiveness in paperless trade
- cross-border compatibility with MLETR jurisdictions
- whether ETA-style frameworks are sufficient without specific ETR reform

## 21. ESG and policy benefits

The lecture notes an **environmental, social, and governance** (`ESG`) dimension, especially the reduction of paper.

But the policy case is broader than paper savings alone. Across the lecture, the benefits of MLETR-style reform include:

- faster transfer of trade documents
- reduced administrative friction
- lower cost
- improved efficiency in transport, insurance, finance, and logistics
- stronger compatibility with digital trade systems
- environmental gains from reduced paper use and document transport

The lecture therefore presents MLETR as both a doctrinal and an economic reform.

## 22. Core doctrinal takeaways

### 1. MLETR is not about ordinary electronic communications only

Ordinary electronic commerce law already addressed writing, signature, and legal recognition in many contexts. MLETR is needed because transferable records depend on special legal functions tied to possession, originality, and transferability.

### 2. The central legal substitution is possession -> control

This is the key conceptual move. Without it, electronic trade documents cannot perform the legal role of paper instruments whose rights are exercised by the holder in possession.

### 3. Singularity/uniqueness is the practical heart of the problem

Electronic data is infinitely reproducible. MLETR works only if the system can reliably identify the operative record and ensure exclusive control.

### 4. Reliability is a flexible but demanding standard

MLETR does not prescribe one technology. Instead, it asks whether the method used is as reliable as appropriate or proven in fact to fulfill the relevant function.

### 5. MLETR preserves substantive law while enabling electronic form

Article 10 does not abolish the substantive requirements of the underlying transferable instrument. It overlays a framework for satisfying them electronically.

### 6. Adoption is commercially critical

Because trade is international, the practical success of ETRs depends not only on local validity but also on international uptake and recognition.

## 23. Exam-focused synthesis

> [!success] If the exam asks "Why does MLETR matter?"
> It fills the gap left by ordinary electronic transactions law by creating a legal framework for the **electronic equivalent of transferable paper instruments**, especially through **identity**, **integrity**, **exclusive control**, **transfer of control**, and the **reliable method** standard.

If asked to explain the significance of MLETR, the strongest answer would say that it fills the gap left by general electronic transactions law by creating a framework for the **electronic equivalent of transferable paper instruments**, especially through the concepts of:

- identity
- integrity
- exclusive control
- transfer of control
- reliable method
- technology neutrality

If asked why ordinary ETA principles were insufficient, the answer is that a negotiable or transferable paper instrument is not merely information in writing. It is often the legally operative embodiment of rights, and those rights can depend on the uniqueness of the original and the ability to transfer them through possession and delivery.

If asked how MLETR deals with electronic duplication, the answer is that it does not pretend duplication is impossible. Instead, it requires a reliable method to identify **the** operative electronic transferable record, maintain its integrity, and ensure **exclusive control** by one person at a time.

If asked about the role of technology, the answer is that MLETR is explicitly **technology neutral**. It can accommodate registries, tokens, centralized or decentralized systems, and distributed ledger technologies so long as the relevant legal functions are reliably achieved.

If asked about policy significance, the answer is that MLETR supports paperless trade, efficiency, legal interoperability, and potentially major economic and environmental gains.

## 24. Short conclusion

The lecture presents MLETR as a major development in electronic commerce law because it tackles one of the last and hardest paper-to-digital transitions: documents and instruments whose legal force depends on originality, possession, and transferability. Its central achievement is to replace paper concepts with functionally equivalent electronic concepts, especially **control** as the equivalent of possession, while preserving reliability, integrity, and commercial trust. In that sense, MLETR is a bridge between traditional documentary trade law and genuinely digital trade practice.
