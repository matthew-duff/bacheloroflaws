# Electronic Contracting - LAWS5151 2026 - Dr Alan Davidson

## Overview
This document summarizes the legal requirements, statutory frameworks, and judicial interpretations surrounding electronic contracting in Australia, with reference to international standards and key case law.

## 1. Fundamental Principles of Electronic Transactions Acts (ETAs)
The ETAs (Commonwealth, State, and Territory) are designed to remove legal impediments to electronic commerce. They are based on the **UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Commerce (1996)** and the **UNCITRAL Convention on the Use of Electronic Communications in International Contracts (2005)**.

### Key Regulatory Principles
*   **Functional Equivalence**: Paper-based and electronic commerce should be treated equally by the law.
*   **Technology Neutrality**: The law should not discriminate between different forms of technology.

## 2. Legal Requirements for Electronic Contracts
For an electronic contract to be enforceable, it must satisfy common law elements (offer, acceptance, consideration, intention) and specific statutory requirements regarding form and signature.

### Validity of Transactions
*   **Qld ETA s 8(1)**: A transaction is not invalid merely because it took place wholly or partly by electronic communications.
*   **Cth ETA s 8(1)** / **ECC Art 8**: Legal recognition is granted to electronic communications.

### Writing Requirements (Qld ETA s 11 / Cth ETA s 9)
A requirement for "writing" is met if:
1.  The information is **readily accessible** for subsequent reference.
2.  The recipient **consents** to the information being given electronically.

**Key Cases on Writing:**
*   *Golden Ocean v Salgaocar* [2012]: A series of emails can constitute "writing."
*   *Kaczmarski v Victorian Legal Services Board* [2017]: An appeal lodged by email satisfies the "writing" requirement.
*   *Re Nichol* [2017]: An unsent text message was held to be a valid "electronic document" (will) under the *Succession Act 1981* (Qld).
*   *Conveyor & General Engineering v Basetec Services* [2014] & *McCarthy v TKM Builders* [2020]: Sending a **link to Dropbox** does not constitute sending the documents themselves if the recipient does not follow the link.

### Signature Requirements (Qld ETA s 14 / Cth ETA s 10)
A signature requirement is met if:
1.  **Identification**: A method is used to identify the person and indicate their approval/intention.
2.  **Reliability**: The method was as reliable as appropriate for the purpose (or proven in fact to fulfill the function).
3.  **Consent**: The recipient consents to the method used.

**Key Cases on Signature:**
*   *Stellard Pty Ltd v North Queensland Fuel Pty Ltd* [2015]: Identification in an acceptance email is not fatal if further evidence (prior conversations, pleadings) establishes the person's identity and intention.
*   *Stuart v Hishon* [2013]: Where a signature is not strictly "required" by law, common law principles apply. A name at the end of an email ("Tom") was held to be a signature.
*   *Peter Smythe v Vincent Thomas* [2007]: A "click" contract on eBay for a vintage aircraft was held to be a binding contract.

## 3. Corporations Act Amendments (2022)
The *Corporations Act 2001* (Cth) was amended to enable technology-neutral signing and sending of documents.
*   **s 110A**: Permits signing electronic forms of documents using methods that identify the person and indicate intention. **No consent is required** for these corporate signatures.
*   **s 127**: Explicitly allows electronic execution of documents (including deeds) by companies.
*   **s 105A**: Defines when an electronic communication is sent and received for Corporations Act purposes.

## 4. The Requirement of Consent
Consent is a critical hurdle for the application of the ETA (except where overridden, e.g., in the Corporations Act).
*   **Inferred Consent**: Agreement to use electronic communications may be inferred from conduct (*ECC Art 8(2)*; *Stellard*).
*   **Insufficient Evidence of Consent**:
    *   *Bedwell v Wantirna Park Estate* [2018]: Merely providing an email address on a form does not constitute consent to receive legal notices electronically.
    *   *State of Tasmania v Herlihy* [2019]: Providing an email on a claim form was not express consent.
*   **Explicit/Implicit Consent**:
    *   *Singh v Legal Aid NSW* [2014]: Receiving and replying to emails demonstrates consent.
    *   *Laremont 24-7 v Invox* [2015]: Consent found through the active use of email.

## 5. Time and Place of Dispatch/Receipt
### Dispatch (Qld ETA s 23)
*   Occurs when the communication leaves the information system under the control of the originator.
*   If it hasn't left the system (e.g., internal email), it is dispatched when received by the addressee.

### Receipt (Qld ETA s 24 / Cth ETA s 14A)
*   **Designated Address**: Receipt occurs when the communication is **capable of being retrieved** by the addressee.
*   **Non-Designated Address**: Receipt occurs when (1) it is capable of being retrieved AND (2) the addressee becomes aware it was sent.
*   **Assumption**: It is assumed capable of being retrieved when it reaches the electronic address.

**Key Cases on Receipt:**
*   *Bauen Constructions v Sky General Services* [2012]: An email in a spam box is still "received."
*   *Demex Pty Ltd v John Holland Pty Ltd* [2022]: Service is effected when received at the nominated email address; there is no requirement that the recipient be aware of the receipt. However, the onus of proof lies on the sender to prove receipt on the balance of probabilities.
*   *QBSA v J M Kelly* [2013]: Onus may shift to the recipient to explain why they did not receive/see the communication if evidence of sending is strong.

## 6. Formation and Terms
### Invitation to Treat (Qld ETA s 26B / Cth ETA s 15B)
A proposal made via electronic communication not addressed to specific parties (e.g., a website advertisement) is an **invitation to treat**, unless it clearly indicates an intention to be bound upon acceptance (*Crosby Holdings Trust & Brickwest v Samarawickrama* [2019]).

### Postal Acceptance Rule
*   The general consensus is that the Postal Acceptance Rule (acceptance effective on dispatch) **does not apply** to instantaneous or near-instantaneous electronic communications like email.
*   *Olivaylle Pty Ltd v Flottweg GMBH* [2009]: Email is "near instantaneous," suggesting the receipt rule (acceptance effective on receipt) should apply, analogous to telex (*Brinkibon* [1983]).
*   *Chwee Kin Keong v Digilandmall.com* [2004] (Singapore): Suggested arguments could be made for the postal rule, but the receipt rule is generally preferred.

### Incorporation of Terms & Jurisdiction
*   Terms can be incorporated via "click-wrap" agreements (*Gonzalez v Agoda* [2017]).
*   **Reasonable Notice**: A website provides reasonable notice if it gives a "legitimate opportunity" to learn the terms (*Dialogue Consulting v Instagram* [2020]).
*   **Jurisdiction Clauses**: Courts generally respect jurisdiction clauses incorporated into electronic terms (e.g., Washington law for Amazon/Microsoft, Singapore law for Agoda).

## 7. Automated Systems and Errors
*   **Automated Message Systems (Qld ETA s 26C)**: Contracts formed by automated systems (without human intervention) are valid.
*   **Input Errors (Qld ETA s 26D)**: An individual has the right to withdraw a portion of a communication containing an input error if the automated system provided no opportunity to correct it, provided they notify the other party promptly and have not received a benefit.

## 8. Attribution (Qld ETA s 25)
A person is bound by an electronic communication **only if** it was sent by them or with their authority. This differs from some international jurisdictions that may place the burden on the purported sender to prove they did *not* send it.
