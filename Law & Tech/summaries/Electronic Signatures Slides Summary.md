# Electronic Signatures and Transactions Slides Summary

## Overview
This document summarizes the lecture slides for LAWS5151 (Electronic Contracting) by Dr Alan Davidson, focusing on the legal framework for electronic contracts, signatures, and the Electronic Transactions Acts (ETA).

---

## 1. Fundamental Principles of Electronic Contracting
Electronic contracts are governed by both common law and statutory frameworks. The primary goal of the legislation is to facilitate e-commerce by removing legal impediments.

### Key Statutory Principles
*   **Functional Equivalence**: Paper-based and electronic commerce should be treated equally by the law.
*   **Technology Neutrality**: The law should not discriminate between different forms of technology.

### International Framework
*   **UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Commerce (1996)**: The template for Australia's ETAs.
*   **UNCITRAL Convention on the Use of Electronic Communications in International Contracts (2005)**: Incorporated into Australian law (ECC).

---

## 2. The Electronic Transactions Acts (ETA)
Australia has nine ETAs (Commonwealth, States, and Territories). 

### Validity of Electronic Transactions
*   **Section 8 (Qld/Cth)**: A transaction is not invalid merely because it took place wholly or partly by electronic communications.
*   **ECC Art 8**: Legal recognition of electronic communications.

### Core Requirements Met Electronically
1.  **Writing** (ss 9-13 Qld): Requirement to give information in writing.
2.  **Signature** (ss 14-15 Qld): Requirement to provide a signature.
3.  **Production** (ss 16-18 Qld): Requirement to produce a document.
4.  **Recording/Retention** (ss 19-21 Qld): Requirements to record or retain documents.

---

## 3. Writing Requirements (s 11 Qld / s 9 Cth)
To satisfy a legal requirement for writing via electronic communication:
1.  **Accessibility**: Information must be readily accessible and usable for subsequent reference at the time it was given.
2.  **Consent**: The recipient must consent to the information being given electronically.

### Key Case Law on Writing
*   **Golden Ocean v Salgaocar [2012]**: A series of emails can constitute "writing" for a guarantee.
*   **Conveyor & General Engineering v Basetec [2014]**: Sending a link to **Dropbox** does not constitute sending the documents themselves; the documents in Dropbox are not part of the electronic communication.
*   **Re Nichol [2017]**: An unsent text message was held to be a valid "electronic document" under the *Succession Act 1981* (Qld).
*   **Kaczmarski v Victorian Legal Services Board [2017]**: An appeal lodged by email after hours is considered "lodged" (though not necessarily "filed").

---

## 4. Signature Requirements (s 14 Qld / s 10 Cth)
To satisfy a legal requirement for a signature:
1.  **Identification & Intention**: A method must be used to identify the person and indicate their approval/intention.
2.  **Reliability**: The method must be as reliable as appropriate for the purpose (or proven in fact to have fulfilled the function).
3.  **Consent**: The recipient must consent to the use of that method.

### Corporations Act Amendments (2022)
*   **Section 110A**: Enables technology-neutral signing for all documents required/permitted under the Act (including s 127 executions).
*   **No Consent Required**: Unlike the general ETA, the *Corporations Act* does not require recipient consent for electronic signatures under s 110A.

### Key Case Law on Signatures
*   **Stellard v North Queensland Fuel [2015]**: Identification can be established by "further evidence" (e.g., prior conversations and admissions).
*   **Stuart v Hishon [2013]**: Where a signature is not legally "required" (e.g., standard email), common law applies instead of the ETA. A typed name ("Tom") at the end of an email can be a signature at common law.
*   **Gonzalez v Agoda [2017]**: Clicking a "Book Now" button can constitute a signature under s 9(1) ETA.

---

## 5. Consent
Consent is a critical hurdle for the application of the ETA (except where excluded, like the *Corporations Act*).

*   **Inferred Consent**: Can be inferred from conduct (e.g., *Singh v Legal Aid NSW [2014]* - replying to emails).
*   **Insufficient Evidence**: Merely providing an email address on a form does not necessarily constitute consent to receive legal notices electronically (*Bedwell v Wantirna Park [2018]*; *State of Tasmania v Herlihy [2019]*).
*   **Autonomy**: Parties cannot be compelled to use electronic communications against their will.

---

## 6. Time and Place of Dispatch/Receipt
### Dispatch (s 23 Qld)
The time when the communication leaves the originator's information system.

### Receipt (s 24 Qld)
*   **Designated Address**: Received when it becomes "capable of being retrieved" by the addressee.
*   **Non-Designated Address**: Received when it is capable of being retrieved **and** the addressee becomes aware of it.
*   **Presumption**: It is assumed capable of being retrieved when it reaches the electronic address.

### Key Case Law on Receipt
*   **Bauen Constructions v Sky General Services [2012]**: A document is received even if it goes to a spam folder.
*   **Demex v John Holland [2022]**: Service is effected when received at the nominated address; no requirement for the recipient to be aware of it. However, the plaintiff bears the onus of proving *when* it was received if metadata is missing.

---

## 7. Formation of Contract
### Invitation to Treat (s 26B Qld)
A proposal made via electronic communication that is not addressed to specific parties (e.g., a website) is an **invitation to treat**, unless it clearly indicates an intention to be bound (*Crosby Holdings v Samarawickrama [2019]*).

### Automated Systems (ss 26C, 26D Qld)
*   Contracts formed by automated systems (without human intervention) are valid.
*   **Input Errors**: Individuals have a right to withdraw portions of a communication containing an input error if the system provided no opportunity to correct it, provided they notify the other party promptly and have not received a benefit.

### Postal Acceptance Rule
*   **General View**: The postal acceptance rule (acceptance upon dispatch) generally does **not** apply to instantaneous or near-instantaneous communications like email.
*   **Brinkibon [1983]**: No universal rule; depends on party intention and sound business practice.
*   **Olivaylle v Flottweg [2009]**: Email is "near instantaneous"; the contract is formed where the acceptance is received.

---

## 8. Terms and Jurisdiction
*   **Incorporation by Reference**: Terms are incorporated if there is "reasonable notice." A "legitimate opportunity" to learn the terms is sufficient, even if not taken (*Dialogue Consulting v Instagram [2020]*).
*   **Jurisdiction Clauses**: Usually respected by courts if effectively incorporated (*Amazon*, *Microsoft*, *Agoda* cases).
*   **Click-Wrap**: "Click" contracts are generally enforceable (*Smythe v Thomas [2007]*).
