## Overview
This module explores the legal nature, functions, and regulatory framework of electronic signatures in Australia and internationally. It distinguishes between general "electronic signatures" and specific "digital signatures," examining how traditional signature principles (authentication and intention) are adapted to the digital environment through the principle of functional equivalence.

## Key Points

### 1. Definition and Functions
*   **Electronic Signature:** Any electronic means of authenticating identity and intent associated with an electronic record (e.g., typed names, PINs, biometrics, digitised manual signatures).
*   **Digital Signature:** A specific type of electronic signature using asymmetric cryptography (public/private keys) and hash functions for higher security.
*   **Functions of a Signature:**
    *   Identify the signatory.
    *   Provide certainty of personal involvement.
    *   Associate the person with the document's contents.
    *   Attest to the intention to be bound (or to witness).

### 2. Legislative Framework
*   **Electronic Transactions Acts (ETA):** The Commonwealth *Electronic Transactions Act 1999* (s 10) and State equivalents (e.g., Qld ETA s 14) provide functional equivalence.
*   **The Three-Part Test for Validity (ETA):**
    1.  **Identification & Intention:** A method is used to identify the person and indicate their intention/approval.
    2.  **Reliability:** The method must be "as reliable as appropriate" for the purpose, OR proven in fact to have fulfilled the identification/intention functions.
    3.  **Consent:** The recipient must consent to the requirement being met via an electronic signature (consent can often be inferred from conduct).
*   **Corporations Act 2001 (Cth):** Recent amendments (2022) permit "technology neutral" execution of documents and deeds by companies (ss 126, 127, 110A), allowing for split execution and removing witnessing requirements for many company documents.

### 3. International Context
*   **UNCITRAL Model Laws:** The Australian ETAs are based on the *Model Law on Electronic Commerce (1996)* and *Model Law on Electronic Signatures (2001)*.
*   **UN Convention (2005):** The *United Nations Convention on the Use of Electronic Communications in International Contracts* influenced the 2011 ETA amendments, particularly the "proven in fact" alternative to the reliability test.

### 4. Judicial Interpretation (Case Law)
*   **Broad Interpretation:** Courts generally take a pragmatic approach. Typed names in emails (*McGuren v Simpson*), automatic footers (*Luxottica Retail*), and even clicking "Book Now" buttons (*Gonzalez v Agoda*) have been recognized as signatures.
*   **Limits of "Automatic" Signatures:** Some tension exists regarding whether automatically inserted email headers constitute a signature if they lack evidence of "intent to sign" (*J Pereira Fernandes SA v Mehta*).
*   **Authority:** Electronic signatures are subject to traditional rules of agency and authority (*Williams Group Australia v Crocker*).[[Law & Tech/sources/Electronic Signatures/Electronic Signatures/ECL-03-signatures26]]

## Significance
This source is critical for understanding how the law maintains technology neutrality while ensuring the fundamental legal functions of a signature—authentication and expression of intent—are preserved. It highlights the shift from formalistic "ink on paper" requirements to a functional approach where the reliability of the method is weighed against the circumstances of the transaction. The recent reforms to the *Corporations Act* and state property laws (e.g., Qld *Property Law Act*) represent a significant modernization of the law regarding deeds and formal documents.
