# Davidson, Electronic Signatures (Chapter 13)

## Overview
This chapter explores the legal regulation, use, and security of electronic signatures, tracing their evolution from 19th-century telegraphy to modern digital technologies like Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) and TLS. It emphasizes that the legal validity of a signature depends primarily on the **intention** of the signatory to authenticate a document, rather than the physical medium used.

### Key Points

*   **Traditional vs. Electronic Signatures**: 
    *   A signature serves to identify the signatory and indicate their intention to be bound by or approve the document's contents.
    *   Historically, courts have accepted various marks (e.g., "X", initials, printed names, rubber stamps) as valid signatures if the requisite intention was present (*Lemayne v Stanley*; *R v Moore*).
*   **Electronic Signing Methods**:
    *   **Typed Names**: Simply typing a name at the end of an email can constitute a signature if intended as such.
    *   **Email Headers**: Generally, automatically generated email headers are *not* signatures because they lack the specific intention to authenticate the message body (*Mehta v J Pereira Fernandes SA*).
    *   **Digitised Signatures**: Scanned images of handwritten signatures are valid but offer low security.
    *   **Digital Signatures (PKI)**: A more secure subset using asymmetric cryptography (public/private keys) and hash functions to ensure both authentication and document integrity.
*   **Legal Framework (Electronic Transactions Acts)**:
    *   Australian ETAs adopt "functional equivalence," deeming an electronic signature valid if:
        1.  A method identifies the person and indicates their intention.
        2.  The method is as reliable as appropriate for the circumstances (or proven in fact to have fulfilled its function).
        3.  The recipient consents to the method.
*   **Corporations and Deeds**:
    *   Recent amendments (e.g., s 110A *Corporations Act*) allow companies to execute documents and deeds electronically.
    *   Common law requirements for deeds (paper, seal, delivery) have been largely superseded by statute (e.g., *Property Law Act 1974* (Qld)), allowing for electronic deeds and signatures without witnessing.
*   **Functions of Signatures**: Identified as evidentiary, cautionary, reliance, channelling (demarcating legal intent), and record-keeping.

## Significance
This chapter is critical for understanding how the law maintains technology neutrality while ensuring commercial certainty. It highlights that while technology provides the *means* for authentication, the **legal test** remains focused on the signatory's intent and the appropriateness of the method for the specific transaction. It also clarifies the modern statutory shift allowing even formal documents like deeds to be executed electronically, reflecting the reality of digital commerce.
