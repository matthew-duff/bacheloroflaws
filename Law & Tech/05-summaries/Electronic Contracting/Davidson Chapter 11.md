[[11-Chapter AD-5151]]

### Reference
Davidson, *Electronic Commerce and the Law of Contract*, 2016 Extracts for LAWS5151.

### Overview
This chapter examines the legal regulation of electronic contracting, focusing on the UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Commerce (1996), the UN Communications Convention (2005), and their implementation in Australia (Electronic Transactions Acts) and other jurisdictions (NZ, US, UK, India).

#### Key Points
- **Functional Equivalence & Technology Neutrality**: The core principles of electronic transactions law. Electronic media should be treated equally to paper if they perform the same function, and laws should not be tied to specific technologies (using terms like "data message" instead of "email").
- **Australian Legislative Framework**: Based on the Electronic Transactions Act 1999 (Cth) and parallel State/Territory acts. Key provisions address:
    - **Writing**: Met if information is accessible for subsequent reference and the recipient consents.
    - **Signatures**: Met if a method identifies the person and indicates intention, is reliable (or proven to work), and the recipient consents.
    - **Time/Place of Dispatch & Receipt**: Dispatch occurs when a communication leaves the originator's control. Receipt occurs when it is "capable of being retrieved" at a designated address.
- **The "Consent" Hurdle**: A significant critique of Australian law is the requirement for the recipient's consent for electronic writing/signatures. This potentially thwarts functional equivalence, as paper communications cannot be refused in the same way.
- **Common Law vs. Legislation**: The author argues that much of the legislation was unnecessary as the common law was already adapting to recognize electronic communications (e.g., *McGuren v Simpson*).
- **Postal Acceptance Rule**: The chapter discusses whether the postal rule applies to email. While some courts lean towards treating email as instantaneous (no postal rule), the author suggests a flexible approach based on the parties' intention and business practice.
- **Exemptions**: Numerous and inconsistent exemptions across Australian jurisdictions (e.g., land interests, wills, witnessed documents) undermine the principle of functional equivalence.

### Significance
This text is critical for understanding the statutory "overlay" on traditional contract law in the digital age. It highlights the tension between the goal of "functional equivalence" and the practical hurdles (like consent and broad exemptions) introduced by legislators. It provides a foundational analysis of how courts interpret "writing," "signature," and "receipt" in an electronic context, which is essential for determining the validity and timing of electronic contracts.
