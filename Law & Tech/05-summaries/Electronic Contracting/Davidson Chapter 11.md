## **1. Fundamental Principles & Frameworks**

The transition from paper-based to electronic commerce is governed by the emergence of a "new order" similar to the _lex mercatoria_.

### **A. Core Principles (UNCITRAL Model Law 1996)**

- **Functional Equivalence:** Electronic forms should be treated equally to traditional forms if they perform the same function.
    
- **Technology Neutrality:** Legislation uses generic terms (e.g., "data message") rather than specific media (e.g., "email") to remain relevant as technology evolves.
    
- **Media Neutrality:** Rules apply regardless of the specific electronic medium used.
    

### **B. Legislative Evolution**

- **UNCITRAL Model Law (1996):** The template for Australian/NZ law. Focused on removing obstacles to electronic data interchange (EDI) and email.
    
- **UN Communications Convention (2005):** Improved definitions, signature tests, and rules for automated message systems.
    
- **Australian Context:** _Electronic Transactions Act 1999_ (Cth) and parallel State/Territory Acts (e.g., _ETA 2000_NSW; _ETA 2001_ QLD). Uniformity is the goal, but minor variations exist.
    

---

## **2. Writing Requirements**

To satisfy a legal requirement for "writing" (e.g., _Statute of Frauds_), an electronic communication must meet two criteria under the _ETA_:

1. **Accessibility:** At the time of communication, it must be reasonable to expect the info will be **readily accessible**for subsequent reference.
    
2. **Consent:** The recipient must consent to the information being given electronically.
    

> **Key Case: _McGuren v Simpson_ [2004]** An email was held to satisfy the requirement for "writing" under the _Limitation Act_ at common law, even though the _ETA_ did not apply retrospectively. The court must accommodate technological change.

## **3. Electronic Signatures**

A signature is legally valid electronically if:

1. **Identification:** The method identifies the person.
    
2. **Intention:** The method indicates the person’s intention regarding the information.
    
3. **Reliability:** The method is either:
    
    - **Appropriately reliable** for the purpose/circumstances; OR
        
    - **Proven in fact** to have fulfilled the identification and intention functions (Paragraph (b)(ii) amendment).
        
4. **Consent:** The recipient must consent to the use of that electronic signature method.
    

### **Technical Distinctions**

- **Electronic Signature:** Any electronic means of authentication (e.g., typing a name, a scanned image).
    
- **Digital Signature:** A specific, secure technology using asymmetric cryptography (public/private keys).

## **4. Time and Place of Dispatch & Receipt**

|Concept|Australian Rule (Post-2011 Amendments)|
|---|---|
|**Time of Dispatch**|When the communication **leaves** the information system under the originator's control.|
|**Time of Receipt (Designated Address)**|When the communication is **capable of being retrieved** by the addressee.|
|**Time of Receipt (Non-Designated)**|When it is capable of being retrieved **AND** the addressee becomes aware it was sent to that address.|
|**Place of Dispatch/Receipt**|Deemed to be the **place of business** (or habitual residence for individuals), regardless of where the server/hardware is located.|

> **Exam Tip:** In _Bauen Constructions v Sky General Services_, an email caught in a **spam filter** was still "received" because it was "capable of being retrieved."

## **5. Contract Formation & The Postal Acceptance Rule**

- **General Rule:** Contract formed when acceptance is communicated to the offeror.
    
- **Postal Acceptance Rule (PAR):** Acceptance is effective upon _posting_.
    
- **Electronic Application:**
    
    - **Instantaneous (e.g., Chat, Voicemail):** PAR does **not** apply; acceptance is effective on communication (_Entores_).
        
    - **Non-Instantaneous (e.g., some Email):** PAR may apply if the parties intended the communication to function like post (_Brinkibon_ guidelines).
        
    - **Threefold Test (_Brinkibon_):** Consider the intention of parties, sound business practice, and risk assessment.

## **6. Critical Issues & Critiques (For Policy Questions)**

### **A. The "Consent" Precondition**

The text argues that the requirement for consent (Paragraph (c)) **thwarts functional equivalence**.

- **Problem:** In the paper world, you cannot refuse "writing."
    
- **Uncertainty:** It leads to "deferred formation" of contracts—is a contract formed when the email hits the inbox, or only when the recipient decides to consent to the electronic format?
    

### **B. Exemptions ("The Blanket Exception Risk")**

Many jurisdictions exempt specific areas (Land, Wills, Powers of Attorney).

- **Critique:** These exemptions are inconsistent and often based on a "mistrust" of technology.
    
- **The Common Law Gap:** If a transaction is "exempted" from the _ETA_, it doesn't mean paper is required; it just means the situation falls back to the common law, where courts often recognize electronic forms anyway (_McGuren v Simpson_).
    

### **C. Attribution**

Under the _ETA_, a person is only bound by an electronic communication if it was sent by them or with their **authority**. This preserves the law of agency but places the risk of "spoofing" or unauthorized access on the recipient.
