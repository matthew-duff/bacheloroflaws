# Internet of Things (IoT) Notes

## Overview
This source provides a comprehensive overview of the Internet of Things (IoT) framework, its technical infrastructure, and the evolving regulatory landscape in Australia, the EU, and the US. It defines IoT as a global infrastructure connecting physical and virtual things through interoperable information and communication technologies to enable advanced services.

### Key Points
- **Definition & Model**: IoT utilizes devices and interconnection solutions to build a shared information base. In manufacturing, it involves sensing and operating on physical-world entities via digital networks (e.g., production lines, self-directed vehicles).
- **Risks**:
    - **Security & Privacy**: Increased access points raise exposure to breaches; devices often have weak default settings and inadequate update options.
    - **Interoperability**: Dependence on seamless integration creates risks of supplier lock-in and compatibility issues.
    - **Lifecycle Weaknesses**: Lack of unique identification and secure configuration makes devices easier to breach.
- **Australian Regulatory Framework**:
    - **Code of Practice (2020)**: A voluntary 13-principle guide focusing on no default passwords, vulnerability disclosure, and secure updates.
    - **Cyber Security Strategy (2023-2030)**: Sets a vision for "safe technology" and legislative reforms.
    - **Cyber Security Act 2024 (Cth)**: Commenced March 4, 2026. It introduces mandatory security standards for "relevant connectable products" (RCPs), requiring manufacturers and suppliers to provide a **Statement of Compliance**.
    - **Enforcement**: The Secretary has powers to issue compliance, stop, and recall notices for non-compliant products.
- **International Context**:
    - **EU**: The *Cyber Resilience Act* mandates security-by-design and CE marking.
    - **US**: The *IoT Cybersecurity Improvement Act 2020* uses federal procurement and NIST standards to influence the market.

## Significance
This source is significant for Law & Tech as it tracks the transition from "soft law" (voluntary codes) to "hard law" (mandatory standards) in the IoT space. The *Cyber Security Act 2024 (Cth)* represents a major shift in Australian law, imposing proactive duties on manufacturers and suppliers to ensure security-by-design, reflecting the high stakes of IoT vulnerabilities in both consumer and industrial (Critical Infrastructure) contexts.
