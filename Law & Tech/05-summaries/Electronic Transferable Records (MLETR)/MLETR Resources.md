[[Resources and Articles on the Model Law of Electronic Transferable Records.]]

### Reference
Seok-Beom Choi, 'Compliance of Electronic Bill of Lading Regulation in Korea with Model Law on Electronic Transferable Records' (2019) 23(3) *Journal of Korea Trade* 68-83.

### Overview
This article analyzes the alignment of the Korean Commercial Act (KC Act) and the Regulation on Implementation of the Provisions of the Commercial Act Regarding Electronic Bills of Lading (B/L Regulation) with the UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Transferable Records (MLETR). 

**Key Points:**
- **Technological Neutrality & Openness**: The current Korean system for electronic bills of lading (e-B/Ls) is a "closed system" because it requires registration with a specific registry agency designated by the Ministry of Justice (e.g., KTNET). This contradicts the MLETR's principles of technology neutrality and non-discrimination.
- **Functional Equivalence of Possession (Control)**: A central concept in the MLETR is "control," which serves as the functional equivalent of "possession" for paper documents. The KC Act currently lacks explicit provisions regarding control, which is necessary for the legal recognition of e-B/Ls as documents of title.
- **Dichotomous Approach**: The author argues that the KC Act's requirement to "attach" an e-B/L is illogical since e-B/Ls lack physical form. Instead, the law should adopt a dichotomous approach where the electronic record (receipt/contract terms) and the "control" (right to claim delivery) are handled separately, similar to the CMI Rules or Bolero.
- **Cross-Border Recognition**: The MLETR prohibits denying legal effect to a record solely because it was issued abroad. The KC Act's rigid registry requirement effectively discriminates against foreign e-B/Ls.

### Significance
The article is significant for understanding the legislative hurdles in domesticating international standards like the MLETR. It highlights the tension between maintaining strict domestic oversight (via designated registries) and achieving the "open-oriented" and "technology-neutral" environment required for global paperless trade. The author provides specific recommendations for revising the KC Act to accommodate blockchain technology and transnational registry models, which are essential for the international negotiability of electronic transferable records.
