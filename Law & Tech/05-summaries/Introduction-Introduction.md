**Reference:** Alan Davidson, *Social Media and Electronic Commerce Law* (Cambridge University Press, 2016), updated for 2026. [[ECL-01-introduction26]]

## Overview
This module introduces the legal framework governing electronic commerce (e-commerce) and social media. It explores the transition from traditional commercial practices to the "Electronic Renaissance," characterized by global communications, high-speed data transfer, and the emergence of "digital personas." The core focus is on how the law adapts to technological change, the role of international standards (like UNCITRAL), and the debate over whether "cyberlaw" exists as a distinct legal discipline.

### Key Points
- **Definition of E-Commerce:** Transacting business in electronic form, including internet transactions, electronic funds transfers (EFT), and Electronic Data Interchange (EDI).
- **The "Electronic Renaissance":** A parallel to the invention of the printing press, where digital technology has liberated information but created new risks regarding privacy, security, and intellectual property.
- **The "Law of the Horse" Debate:** 
    - **Judge Easterbrook** argued that "cyberlaw" is not a discrete field, much like there is no "law of the horse"—it is simply the application of existing principles to new contexts.
    - **Lawrence Lessig** countered that the unique nature of cyberspace (its "code" and social norms) requires a distinct understanding of how law, architecture, and norms interact.
- **Regulatory Frameworks:**
    - **UNCITRAL:** The UN Commission on International Trade Law provides Model Laws (e.g., Model Law on Electronic Commerce 1996) to harmonize international e-commerce rules.
    - **Australian Context:** The *Electronic Transactions Act 1999* (Cth) and the *Privacy Act 1988* (Cth) are central. The **ACMA** (Australian Communications and Media Authority) acts as a "converged" regulator for telecommunications, broadcasting, and the internet.
- **Security & Authentication:** Security is paramount, relying on Public Key Cryptography (RSA), digital signatures, and certificates to authenticate identities and ensure transaction integrity.

## Significance
This introductory material is significant as it establishes the foundational tension in Law and Technology: the lag between rapid technological advancement and the slower evolution of legal systems. It highlights that order in this field often emerges "endogenously" from technical practice and commercial custom (a digital *lex mercatoria*) before being formalized by legislatures. Understanding this dynamic is crucial for legal practitioners advising on jurisdiction, digital identity, and the validity of electronic contracts in a borderless digital environment.
