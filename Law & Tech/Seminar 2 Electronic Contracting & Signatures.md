## 🖋️ Formality: "In Writing"

- **Historical Context**: Paper, vellum, stone, bark...
- **General Rule**: Contracts need not be in writing (can be verbal).
- **Statutory Requirements**:
    - **Statute of Frauds 1677** legacy.
    - **Property Law Act 2023 (Qld)**:
        - `s 7`: A land contract is not enforceable unless in writing/memorandum, **signed by the party to be charged**.
        - `s 8(1)`: Creation of a legal or equitable interest in land must be in writing and signed.
    - **Others**: Insurance contracts, IP assignments, Bills of Exchange.

### 📚 Key Formality Cases

> [!quote] *Marbryde Pty Ltd v Mainland Property Holdings No 8 Pty Ltd* [2021] QSC 344
> - Plaintiff relied on **s 14 ETA (Qld)** to argue email met **s 59 PLA** writing/signature requirement for a land contract.
> - [27]: *"The decisive issue is always the **intention of the parties**, which must be objectively ascertained from the factual context."*
> - Distinguished from *Stellard*: person signing and sending the email was **not a party nor an agent** to the contract.

> [!quote] *Stellard Pty Ltd v North Queensland Fuel Pty Ltd* [2015] QSC 119
> - Acceptance email satisfied s 59 PLA.
> - Recognised "joinder of documents" cases — several emails read together.
> - [69]: Arguments that emails lacked the precision necessary to satisfy s 59 not advanced with substance.

> [!quote] *Lockheed-Arabia v Owen* [1993] 3 All ER 641
> - Photocopy constituted "writing"; statutes should be read to accommodate technological change.

> [!quote] *Wilkens v Iowa Insurance Commissioner* (1990) 457 NW 2d
> - Requirement for "written record" satisfied by records kept on a computer system.

## 🌐 The Electronic Transactions Acts (ETAs)

**Nine ETAs** across Australia (Commonwealth + 8 States/Territories) to **remove legal impediments** to the use of electronic documents and electronic signatures.

**Based on:**
- UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Commerce 1996
- UNCITRAL Convention on the Use of Electronic Communications in International Contracts 2005 (ECC)

### 💡 Two Key Principles

> [!important] Foundational Principles of the ETAs
> 1. **Functional Equivalence** — As far as possible, paper-based and electronic commerce should be treated equally by the law.
> 2. **Technology Neutrality** — The law should not discriminate between different forms of technology.

### 📅 Australian Implementation Timeline

Australia was the **2nd country internationally** to adopt the UNCITRAL Model Law.

| Jurisdiction | 1st Stage | ECC Incorporated (2nd Stage) |

### UNCITRAL Electronic Communications Convention (ECC 2005)

> [!note] Art 8 — Legal Recognition of Electronic Communications
> *"A communication or a contract shall not be denied validity or enforceability on the sole ground that it is in the form of an electronic communication."*
>
> *"Nothing in this Convention requires a party to use or accept electronic communications, but a party's agreement to do so may be inferred from the party's conduct."* — Art 8(2)

**Ratified by**: Sri Lanka, Philippines, South Korea, Saudi Arabia, Colombia, China, Thailand, Singapore, and others.
Australia (2011–2013 incorporation into ETAs), USA, EU — **not yet full ratification**.

## 🥊 When Does Common Law Apply Instead of the ETA?

> [!important] Six Situations Where CL Applies
> 1. Transaction **predates** the ETA.
> 2. **Private international law** rules.
> 3. **Terms of the Act** — signature not "required"; or no consent given.
> 4. **Exemptions** — Cth: 51 exemptions (+ Schedule 1); Qld: Schedule 1 (e.g., documents requiring a witness other than the author).
> 5. **Excluded by other legislation** — Immigration, Bankruptcy, some Rules of Court.
> 6. **Applied without explanation** — courts sometimes ignore the ETA entirely (*Islamic Council*).

> [!tip] Exemption ≠ Paper Requirement
> *Joseph Mathew v Singh Chiranjeev* [2009] SGCA 51: Where there is an exemption, writing and signature requirements can still be met at **common law**. CA confirmed email correspondence satisfied the writing requirement in the Civil Law Act despite the Singapore ETA excluding land contracts.

### Notable CL Interpretations

> [!quote] *Islamic Council of SA v Australian Federation of Islamic Councils* [2009] NSWSC 211
> [20]: *"While 'writing' often contemplates writing on paper, it is nonetheless writing... if it appears on a computer screen, as a result of the entry of data into a computer."*
> (Common law — no ETA analysis applied. Compare to the ETA approach.)

| Case | CL Principle |
| :--- | :--- |
| *Stuart v Hishon* [2013] NSWSC 766 | ETA applies where signature is "required". Not required here → CL applies → **"Tom"** at end of email = valid CL signature. |
| *Parkview Constructions v Total Lifestyle Windows* [2016] NSWSC 1911 | USB stick served as documents in an envelope. ET Act of "doubtful relevance" — required **consensual cooperation** of recipient. |
| *McGuren v Simpson* | Email affirming debt ("Yes I spent the money") resets the limitation period — limitation statute satisfied electronically. |
| *Kavia Holdings v Suntrack Holdings* [2011] NSWSC 716 | Sender's name on email = "signing" (CL, no mention of ETA). |

## 📝 Writing Requirement (s 11 ETA Qld)

> [!law] s 11 Electronic Transactions (Queensland) Act 2001
> A requirement to **give information in writing** is met by electronic communication if:
> - **(a)** at the time the information was given, it was **reasonable to expect** the information would be **readily accessible so as to be useable for subsequent reference**; and
> - **(b)** the person to whom it is required to be given **consents** to the information being given by an electronic communication.

### Key Terms to Consider

- *'giving information'*
- *'required to give information in writing'*
- *'requirement is taken to have been met'*
- *'electronic communication'*
- *'at the time the information was given, it was reasonable to expect'*
- *'the information would be readily accessible so as to be usable for subsequent reference'*

### ⚠️ The "Dropbox" Rule

> [!warning] Sending a Link ≠ Sending a Document
> - *Conveyor & General Engineering Pty Ltd v Basetec Services Pty Ltd* [2014] QSC 30 — McMurdo J: sending a link to Dropbox means the documents **are not part of the electronic communication**.
> - *McCarthy v TKM Builders Pty Ltd* [2020] QSC 301 — Martin J confirmed: a Dropbox link does not constitute sending the documents.
> - **Also applies to SharePoint and Office 365.**
> - In both cases, the parties stated they had **not** followed the links.

## ✍️ Why Do We Require Writing & Signature?

> [!note] Policy Rationale for Writing/Signature Requirements
> - Statute of Frauds concerns.
> - **Reliable evidence** that the party intended to proceed with the transaction.
> - **Encourages deliberation** and reflection; emphasises significant legal consequences.
> - **Authenticates the identity** of the other party.

> [!fun] A Signature Can Be Almost Anything
> Probate granted for inscription on a tractor fender: *"In case I die in this mess, I leave all to the wife. Cecil Geo Harris."*
> Also: *"Vse zene"* ("All to wife") — written on a bedroom wall. Both recognised as valid wills.

## 🖊️ Signature Requirement

### ⚖️ What is a Signature?

- **Traditional**: Historically a mark (*R v Moore*, 1884). Purpose: authenticate identity and associate signatory with content.
- **Electronic**: Any means of electronic authentication — typed name, PIN, biometrics.
- **Digital Signature**: Specific asymmetric cryptosystem using public/private keys.
- Can be located **anywhere** on the document; made by the person or an authorised agent.

### 📜 s 14 ETA (Qld) — Electronic Signature

> [!law] s 14 Electronic Transactions (Queensland) Act 2001
> A signature requirement is met if:
> - **(a)** a method is used to **identify the person** and to **indicate the person's approval or intention** in respect of the information communicated; and
> - **(b)** the method was either:
>     - **(i)** as **reliable as appropriate** for the purpose (in light of all circumstances, including any relevant agreement); **OR**
>     - **(ii)** **proven in fact** to have fulfilled the functions described in (a), by itself or together with further evidence; and
> - **(c)** the person to whom the signature is required **consents** to the requirement being met by the method used.

> [!note] Ex Ante vs Ex Post Approaches
> - **Ex Ante — s 14(b)(i)**: Reliability assessed *prospectively* — was the method as reliable as appropriate at the time?
> - **Ex Post — s 14(b)(ii)**: Reliability proved *retrospectively* — did it actually work in practice?

### 🏢 Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) — Electronic Signing

**2022 amendments** enable all documents required or permitted under the Corporations Act to be signed electronically.

> [!law] s 110A — Technology Neutral Signing
> A person may sign a document:
> - **(a)** by signing a **physical** form by hand; or
> - **(b)** by signing an **electronic** form using electronic means;
>
> if the method **(i)** identifies the person and indicates their intention, **AND** **(ii)** was as reliable as appropriate **OR** proven in fact to have worked.
>
> **No consent required** under the Corporations Act (unlike the ETAs).

> [!law] s 127 — Execution of Documents (including Deeds)
> - Note 2 to s 127(1): *"The requirement to sign may be satisfied electronically."*
> - s 127(2A): Witnessing the affixing of a common seal may be undertaken "by electronic means or by being physically present."
> - **Split Execution**: Directors can sign different copies.

> [!law] Acts Interpretation Act 1901 (Cth) — Definition of "Document"
> *"document"* means any record of information, and includes:
> - **(a)** anything on which there is writing; and
> - **(b)** anything on which there are marks, figures, symbols or perforations having a meaning for persons qualified to interpret them; and
> - **(c)** anything from which sounds, images or writings can be reproduced with or without the aid of anything else; and
> - **(d)** a map, plan, drawing or photograph.

## 📦 Production Requirement (s 17 ETA Qld)

> [!law] s 17 Electronic Transactions (Queensland) Act 2001
> If a person is **permitted** to produce a document in paper form, they may instead produce an **electronic form** by electronic communication where:
> - **(a)** the method of generating the electronic form provided a **reliable way of maintaining the integrity** of the information; and
> - **(b)** it was **reasonable to expect** the information would be readily accessible for **subsequent reference**; and
> - **(c)** the person to whom the document is permitted to be produced **consents** to electronic production.

## 🤝 The Consent Requirement

> [!important] Policy Basis
> *"This provision is based on the Government's general policy that a person should not be compelled to use an electronic communication to conduct a transaction in order to satisfy requirements or permissions to give information in writing under Commonwealth law."* — Cth Explanatory Memorandum

- **Consent** is required for writing (s 11), signature (s 14), and production (s 17).
- **No consent required** under the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) 2022 regime.
- Consent must be given **at the time** of the relevant communication.
- Consent can be **express** or **inferred from conduct**.

> [!note] UNCITRAL ECC Art 8(2)
> *"Nothing in this Convention requires a party to use or accept electronic communications, but a party's agreement to do so may be inferred from the party's conduct."*

## 🏢 Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) — 2022 Electronic Communications Regime

> [!note] Global Communications Regime (from 2022)
> The Meetings and Documents Act created a **global communications regime** covering all documents required to be sent under Chapters 2A–2M, 5–5D, 6–6C, 8A, 9 and Schedule 2 (excluding documents lodged with ASIC, the Registrar or the Takeovers Panel).

> [!law] s 110D — Technology Neutral Sending of Documents
> A document may be sent to a recipient:
> - **(a)** in physical form; or
> - **(b)** as sufficient information (physical) to allow recipient to access it electronically; or
> - **(c)** in electronic form by electronic communication; or
> - **(d)** as sufficient information (electronic) to allow electronic access; or
> - **(e)** by making it readily available **on a website** (where applicable).
>
> Condition: it must be **reasonable to expect** the document would be readily accessible for subsequent reference.

> [!law] s 28A Acts Interpretation Act 1901 (Cth) — Service
> Service may be effected personally, by post to last known address, or on a body corporate at its head/registered/principal office.
> *Note: The ETA deals with giving information electronically.*

> [!law] s 39 Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) — Service
> Service may be effected on an individual by delivery, post, telex, facsimile or **similar facility**; or on a body corporate at its head/registered/principal office. Applies whether the expression "deliver," "give," "notify," "send" or "serve" is used.

## ⏱️ Timing of Electronic Communications

### 📤 Time of Dispatch (s 23 ETA Qld / ECC Art 10(1))

> *The time of dispatch is:*
> - **(a)** when the communication **leaves an information system** under the control of the originator; or
> - **(b)** if it has **not left** the originator's system — the time it is **received** by the addressee (e.g., same server scenario).

### Corporations Act — s 105A

Mirrors the ETA dispatch rule. Applies *unless otherwise agreed* between originator and addressee.

### 📥 Time of Receipt (s 24 ETA Qld)

> *Unless otherwise agreed:*
> - **(a)** at a **designated address** — time when capable of being **retrieved** at that address; or
> - **(b)** at an **other address** — time when capable of being retrieved **AND** the addressee has **become aware** it was sent to that address.
>
> **Presumption** (s 24(2)): It is assumed capable of being retrieved when it reaches the addressee's electronic address.

> [!note] s 105A(4) Corporations Act — Simplified Receipt Rule
> *"An electronic communication is received when the electronic communication becomes capable of being retrieved by the addressee at the addressee's **nominated electronic address**."*
> No requirement for awareness — simpler than the ETA (administratively easier for mass communications to creditors/members).

### Timing Cases

| Case | Key Finding |
| :--- | :--- |
| *Bauen Constructions v Sky General Services* [2012] NSWSC 1123 | Email caught in a **spam/junk box** is still "received" — it is archived and accessible (capable of being retrieved). |
| *Re David Scott Ellis; Ex Parte Triple M Mechanical* [2013] WASC 161 | ".rar" files — Arbitrator: NOT received. Court: IS received. |
| *Reed v Eire* [2009] NSWSC 678 | **Metadata** as evidence of time of receipt. [31] |
| *Austar Finance v Campbell* [2007] NSWSC 1493 | Fax "received" when sent and received in the ordinary way. |
| *QldBSA v JM Kelly (Project Builders) Pty Ltd* [2013] QCA 320 | Onus shifts to **recipient** — "did not adduce evidence explaining why the staff member did not give evidence." |
| *Durney v Deakin University* [2014] VSC 577 | Student claiming not to check emails — **deemed received** by ETA. |
| *Falgat Constructions v Equity Australia Corp* [2006] NSWCA 259 | "Receipt" equates to when document comes to the **attention** of the person/agent. |
| *A-Tech Australia v Top Pacific Construction* [2019] NSWSC 404 | Applied *Falgat* principle: "clear admission that payment claims were served in accordance with the Falgat principle." [98] |
| *Westpac Banking Corp v Dixon* [2011] FMCA 211 | "Gives" interpreted in the context of receipt. |
| *Wong and Minister for Health* [2017] AATA 2296 | Receipt interpreted to include "lodged." [25] |

> [!quote] *Demex Pty Ltd v John Holland Pty Ltd* [2022] QSC 259
> **Facts**: $5.4M dispute. Email sent **Saturday 25 Sept** — first read **Monday 27 Sept**.
> - If received Saturday: first counting day = Monday.
> - If received Monday: first counting day = Tuesday.
>
> **Plaintiff argued**: (1) obvious inference email received near time of sending; (2) judicial notice; (3) presumption of regularity.
> **Defendant argued**: plaintiff bears the evidential onus and failed to prove when received.
>
> **Held**: Issue proved on balance of probabilities. Court would **not assume** receipt at or about the time of sending. No metadata/timestamp evidence produced. Email taken as received **Monday** → first counting day **Tuesday**.
>
> **Lesson**: Always preserve and adduce **metadata evidence** of time of receipt.

## 📬 Postal Acceptance Rule

> [!important] Which Rule Applies to Email Acceptance?
> - **Postal Acceptance Rule**: Acceptance effective at **dispatch** (*Adams v Lindsell* (1818) 1 B & Ald 681; *Henthorn v Fraser* [1892] 2 Ch 27).
> - **Instantaneous Rule**: Acceptance effective at **receipt** (*Entores v Miles Far East* (1955); *Brinkibon v Stahag Stahl* [1983] 2 AC 34 (HL)).

> [!quote] *Brinkibon v Stahag Stahl* [1983] (HL) — Lord Wilberforce
> *"No universal rule can cover all such cases: they must be resolved by reference to the **intention of the parties**, by **sound business practice** and in some cases by a judgment where the **risks should lie**."*

> [!quote] *Chwee Kin Keong v Digilandmall.com Pte Ltd* [2004] SGHC 71 (Singapore)
> Once an email is sent, *"the sender loses control over the route and delivery time of the message. In that sense, it is akin to ordinary posting."* Email is *"not instantaneous."*
> → Could cogently argue the **postal rule applies** to email acceptances (obiter).

> [!quote] *Olivaylle Pty Ltd v Flottweg GMBH & Co KGAA (No 4)* [2009] FCA 522 — Logan J
> *"Experience suggests that email is often, but not invariably, a form of near instantaneous communication... there are analogies to be drawn with the way the law developed in relation to telex communications in an earlier era."*
> → PAR *"may be confined to situations where it can be inferred an offeror intended acceptance to be communicated upon dispatch."* — Cth 2011 EM

> [!note] Also consider: CISG; ECC Art 20 — relevant in international contracts.

## 📍 Place of Dispatch and Receipt

- Determined by **place of business** — closest connection — habitual residence.
- *Jagatramka v Coeclerici Asia (PTE) Limited (No.2)* [2015] FCCA 2743 [104].

> [!law] s 105B Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) — Deemed Place
> - **(a)** Register of members;
> - **(b)** Registered office;
> - **(ba)** Principal place of business in Australia; or
> - **(c)** Most recent physical address nominated; or if none, usual residential address in Australia.

## 🔗 Attribution

> *"The purported originator of the electronic communication is bound by that communication **only if** the communication was sent by the purported originator or **with the authority** of the purported originator."*

- Compare to UNCITRAL MLEC (no equivalent provision in the ECC).

## ⚠️ Error Provisions (s 26D ETA Qld)

> [!law] s 26D — Error in Electronic Communications Regarding Contracts (Automated Systems)
> If:
> - **(a)** an individual makes an **input error** in a communication exchanged with an **automated message system**; and
> - **(b)** the automated system does **not provide an opportunity to correct** the error;
>
> The person has the right to **withdraw** the erroneous portion if:
> - **(c)** they **notify the other party** of the error as soon as practicable after discovering it; and
> - **(d)** they have **not used or received any material benefit** from goods/services received from the other party.

## 🤖 Electronic Agents (s 26C ETA Qld)

> [!law] s 26C — Automated Message Systems and Contract Formation
> A contract formed by:
> - **(a)** an automated message system **and** a natural person; or
> - **(b)** the interaction of automated message systems;
>
> is **not invalid, void or unenforceable** on the sole ground that no natural person reviewed or intervened in each action carried out by the automated message systems or the resulting contract.

**Examples of electronic agents:**
- Online shopping agents programmed to surf the internet and make purchases.
- Inventory control systems that place orders when stocks reach a particular threshold level.

## 📢 Invitation to Treat (s 15B ETA Cth)

> [!law] s 15B — Invitation to Treat Regarding Contracts
> A proposal to form a contract through electronic communications that:
> - **(a)** is **not addressed to one or more specific parties**; and
> - **(b)** is **generally accessible** to parties making use of information systems;
>
> is to be considered an **invitation to make offers**, unless it **clearly indicates** the intention of the party to be **bound in case of acceptance**.
>
> Extends to proposals using interactive applications for the placement of orders.

> [!quote] *Crosby Holdings Trust & Brickwest Pty Ltd v Samarawickrama* [2019] VCAT 627
> [36]: The language used indicated, *"with sufficient clarity"*, that it was the intention of the party to be bound in the event of acceptance. Therefore the **presumption was displaced** — it was an **offer**, not merely an invitation to treat.

## 📄 Terms of Contract

### How Are Terms Incorporated?

Terms are incorporated into a contract by:
1. **Signature** — binds party regardless of reading (*L'Estrange v Graucob* [1934]).
2. **Reasonable notice**.
3. **Course of dealing**.
4. **Agreement**.

> [!quote] *L'Estrange v Graucob* [1934] 2 KB 394
> Signing a document binds the party to its terms, regardless of whether they read them.

> [!quote] *Oceanic Sun Line Special Shipping Company Inc v Fay* [1988] HCA 32 — Brennan J
> *"If a passenger signs and thereby binds himself to the terms of a contract of carriage... it is immaterial that the passenger did not trouble to discover the contents of the contract."*

> [!quote] *Toll (FGCT) Pty Ltd v Alphapharm Pty Ltd* [2004] HCA 52
> Affirmed *L'Estrange*: signing a document with a request to read it is a representation of assent to its terms.

### Common Terms in Online Transactions

- Names of the parties.
- Subject matter — clear description of goods or services.
- Price, delivery, and payment terms.
- Warranties, liability, insurance, IP rights, and dispute resolution.

### Website Disclaimers

Website terms are reminiscent of the **ticket cases** — reasonable notice of terms must be given.

> [!note] Browsewrap vs Clickwrap
> - **Browsewrap**: Terms linked from the page; no active assent. Generally **not enforced** against consumers (*Nguyen v Barnes & Noble*).
> - **Clickwrap**: User actively clicks to agree. Generally **enforced** (*Gonzalez v Agoda*; *Dialogue Consulting v Instagram*).
>
> *"A website satisfies the reasonable notice standard if it provides a person with a legitimate opportunity to learn that his or her use is subject to terms of use, even if the person does not take the opportunity."* — *Dialogue Consulting v Instagram* [2020] FCA 1846 [223]

## 🌏 Jurisdiction Clauses

If effectively incorporated, a **jurisdiction clause** may alter the governing law and forum. Courts may override such clauses in certain circumstances.

> [!example] Amazon.com
> *"By visiting Amazon.com, you agree that the laws of the **State of Washington**... will govern these Conditions of Use and any dispute of any sort that might arise between you and Amazon.com or its affiliates."*

> [!example] Telstra BigPond
> *"This agreement is governed by the law in force in the **State of New South Wales**, Australia, and the parties irrevocably submit to the **non-exclusive jurisdiction** of the courts of New South Wales."*

> [!quote] *Gonzalez v Agoda Company Pte Ltd* [2017] NSWSC 1133
> - Gonzalez (NSW) — Agoda (Singapore) — booking a hotel room in Paris.
> - Clause: exclusive jurisdiction in **Singapore**.
> - [123]: Clicking "Book Now" = signature (s 9(1) ETA); terms were **above** the signature; Singapore clause **incorporated and enforced**.
> - Approved in *Dialogue Consulting Pty Ltd v Instagram, Inc* [2020] FCA 1846 [214–226].
> - See also *Carnival plc v Karpik (The Ruby Princess)* [2022] FCAFC 149; *Karpik v Carnival plc* [2023] HCA 39.
