THE EVOLUTION AND REFORM OF STANDING IN
AUSTRALIAN ADMINISTRATIVE LAW

Matthew Groves*

ABSTRACT

The standing rule in administrative law requires parties to demonstrate that they have
a special interest in the subject matter of the decision they wish to challenge in judicial
review proceedings. This article examines the problems the special interest requirement
has presented in judicial review, particularly to environmental groups, and how it has
been liberalised in lower courts. The article then examines several possible reforms to
standing and concludes that a standing rule should be retained but relaxed.

I 
INTRODUCTION

Standing in judicial review proceedings is a stepping stone on which all else depends.
While other barriers can impede a case, such as costs or a lack of reasons,1 standing can
preclude litigants from gaining remedies against unlawful decisions. The restrictive
common law approach was affirmed at the start of the 20th century,2 and refined by the
High Court in Australian Conservation Foundation v Commonwealth.3 That approach has
been slowly chipped away in the lower courts, which have taken a more relaxed
approach to the standing of associations and representative groups in environmental
cases.4 But standing principles remain, and they remain unclear and very discretionary.
The High Court did little to resolve those uncertainties in Argos Pty Ltd v Corbell.5 The
Court held that a business owner who might suffer loss or detriment due to the greater
competition that might result from an administrative decision could be a 'person
aggrieved' for standing rules. This finding clarified standing in commercial cases but
gave little general guidance about standing.
This article examines the modem evolution of standing principles and how they
might be reformed. The article first considers the accepted reasons for standing rules

II WHAT IS THE PURPOSE OF STANDING?

Douglas argued that the reasons for standing are generally assumed rather than
explained and the few instances where courts try to justify standing are 'rarely
persuasive'. 7 Douglas was right because courts typically presume, or note without
explanation, the rationale of standing.8 Some reasons for standing rules are, however,
apparent. One is functional. According to one member of the Federal Court, standing
rules are:
designed to ensure that applicants only litigate their own business. For an applicant to have
standing demands a connection between the applicant's interests and the relief sought. As
a general rule the Court will not recognise busybodies who interfere in things that do not
concern them.9

Such statements indicate that standing rules keep people within their own business
and outside that of others. The role of the courts is to maintain those boundaries. This
conception of standing is both limited and limiting. It is limited because the main role of
the courts is to ensure that litigants do not unduly trespass into matters that do not affect
them in a direct or sufficient way. 10 It is limiting because the requirement of a direct or
sufficient interest draws attention to the effect of a decision upon the person seeking to
challenge a decision, rather than the lawfulness of that decision. An unlawful decision
may therefore stand because of standing, or rather the failure of a party to satisfy the
court that it is sufficiently affected by the decision.1 1

explained the entrenched nature of their supervisory judicial review jurisdiction, which

III THE MODERN AUSTRALIAN STARTING POINT: ACF

Standing became important in the second half of the 19th century after the fusion of
courts of law and equity, when questions arose about whether parties other than the
Attorney-General could enforce public duties. 29 The principles governing this issue
were recast in the influential English decision of Boyce v Paddington Borough Council.30

Buckley LJ held that private individuals could sue for the performance of public duties:
first, where the interference with the public right is such as that some private right of his
is at the same time interfered with ... and, secondly, where no private right is interfered
with, but the plaintiff, in respect of his public right, suffers special damage peculiar to
himself from the interference with the public right.3 1

This approach accepted the standing of people other than the Attorney-General to
raise questions of public law or public duties, so long as they are affected, or 'damaged',
differently to other members of the public. Mr Boyce easily met that test because the
decision he challenged would have greatly dininished the view he enjoyed from his
property. The position of others who sought to challenge public law decisions was more
difficult because Buckley LJ did not define either the 'public right' capable of protection
or the kind or severity of damage required to constitute 'special damage'.
The High Court refined the criteria of special damage in ACF.32 That case involved a
decision of the Reserve Bank to approve foreign investment for a tourist development
in Queensland. The Australian Conservation Foundation ('the ACF') challenged the
decision of the Reserve Bank on the seemingly strong ground that its decision about
foreign investment was made before the required environmental impact procedures
were completed. 33 The ACF claimed standing on two separate arguments. The first
invited the High Court to 'disregard the existing authorities' and sanction open
standing, enabling 'any private citizen to enforce public duties, unless the court in its
discretion considered it inadvisable that the action should be allowed to proceed.' 34 A
majority of the High Court held that such a radical step was one for parliament to take,35

which was unsurprising given that the Australian Law Reform Commission was
examining standing at that very time.36 The second basis of the ACF's claim to standing
rested on a modified version of the special damage test -

Both ACF and Onus require some level of special interest by a party who seeks

Boyce,57 which required such an interest in part to keep the feared busybody at bay.
Gaudron, Gummow and Kirby JJ noted that the continued requirement of an interest

bodies' that required claimants to show both a possible error of law by the decision
maker and a special interest on their own part.58 Their Honours identified a paradox in
this continuing requirement of a special interest as a basis for standing in public law.
They proceeded on the assumption that remedies should be available against public
authorities that exceeded or misused their powers, yet the principles by which anyone
other than the Attorney-General could seek relief against such action remained

special interest requirement should be flexible because:
The public interest may be vindicated at the suit of a party with a sufficient material interest

this criterion is to be construed as an enabling, not a restrictive, procedural stipulation. 61

Although Gaudron, Gummow and Kirby JJ did not actually loosen the special
interest test, their conception of standing as an enabling rather than restricting concept
hinted that it should not preclude otherwise worthy claimants in public law and could
be applied more flexibly. The next sections explain how the standing test in the ADJR
Act affected standing rules and how a more flexible approach has taken hold in
environmental cases.

This article examined several possible ways that standing could be reformed. The
adoption of open standing lies at one extreme and would represent a radical break with
the incremental development of standing. A less radical alternative, such as the adoption
in judicial review legislation of the standing provisions governing groups in proceedings
before the AAT, would remove many of the problems surrounding standing but not the
standing rule itself. That legislative step appears a better alternative to the UK approach,
which has seen courts devise rules as liberal as they are discretionary. In my view, the
discretion which lies at the heart of standing in the UK creates uncertainty and grants
too much uncertain power to the courts. That undesirable level of judicial discretion
could be avoided in Australian law if parliaments bit the bullet and reformed standing.
If they do not, they cannot complain if the courts bite instead.
