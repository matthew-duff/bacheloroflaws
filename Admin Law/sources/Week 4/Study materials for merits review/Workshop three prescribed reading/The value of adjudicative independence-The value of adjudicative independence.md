The Value of Adjudicative Independence: 
Overlapping Conceptions of Administrative 
Justice in the Administrative Appeals Tribunal’s 
Review of Visa Cancellations

Dr Chantal Bostock*

Administrative justice is a highly contested concept. Mashaw and others 
discerned at least two models, respectively dealing with bureaucratic 
rationality and legality. Bureaucratic rationality emphasises accurate, efficient 
and cost-effective decision-making while in contrast, the legal model focuses 
on “court-centred adjudication”, in which the parties’ competing interests 
are fairly determined. I argue that these two conceptions of administrative 
justice are set against one another throughout the visa cancellation system 
but that when the Administrative Appeals Tribunal performs its decision-
making function, the legal model dominates. When analysed through the 
lens of these competing models of administrative justice, we gain insight 
and understand the reasons for the resulting tension and criticism of Tribunal 
decision-making. Adjudicative independence, however, is an inherent aspect 
of fairness, which is in turn the key value of the legal model. For that reason, 
the Tribunal must be allowed to undertake the function it was designed to 
fulfil, namely independent merits review.

INTRODUCTION
Section 501 of the Migration Act 1958 (Cth) (Migration Act) provides for the cancellation of visas on 
the grounds of character.1 While s 501 is regularly amended, in 2014, amendments were made to the 
Migration Act, which enabled mandatory and automatic cancellation upon the applicant being sentenced 
to a term of imprisonment of 12 months or more and being imprisoned.2 The change in the law resulted 
in an increase in visa cancellations of 1,400%.3 Thus, in 2016/2017, 1,277 visas were cancelled under 
s 501, most of which were mandatory cancellations.4 Currently, there is a bill before Parliament, which 
seeks to further amend the character test by providing “a clearer and more objective basis for refusing or 
cancelling the visa of a non-citizen whose offending has not attracted a sentence of 12 months or more, 
but who nonetheless poses an unacceptable risk to the safety of law-abiding citizens and non-citizens”.5

In certain circumstances, those subject to a visa cancellation may seek review of the decision at the 
Administrative Appeals Tribunal (the Tribunal).7 The Tribunal was designed and established to provide 
independent merits review and, along with the courts and other institutions, forms part of Australia’s 
system of administrative justice. While administrative justice is difficult to define for reasons which 
I explain, I draw here upon Mashaw’s models of administrative justice. Although he developed three 
models, Creyke observes that in the Australian context, only two are practically relevant: the struggle 
is primarily between bureaucratic rationality and a model of administrative justice, which focuses on 
“court centred adjudication”.8 This is the so-called legal model.9 The goal of bureaucratic rationality is 
accurate decision-making, as defined by government, in an efficient and cost-effective way10 while the 
legal model focuses on “deciding competing interests of litigants”.11 The legal model “clearly supports 
the emphasis on safeguarding the rights and interests of individuals”.12 Independence in this context is 
particularly “valued” because it is seen to “ensure fairness”.13 There are different forms of independence, 
but here I focus on adjudicative independence, which means “non-interference, non-delegation and the 
exercise of unbiased, individual judgment”.14

The two models of administrative justice vie in the visa cancellation system. As will be seen, at 
Departmental level, the bureaucratic rationality model trumps while the legal model dominates in the 
courts. In the Tribunal’s review of a visa cancellation, the clash of “rival goals and values” is more acute.15 
By exploring the Tribunal’s review framework and functions, we see which model of administrative 
justice dominates at a particular point, thus allowing us to determine which value should be given 
precedence.

adjudicative independence is critical, given that it is an aspect of fairness, a key value of the legal model.

ADMINISTRATIVE JUSTICE
As a concept, administrative justice has been described as “essentially contested”.17 WB Gallie was quoted 
as concluding that “the normative and other values which surely lie at the heart of any form of justice 
will inevitably be contested to some extent”.18 Mashaw has argued that there are different, competing 
models of administrative justice operating in particular environments, each with its own values, goals 
and organisational systems.19 In the Australian context, Creyke observed that the struggle exists between 
two particular “facets of administrative justice” (being those related to bureaucratic rationality and moral 
judgment), rather than the triumvirate of interests to which Mashaw refers –“bureaucratic rationality”, 
“professional treatment” and “moral judgment”.20

Mashaw’s model of bureaucratic rationality sees administrative justice focus on the accuracy of decision-
making in the most cost-effective manner. Halliday and Scott noted:
In devising the programme, legislators or policy makers have decided who is eligible and who is not – it 
has made the value judgments about deservingness. The task of the administrative system is to implement 
those preferences on a grand scale in as accurate and consistent a way as possible.21

Thus, Halliday and Scott saw “discretionary judgments by individual officers [as] antithetical to the 
goals of accuracy and consistency”.22

Building upon Mashaw’s work, Kagan argued that when political authorities do not trust administrative 
officials “to remain faithful to the central government’s policy goals”,23 administrative organisation 
is characterised “by a high degree of hierarchical authority and legal formality” and may be called 
bureaucratic legalism.24 It “emphasises uniform implementation of centrally devised rules, vertical 
accountability, and official responsibility for fact finding”.25

Another model in Mashaw’s scheme is the moral judgment model,26 which Adler renames the legal 
model. This model is based on the notion of an “adjudicatory process”,27 which “is not just to resolve 
disputes about facts, but is also to decide between the competing interests of litigants”.28 Individuals 
must therefore persuade the decision-maker of the merits of their claims. Mashaw explained that “the 
parties control how much of their lives or relationships is put at issue and what factual and normative

In short, we can see that at primary level, the visa cancellation system is primarily formal and hierarchical 
and thus constitutes an example of Kagan’s system of bureaucratic legalism, a method of administrative 
organisation which implements Mashaw’s model of bureaucratic rationality. The goal of the system is 
thus accuracy of decision-making, accuracy being determined by the government’s view of what is the 
correct decision and consistency in decision-making, in other words, where decision-makers comply 
with the government’s view of correct decision-making.

JUDICIAL REVIEW
Applicants may challenge the legality of a visa cancellation decision by way of judicial review. 
Unsurprisingly, the legal model of administrative justice, implemented in the form of adversarial legalism, 
dominates in the judicial review sphere. Using Kagan’s language once again, the courts’ decision-
making processes are “closely-constrained by formal procedural rules”.55 Thus, the requirements for 
lodging and transferring judicial review applications, and for lodging and filing documents, interlocutory 
applications and other matters relating to the review process, are codified.56 To succeed, applicants must 
demonstrate that the decision is affected by jurisdictional error, a finding which itself involves balancing 
and determining “competing claims”, as I now demonstrate by reference to one ground of review, namely 
legal unreasonableness.57

Reasonableness in decision-making is “a presumption of the law”,58 stipulating that “the legislature 
is taken to intend that a discretionary power, statutorily conferred, will be exercised reasonably”.59 
As Allsop CJ noted in Minister for Immigration and Border Protection v Stretton, “the judgments in 
Li identified two different contexts in which the concept of legal unreasonableness was employed: 
a conclusion after the identification of jurisdictional error for a recognised species of error, and an 
‘outcome-focused conclusion’ without any specific jurisdictional error being identified”.60

purpose of the statute conferring the discretionary power and its real object”.62 In addition “the statute

TRIBUNAL REVIEW OF VISA CANCELLATIONS
The Tribunal was set up in accordance with a wider administrative law package, intended to improve 
individuals’ access to justice. Along with other important administrative law reforms, the passage of 
the Administrative Appeals Tribunal Act in 1975 provided for “a single independent tribunal with the 
purpose of dealing with appeals against administrative decisions on as wide a basis as possible”.77 As the 
then Attorney-General noted “[I]n the words of the Franks Committee on Tribunals and Inquiries, the 
Tribunal is not to be an appendage of Government departments”:78

The Tribunal is to be regarded as machinery provided by Parliament for adjudication rather than as part of 
the machinery of departmental administration.79

Some of the earliest decisions of the Federal Court relating to the powers of the Tribunal are still cited 
today. The Tribunal stands in “the shoes” of the original decision-maker, exercising “all the powers 
and discretions” of the primary decision-maker to, among other things, affirm or set aside the primary 
decision.80 Sir Gerard Brennan described the role of merits review as follows:
Before the Tribunal intervenes to set aside or vary a decision under review it must come to the view that 
– the facts are different from what they were believed to be by the primary administrator; the law applies 
differently from the way in which the primary administrator applied it; or if there be a discretion, there is 
a way of exercising it preferable to the way in which the primary administrator exercised it.81

It must therefore decide whether the decision is “the correct or preferable one on the material before the 
Tribunal”.82 When exercising a discretion and determining which decision is preferable, the Tribunal does 
not exercise a “supervisory role”: it is reviewing the decision itself, not the reasons for the decision.83 
Thus, “the duty of the Tribunal is to satisfy itself whether a decision in respect of which an application 
for review is duly instituted is a decision which in its view, was objectively, the right one to be made”.84

In the Tribunal’s review of visa cancellation decisions, the bureaucratic rationality and the legal models 
of administrative justice mingle and merge at different points in time. Bureaucratic rationality trumps 
when it comes to the substantive law and procedures governing this area of review. Thus, the substantive 
law, such as the character test, is codified. Residual discretion, found in ss 501(2) and 501CA(4)(b)(ii), 
is arguably effectively shaped by a Ministerial Direction as it “gives directions to the Tribunal as to the 
policy it must apply in the exercise of the discretion conferred on it by s 43 of the Administrative Appeals 
Tribunal Act in exercising the power conferred by s 501 of the Migration Act”.85 While “the weighing 
process in each case is in substance left … to the individual decision maker exercising the power under 
s 501”, “failure to comply with a direction under s 499 may be a jurisdictional error”.86

The Tribunal’s procedures are codified, offering little scope for discretion on the part  of Tribunal 
members. For example, the review application must be lodged within nine days after the day on which 
the applicant was notified87 and the Tribunal must determine the review application within 84 days, 
or the review application is taken to be affirmed.88 In addition, there are strict rules  governing the 
applicant’s submission of new material prior to the hearing.89 In short, under this type of administrative 
justice, accurate, efficient decision-making is highly valued. Such decision-making is usually defined by 
government practice.
The legal model comes to the forefront, however, when the Tribunal performs its core decision-making 
task, in other words, making the correct or preferable decision. As noted above, the goal of the legal 
model of administrative justice is the fair adjudication of competing claims. Thus, the legal model, 
implemented in the form of adversarial legalism, requires focus on the parties’ circumstances and claims, 
the balancing of the various interests and the determination of contentious demands. The Tribunal hears 
from the parties, it assesses credibility, it considers the evidence and material and ultimately, weighs the 
competing interests, in accordance with the law, including the applicable Direction.

THE VALUE OF ADJUDICATIVE INDEPENDENCE
In visa cancellation decisions, there are multiple, complex, competing interests. On one hand, there is the 
following relatively straightforward view:
[I]f you can’t obey the law then you don’t deserve to be here. The taxpayer should not be paying for non-
citizens’ incarceration. Simply arrest and deport. There is no other solution. Without the hard line we are 
endangering the lives of Australians.90

A less black and white approach involves the exploration of factors such as the circumstances, the 
recency and the pattern of offending and the risk of reoffending.
On the other hand, many long-term residents have profound ties to Australia. They arrived in Australia 
as children and became integrated, studying, working, paying taxes and being involved in their local 
community. They are in effect “Australian in all but law”.91 The impact of visa cancellation and removal 
upon these residents may be devastating, particularly when they have no ties, not even the language, to 
their country of citizenship.92 Long-term residents facing removal may be poorly educated and gravely 
affected by mental health, drug and alcohol problems.93 Others may be refugees or asylum seekers, who 
face persecution in their country of citizenship.94 Others are not “deserving” in this way and have simply 
been Australians in fact if not in law for many years. What each of these residents facing deportation has 
in common is that they are arguably facing a system which is punitive in fact, even if it is not in law. Such 
a system responds to the black and white approach cited above.
Complicating matters further, many long-term residents have Australian citizen family members, who 
must decide whether to remain in Australia or relocate. As Brennan J, in his capacity as the then President 
of the Administrative Appeals Tribunal, said in Pochi v Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs:
[I]t is certain that deportation of the applicant would destroy or gravely damage a growing Australian family.95

Frustration with Tribunal decision-making is understandable when analysed in terms of bureaucratic 
rationality, which emphasises accurate and efficient decision-making. While that tension may be 
“inevitable”, it may be reduced “if each element of our system of government understands and respects 
the role of the other”, 100 thus allowing “some sort of workable decision process”.101 Here, I argue that 
it is more appropriate to view the Tribunal’s decision-making function through the lens of the legal 
model of administrative justice, rather than bureaucratic rationality. Once we have done so, we see 
that adjudicative independence is essential in order for the Tribunal to effectively perform its decision-
making function, namely considering the evidence and the material, balancing the interests and arriving 
at the preferable decision as it sees it. Fleming observed that without adjudicative independence, the 
Tribunal cannot be an “effective check on executive power” in practice and in appearance:102

to ensure that there is no perception (let alone any reality) that tribunals are in any way subject to undue

The limitations of judicial review further heighten the need for adjudicative independence. While 
judicial review is an important means of challenging the decision, the available remedies are limited. 
Thus, the court cannot overturn the visa cancellation under s 501(2) or revoke the visa cancellation under 
s 501CA(4). As a result, nothing prevents the Minister or his or her delegate from making the same 
decision again on remittal.104 Finally, it is unnecessary to criticise the Tribunal for setting aside a primary 
decision, given that the Executive has, at its disposal, various mechanisms to challenge or overturn its 
decision.105

CONCLUSION
There are different models of administrative justice. Accuracy and efficiency are prioritised under 
bureaucratic rationality while fairness is prioritised under the legal model. Primary visa cancellation 
decision-making corresponds principally to the model of bureaucratic rationality, insofar as the law is 
codified with remaining decision-making discretion shaped by the legally binding Ministerial Direction. 
Decision-making is hierarchical and may be personally undertaken by the Minister, thereby eliminating 
the possibility of merits review. In contrast, in the court setting, the legal model of administrative justice 
dominates. Using the example of legal unreasonableness and working in a procedurally constrained 
environment, we see that the court must decide which competing claim prevails when determining 
whether a jurisdictional error has occurred.
Also operating in a procedurally confined environment, the Tribunal must decide what is the correct or 
preferable decision on the evidence and material before it. When the Tribunal is criticised for setting 
aside a primary decision in the visa cancellation context, it highlights the competing values underpinning 
the two different models. In effect, the Tribunal is caught in this “conflict”.106

Thomas recognises that “few, if any, adjudication systems are able to completely satisfy all the demands 
placed upon them”, an observation particularly apt in the Tribunal’s review of visa cancellation cases.107 
This area of decision-making causes great controversy because it raises fundamental questions relating 
to membership of the community, justice and fairness, in an often emotionally charged environment. 
Here, given the nature of its core decision-making task in which competing interests must be balanced, 
I have argued that the legal model dominates and therefore the Tribunal’s adjudicative independence 
must be maintained and respected:
Decisions must be based on facts, evidence and properly delegated discretion. Independence allows for 
sufficient freedom to structure the process consistent with the legislation, fairness and natural justice and 
to deal with the matter placed before the adjudicator.108

Otherwise, the Tribunal is not undertaking the very function it was intended to perform, a function which 
will assume greater importance, as the number of cancellations grows.109
