THE NATURE OF MERITS REVIEW: A BOLD VISION 
REALISED IN THE ADMINISTRATIVE APPEALS TRIBUNAL

Margaret Allars*

ABSTRACT

This article examines the nature of merits review, focusing upon the Administrative 
Appeals Tribunal. Some forms of merits review are less than full merits review and 
some are hybrid merits review. It is contended that a proper appreciation of merits 
review may expose misperceptions concealed in some claims that judicial review 
resembles merits review.

INTRODUCTION

In 2009 the High Court of Australia was presented with its first opportunity to examine 
the nature of the function of the pre-eminent tribunal in Australia: the Administrative 
Appeals Tribunal ('AAT'). By that time the AAT was vested with the most diverse 
jurisdiction and managed the highest volume of litigation of any merits review 
tribunal in Australia. Yet in the 34 years of its operation the occasional appeals which 
found their way to the Court had not raised the question. This is surprising, given the 
uninformative nature of the key statutory provision describing the AAT's function. In 
Shi v Migration Agents Registration Authorihi the Court was content to leave the 
existing conception of the AATs merits review function unscathed. That conception 
had emerged quickly in the late 19705, when the Federal Court outlined its framework 
and the AAT itself forged the substance. Departure from the existing conception of its 
function would undoubtedly have been detrimental to the continued success of the 
AA T. The AAT represents the realisation of a bold vision for a key part of the structure 
of the system of review of administrative decisions in Australia. Yet its merits review 
function deserves closer analYSiS, lest misconceptions as to its nature provide a flawed 
platform for critique of the function of courts in judicial review. 
Merits review in its ordinary and full form is a de novo exercise of administrative 
power. It is readily distinguished from judicial review and from the panoply of other 
types of 'review' or 'appeal' conducted in tribunals and in the appellate jurisdictions of

The key reason why the AAT was not, and never could be, simply an extension of 
the administrative process,61 was that it was created as an independent tribunal with 
its own empowering statute, containing provisions ensuring the independence of its 
members. Nor could it be assumed that an independent tribunal should apply a 
uniform standard of 'good government'. There is a risk that the requirements of good 
government may be equated with the requirements of efficiency and implementation 
of current policy at the expense of individual rights. That risk was not realised. The test 
of application of standards of good government did not prevail as the proper 
description of the function of the AAT. The application of such an external criterion 
was also unsustainable because it was inconsistent with the concept of merits review.

CONCLUSIONS: JUDICIAL REVIEW COMPARED

In Australian Broadcasting Tribunal v Bond,187 Mason CJ referred to a dichotomy 
between the full merits review jurisdiction exercised by the AAT and judicial review. 
The AAT has no role in examining the legal correctness of the decision below,188 in the 
sense of determining whether 'judicial review' grounds are established in relation to 
that decision, such as a denial of procedural fairness, failure to take into account a 
relevant consideration or other principles relating to abuse of power, or estoppel.189 In 
particular, any contention that the original decision-maker denied the applicant 
procedural fairness is misconceived, since such a defect is cured by the AAT itself 
giving a fair hearingl90 It does not follow that the AAT may not decide any legal 
issues, or would not express a conclusion, that the decision under review involved a 
legal error. As Brian Lawlor illustrated,191 the AAT may be required to determine 
whether the decision-maker below acted within power (and hence the ambit of the 
power available to the AAT), or the proper construction of the applicable statutory 
provisions, or the validity of a policy. The AAT itself must ensure that it affords the 
parties procedural fairness and conforms to the principles relating to narrow and 
broad ultra vires. In deciding these legal issues the AAT does not differ from other 
administrative decision-makers. 
The dichotomy applies equally where full merits review jurisdiction is vested in a 
court. Putting aside procedure statutes, State Supreme Courts are vested with an 
inherent judicial review jurisdiction to supervise administrators, tribunals and inferior 
courts by granting prerogative relief, injunctions and declarations. In addition, these 
courts are vested with various kinds of jurisdiction to review or otherwise supervise 
certain administrative decisions. While Chapter III of the Commonwealth Constitution

trespass upon the merits of the administrative decision.204 The court does not seek to 
do over again what the administrator did, by performing afresh the task of evaluating 
the weight to be given to evidence, drawing factual inferences and choosing policy.

making a finding based on no evidence, more obviously than others involve review of 
the draWing of factual inferences. Accepting this, these grounds do not permit the 
court to re-exercise the power to draw factual inferences from the evidence. Applying 
external common law standards, the Court scrutinises the correctness of the decision-
maker's finding or reasoning process for legal error which, if identified, may be

The view that the AAT is to apply an external criterion of the standard of good 
government was rejected in the early days of the AAT. The idea that the AAT stands in 
the shoes of the original decision-maker, and the formula 'correct or preferable 
decision on the material before the Tribunal', are not standards of review to be applied 
by the AAT. There is no basis in case law or practice for assuming that external review 
tribunals must apply their own standards and that these standards must be different 
from the standards applied by the original decision-maker222 If that were the case, full 
merits review could only be vested in internal review decision-makers within the 
particular agency. Before and after Drake (No 1) and Slti there has been no 
constitutional doubt that federal and state legislatures may vest full merits review 
functions in tribunals, and that state legislatures may vest full merits review functions 
in state courts. 
The independence of the AAT from each original decision-maker does not mandate 
that the AAT must apply different standards of review, or that the AAT is part of the 
judicial arm of government.223 Independence in decision-making is not a characteristic 
peculiar to the judicial branch. Independence in the exercise of discretion is a 
fundamental rationale for many of the grounds of judicial review applicable to 
tribunals and other administrators, in particular those which guided the Full Federal 
Court in Drake (No 1) in analysing the nature of the AAT's function. 
The gulf between judicial review and full merits review is underlined by the variety 
of types of review or appeal that fall between an appeal in the strict sense and full 
merits review. The critical feature distinguishing full merits review from an appeal by 
re-hearing in the second or third categories in Fox v Percy, and from hybrid merits 
review, is that in fuII merits review all the powers and discretions of the original 
decision-maker, and remedial powers, are exercised without the Court having a 
function of identifying error in the decision below. 
Merits review was not a bold prospect in the 1970s. The project of building an 
extensive system of nlerits review of federal administrative decisions was. That project 
has been executed, with power to engage in full merits review characterisiog all but 
minor areas of the extensive jurisdiction conferred upon the AAT. Analysis of the 
nature of merits review makes conspicuous the discrepancy between such review and 
even the boldest instances of judicial review.
