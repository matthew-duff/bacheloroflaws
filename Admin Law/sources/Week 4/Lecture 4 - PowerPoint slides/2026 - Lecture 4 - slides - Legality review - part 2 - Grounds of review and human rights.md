# Slide 4

Unreasonableness

4

The codified ground of review:
“… an exercise of a power that is so unreasonable that no reasonable person could have so exercised the power”
The unreasonableness ground of legality review has prompted concerns in Australia of the risk, if this ground is not limited, that the courts might rely on the ground when they simply disagree with the decision of the decision-maker whose decision is being reviewed and engage in merits review.  The ground has therefore generally been limited to cases of serious irrationality, illogicality or capriciousness
Statutory interpretation is again important, and the unreasonableness ground also overlaps with other grounds
A starting point for discussion of the unreasonableness ground of review is the decision of Lord Greene MR in Associated Provincial Picture Houses Ltd v Wednesbury Corporation [1948] 1 KB 223
The most significant High Court decision addressing the ground has been Minister for Immigration and Citizenship v Li (2013) 249 CLR 332.  The High Court recognised that unreasonableness might relate to the reasoning process by which the decision was made or might relate to the outcome before the decision-maker that “lacks an evident or intelligible justification”.  See also ABT17 v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection (2020) 269 CLR 439, [19], per Kiefel CJ, Bell, Gageler and Keane JJ
There has been ongoing debate regarding possible linkages between the unreasonableness ground of review and proportionality analyses

Jurisdictional error and ouster (privative) clauses

34

The codified ground of review:
“… that the person who purported to make the decision did not have jurisdiction to make the decision”
Note the historical division of the grounds of legality review into:
“ultra vires grounds” (applicable to local authorities and executive government officials) and 
“jurisdictional error grounds” (applicable to courts and tribunals)
Contrast want of jurisdiction (including absences of jurisdictional facts), excess of jurisdiction (for example, denials of procedural fairness) and failures to exercise jurisdiction
Jurisdictional error grounds of review are generally broader for tribunals than they are for courts [ie a narrower (and more serious) range of errors of law are normally required to deprive a court of its jurisdiction]
Jurisdictional errors involve different consequences for courts and tribunals
Remember the constitutional dimension – section 75(v) of the Constitution:
“In all matters: …
(v) in which a writ of Mandamus or prohibition or an injunction is sought against an officer of the Commonwealth;
the High Court shall have original jurisdiction.”
Judicial review on grounds of jurisdictional error is constitutionally entrenched which has implications for efforts by Commonwealth, State and Territory parliaments using ouster (privative) clauses to remove or limit the power of the courts to engage in legality review

# Slide 45

Jurisdictional error at common law

45

Materiality

LPDT v Minister for Immigration, Citizenship, Migrant Services and Multicultural Affairs (2024) 280 CLR 321 at [2]-[16], per Gageler CJ, Gordon, Edelman, Steward, Gleeson and Jagot JJ:

[Potentially reflecting the required gravity of a jurisdictional error, and the possibility that the error in some sense may have caused the decision, the High Court has also emphasised that error, to be jurisdictional, must have been “material” to the decision

“Jurisdictional error can refer to breach of an express or implied condition of a statutory conferral of decision-making authority which results in a decision made in the purported exercise of that authority lacking the legal force attributed to exercise of that authority by statute. … In most cases … an error will only be jurisdictional if the error was material to the decision that was made in fact, … in the sense that there is a realistic possibility that the decision that was made in fact could have been different if the error had not occurred. … That is because it is now accepted that a statute which contains an express or implied condition to be observed in a decision-making process is ordinarily to be interpreted as incorporating such a ‘threshold of materiality’ in the event of non-compliance”]

The consequences of jurisdictional error

49

The consequences of jurisdictional error are often different for a court committing a jurisdictional error compared to a tribunal committing such an error
