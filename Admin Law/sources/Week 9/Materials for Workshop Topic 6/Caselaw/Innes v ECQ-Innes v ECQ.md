Queensland’s Local Government Elections were held on 28 March 2020 during the COVID-19
pandemic, declared by the World Health Organisation on 10 March 2020. On 13 March 2020,
the Commonwealth Government announced restrictions on gatherings of more than 500 people
and, the same day, the Queensland Electoral Commission (“Electoral Commission”) issued a
media release that the elections were proceeding.

On 19 March 2020, the Chief Health Officer of Queensland, in accordance with emergency
powers arising from the declared public health emergency, made a direction under s 362B
Public Health Act 2005 prohibiting non-essential indoor gatherings of 100 persons or more.
The same day, assent was given to the Public Health and Other Legislation (Public Health
Emergency) Amendment Act 2020, which inserted a new Pt 9A into the Local Government
Electoral Act 2011. Section 200A set out the purpose of that part, namely “to facilitate the
holding of the quadrennial election for 2020 in a timely way that minimises serious risks to the
health and safety of persons caused by the public health emergency involving COVID-19” and
which, inter alia, amended the Local Government Electoral Act 2011 to confer upon the
Minister (not the Electoral Commission) the power to conduct the local government elections
by postal ballot: s 200E. That power was not exercised. Certain directives (more fully set out at
[85]) were made on various dates.

The local government elections proceeded on 28 March 2020 and in-person voting was
allowed, but visitor elector voting was “cancelled” on 15 March 2020 and the visitor elector
applications were converted (automatically) into applications for a postal vote. Other forms of
voting, including postal voting and telephone voting, were permitted, but the cut-off date for
postal voting was not extended.

The applicant stood for election as mayor of the Sunshine Coast Regional Council. He was
unsuccessful. He applied to the Court of Disputed Returns under s 136 Local Government
Electoral Act 2011 disputing the election and seeking orders to the effect that the election result
be quashed. At the heart of the application was the contention that the election ought to have
been postponed because of the health risk to voters of COVID-19. The applicant contended that
the decision to hold the election (or not to postpone it), having regard to prevailing health
conditions, entailed a breach of voters’ human rights. The Attorney-General intervened on that
issue.

The applicant had also sought documents relating generally to the conduct of the whole of the
local government elections from the Electoral Commission contending he was entitled to these
documents under s 140 Local Government Electoral Act 2011 which he contended ought be
interpreted this way because of s 48 Human Rights Act 2019, having regard to the human right
in s 23 Human Rights Act 2019 to take part in public life.

The applicant relied on various grounds. He claimed that the elections were conducted unfairly
because of the decision to proceed with voting in-person and not to either postpone the
elections or to proceed by way of complete postal voting. He claimed it was not “essential” to
require persons to attend a polling booth to vote in-person, and to require them to do so
amounted to “a fundamental breach of Human Rights NOT to be exposed to ‘risk of harm’”.
The alleged unfairness was asserted to result from the applicant’s “responsible approach to
campaigning”, insofar as the applicant discouraged voters from voting in the prevailing health
crisis, to his own disadvantage.

Part 2 is entitled “Human Rights in Queensland”. It contains the 23 human rights protected
under the Act. One of these is in s 23. Division 3, Pt 3 is entitled “Interpretation of laws”. It
includes s 48, which states, in s 48(1), that all statutory provisions are to be interpreted in a way
that is compatible with human rights “to the extent possible that is consistent with their
purpose”. Sections 58 and 59 place obligations on public entities both as to the giving of
“proper consideration to a human right relevant to a decision” and to ensure that the public
entity does not “act or make a decision in a way that is not compatible with human rights”: see
[259]. Section 8 defines what is “compatible with human rights” and s 13(1) provides for the
limiting of human rights “only to reasonable limits that can be demonstrably justified in a free
and democratic society based on human dignity, equality and freedom” and in s 13(2) lists
seven factors that “may be relevant” to determining this: see [287].

Held, dismissing the application and making orders accordingly:

(1)
That as there was nothing ambiguous about s 136 Local Government Electoral Act
2011, there was no scope for s 48 Human Rights Act 2019 to affect its interpretation.
The documents referred to in s 140 Local Government Electoral Act 2011 related only
to the mayoral election that was the subject of challenge and not all of the local
government elections held that day. [247]–[249], [255]–[256].

Momcilovic v The Queen (2011) 245 CLR 1 applied.

State of Queensland (through the Department of Housing and Public Works) v Tenant
[2020] QCAT 144, [176]–[187] considered.
(2)
That:
(a)
s 136 Local Government Electoral Act 2011 provides that the election of a person
during a local government election may be disputed by an application to the Court
of Disputed Returns under Pt 7. The jurisdiction of the Court of Disputed Returns
is thus confined by the Local Government Electoral Act 2011 to jurisdiction over
disputes about the election of a person. The Court does not have jurisdiction to
consider complaints about elections generally: including, for example, a complaint
that a certain electoral district should have conducted a full postal ballot or a
complaint that the 2020 local government elections should have been held at all;
[13]–[14];

(b)
s 136 Local Government Electoral Act 2011 limits the disputes over which the
Court of Disputed Returns has jurisdiction to disputes about the election of a
person. Section 136(2) is a limiting, not an empowering or permissive, provision.
[255].

(3)
That an allegation of “unfairness” per se does not provide a basis for a successful
application to the Court of Disputed Returns, but if unfairness is the product of a breach

(a)
under s 5(2)(a), to the extent that the Court of Disputed Returns has functions

under ss 58 and 59, to the extent that the applicant has an existing right to claim

The jurisdiction of the Court of Disputed Returns and the onus of proof

Section 136 LGEA provides that the election of a person during a local
government election may be disputed by an application to the Court of Disputed
Returns under Pt 7 LGEA.

The jurisdiction of the Court of Disputed Returns is thus confined by the LGEA to
jurisdiction over disputes about the election of a person. The Court does not have
jurisdiction to consider complaints about elections generally – including, for
example, a complaint that a certain electoral district should have conducted a full
postal ballot or a complaint that the 2020 local government elections should have
been held at all.

In Caltabiano v Electoral Commission of Queensland (No 2) [2010] 2 Qd R 1
(“Caltabiano”), Atkinson J heard an application to the Court of Disputed Returns
by an unsuccessful candidate in the 2009 Queensland State election.

Because the application concerned the results of a State – rather than a local
government – election, the application was governed by the Electoral Act 1992 as
it stood in 2009 (“EA 2009”). The provisions of the EA 2009 dealing with the
Court of Disputed Returns are equivalent to the provisions of the LGEA which
apply in this case. Thus, her Honour’s discussion in Caltabiano [2010] 2 Qd
R 1, 40–42 [180]–[187] of the role of the Court, the onus of proof and related
matters, are apposite here.

adapted for the provisions of the LGEA (footnotes omitted):

Approach to this application

Having regard to the above, I have taken the following approach to this
application.

Bearing in mind the onus of proof (see below):

•
I will assess the “validity” of the SCRC election in light of the statutory
requirements of the LGEA. In other words, I will consider whether there
have been any breaches of the statutory requirements of the LGEA;

•
If there is satisfactory proof of a breach, then I will consider its effect on
the election result and whether there are good grounds for believing that
Mr Jamieson’s election does not reflect the preference of a majority of
electors, voting by way of a free and deliberate choice;

•
Even if I am satisfied that there has been a breach of any of the statutory
requirements of the LGEA, if the quantitative effect of the breach is
small (relative to Mr Jamieson’s winning margin), then the fact that there
has been a breach is not enough to require the setting aside of the election
result;

As to the onus of proof: as explained by Atkinson J in Caltabiano, it is borne by
the applicant. Her Honour said, “[t]he election result is presumed to be valid
unless the Court is persuaded to the contrary by proof of the facts set out in the
application” (emphasis added).2

It is therefore for Mr Innes to persuade me (on the basis of evidence) of the matters
he alleges in his application. And, if Mr Innes is able to satisfy me that there has
been a breach of a statutory requirement or corruption etc, it is for Mr Innes to
persuade me that the effect of the breach or corruption etc is that the result of the
SCRC election does not represent the free choice of a majority of electors.

It is not the case that the ECQ and Mr Jamieson have to defend themselves by
producing evidence to counter Mr Innes’ accusations.

That is not to say that the ECQ and Mr Jamieson need not tender evidence. Both
could have done so. And, in the case of the ECQ, it did. The point is that it is for
Mr Innes to persuade me, on the strength of evidence (which may include the
evidence produced by the ECQ), that (for example) the ECQ conducted the
election unlawfully. It is not for the ECQ to disprove that allegation and to prove
to me that it conducted the election lawfully.

Nor is it the role of this Court to conduct an investigation into the conduct of the
SCRC election to determine whether it was “fair” or whether Mr Jamieson’s
nomination or election was valid. It is for the parties to produce evidence to the
Court: not for the Court to go in search of it. And it is for the Court to decide on the
evidence placed before it whether Mr Innes’ application ought to be granted.

As to the standard of proof: to succeed, Mr Innes must satisfy me of his position,
on the basis of evidence, on the balance of probabilities.

Declared institutions

Under ss 46(3) and 49 LGEA, all or part of an institution or place may be declared
a mobile polling booth which may be used for voting. Typically, aged care
facilities and hospitals are so declared to facilitate voting within those institutions.

In the lead up to the 2020 SCRC election, 46 institutions were declared to be
“mobile polling booths”. These institutions consisted of 4,536 beds. I have
proceeded on the basis that every bed was occupied and there were 4,536 declared
institution residents.

On 13 March 2020, by media release (see below), the ECQ informed the public
that it would be “offering telephone voting to all electors in ‘declared institutions’
such as aged care facilities, rather than conducting in-person mobile polling”.

Mr Huey suggested that this media release also stated that visitor voting would not
be made available. I do not read the media release in that way. However, it plainly
“cancelled” in-person mobile voting at declared institutions.

Mr Huey acknowledged that, once it was decided that in-person mobile voting at
declared institutions would be cancelled, electors in those institutions had “only a
short window of a day or two” to apply for a postal vote (online or via the ECQ’s
call centre).

In 2016, 1,177 electors in declared institutions voted. (There was no evidence
before me about whether those electors were in the same declared institutions as
those declared for 2020 or what that number represented as a proportion of beds.)
Assuming every bed was occupied by an elector, and that there were the same
number of beds in 2016 as there were in 2020, 1,177 voters represent
approximately 26 per cent of electors in declared institutions.

Mr Huey conducted a study of a sample of three of the 46 declared institutions for
the 2020 SCRC election. He found that only 10 per cent of those enrolled in those
three declared institutions (about 200 electors were enrolled) succeeded in voting
using the alternative postal and telephone options. Mr Huey observed that not all
persons resident in declared institutions do vote, essentially because of their age.

Electoral visitor voting

In the lead up to the 2020 SCRC election, four persons successfully applied to be
visitor electors under s 77 LGEA. Normally, issuing officers visit visitor electors
prior to polling day to assist them to vote. However, visitor elector voting was
“cancelled” on 15 March 2020 and the visitor elector applications were converted
(automatically) into applications for a postal vote.

The impact of COVID-19

Conduct alleged to be unlawful under the HR Act

Section 58 creates two obligations. The first is to act in a way that is compatible
with human rights; that is, the substantive limb (s 58(1)(a)). The second is to give
proper consideration to human rights when making a decision; that is, the
procedural limb (s 58(1)(b)).

Upon a generous reading of Mr Innes’ submissions, he alleges that the ECQ
engaged in conduct which was unlawful in a substantive and procedural sense.

The substantive allegations appear to be that the ECQ acted unlawfully by:

•

•

•

•

The Attorney-General infers from this that Mr Innes is making an allegation of

Procedural HR unlawfulness

I will deal briefly with the alleged procedural unlawfulness now.

The procedural limb of s 58 was considered in PJB v Melbourne Health (2011) 39
VR 373, 442 [311] in which Bell J stated (footnotes omitted):

“The so-called ‘procedural limb’ of s 38(1) that ‘proper consideration’ be
given to relevant human rights requires public authorities to do so in a
practical and common-sense manner. As Emerton J said in Castles v
Secretary of Department of Justice, there is ‘no formula’ and the authority
must ‘seriously turn his or her mind’ to the human rights impact of what is
proposed and identify ‘the countervailing interests or obligations’. That can
be done in a variety of ways which may be suited to particulars [sic]
circumstances. Decisions-makers [sic] are not expected to approach the
application of human rights like a judge ‘with textbooks on human rights at
their elbows’, said Lord Hoffmann in R (SB) v Denbigh High School.”

Mr Innes’ complaint that the ECQ did not seek advice from the Human Rights
Commission does not advance an argument under s 58(1)(b). The HR Act does not
cast upon an entity like the ECQ an obligation to consult with, or seek advice
from, the Human Rights Commission or Commissioner in their decision-making.
And indeed the functions of the Human Rights Commission do not include
engaging with an agency during its decision-making.

The operation of ss 58 and 59 HR Act

Sections 58 and 59 are commonly referred to as the “piggyback” provisions.

A person who claims that a public entity has breached s 58 can only bring legal
proceedings to vindicate that claim by attaching it to an independent ground of
unlawfulness (s 59(1)). In other words, a contravention of s 58 does not of itself
give rise to any claim to relief or remedy. To achieve relief or remedy, the person
alleging a breach of s 58 must also have a claim arising independently of the HR
Act upon which the s 58 claim might “piggyback”.

Maxwell P analysed the operation of the analogous provisions of the Victorian
Charter (ss 38 and 39) in Director of Housing v Sudi (2011) 33 VR 559 (“Sudi”).

That matter concerned the powers of the Victorian Civil and Administrative
Tribunal (“VCAT”) in the exercise of its original jurisdiction. The applicant, the
Director of Housing, applied to VCAT under the Residential Tenancies Act 1997
(Vic) (“RTA”) for an order for possession of premises occupied by the respondent,
Mr Sudi, and his son.

Bell J, sitting as the President of VCAT at first instance, concluded that VCAT had
power to review the lawfulness of the director’s decision to make the application
to the tribunal. His Honour decided that the Director of Housing’s decision was
unlawful under s 38(1) of the Charter and that the purported application was
therefore a nullity. This amounted to a collateral review of the Director’s decision.

On appeal, Maxwell P had to decide whether VCAT had the power to conduct
such a collateral review. Relevantly, one of the questions for his Honour was
whether the provisions of the Charter (specifically, s 38) evinced a legislative
intention to confer on VCAT a power of collateral review which the RTA itself did
not confer.

In answering this question in the negative, Maxwell P said, (2011) 33 VR 559,
579–580 [94]–[96]:

“Under s 38(1) of the Charter, it is unlawful for a public authority to act ‘in
a way that is incompatible with a human right’. As noted earlier, the
conclusion of the tribunal in the present case was that the director (who is
undoubtedly a public authority for this purpose) had ‘acted unlawfully’
under s 38(1) in seeking to evict Mr Sudi and his son, and in applying for
the possession order.

‘If, otherwise than because of this Charter, a person may seek any relief or
remedy in respect of an act or decision of a public authority on the ground

I find that s 58(2) applies to the decision to conduct the election including by way
of in-person voting. The ECQ had to apply the law. It did not have the option of
conducting the election by postal ballot because the SCRC had not applied for
such a ballot under s 45AA LGEA. Nor had the Minister directed a postal ballot
under s 200E LGEA. Thus, the election had to be conducted in a way that allowed
ordinary votes under ss 67 and 68 LGEA.

The other three acts or decisions were not mandated by law. The next step then is
to consider the limits they placed on human rights.

I proceed on the basis that an act or decision will “limit” a human right if it places
limitations or restrictions on, or interferes with, the human rights of a person.

I proceed on the basis that it is not necessary for an identifiable individual to be
affected in order for a human right to be engaged so as to trigger the obligations
imposed on public entities by s 58(1). A potential effect on the rights of a class of
persons is sufficient.

Implicit in the applicant’s arguments is a suggestion that the cancellation of
mobile polling booth voting and visitor elector voting and the issues around postal
and telephone voting limited persons’ rights to take part in public life. The right to
take part in public life is, as the Attorney-General submitted, key to building and
sustaining a robust democracy.

I am prepared to proceed on the basis that the right of some persons to participate
in public life might have been affected or limited by the decision to cancel mobile
polling booths and elector visitor voting (although I do not, on the evidence, find
that as a fact).

I am also prepared to proceed on the basis that the right of some persons to
participate in public life might have been affected or limited by issues around
postal and telephone voting (although I do not, on the evidence, find that as a fact).

I now turn to s 13 considerations. Plainly, the decision to cancel mobile polling
booth voting and visitor elector voting was intended to protect the physical, and
perhaps mental, health of voters and ECQ staff during the pandemic (s 13(2)(b)).
Any consequential limit on human rights was, in my view, reasonable and
justifiable and tempered by the availability of other options.

In my view, the decision to cancel promoted an important proper purpose
(s 13(2)(e)) and the limit went no further than necessary, especially given that
alternative voting options were provided. Indeed, at least arguably, not to cancel
and to force voters and ECQ staff into close proximity would have impinged upon
human dignity.

As to issues with postal votes, I infer that any limit was unintentional. It is
unreasonable to expect the ECQ to have used any other mode of delivery of postal
ballots than the mode adopted (that is, via Australian Post) (see s 13(2)(d)). And
any resulting limit was, in my view, reasonable in the circumstances (s 13(1)).

Other matters

The CHO and the SCRC are public entities under s 9 HR Act. I did not find that
either acted unlawfully.

However, Mr Innes’ allegations against the CHO, taken at their highest, allege a
breach of the human right to take part in public life by not “guarantee[ing] the free
expression of the will of the electors” (in encouraging voters not to tarry at polling
booths). As I have mentioned, in my view, such an allegation is based on a wholly
unreasonable interpretation of the statements made by the CHO about voting. On
no reasonable view of her statements could it be said that human rights were
limited.

As to Mr Innes’ claims against Mr Jamieson, for the most part, they concern his
conduct in a private capacity, as a mayoral candidate. His conduct in his private
capacity is not caught by s 58 or s 59 (see s 58(4)).

Even if some of Mr Innes’ complaints about Mr Jamieson might be more
accurately characterised as complaints about his conduct as Mayor (and therefore
within the piggybacking provisions – see s 9(1)(d) HR Act)), as above, in my
view, Mr Innes’ complaints about Mr Jamieson were unfounded. It cannot be said
that his conduct limited human rights.

I have not overlooked that Mr Innes, and the respondents, are entitled to the right
to a fair trial according to law, under s 31 HR Act and independently of it.

In this matter, I have borne in mind the duty the Court owes to Mr Innes as a self-
represented litigant. Consistently with my appreciation of that duty, I took a
generous approach to his arguments so as to ensure his access to justice.

I note for completeness the very recent decision of Attorney-General v Sri [2020]
QSC 246, in which Applegarth J considered that the right to public assembly
under the HR Act was relevant to his decision whether to grant an injunction to
restrain an unlawful protest. My approach in this matter to HR Act issues was
guided by his Honour’s.
