Chapter 5
Part 3 — Application of Human Rights 
in Queensland

Introduction

5.1  The various divisions of pt 3 of the Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld) (‘HRA’) 
set out the role and obligations of the Queensland Parliament, the courts and the 
executive in respect of the application of enumerated human rights. Part 3 is a 
critical aspect of the regulatory ‘dialogue’ model that the Queensland Government 
believed represents a ‘good middle ground’ for human rights protection, under 
which ‘each of the three arms of government — the executive, the legislature 
(parliament) and the courts — have a legitimate role to play, while the parliament 
maintains sovereignty’.1 Under this dialogue model, the three branches of 
government have separate and complementary roles in protecting and promoting 
human rights.2 Although use of the term ‘dialogue’ has attracted criticism 
from members of the High Court of Australia in the case of Momcilovic v The 
Queen (‘Momcilovic’),3 the metaphor is broadly accepted as a description of the 
interactions between all three branches of government under a statutory model of 
human rights protection. In short, the Legislative Assembly communicates with 
the courts through the legislation it passes and (infrequently, it is imagined) by 
use of the override declaration. The courts speak to the Assembly by interpreting 
legislation compatibly with human rights; and, when that interpretation is not 
feasible, the Supreme Court makes a declaration of incompatibility, putting the 
executive and Parliament on notice about offending provisions. The HRA is not

5.2  The dialogue model is intended to guarantee that particular human 
rights are considered in all areas of government. The model may be regarded as 
strengthening the democratic process by prompting feedback to the executive 
and Parliament about human rights in law-making and administration, and as 
providing for human rights–based checks on policy development, law reform 
and administrative decision-making by public entities (or authorities). Legislative 
sovereignty rests with the Legislative Assembly because the courts cannot force the 
Parliament to adopt a particular position on a human rights issue. Additionally, in 
limited circumstances the Parliament can enact legislation that expressly overrides 
human rights.

5.3  Part 3 of the HRA is based on, and broadly consistent with, the model of 
human rights legislation adopted by Victoria via its Charter of Human Rights 
and Responsibilities Act 2006 (Vic) (‘Charter’).4 Indeed, the HRA resembles the 
Charter in its structure and, seemingly, in certain operative respects too. The 
HRA’s drafting is rightly viewed by some commentators as better, based as it is 
on years of comparative experience and jurisprudence in Victoria, the Australian 
Capital Territory and elsewhere.5 Part 3 of the HRA speaks to how government 
accountability for human rights protection is to be furthered through a framework 
of oversight mechanisms, within and across government institutions, and via 
distinctive substantive and procedural obligations on public entities that augment 
traditional administrative law obligations. Yet pt 3 also reveals a degree of political 
ambivalence and caginess about human rights protection, insofar as there is no 
legislative commitment to a timetable to promote effective committee scrutiny 
of Bills, and there are no legal consequences for a failure to comply with the key 
requirements in this part.

Division 1 — Scrutiny of new legislation

5.4  Part 3 div 1 of the HRA provides for parliamentary mechanisms for human 
rights-based scrutiny of draft legislation. These provisions speak to the central role

38 Statements of compatibility

(1) A member who proposes to introduce a Bill in the Legislative Assembly must prepare
a statement of compatibility for the Bill.
(2) The statement of compatibility must state —
(a) whether, in the member’s opinion, the Bill is compatible with human rights and,
if so, how it is compatible; and
(b) if, in the member’s opinion, a part of the Bill is not compatible with human rights,
the nature and extent of the incompatibility.
Note — 
Subsections  (1) and (2) apply to Ministers introducing government Bills and 
members introducing private members’ Bills. 
(3) A member who introduces a Bill in the Legislative Assembly, or another member
acting on the member’s behalf, must table the statement of compatibility prepared
under this section when introducing the Bill.
(4) The statement of compatibility is not binding on any court or tribunal.

Definitions

5.5  Schedule 1 (Dictionary) of the HRA defines member for pt 3 as ‘a member of 
the Legislative Assembly’; and human rights with reference to s 7.

5.6  Schedule  1 also defines compatible with human rights with reference to 
s 8, which in s 8(b) refers to the general limitation provision in s 13. The term 
‘compatible with human rights’ is a unifying concept used throughout the HRA: 
it is central to many key provisions and consistently applicable to the actions of 
the executive, legislative and judicial branches of the State of Queensland.6 This 
single definition is intended to provide clear guidance to everyone involved with 
interpretating legislation and to overcome interpretational difficulties associated 
with the Victorian Charter.7 Section 13 sets out the general character of constraints 
on the Queensland Parliament’s commitment to the protection and promotion 
of human rights enumerated in the HRA. For detailed discussion of ss 8 and 13, 
see Chapters 2–4 at 2.22ff, 3.87ff and 4.##ff.

Scope of the provision

5.7  Section  38 of the HRA is an important operative provision that is one 
component of the ‘dialogue model’ of human rights protection that the Act aims 
to promote (see  5.1–5.2 above). This section  entails pre-legislative assessment 
and certification by a Minister or other parliamentarian that new legislation is 
(or is not) compatible with human rights, supported by justifications. This is a

5.20  First, s 14A(1) of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) (‘QAIA’) requires a 
purposive construction of all Queensland statutes. As the High Court explained in 
Lacey v Attorney-General (Qld), the application of the rules of construction ‘involve 
the identification of a statutory purpose, which may appear from an express 
statement in the relevant statute, by inference from its terms and by appropriate 
reference to extrinsic materials’.26 A statement of compatibility for a particular 
statute is one of the extrinsic materials that may assist in the identification of its 
statutory purpose.

5.21  Second, s 14B(1) of the QAIA provides that courts may have regard to 
extrinsic materials capable of assisting in interpretation: (a)  to provide an 
interpretation of an ambiguous or obscure provision; (b) to avoid an interpretation 
that is manifestly absurd or unreasonable where the ordinary meaning of the 
provision leads to that result; or (c) to confirm the interpretation conveyed by the 
ordinary meaning of the provision.

5.22  Extrinsic material includes any ‘relevant document, that was laid before, 
or given to the members of, the Legislative Assembly by the member bringing 
in the Bill before the provision was enacted’.27 Statements of compatibility, 
being documents that are given to the Legislative Assembly before enactment 
of a Bill, classify as extrinsic material and so may be referred to as an aid for 
statutory interpretation under the statutory rules of construction in s 14A and 
s 14B.28 In short, statutory interpretation may be conducted against the backdrop 
of a legislative process typically concerned and directed to ensuring a statute’s 
compatibility with human rights, including any rights limitations being justified 
and proportionate.

Related provisions

5.23  Section 38 of the HRA applies to proposed primary legislation. Additionally, 
s 41 of the HRA imposes an obligation on the executive branch to prepare a ‘human 
rights certificate’ in relation to the tabling of subordinate legislation in Parliament. 
The obligation under s 41(2) is consistent with the statement of compatibility 
requirements under s 38(2) and facilitates rights-based scrutiny of subordinate 
legislation.

The portfolio committee responsible for examining a Bill introduced in the Legislative

(a) consider the Bill and report to the Assembly about whether the Bill is not compatible
with human rights; and
(b) consider the statement of compatibility tabled for the Bill and report to the Assembly

Definitions

5.25  Schedule 1 (Dictionary) of the HRA defines portfolio committee as a portfolio 
committee established under s 88 of the Parliament of Queensland Act 2001 (Qld); 
human rights with reference to s 7 of the HRA; and compatible with human rights 
with reference to s 8 of the HRA.

Scope of the provision

5.26  Section 39 of the HRA is an important feature of the framework for human 
rights–based scrutiny of Bills. It is a mechanism that alters legislative practices 
by providing for mandatory scrutiny of proposed legislation and statements of 
compatibility by parliamentary portfolio committees. This section  serves to 
reinforce the notion that Parliament, and not the courts, has primary responsibility 
for the protection of human rights in Queensland.

5.27  In Queensland there is no stand-alone human rights committee established 
with the responsibility for examining and assessing the human rights implications 
of draft legislation before Parliament.29 Rather s 39 (in conjunction with s 40 and 
s 57) places responsibilities upon a ‘portfolio committee’ established under s 88 
of the Parliament of Queensland Act 2001. Arguably, portfolio committees will 
have relatively greater expertise on the policy area of legislation for which they are 
responsible, compared to a specialist parliamentary committee with responsibility 
only for human rights–based legislative scrutiny.

5.31  The Victorian experience suggests that robust and influential human rights

5.32  To be fully effective, portfolio committees must be afforded sufficient time 
to fulfil their mandate under s 39. However, the HRA is silent on the provision of 
a timetable for portfolio committee scrutiny that would aid balanced and robust 
parliamentary scrutiny. This is, arguably, a weakness in the legislative scheme, and 
one that may lead to Bills being moved through the Legislative Assembly too 
rapidly, without human rights issues being adequately identified, understood or 
debated.37 The Queensland Department of Justice and Attorney-General (‘DJAG’) 
only partly responded to this concern prior to the enactment of the HRA, stating 
that the committees would be allowed adequate opportunity to consider potential 
human rights implications of a Bill as ‘most bills introduced into the Legislative

the bill within a specified period of time (between six weeks and six months)’.38

5.33  The absence of a legislative provision in the HRA (or related reform to 
parliamentary procedures) delaying the passage of a Bill until there has been 
adequate time for both public input and effective committee scrutiny, prior to 
parliamentary debate, may be regarded as a weakness with the committee’s oversight 
and accountability role. Indeed, the Queensland Human Rights Commission

5.102  Section 49(2) stipulates two requirements that must both be satisfied in 
order for a referral to be made to the Supreme Court: (a) a party to the proceeding 
has made an application for referral; and (b)  the (original) court or tribunal 
considers the question is appropriate to be decided by the Supreme Court. That 
is, a court or tribunal cannot refer a proceeding of its own motion; it can do so 
only if both conditions in s 49(2) are met. Additionally, there is no right to obtain 
a referral of a question of law. Even if both the conditions are satisfied, s 49(2) 
confers an overriding and broad discretion, rather than the imposition of a duty, 
on the court or tribunal as to whether to refer a question of the type expressly 
mentioned in s 49(1).

5.103  The referral provision in the HRA, like its Victorian counterpart, contains 
no specific criteria to be considered when determining how to exercise the statutory 
discretion. In considering the comparable provision in s 33 of the Charter, the 
Victorian Court of Appeal stated that the referral power is unfettered, although 
‘like all judicial discretions, it is to be exercised in light of the relevant facts and 
circumstances, including such considerations as may be contained in and drawn 
from the Charter’.123 In deciding whether to exercise the discretion to refer, a court 
or tribunal should consider the nature and scope of the question; the circumstances 
in which the question arose; the difficulty of the question; its importance to the 
parties or the public; a conflict of authorities; or that the parties have stated they 
will appeal whatever the decision at first instance.124
