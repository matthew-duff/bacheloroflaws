The constitutional context of administrative law:The separation of powers doctrine

32

Three branches of government at Federal, State and Territory level and three types of power
Judicial warnings regarding attempts to define powers exhaustively (consider, for example, the so-called “borderland” principle or “chameleon” doctrine – Creyke et al, 66-67)
Legislation and administration contrasted:
Latham CJ in The Commonwealth v Grunseit (1943) 67 CLR 58 at 82-3:
“The distinction between legislation and the execution of legislation is that legislation determines the content of the law as a rule of conduct or a declaration as to power, right or duty, whereas executive authority applies the law in particular cases.”
Legislative power involves the making of general rules that are binding on the community or sections of the community (such as a particular industry).
Administrative power often involves the application of general rules (for example legislation) in particular circumstances, for example in respect of particular persons or corporations (rather than to the community generally).  When the Executive branch creates high level or low level (operational) policies this is also treated by the courts as an exercise of administrative power.
Judicial powers or functions:
“… [T]here are some functions that are inherently or exclusively judicial.  Examples are the power to sentence a person to a term of imprisonment, to impose a monetary fine, to issue a coercive order such as an injunction, or to make a binding and conclusive declaration of the law” – Creyke et al, 66

The constitutional context of administrative law:The separation of powers doctrine

36

The Commonwealth Constitution has been interpreted as requiring a strict separation of executive and judicial powers:
Section 1: “The legislative power of the Commonwealth shall be vested in a Federal Parliament …”.
Section 61: “The executive power of the Commonwealth is vested in the Queen and is exercisable by the Governor-General as the Queen's representative, and extends to the execution and maintenance of this Constitution, and of the laws of the Commonwealth.”
Section 71: “The judicial power of the Commonwealth shall be vested in a Federal Supreme Court, to be called the High Court of Australia, and in such other federal courts as the Parliament creates …”.
The High Court has, however, permitted the Federal Parliament to delegate legislative power (through the making of subordinate legislation) to the Executive branch – see Victorian Stevedoring and General Contracting Co Pty Ltd v Dignan (1931) 46 CLR 73 [extracted in Creyke et al, 55-56]
Judicial review of the legality of subordinate legislation is an important part of administrative law

# Slide 37

The constitutional context of administrative law:The complexity of the separation of powers doctrine in Australia

37

The separation of powers doctrine at the Federal level has been held to restrict the conferral of judicial powers on the Federal Executive branch and to restrict the conferral of non-judicial powers on Federal Courts

This same strict approach to the separation of powers does not apply to State and Territory legal systems.  State and Territory courts can therefore exercise, in certain circumstances, executive powers; and State and Territory tribunals can therefore exercise, in certain circumstances, judicial powers

The strict approach to separation of executive and judicial powers at the Federal level provides the basis for an extremely important distinction in administrative law between “merits review” and “legality review”

# Slide 40

The separation of powers doctrine in AustraliaMerits Review and Legality Review – at the Federal level

40

Merits review (carried out by Federal tribunals that form part of the Executive branch of the Federal government)
The most significant of these Federal tribunals is the Administrative Review Tribunal (which replaced the Administrative Appeals Tribunal in 2024)
Merits review generally entails reviewing the merits of Executive decision-making (eg whether someone should receive a payment or a licence from the government) often with the power for the tribunal to substitute what it considers to be the correct or preferable decision (on the merits) without having to find any error in the Executive decision being appealed
Merits review often involves the consideration of policies adopted by the Executive branch and sometimes involves exercising broadly conferred executive discretions
Legality review (carried out by the High Court, the Federal Court, and the Federal Circuit and Family Court) 
The High Court has original jurisdiction to undertake legality review under s75(v) of the Constitution of the actions of officers of the Commonwealth.  The Federal Court has statutory jurisdiction to undertake legality review of Federal Executive decisions
Legality review focuses on the lawfulness of Executive branch decision-making through the application of grounds of judicial review that initially developed at common law but are now also set out in legislation
A court engaging in legality review that finds that an Executive branch decision is unlawful will normally refer the matter back to the Executive decision-maker to decide according to law.  The court undertaking legality review does not substitute its views on the merits of the original Executive branch decision for that of the original Executive branch decision-maker

# Slide 47

Merits Review by State and Territory Tribunals and Courts of State and Territory Executive branch decisions

The separation of powers doctrine is not as strictly applied in the States and Territories.  For example, the Queensland Civil and Administrative Tribunal (QCAT) undertakes merits review but also exercises judicial power in relation to certain forms of legal disputes.  Despite its name, QCAT has been held to be a court – see Owen v Menzies [2013] 2 QdR 327, [7]-[20], [43]-[52] and [103].  On the separation of powers doctrine in the context of the Territories, see Kruger v Commonwealth (1997) 190 CLR 1, 40-44, 53-58, 62, 141-142 and 176

State and Territory merits review bodies generally have power to substitute what they consider to be the correct or preferable decision for the State and Territory Executive decisions being reviewed

# Slide 48

Legality Review by State and Territory Supreme Courts of State and Territory Executive decisions

State and Territory Supreme Courts have the general power to undertake legality review of State and Territory Executive decisions

Federal and State/Territory judicial review operate separately from each other, eg the High Court’s role under s75(v) of the Constitution is limited to legality review regarding “Officers of the Commonwealth”;  review under the ADJR Act is limited to administrative decisions and conduct under Commonwealth enactments

Legality review of State and Territory government decisions and actions is undertaken by State and Territory Supreme Courts, in some cases under State legislation modelled on the Federal ADJR Act and via the prerogative writs (certiorari, prohibition and mandamus or their statutory equivalents) and via declaratory and injunctive relief

Basically the same grounds of legality review already considered in the Federal context apply in the State and Territory context

The High Court in Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW (2010) 239 CLR 531 held that legality review by State Supreme Courts of State Government decisions on the grounds of jurisdictional error was implicitly entrenched by virtue of s 73 of the Federal Constitution
