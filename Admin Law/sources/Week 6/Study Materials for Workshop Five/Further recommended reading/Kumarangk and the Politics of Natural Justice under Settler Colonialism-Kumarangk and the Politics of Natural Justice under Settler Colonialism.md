Kumarangk (Hindmarsh Island) and

the Politics of Natural Justice
under Settler-Colonialism

Robert van Krieken

This article examines the impact of the application of apparently impartial
principles of procedural fairness and natural justice on the construction of

"authentic" and "inauthentic" knowledge of Aboriginal culture. It discusses
the progression of the Kumarangk (Hindmarsh Island) court cases and the
legal construction of public participation in the making of political decisions
affecting Aboriginal interests in land. In examining the politics of competing
interests in land, this article reflects on the tension between Indigenous interests
in land and settler developmentalism in relation to the Australian jurisprudence
of procedural fairness and natural justice. The arguments running through the
article concern the questions of the ways in which the liberal restraint on power
is embodied in the impartial principles of administrative law, where that power
creates rather than infringes upon rights, why it generates a particular legal
construction of Aboriginal interests in land and cultural heritage, and the
extent to which this plays a role in the maintenance of relations of settler-
colonial dispossession.

INTRODUCTION

Between 1993 and 1996, in the saga surrounding the building of a bridge
between the South Australian township of Goolwa and Hindmarsh Island,
Australians witnessed one of the more spectacular practical, legal, political,
and ideological victories of the forces of economic development over
Aboriginals hoping to retain their traditional relationship to the land. Hind-
marsh Island is located in Lake Alexandrina at the mouth of the Murray River
and is known to the local Ngarrindjeri people as Kumarangk (pregnancy). The
issue of the bridge revolved around a number of legal and political events that
were simultaneously distinct and interwoven. These events included an
attempt by the Commonwealth Minister for Aboriginal Affairs to prevent the
building of the bridge on the authority of the Aboriginal and Torres Strait

Islander Heritage Protection Act 1984 (Cth) (Heritage Act), a successful
legal challenges to those actions, and the spectacle of a State Royal
Commission charged with determining the veracity of particular expressed
Aboriginal beliefs, deciding that they had been fabricated (Stevens 1995;
Andrews 1998).

The struggles for and against the building of the bridge constituted one
of the high points of the Australian culture wars, mobilizing key opponents of
Aboriginal interests in land in the wake of the High Court's Mabo and Ors v.
Queensland (No. 2) 1991-92 decision recognizing native title, with a particu-

lar ferocity and concentration of purpose. It was also politico-legal Grand
Opera,3 with envelopes containing restricted Aboriginal religious informa-

tion circulating the Australian Parliament, landing in the wrong offices, and
being photocopied en masse, as well as being tabled in Parliament by Ian

McLachlan, the local Federal Liberal member, leading to his resignation
from the Opposition shadow ministry (Environment and Heritage)4 for
having misled Parliament (Simons 2003b; Tickner 2001, 255-86).' In the

end, managing the competing interests, expectations, and demands became
so unwieldy that the incoming Liberal Federal government passed special
legislation, the Hindmarsh Island Bridge Act 1997 (Cth), in large part so that
the new Minister for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Affairs, John
Herron, would not have to spend six weeks carefully ploughing through the

In this article I use a critical examination of the jurisprudence of the
Hindmarsh Island and other Heritage Act cases6 to reflect on the operation of
administrative law, specifically in relation to the politics of competing inter-
ests in land under settler-colonialism. I also examine the broader significance
of these cases for our understanding of the operation of law, the courts, and
judicial review in Australian political and social life. These cases expose a

range of problems accompanying the political and legal operation of demo-
cratic participation, which can become a forum for problematic and contested
forms of political activity-producing a need to develop rules for such par-
ticipation in the state's decision-making processes (Evatt 1996, 1997). They
also show how Indigenous interests in land pose complex challenges to the
liberal pluralist model of political and social life lying at the heart of con-
temporary public law, raising the question of exactly how developmentalist
interests in land are legitimately reconciled with those of the country's
Indigenous inhabitants.

My general line of argument has two main threads: first, that the Kuma-
rangk cases and other Heritage Act cases are a useful illustration of some

problems integral to the model of judicial review characteristic of one par-
ticular strand of liberal thought as a restraint on governmental power.' The
understanding of participatory democracy built into this model, and its asso-
ciated jurisprudence, is correspondingly that of pitting "the people" and their
interests, rights, and expectations against the state via the very mechanisms
of democratic participation, including court proceedings. Constitutional law
is the more visible aspect of the liberal restraint of government, but admin-
istrative law and core principles such as natural justice and ultra vires are
equally important, constituting in many senses liberal restraint's second and
less visible limbs. However, the jurisprudence of administrative law tends to

This leads to the second thread of my argument: the importantance of
reflecting on the extent to which the jurisprudence of natural justice has
emerged in Australian courts, an example of what Stanley Fish (1999) calls
"the trouble with principle." This "trouble," Fish explains, is the problematic
moral and political outcomes of attempts to organize law around the appli-
cation of supposedly neutral, abstract principles (such as the content of
procedural fairness) made to operate in detachment from the specific social,
historical, and cultural characteristics of particular situations. Despite the
repeated judicial assertions that the expression "procedural fairness" is meant
to convey "the notion of a flexible obligation to adopt fair procedures which
are appropriate and adapted to the circumstances of the particular case,"' in
the Kumarangk cases we find that the same legal principles developed in the
context of protecting individual citizens from the actions of government
(McMillan 2002) are applied in ways that afford equal shelter to property
developers impatient with Aboriginal resistance to the never-ending march
of progress. In settings such as Australia, with both an Indigenous and a
dominant non-Indigenous population, it remains important to address the
questions of the extent to which judicial review, as well as restraining the
power of government, may also be operating to maintain particular kinds of
settler-colonial power relations.

particularly unwieldy because it lay at the intersection of a number of differ-
ent issues and concerns, some more relevant than others. In the background,

More specifically in relation to the Hindmarsh Island Bridge, there were
at least five particular issues at play. First, and the most prominent in the
public debate, was the issue of whether it was fair to say that the group of
Ngarrindjeri women (generally referred to as the "proponent women," led by

what was the "correct" anthropological interpretation of the issues; this
tended to revolve around critiques and defenses of University of Adelaide
anthropologist Deane Fergie's assessment of the significance and meaning of
the area to the local community (Fergie 1995, 1996; Partington 1995;
Brunton 1996; Tonkinson 1997; Simons 2003b). Third, the actual legal issue
around which the relevant court cases revolved was whether the Minister for
Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Affairs, Robert Tickner, and other key
personnel had acted as the Heritage Act required. The fourth matter was
whether the particular piece of legislation at issue, the Heritage Act, was
adequate to the task of protecting Aboriginal cultural heritage. The concerns
included the provisions for dealing with confidential information and the
time frame within which the Minister had to act (Evatt 1996, 1997; Goldflam

discussions of how the legislation could be improved to more adequately
protect Indigenous interests (Hancock 1995, 1996a, 1996b). Fifth, there was
also the question of whether Australian law itself is adequate to the task of
protecting Aboriginal cultural heritage (Taubman 2002). At this point com-

secret and capable of confining the economic rights of others; the other

Everyone thinking in terms like these would have ridiculed and mocked-as
they did on many other occasions-a wholly united Ngarrindjeri people
entirely firm in their religious convictions, backed by armies of anthropolo-
gists all attesting to the same thing and libraries full of histories of the area all
declaring it the most sacred of Aboriginal sacred sites (unlikely, I know). The
joke doing the rounds during the dispute around Coronation Hill, which
became a uranium mine, was as follows: Q: How do you find an Aboriginal
sacred site? A: With a Geiger counter. From this perspective, the only way any
Aboriginal conception of sacredness and significance can avoid being
"invented" and "fabricated" by definition is if it has absolutely no bearing on

any form of economic development or rights to property. In this sense, the
appearance of the dissident women was a windfall for a strategy that would
have been pursued by the developers anyway (see Andrews 1998).

To at least some extent, it has become increasingly difficult for Lockean
advocates of unhindered development to pretend that Aborigines simply are
not there, or can be treated as if, for all practical purposes, they are not there.
It has become harder to define Indigenous belief systems as "barbaric" and to
deny them any political or legal effect simply, and especially, because they
stand in the way of economic development. The resultant alternative strategy
has been to seek out, encourage, and nourish differences within Aboriginal

communities about the nature of those beliefs, and then triumphantly reveal
them to be "untrue" because there is dispute over them, declaring their
representatives to be "bad" Aborigines. Any Aborigine who maintains an
understanding of his or her relationship to land as standing in the way of
development can be portrayed as a corrupt, partisan Aborigine, with authen-
ticity attributed to those who have modernized their understanding of land
and its utilitization.

Many commentators have suggested that the court cases surrounding
Kumarangk (as well as the other Heritage Act cases) demonstrate some
fundamental flaws and biases in the Australian legal system itself. Hilary
Charlesworth (1997), for example, argued that "the epic of Hindmarsh
Island/Kumarangk suggests major flaws in the Australian legal system with
respect to protection of Indigenous heritage. It has underlined the inability of
Australian law to accommodate certain forms of belief and knowledge in the
little boxes it has established" (19; see also Tehan 1996). Added to this has
been the particular stance of John Howard's Liberal-National Party Coalition
government between 1996 and 2007, which was particularly hostile to the
recognition of distinct Aboriginal interests of any sort.

However, I argue for a different framing of the problem. Kumarangk was
not in fact typical or exemplary of the operation of the Heritage Act, as Hal
Wootten (2006) explains on the basis of his own experience as the reporter,
whose job it is to assess the case for and against a declaration. There has been
a range of outcomes in attempts to protect Aboriginal cultural heritage, and
these all need to be taken into account before making bold claims about

One could also pay critical attention to the political strategies of those
supportive of unrestricted development, including land developers, the

certainly played their own roles in the Kumarangk cases, but they did not

legal system itself. In response to the strategies used to delegitimize Aborigi-

the way of financial gain, I prefer to explore the possibilities of another
approach: by digging deeper into the mechanisms of natural justice and
procedural fairness as they are applied to Aboriginal interests in land and the
maintenance of some vestiges of their relationship to the land as an issue in
its own right. My aim is to reflect on what it is about administrative law and
the legal construction of Aboriginal rights that makes the sustenance of those
rights perhaps not entirely impossible but certainly precarious, and for
precisely which reasons.

BACKGROUND

Before examining the Hindmarsh Island story, it is important to pay
attention to a number of features of its prehistory that put into place many of
the essential aspects of how the events unfolded-but that have tended to
drop out of the picture generally drawn of the events between 1993 and 1996.
There are at least four preceding considerations to these events. First, the

1967 constitutional referendum removed the exclusion "other than the
Aboriginal race in any State" from the Commonwealth's power to legislate
with respect to "the people of any race."" The events concerning the bridge
were attempts by the Commonwealth to intervene on behalf of Aboriginal
claimants opposing development on their land, against the position of the
South Australian government and the developers themselves. Second is the
Commonwealth v. Tasmania 1983 (Tasmanian Dam) case, in which the Con-
stitution's "race power" was constructed in a particular way in relation to its
application to Aborigines. In this case, it was understood as one of the
possible heads of power for the Commonwealth government to override state
governments on various issues, including protection of Aboriginal interests
in land. Third, following closely behind the Tasmanian Dam case, was the
election in 1984 of the Hawke Labor government on a platform that included
a firm stance on maximizing the recognition of Aboriginal claims to land and,
failing that, the protection of their cultural heritage. The Heritage Act was
the Hawke government's response to Aboriginal demands "for priority to be
given to enacting legislation which can counter the real, and at times sudden,
threats which are made to significant sites and objects [of Indigenous heritage
value]" (Ryanl984, 2586).1

Against this background, rather than going through the narrative in
straightforward temporal sequence (see Clarke 1996), it is important to dis-
tinguish the two separate story lines running through the Kumarangk cases,
each in a number of Acts. Clearly they interweave with each other, but for
analytical purposes it is important to keep them conceptually distinct.

Although the precise content of procedural fairness varies from case to
case, then, generally there remain two core concerns surrounding the exercise
of a power adverse to particular rights, interests, and legitimate expectations:
first, the right to disclosure of the information forming the basis of the decision
maker's determination and, second, the right to a hearing enabling a response
to that information.a In practice, the two concerns are often interrelated,
since perceived inadequate disclosure is frequently experienced as central to
the inadequacy of the hearing. The Federal Court has summed up the current
common law position on the entitlement to information available to a deci-
sion maker as extending to "the right to rebut or qualify by further informa-
tion, and comment by way of submission, upon adverse material from other
sources which is put before the decision maker" (Commissioner for ACT
Reference v. Alphone Pty Ltd 1994, 715, Northrop, Miles, and French JJ).
Decision makers are thus "required to advise of any adverse conclusion which
has been arrived at which would not obviously be open on the known
material" (715). The entitlement to comment on "adverse material" does not,
however, extend to the decision maker's "mental processes or provisional
views" (715) before the decision is made.

the report. The State of Western Australia sought review of Tickner's deci-
sion and production of the report in the Federal Court (Crocodile Park 1), but
Tickner declined, citing that both the state's solicitor and its barrister were
women.

relatively unexplored, then, is how the doctrine of procedural fairness is to
operate in those "extraordinary" cases where a problem of confidentiality does
arise, especially within the complex nexus between confidentiality and the
principles governing disclosure, two areas of legal principle that need to be
seen as intimately related to each other.

CONFIDENTIALITY

Australian common law already makes a range of provisions for the
confidentiality of information heard before courts, under the broad umbrella
of basing the rules governing the content of procedural fairness on the
circumstances of the particular case. In Victorian Broadcasting Network (1983)
Pty Ltd v Minister for Transport and Communications (1990), for example,
Justice Hill had no difficulty concluding that at least some of the financial

information before the decision maker was "clearly" confidential and that the
applicant had no entitlement to test the validity of that information (697-
98). More generally, P. D. Finn (1984) has noted that restraints on disclosure
arise in relation to trade secrets and commercial information, private and
professional relationships, public and private institutions, and the "public

interest" (497). Deane Fergie (1995, 20) observes, for example, that the
developers in the Kumarangk cases were equally concerned about protecting
their commercial confidences.

The notion of "public interest immunity" was explored in detail in
Aboriginal Sacred Sites Protection Authority v. Maurice (Re Warumungu Land
Claim) 1986; hereafter Aboriginal Sacred Sites), where the Northern Territory
Sacred Sites Protection Authority sought protection from disclosure for mate-
rials gathered by anthropologists and linguists from Aboriginal sacred sites in
support of a land claim under the Aboriginal Land Rights (Northern Terri-
tory) Act 1976 (Cth). Justice Woodward argued that there should indeed be
a new category of public interest immunity "covering secret and sacred
Aboriginal information and beliefs" (Aborginal Sacred Sites 114-15). In that
same case, although Justice Toohey did maintain that the relevant legal
claims had to be subject to some "forensic investigation" (130), this "is not to
say that . .. there may not be an aspect of public interest immunity which
a court or tribunal must balance against the public interest in favour of
disclosure" (131).

Justice Toohey pointed out that any ultimate rejection of the public
interest immunity argument did not have as its corollary the notion "that any
documents in [the Court's] possession or in the possession of others for its
purposes must necessarily be disclosed to the public at large or even to all
those participating in the proceedings of a court or tribunal" (130). Justice
Woodward, too, made it much more explicit how the principles of disclosure
and confidentiality should be balanced, and according to what principles:

In my opinion, the proper protection of minority rights is very much in
the public interest, as is respect for deeply held spiritual beliefs. In
particular, the rights and beliefs of the Aboriginal people of Australia
should be accorded a special degree of protection and respect in Austra-
lian courts. Thus I can well imagine a court finding on balance, for
example, that the outrage in an Aboriginal community caused by a

forced disclosure of information about a sacred site, would outweigh the
importance in that particular criminal or civil trial of precisely identi-
fying the place or explaining why it was sacred. (114)

In general, then, as Allars (1996, 30) observes, contemporary administrative
law can and does make exceptions to openness and full disclosure, especially
when there are statutory provisions made. In both the Crocodile Park cases
and Kumarangk cases, the Federal Court was spared from deliberating on
whether it should indeed frustrate the primary purpose of the statute in favor
of legislative aims concerning disclosure and fairness implied by the common
law, primarily because the other procedural defects in the Minister's action
were so serious and inescapable (Norvill v. Chapman 1995, 254).

From this perspective, Justice O'Loughlin rejected the Chapmans' "full
disclosure" argument, stating that the statute did not require it of either the
reporter or the Minister, particularly when balanced against the primary
concern of the statute-the expeditious protection of areas and sites of
Aboriginal heritage significance from damage and injury-within a particular
time frame:

In my opinion, the statute, concerned as it is with the preservation and
protection of areas and objects that are of particular significance to
Aboriginals in accordance with Aboriginal tradition, requires the Min-

ister and the reporter to act expeditiously to prevent injury or desecra-
tion. Such an obligation can not be achieved if the reporter is required
to conduct an inquiry where numerous interested parties are entitled to
receive, consider and make submissions with respect to the representa-

tions of another interested party. (Chapman v. Tickner 1995, 90)

For Justice O'Loughlin, then, Parliament, in its particular drafting of the
Heritage Act, impliedly restricted the content of procedural fairness through
its imposition of a particular procedure and time frame on its overall aims and
intentions. The aims and intentions of the act, its time constraints, and the
requirements of procedural fairness are all balanced in a particular way, in
which the outer limits of the content of procedural fairness are seen as the
achievement of the act's aims. These limits resulted in

no obligation on the part of the Minister to give to [the Chapmans] any
opportunity to make submissions on the reports of Professor Saunders
and on the reports of such anthropologists and archaeologists as may
have been referred to or relied upon by her in the compilation of her
report. (94)

The particular concern shown for balancing the principles of confiden-
tiality and disclosure within the overall purpose of the Heritage Act thus
resonates more with the earlier approaches of Justices Woodward and Toohey

than does the weight placed by other Federal Court Justices on disclosure.
This position is supported by Lord Reid's observations that before the courts

It is fair to say, then, that the issue of the Chapmans' right to question

seen as relatively isolated cases of a lack of sympathy with Aboriginal culture,
rather than as a manifestation of a deep-seated conflict between different sets
of legal and political principles. "With a small amount of adjustment and
reinterpretation," wrote Nathan Hancock (1996a), "it may be possible within
the current system to establish cultural heritage claims without trespassing on
the very Aboriginal laws, customs and traditions which the Heritage Act was
intended to protect" (20).

CONCLUSION: PROCEDURAL FAIRNESS AS
"WHITEFELLA LAW"?

In Mr. Sebastian's affidavit he refers to what he describes as "white fella
law." By that reference, I have taken Mr. Sebastian to be referring to the
law of this country other than Aboriginal law. I do not think it is
appropriate to describe that body of law as "white fella law." The truth of
the matter is that it is Australian law for all Australians regardless of
their colour. (Crocodile Park 1, 150, per Carr J)

The response to this observation could easily be that for an issue as
contested as this one-Indigenous versus non-Indigenous interests in land-it

is simply very difficult to manage effectively questions of justice, fairness, and,
above all, legitimacy. In the absence of a process that addresses that difficulty
with sufficient clarity and lack of ambiguity, one needs to fall back on the
abstract principle, even if it does defeat the policy intentions of the legislation.

Why, then, did Frank Sebastian, chairman of the Yawuru Aboriginal
Corporation, make the "whitefella law" remark? The political and legal
theory operative within the principles governing procedural fairness depends
on an underlying or background social and cultural theory. How one under-
stands the way participation and procedural fairness can and should operate
is itself based on a particular conception of society, the person, knowledge,
and property. In this respect, instead of categorizing legal liberalism as a
unified standpoint, it can be regarded as bifurcated into two different "faces"
(Gray 2000) with different consequences attached to each.20 In particular,
one can operate with either a contracted or expanded view of social relations.
A contracted view is a universalistic one in which real, existing economic and
political interests, as well as social relations of power, recede from view, and
the world is composed only of more or less equal citizens and agencies of
government. An expanded view recognizes prior interests and power relations
and the position of the law within that field of unequal interests and power.

While it is true, then, that there is not an irreconcilable conflict between
"whitefella law" and Aboriginal law, the actual operation of Australian law
can look, feel, and sound remarkably like "whitefella law" when legislatures
and courts work with the contracted view of social relations, keeping the
existing power relations of settler-colonialism invisible and pretending that
the rights of property developers to make money should be thought about in

This intellectual tradition in political and legal liberalism is character-
ized, then, by an understanding of the relationships between government;
democratic participation; judicial review; and individual and group rights,
interests, and expectations. This conception of the law foregrounds and
"thinks" in terms of the linkage of democratic participation and judicial
review to the creation, defense, and expansion of rights, interests, and expec-
tations, on the one hand, and the linkage of state power, legislation, and
executive action with their destruction, prejudice, and defeat, on the other.
So rights are seen in terms of natural law, and it is surmised that the "problem"
with states is that they often get in the way of those rights and that the
function of law is to help constrain that tendency.

Many cases of judicial review are quite correctly captured by this under-
standing, but others are not. The Kumarangk cases should be regarded as a key
example of judicial review that manifests another form of liberal legalism
operating according to the quite different linkages emerging when rights are
created, defended, or expanded by the state. The effect of law and judicial
review is then in fact to oppose, sometimes to defeat or destroy, such rights
and interests, albeit in the name of opposing rights. In practice, of course,
both models will operate in a case of competing rights, interests, and expec-
tations. Indeed, the Chapmans' property rights themselves are not "natural"
entities but an effect of state power, merely a less visible, "background" one
(Sened 1997, 7). However, even when the courts are aware that the case
before them concerns competing interests, the Kumarangk cases usefully

tions, rather than from the reality of settler-colonial inequality. This contra-

consideration remain largely ineffective. 22 Participation and procedural
fairness thus end up becoming merely battlegrounds upon which those
power relations are fought out by other, legal, means.

consistently to identify the extent to which the protection of the rights,

interests, and legitimate expectations of entrepreneurs, property developers,
refugees, insurance companies, individuals threatened with deportation
because they are accused of crimes, and so on, are protected according to a
universal set of democratic principles. This precaution might in fact have a
paradoxical operation, one that works to the benefit of the powerful at the
