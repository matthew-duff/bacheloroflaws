In the aftermath of Mabo, another politico-legal event occurred during 
the 1990s that illuminated the intersections between Indigenous 
culture, anthropology, history and law. What has since become known 
as ‘the Hindmarsh Island affair’ was a challenge to the building of a 
bridge connecting the mainland to Hindmarsh Island (Kumurangk to 
the Ngarrindjeri) in South Australia, on the grounds that the bridge 
would harm Aboriginal culture and spirituality. It was a paradigmatic 
example of the tensions between development and heritage, and between 
competing understandings of Aboriginal culture. It polarised national 
opinion over the extent to which Indigenous culture had survived and 
when it ought to be recognised by Australian law.

The Aboriginal Heritage Protection Act: framing the facts

Hindmarsh Island is located at the mouth of the Murray River, just 
inland from where it enters the sea. The Murray flows around both 
sides of the island, and its water merges with the salt water from

The logic of elimination: the role of anthropology in law

In forcing public discussion about the continuation and sacredness of 
Indigenous beliefs in a nation state that has developed to deny those 
beliefs, the outcomes were necessarily unpredictable, incomplete and 
inconsistent. Yet the disciplinary methods employed in the case carry 
within them their own version of epistemological end game. The 
uncertainty of outcomes for the Ngarrindjeri is, paradoxically, a product 
of the certainty with which legal discourse – in this case, statutory law 
– has controlled, classified and contained (white) disciplinary expertise 
intended to illuminate Indigenous beliefs.
Anthropology sits at the heart of the evidence in this case. Although the 
issue of fabrication was not a specific cause of action, it became one as a 
result of the approach taken by the respondents’ legal team. These lawyers 
took ‘some convincing’ not to pursue a straightforward response to the 
Chapmans’ claim of financial loss, in part because the Royal Commission’s 
findings on the anthropological issues had been so powerful, and so 
politically sensitive. But as Steve Hemming, an interdisciplinary history

Boundaries of expertise: the limits of anthropology

To understand how this case is emblematic of broader issues raised 
by the relationships between anthropology, law and history, and their 
control of Indigenous subjects, it is important to understand the layering 
of anthropological evidence that appeared before the court. The outer 
layer is the debate between the expert anthropologists called at the 
Federal Court trial, Professors Kenneth Maddock (for the applicants) and 
Howard Morphy (for the respondents). These men, eminent in their field, 
were asked to evaluate the anthropological techniques and conclusions 
of Dr Fergie in making her initial report. This is a regularised exchange 
of expertise, yet the ‘tribe’ on which they are asked to comment is not 
the Ngarrindjeri directly; it is anthropologists, their own. The inner 
layer is the dispute over whether Deane Fergie’s report followed the 
acknowledged conventions of professional anthropological practice, or 
whether she was somehow deviant, and lax, allowing a fabrication of 
Ngarrindjeri beliefs to steam-roll over commercial proprieties. (In that 
sense this case may remind us of the Irving v Lipstadt and Penguin trial in 
2000, where what was at issue was not the past itself, but historians’ 
mode of accessing and representing it.)
In her original report for the section 10 declaration, Dr Fergie 
describes the scope of her report as limited by the timeframe and specific 
concerns of the Aboriginal Heritage Protection Act reporting process. Her 
report, she said, ‘is not intended to provide a comprehensive survey of the 
anthropological and ethnographic literature on this region’. She made 
it clear that she was not an expert in Ngarrindjeri culture per se, but had 
merely been asked to perform a limited function as described by the Act, 
to facilitate discussion between Professor Saunders and the Ngarrindjeri 
women.30 Despite the limits to her ability to undertake grand-scale 
ethnographic research, Fergie carefully explains her observations of the 
consultations between Saunders and the small groups of women who 
guarded the women’s secret knowledge.31 She described their opposition 
to the bridge as based on ‘a profound conviction that the building of a

Credible experts

There is a broad legal point to be made here about the expert’s role 
and his or her duty to the court, an issue discussed by Gary Edmond in 
relation to Justice von Doussa’s judgment in this case.59 Edmond argues 
that the idea that experts owe a duty to the court does not operate in 
a vacuum, since they already owe ‘a variety of professional, ethical, 
pecuniary and personal allegiances’, and that the Hindmarsh Island 
Bridge case ably demonstrates this point. As Edmond states, Fergie 
simultaneously held ‘a duty to the court, a duty to her profession, a duty 
to her employer, a duty to the university and their insurer, an ethical 
duty to the local Aboriginal peoples and was concerned to defend her 
reputation, dignity and assets’. Edmond identifies an important point: that 
Justice von Doussa in his assessment of the competing anthropological 
evidence ‘escaped this morass through the (re)construction of her 
contract and instructions’, finding that Fergie’s ‘contractual engagement 
with the ALRM [Aboriginal Legal Rights Movement], in relation to a 
controversial application under the Heritage Protection Act (Cth), entitled 
her to act as an advocate’.60

The case became, in essence, a question of the credibility of witnesses, 
of differing systems of beliefs, and of disciplinary practices. Rather than 
basing a finding of credit on objective anthropological practice, the 
judge bases it on whether or not the anthropologists’ involvement in the 
legal proceedings were undertaken on an objective basis. The ultimate 
duty therefore remains to the court, as opposed to the profession of 
anthropology itself.61

Anthropology and the subordination of history

The obvious consequence of law’s discursive appropriation of this 
particular kind of anthropology is that history must necessarily be 
subordinated. If the moment of invasion is a sui generis moment, long 
past, with no ongoing discursive or practical repercussions, and if an 
authentic indigeneity is only able to be captured through ‘traditions’ 
that are somehow suspended in time, history as the political context 
in which Indigenous Australians live becomes inaccessible. When the 
law demands documentary historical sources, their reading is filtered 
through an anthropological discourse that law has already sanctioned. 
In Australia since the Gove case, anthropology has become so integral 
to law’s consideration of Indigenous challenges that it is understood as 
a profession with particular skills. History, on the other hand, is seen as 
remarkably like the law, and as a result it is difficult for the legally trained 
to grasp what unique skills an historian could bring to the evidentiary 
table.
This was borne out in our interviews. When asked what the differences 
were between law, history and anthropology, one lawyer responded:

History is part of the evidence, consists of primary documents, and 
historians can locate and identify those documents. A historian is therefore 
someone who writes a narrative about the sources, gives a summary, and 
in the course of doing so has a right to express an opinion … when looking 
at primary sources. History and law are not different. History locates 
them [documents] and comments on them: that is its benefit. Law uses 
those primary documents, and can use them as a reference point: to refer 
the historian’s opinion back to them.

When asked about the differences between anthropology and history, 
this same lawyer commented:

Anthropology is mixed up with history: anthropology written in the 
past, like Berndt and Strehlow, or even Curr (less well trained of course 
but interesting), is useful as an historical source, for both historians and 
anthropologists.
