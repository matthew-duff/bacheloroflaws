# Slide 6

Procedural fairness (natural justice)

The codified ground of review:
“… that a breach of the rules of natural justice occurred in connection with the making of the decision”
“Natural justice” and “procedural fairness” are used interchangeably, although the term “procedural fairness” emphasises the procedural, rather than substantive, nature of the ground of review
Two basic rules:
The hearing rule – “audi alteram partem”
A decision-maker must generally give an applicant a reasonable opportunity to prepare and to present the applicant’s case including sufficient notice of the case being made against the applicant
The bias rule – “nemo debet esse judex in propria sua causa”
A decision-maker must be free from actual and apprehended bias.  Apprehended bias will arise in cases where a fair-minded and informed lay observer might apprehend that the decision-maker might not be sufficiently impartial
Remember the importance of statutory interpretation, especially in natural justice cases -  Creyke et al, 485-487
Procedural fairness obligations also arise in relation to non-governmental tribunals (eg disciplinary tribunals set up by social clubs or sporting bodies) and such obligations are generally contractual in character
Remember again that the grounds of legality review overlap and this often occurs in cases of breach of the rules of natural justice

The current approach to whether natural justice obligations apply

Minister for Immigration and Border Protection v SZSSJ (2016) 259 CLR 180, [75], per French CJ, Kiefel, Bell, Gageler, Keane, Nettle and Gordon JJ:

[“… it must now be taken to be settled that procedural fairness is implied as a condition of the exercise of a statutory power through the application of a common law principle of statutory interpretation. The common law principle,  sufficiently stated for present purposes, is that a statute conferring a power the exercise of which is apt to affect an interest of an individual is presumed to confer that power on condition that the power is exercised in a manner that affords procedural fairness to that individual. The presumption operates unless clearly displaced by the particular statutory scheme.”

Gageler CJ has called it a “draw” between Sir Anthony Mason’s common law approach and Sir Gerard Brennan’s emphasis on statutory interpretation – see Gageler, ‘The intersection of the common law, statute law and notions of fairness’ [2016] International Family Law 15 at 17]

The legality of subordinate legislation

Failure to stay within the limits of primary legislation may result in the invalidity of subordinate legislation

Compare the ground of review:
“…that the decision was not authorized by the enactment in pursuance of which it was purported to be made”

This ground can apply to purported exercises of administrative power but note the narrow drafting of the ADJR Act and Part 3 of the JR Act (which require that decisions be of an “administrative character”) and their apparent exclusion of legality review of decisions to create subordinate legislation. Illegality related to sub-delegations

Unlawful sub-delegation (ie where the legislature gives a power to one decision-maker who purports to delegate that power to another decision-maker) is potentially covered by a number of legality grounds of review:
“… that the person who purported to make the decision did not have jurisdiction to make the decision”
“… that the decision was not authorized by the enactment in pursuance of which it was purported to be made”
Separation of powers related concerns appear to arise – sub-delegation of legislative and judicial powers is more likely to be invalid than sub-delegation of executive branch powers – see, for example, Dainford Ltd v Smith (1984-1985) 155 CLR 342, 349, per Gibbs CJ, and Aronson et al, 344-345
There is a distinction between sub-delegation and agency
A sub-delegate is entitled to exercise a power in the sub-delegate’s own name
An agent is the alter-ego of the principal and exercises the power in the name of the principal – see the so-called “Carltona principle, derived from Carltona Ltd v Commissioners of Works [1943] 2 All ER 560 at 563, per Lord Greene MR
Contrast also the position of an administrative assistant – Creyke et al, 452
Statutory interpretation is again critical and State and Federal Acts Interpretation Acts apply special rules in relation to sub-delegations – see Creyke et al, 454-455

# Slide 70

Mandatory relevant and irrelevant considerations

70

The codified grounds of review:
taking an irrelevant consideration into account in the exercise of a power
failing to take a relevant consideration into account in the exercise of a power

There are generally three types of considerations in relation to discretionary decision-making:
Legally relevant (or “mandatory” relevant considerations)
Legally irrelevant (ie the law requires that they must not be taken into account)
Those that can be lawfully considered but which do not need to be taken into account

Various factual and legal questions are raised by these grounds of review:
What considerations were factually taken into account by the decision-maker? Answering this question will involve evidential issues discussed in Creyke et al, 605-606, 609-610, 616-620 and 629-631
What considerations was the decision-maker legally obliged to consider (ie mandatory relevant considerations) or legally obliged not to consider (ie mandatory irrelevant considerations)?  When exercises of statutory powers are being considered, the subject-matter, scope and purpose of the statute will be of fundamental importance, as will the nature of the decision-maker and common law rules regarding the implication of legally relevant or legally irrelevant considerations.

These are grounds of extended/abuse of power ultra vires and excess of jurisdiction.  The High Court in Craig v South Australia (1995) 184 CLR 163 at 179-180, per Brennan, Deane, Toohey, Gaudron and McHugh JJ, held that these grounds do not apply in the same way to courts as they do to tribunals.
