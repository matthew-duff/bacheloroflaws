# 📘 Chapter 3 Exam Notes: The Company as a Separate Legal Entity

## 🎯 1. The Separate Legal Entity Doctrine
**Core Principle:** Once the requirements for incorporation are met and a company is registered, it is recognized as its own **separate legal entity**.
- It exists independently from its shareholders, directors, and founders.
- **Capacity:** It can sue, be sued, enter contracts in its own name, employ staff, borrow money, and own property.
- **Statutory Recognition:** Section 124(1) of the *Corporations Act* states that a company has the legal capacity and powers of an individual.

### 📌 *Salomon v Salomon & Co Ltd (1897)* (The Landmark Case)
- **Facts:** Aaron Salomon ran a boot manufacturing business for 30 years as a sole trader. He incorporated *A Salomon & Co Ltd* to gain limited liability and give his family an interest. There were 7 shareholders (Salomon, wife, and 5 children, holding 1 share each; Salomon personally held 20,001 shares). The company purchased the business. Salomon became a secured creditor via debentures. The business failed, the company went into liquidation, and remaining assets were insufficient to pay unsecured creditors once Salomon's secured debt was prioritized. The liquidator argued the company was a "one-person" company and a "mere alias" or agent for Salomon.
- **Held:** The House of Lords affirmed the separate legal entity doctrine. The company was validly incorporated. The motives behind incorporation are legally irrelevant. Salomon was not liable to indemnify the company for its debts.
- **Significance:** Confirmed that incorporation and the benefits of limited liability apply equally to small, "one-person" private companies as they do to large joint-stock enterprises. It cemented the legitimacy of minimizing personal risk using the corporate form.

## 🛡️ 2. Limited Liability
**Core Principle:** A member (shareholder) need not contribute to the company more than the amount (if any) unpaid on the shares that they own (reflected in **s 516** of the *Corporations Act*).
- **Economic Justifications:** Encourages investment, enables risk-taking, decreases monitoring costs for shareholders, and allows portfolio diversification.
- **Risk Allocation:** The financial risk of business failure is externalized to creditors. Contract creditors (e.g., banks) are assumed to be efficiently capable of self-protecting by demanding security or personal guarantees. Involuntary creditors (e.g., tort victims) cannot self-protect.

### 📌 *Lee v Lee’s Air Farming Ltd (1961)*
- **Facts:** Mr. Lee was the major shareholder, governing director, and sole employee of a crop-dusting company. He died in a plane crash for work. His widow claimed workers' compensation, but the insurer argued Lee couldn't simultaneously be both the employer (the company) and the employee.
- **Held (Privy Council):** A person can wear "multiple hats" (have multiple roles) in relation to a company. Because the company is a separate legal entity, it could successfully and legally enter into a contract of service with Lee.

### 📌 *Andar Transport Pty Ltd v Brambles Ltd (2004)*
- **Facts:** Wail incorporated Andar (a one-person company) to contract with Brambles for laundry delivery. Wail was the controlling shareholder, director, and driver. Wail was injured on the job and sued Brambles, who claimed contributory negligence by Andar (arguing that as an employer, Andar failed to provide a safe system).
- **Held (High Court):** Andar owed Wail a duty of care to provide a safe working environment, even though Wail was the controlling mind of the company. In law, Wail and Andar had different duties and responsibilities, reaffirming the strict separation between a one-person business owner and their company.

## ✂️ 3. Piercing the Corporate Veil (Common Law Exceptions)
Courts are extremely reluctant to depart from the separate legal entity principle. As noted in *Briggs v James Hardie*, there is "no common, unifying principle" for piercing the veil. It is heavily fact-driven and considered an option of "last resort".

### A. Evasion of a Legal Obligation
- The veil is pierced when a company is established to actively avoid a pre-existing legal obligation or carry out illegal activities.
- **📌 *Gilford Motor Co Ltd v Horne*:** Horne had a restraint of trade clause in his employment contract. After resigning, he set up a new company (shares technically held by his wife and an associate) specifically to compete. The court granted an injunction against both him and the company, holding the company was formed for the *sole or dominant purpose* of evading his specific legal obligation to his former employer.
- **📌 *Prest v Petrodel Resources Ltd (2013, UKSC)*:** Arising from a high-profile divorce case where properties were transferred to a husband’s companies. Lord Sumption thoroughly discussed veil-piercing:
  - **Concealment Principle:** Using a company structure to conceal the real identity/facts (doesn't legally require "piercing" the veil, just looking behind it to find the true actors).
  - **Evasion Principle:** Specifically interposing a company to defeat, frustrate, or evade an existing legal right or obligation. (Considered the true, rare exception).
  - *Note:* The Court ultimately ordered the property transfer via a resulting trust rather than strictly piercing the veil.

### B. Fraud
- The veil may be pierced if the corporate structure is being used as a sham to perpetuate or conceal a deliberate fraud.
- **📌 *Re Darby*:** Darby and Gyde created a company in the Channel Islands (CILIC), to serve as the "promoter" of an English company (WSQ) to commit prospectus fraud. The Court looked behind CILIC, named it a "mere alias" and dummy device, and held Darby and Gyde personally liable for the fraud.

### C. Agency
- Total ownership or control of a company does *not* automatically create a principal-agent relationship.
- **📌 *Smith, Stone & Knight Ltd v Birmingham Corporation*:** A parent manufacturing company successfully claimed compensation when its un-leased subsidiary's land was compulsorily acquired. The court found an implied agency relationship (that the subsidiary was merely carrying on the parent's business) by satisfying six specific factual tests (profits treated as parent's, appointed by parent, parent is the 'brain', parent controls capital/skill, constant control).
- *Status Today:* This approach is heavily criticized. As Rogers AJA pointed out, applying this loosely would leave limited liability in corporate groups "in tatters". It is rarely successful today.

## ⚖️ 4. Statutory Inroads
Parliament has intervened where the common law doctrines are insufficient.
- **Insolvent Trading (s 588G *Corporations Act*):** Directors become personally liable for the company's debts if they continue trading or incurring debt when there are reasonable grounds for suspecting the company is insolvent (or will become insolvent), and the director was (or should have been) aware of them.
- **Parent Company Liability (s 588V *Corporations Act*):** Pierces the veil in corporate groups to make a parent company financially liable for the debts of a subsidiary involving insolvent trading (where the parent company had reasonable grounds to suspect the subsidiary's insolvency).

## 🏢 5. Corporate Groups
- **General Principle:** Each company within a corporate group (parent vs. subsidiaries) is treated strictly as a distinct and separate legal entity. The law traditionally rejects treating a corporate group as a single blended economic unit.
- **📌 *Walker v Wimborne*:** The High Court emphasized that directors of a specific company owe duties to *that company alone*, not to the corporate group as a whole. Moving funds between companies within a group to save one from insolvency was a breach of directors' duties to the individual company making the payment.
- **The "Involuntary Creditor" / Tort Liability Problem:** When a subsidiary commits a tort but lacks assets to pay (e.g., asbestos liabilities isolated in subsidiaries during the *James Hardie* case), injured parties cannot easily sue the wealthy parent company.
  - *Debate:* While heavily criticized as unfair to tort victims (who, unlike contract creditors, don't choose the risk), Australian courts and law reform committees generally prioritize upholding the veil to protect parent companies, stimulate investment, and maintain corporate certainty. In *Briggs v James Hardie*, there was a suggestion that different legal considerations *might* apply in tort versus contract, but no definitive common law revolution occurred.

## 🚨 6. Corporate Liability for Civil and Criminal Wrongs
Although a corporation physically acts through natural persons, it attracts primary liability as a separate juristic person.

### A. Corporate Criminal Liability (Common Law)
- Ordinarily requires *mens rea* (guilty intent) and an *actus reus* (guilty act).
- **Organic Theory / "Directing Mind and Will":** The mental state of directors or high-level managers represents the central coordinating "brain" of the company (*HL Bolton Engineering v TJ Graham*).
- **📌 *Tesco Supermarkets Ltd v Nattrass*:** Tesco was charged with falsely advertising reduced prices due to a local store manager's error. The House of Lords acquitted Tesco, ruling the store manager was relatively low on the hierarchy and was merely carrying out orders—he was simply "hands", not the "directing mind" of the corporation.
- **Policy Adaptation:** In *ABC Developmental Learning Centres v Wallace* (where a toddler escaped childcare), a Victorian court deviated from strict organic theory. It attributed a junior employee’s negligence directly to the company due to the strong public policy objectives implicit in child protection legislation.

### B. Corporate Criminal Liability (Statute: *Criminal Code Act 1995 (Cth)*)
Applies to Commonwealth offences (Part 2.5 of the Criminal Code, encompassing the *Corporations Act*):
- **Physical Element (s 12.2):** Attributed immediately to the company if the physical action is committed by an employee, agent, or officer acting within the actual/apparent scope of their employment or authority.
- **Fault Element (*mens rea*) (s 12.3):** More flexible than common law. Attributed if the company "expressly, tacitly, or impliedly authorized or permitted" the commission of the offence. This can be met if:
  1. The board of directors or a "high managerial agent" committed or authorized the offence.
  2. A **"corporate culture"** existed that directed, encouraged, tolerated, or led to non-compliance.
  3. The company failed to actively create and maintain a corporate culture that required compliance.

### C. Liability in Tort
- **Vicarious Liability:** An employer (company) is indirectly and strictly liable for the torts of its employees/agents committed within the ordinary course and scope of their employment.
- **Direct Liability:** The company is directly liable where it personally caused the tort (e.g., corporate negligence, passing off, defamation).
