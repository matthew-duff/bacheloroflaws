INTRODUCTION

2.1.1 
The Commonwealth Constitution says very little about administrative law. The 
Commonwealth and state constitutions were mostly drafted in an era when the British 
style of constitution-making was to place trust in democracy and the political system to 
control how power is exercised by governments. Moreover, administrative law as we now 
define it was not then a recognised system for controlling government action. The furthest 
the Commonwealth Constitution went was to create a court system, and to confer original 
jurisdiction on the High Court in s 75(v) to grant the remedies of mandamus, prohibition 
and injunction against an officer of the Commonwealth. That jurisdiction has developed in 
importance over the years, so that it is now recognised as securing'a basic element of the rule of 
law'and providing 'an entrenched minimum provision of judicial review': PlaintijfS157/2002 
v Commonwealth 17 .3.24C at CLR 482,513. However, it falls short of being a comprehensive 
guarantee. There is otherwise no explicit constitutional foundation for open government, 
administrative tribunals, the ombudsman, or for judicial review in the comprehensive fashion 
later established by the Administrative Decisions (judicial Review}Act 1977 (Cth) (ADJRAct). 
The state constitutions exhibit much the same design, except that in Victoria the Constitution 
Act 1975 (Vic) gives recognition to the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court (s 85), to the court's 
power to award habeas corpus (s 86), to the independence of the ombudsman (s 94E), and to 
the need for access to information legislation (see also ss 18 and 85 concerning amendment 
of those provisions).

2.1.2 The lack of express detail in the Constitution does not mean it is unimportant to 
administrative law. Many aspects of our government and judicial framework which are 
established or regulated by the Constitution exert significant influence over administrative 
law. An example given in 2.2.16 is that the separation of judicial and executive power in the 
Commonwealth Constitution embodies the legality/merits distinct.ion that is a cornerstone 
of Australian administrative law. Additionally, as examined in 2.2.1 ff, the constitutional 
framework embodies important unwritten principles and values -
such as the rule of law-
that convey essential meaning about the system of law and government.

Separation of powers

2.2.12 The doctrine of the separation of powers stipulates that the three major organs of 
the governmental system each perform a single and different function: the legislature enacts 
laws; the executive applies those laws in indiVldual cases; and, in the event that a dispute arises 
about the meaning or application of a law, the dispute is resolved conclusively by the judiciary.

2.2.13 Broadly, the objective of the separation of powers is to place checks and balances 
on the exercise of governmental power, while at the same time ensuring that the different 
functions of government are discharged by the arm of government best suited to the task. The 
role of the judiciary, under a system of separation of powers, is to undertake judicial review of 
executive action: see also Attorney-General (NSW) v Quin 3.2.4C.

2.2.14 The separation of powers as defined in 2.2.12 is not practised in a pure form in 
Australia. Responsible government is itself a fundamental breach, since ministers both 
constitute the executive and sit in the parliament. Another breach is the widespread 
delegation from the legislature to the executive of the power to make subordinate legislation. 
The amalgamated exercise by some independent regulatory agencies of rule-making, 
administrative, adjudicative and punitive powers also tilts at, if not breaches, the separation 
of powers.

2.2.15 The separation of powers is nevertheless important to Australian administrative law, 
both as to its structure and its operation. The effect is more pronounced on Commonwealth 
administrative law, because the separation of powers is a constitutionally entrenched doctrine; 
this is taken up further in 2.3.1 ff. Beyond the constitutional requirements, the pattern generally 
followed at both Commonwealth and state level has been to allocate to courts the function 
of judicial reVlew, and to allocate to administrative tribunals the function of merits review.

Responsible government

2.2.18 Government in Australia at both federal and state level is in accordance with the 
principles of responsible government (also called Westminster or parliamentary government).

executive. There is a well-established convention that the nunierous powers conferred upon the

2.3.65 1n 1992 the High Court recognised that, implied within the concept of 
representative government and Australia's democratic structure, is constitutional 
protection for communications that are 'political' in nature: Nationwide News Pty Ltd 
v Wills (1992) 177 CLR 1 and Australian Capita/Television v Commonwealth (1992) 177 
CLR 106. Once a burden on political communication is identified, the relevant inquiry 
is: (1) whether the law has a legitimate purpose consistent with the constitutional system 
of representative government; and (2) if so, whether the law is reasonably appropriate and 
adapted to the achievement of that purpose: Lange v Australian Broadcasting Corporation 
(1997) 189 CLR 520.

2.3.66 In applying the implied freedom in an administrative law context,courts distinguish 
between (1) determining the validity of the statutory provisions that are relied on for an 
executive action; and (2) determining the validity of that executive action. The validity of a 
law that could burden freedom of political communication by authorising executive action 
that has a burdening effect is to be determined by asking if the burden of the law is justified 
across the range of potential executive outcomes: Wotton v Queensland (2012) 246 CLR 1; 
[2012] HCA 2. If the burden is justified, that is the end of the constitutional inquiry. The law 
is valid and the validity of any particular executive action is to be gauged by reference solely 
to the authorising statutory provisions. In Comcare v Bane1Ji (2019) 267 CLR 373; [2019] 
HCA 23, Edelman J observed: 'The constitutional constraint does not operate directly upon 
the exercise of executive power. It invalidates the executive act only by operating upon the 
legislation, disapplying the legislative authority for the executive act': at [209].
