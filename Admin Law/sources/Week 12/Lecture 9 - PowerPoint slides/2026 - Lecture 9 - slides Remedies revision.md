Court costs and the JR Act - Reasons

2

[Section 50 of the JR Act provides the Supreme Court with the power to make a special costs order if someone commences court proceedings to vindicate their statutory right to reasons.  Normally, in litigation, costs follow the event, ie the party that loses the litigation pays the court costs of the winner.  JR Act s50 varies that rule

The special costs provisions of the JR Act have no equivalent in the ADJR Act]

Interlocutory injunctions in public law litigation

7

Australian Broadcasting Corporation v O’Neill (2006) 227 CLR 57, [65]-[71], per Gummow and Hayne JJ (with whom Gleeson CJ and Crennan J agreed, [19]):

[Interlocutory injunctions are functionally equivalent to stays under s29 of the JR Act

There are 2 inquiries for interlocutory injunctions: “… first … whether the plaintiff has made out a prima facie case, in the sense that if the evidence remains as it is there is a probability at trial of the action the plaintiff will be held entitled to relief … and … second … whether the inconvenience or injury which the plaintiff would be likely to suffer if an injunction were  refused outweighs or is outweighed by the injury which the defendant would suffer if an injunction were granted”]

[In commercial litigation, where a plaintiff seeks an interlocutory injunction, a third requirement is that the plaintiff must give an “undertaking as to damages” to compensate the defendant for the effects of the interlocutory injunction and the plaintiff’s case ultimately fails

# Slide 10

Injunctions to enforce the criminal law

10

Onus v Alcoa of Australia Ltd (1981) 149 CLR 27 at 35, per Gibbs CJ, and 63, per Wilson J:

[“The case is … one in which two private citizens who cannot show that any right of their own has been infringed bring an action for the purpose of restraining another private citizen (Alcoa) from breaking the criminal law by acting in contravention of s. 21 of the Relics Act”

“It is … a fact in the present case that the appellants seek to use the civil process in order to enforce the criminal law. It is a jurisdiction which must be jealously guarded, its invocation even by the Attorney-General being confined normally to rather special circumstances: see, for example, Gouriet v Union of Post Office Workers … . It may be very pertinent to the question whether the relief by way of injunction should be granted, although, if relics are irreplaceable, preventive action by way of injunction would obviously be more consistent with the objects of the Act than punishment following their damage or destruction”]

# Slide 11

Injunctions to enforce the criminal law

11

Gouriet v Union of Post Office Workers [1978] AC 435, 481, per Lord Wilberforce:

[The invocation of “… assistance of civil courts in aid of the criminal law … is an exceptional power confined, in practice, to cases where an offence is frequently repeated in disregard of a, usually, inadequate penalty … or to cases of emergency. … It is not without its difficulties … [T]his jurisdiction – though proved useful on occasions – is one of great delicacy and is one to be used with caution”

Why must this use of injunctions be “jealously guarded”?

“If Parliament has imposed a sanction (eg, a fine of £1), without an increase in severity for repeated offences, it may seem wrong that the courts-civil courts-should think fit, by granting injunctions, breaches of which may attract unlimited sanctions, including imprisonment, to do what Parliament has not done. Moreover, where Parliament has (as here in the Post Office Act 1953) provided for trial of offences by indictment before a jury, it may seem wrong that the courts, applying a civil standard of proof, should in effect convict a subject without the prescribed trial”]
