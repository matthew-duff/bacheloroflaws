Topic Nine

the right to reasons, time limits,

exclusions from review

This guide considers the entitlement, created by the ADJR Act and JR Act, to written reasons for decisions to which these Acts apply. We then consider the short time limits found within the Acts (for seeking reasons and for seeking judicial review), as well as attempts to exclude administrative action from judicial review and how the ADJR Act and the JR Act have relevance to such attempts. In the context of this latter topic, we will be forced to revisit the concept of jurisdictional error.

## 1.	THE RIGHT TO REASONS

The ADJR Act and JR Act Parts 3 and 4 achieved three major reforms.  They:

- simplified administrative law judicial review procedures;

- codified the grounds of legality review; and

- created rights to seek reasons for administrative decisions.

WHY IS A RIGHT TO REASONS IMPORTANT?

“A personal reminiscence.  My family had little connection with the law.  When, as an articled clerk, I first ventured into Sydney courtrooms, I could not believe my good fortune.  Every day was a Perry Mason drama, in which I was a very minor character.  Was my life really to be filled with such exciting conflicts, resolved by such a majestic system?  More than three decades later, a measure of the same excitement and admiration lingers.

Most of the judicial officers I saw in those early days were articulate, logical, courteous.  They explained their decisions at the end of the case with conviction and sincerity.  Even when I disagreed, I did not doubt the integrity of their opinions.  But there was one judge into whose courtroom I always entered with trepidation.  He stared about with fury as the oath was administered, lest the slightest paper should rustle in the courtroom.  False dignity was everywhere about him.  Justice was often a stranger to that place.  Typically, in dismissing a claim by my client, he would intone no more than two sentences:

‘This claim fails.  There will be an award for the respondent.’

My heart was pumping.  The next case was called.  A flurry of books.  The papers were collected.  Bundled out of the courtroom with a confused client, it generally fell to me to try to explain what had happened.  The recriminations ensued.  The barrister shook hands and disappeared.  I, at 19 years, had to do what the judge had failed to do: explain to a client for whom the decision was often vitally important, the reasons for the decision.  No other judge would ordinarily impose such an obligation on me.  The sense of injustice and of an immaturely perceived denial of due process of law overwhelmed me at the time.  The searching eyes of the disbelieving client remain with me more than three decades later.”

From Kirby J, ‘Reasons for Judgment: Always Permissible, Usually Desirable and Often Obligatory’ (1994) 12 Australian Bar Review 121.  The focus of the article is on the right to reasons for judicial decisions (although see 130-131).

We are now considering the right to reasons for an administrative decision.  You might ask, however, whether similar issues of justice identified by Kirby J don’t still arise for administrative decisions.

ADMINISTRATIVE DECISIONS - NO COMMON LAW RIGHT TO REASONS

At common law there is no general requirement that an administrative decision-maker give reasons for her or his decision - see Public Service Board of NSW v Osmond (1986) 159 CLR 656 at 662 and 667-670.  Compare Edwards v Giudice (1999) 169 ALR 89 at [10]-[11] and [44]-[46]; Attorney-General of New South Wales v Kennedy Miller Television Pty Ltd (1998) 43 NSWLR 729 at 734-735 and 739; and Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW (2010) 239 CLR 531 at [83] where French CJ, Gummow, Hayne, Crennan, Kiefel and Bell JJ observed that “in … some cases the failure to give reasons may constitute a failure to exercise jurisdiction”.  For an English perspective, see M Elliott, ‘Has the Common Law Duty to Give Reasons Come of Age Yet?’ [2011] Public Law 56.

The making of an administrative decision without providing the reasons for the decision means that, in one sense, there has been an unaccountable exercises of power.  Kirby J had this to say about unaccountable exercises of power:

“Unaccountable power is tyranny.  If the exercise of power is accounted for, and is thought unlawful or unjust, it may be remedied.  If it is hidden in silence, the chances of a brooding sense of injustice exists, which will contribute to undermining the integrity and legitimacy of the polity that permits it” - at 133.

RIGHT TO REASONS - ADJR ACT AND JR ACT

The ADJR Act and JR Act include provisions which overcome this inadequacy of the common law.

What did these statutes seek to achieve?  Four points about the right to reasons have been emphasised by courts.

The right to reasons in the Acts is remedial.  One consequence of this is that courts have been careful not to construe the right “grudgingly or with a penchant for technicality” - see Gummow J in Australian Institute of Marine and Power Engineers v Secretary, Department of Transport (1986) 13 FCR 124 at 130.

2.	The right to reasons is meant to overcome the grievances of members of the public who are not told why something which affects them has been done by the government, ie it allows aggrieved members of the public to see how a decision was made, to assess whether to challenge the decision, and to form a view on the best way to challenge the decision - see Ellicott J in Burns v ANU (1982) 61 FLR 76 at 80 and 86; endorsed in Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Taveli (1990) 23 FCR 162 at 164 (per Davies J) and at 178 (per French J).  In RP Data Pty Ltd v Brisbane City Council (1995) 90 LGERA 42, at 45 and 47, the Queensland Court of Appeal suggests that an applicant for judicial review that doesn’t seek reasons will have a harder time establishing the illegality of the decision.  In Chandra v Webber (2010) 187 FCR 31 at [92]-[98], the Federal Court treated the failure to seek reasons as a discretionary factor in favour of denying relief in judicial review proceedings.  Finally, an important aspect of this second point is that the right to reasons is not dependent on showing that the decision in question is unlawful.  All that is required to have a right to reasons is that the ADJR Act or Part 3 JR Act are potentially applicable to the decision, ie a person is still entitled to reasons even if no ground of illegality can be established.  Lawful decisions of an administrative character made under an enactment are subject to the right to reasons under the ADJR Act/JR Act Part 4.

The right to reasons also has the potential to benefit decision-makers.  Courts have recognised that the right to reasons was designed to lead to better decision-making by requiring administrators to identify for themselves the reasons for their decisions.  The “overarching objective (of the Acts) must be to improve and maintain the standards of official fairness, rationality and compliance with law” - French J in Taveli at 178.  See also the Administrative Review Council’s 2025 Best Practice Guide for reasons:

https://www.ag.gov.au/legal-system/publications/administrative-review-council-best-practice-guide-statements-reasons

Courts have acknowledged that there must be a balancing between the needs of those aggrieved by a decision to know the reasons for the decision and “the necessity that the administration of this country be carried on effectively without undue intervention by the courts in the administrative process” - see Lockhart J in Ansett Transport Industries (Operations) Ltd v Secretary, Department of Aviation (1987) 73 ALR 193 at 197.

For a general discussion of the statutory right to reasons, see EARC’s report, chapters 11 and 12.

PART 4 JR ACT/ADJR Act

The central provision in Queensland is s32 of JR Act which provides that:

if you have a decision to which Part 4 applies; and

you are a person who is entitled to make an application to Supreme Court under s20 of the JR Act in relation to that decision;

then you may request that the decision-maker provide a written statement in relation to that decision.

The equivalent provision of the ADJR Act is s13.

A DECISION TO WHICH PART 4 APPLIES - SECTION 31 JR ACT

A decision to which Part 4 applies is defined in JR Act s31.  Essentially it has the same definition as that in s4, ie a “decision” to which the JR Act applies, except that excluded from the scope of the expression defined in s31 are:

- decisions for which a statement of reasons has already been given; and

- decisions excluded by Schedule 2 of the JR Act.

Generally, in relation to schedule exclusions, the courts will construe exclusions of the right to reasons strictly – see, for example, Deane v Commissioner of Stamp Duties [1996] 1 QdR 133 at 137 and Hocken v Pointing [1993] 2 QdR 659 at 661; cf Pepper v Attorney-General [2008] 2 QdR 353 at [1] and [13]-[34].  It is important to always carefully check the schedule.  Note, for eg, that clause 13 of the Schedule 2 excludes a right to reasons for tendering decisions.

The ADJR Act excludes decisions from the ADJR Act equivalent of JR Act s32, not only by schedule (ie Schedule 2 of the ADJR Act), but also via regulations.  EARC, as a deliberate step, recommended against a power in the Qld JR Act to exempt decisions from the reasons requirement via regulations.  EARC sought to ensure that any limitation of the right to reasons in Qld would be the responsibility of parliament alone and not that of administrators acting through regulations - see EARC’s report at 113.  While the Queensland Parliament followed EARC’s recommendation, note the effect of s138 of the Prostitution Act 1999 (Qld).

The ADJR Act equivalent of s31 also excludes decisions for which reasons are available under s268 of the ART Act.

JR ACT PART 4 AND BEING ENTITLED TO MAKE A JR ACT SECTION 20 APPLICATION

The second aspect of JR Act s32 is the need to be person who is entitled to make an application under s20 JR Act.  An applicant must be aggrieved in order to be entitled to apply under JR Act s20.  Therefore under JR Act ss 31 and 32, applicants for reasons will need to establish all of the elements discussed in the last few classes, ie applicants will have to show that there has been a decision (conduct or a failure to decide is not enough) of an administrative character made under an enactment or a non-statutory scheme or program.  Applicants will also have to show that they are aggrieved by the decision.

Note that review under JR Act Parts 3 and 5 can overlap and so it is possible to obtain reasons under Part 4 when seeking review of a decision under Part 5, provided that decision is also potentially reviewable under Part 3 (in particular under s20).  However, for those decisions which are not potentially reviewable under s20, reasons cannot be ordered under JR Act Part 4 regardless of whether review is available for the decisions under JR Act Part 5.

“Entitled” in s32 has been interpreted generously.  A person is entitled to make an application under s20 of the JR Act (or its ADJR Act equivalent) even after the person, in fact, has made that s20 application - see United Airlines v Secretary, Department of Transport and Communications (1990) 26 FCR 598 at 608.  Sackville J has also held that an applicant was still able to obtain reasons despite the fact that the decision in question had ceased to operate - obtaining reasons still had some utility for the applicant - see North Coast Environment Council Inc v Minister for Resources (1994) 55 FCR 492 at 496 and 515.

STATEMENT CONTAINING REASONS

In relation to the “written statement” referred to in JR Act s32, see s34 of the JR Act which provides that the statement must contain the “reasons” for the decision.  See s3 of JR Act which provides that the “reasons” in relation to a decision means:

- findings on material questions of fact;

- a reference to the evidence or other material on which the findings were based;

as well as the reasons for the decision.

OTHER WAYS OF FINDING OUT ABOUT DECISIONS

It is useful to distinguish the right to reasons under the JR Act/ADJR Act from other methods available to find out about what a government decision-maker has done.  The ADJR Act/JR Act Part 4 does not let you look at the specific evidence, ie the documents etc, upon which decision-maker based her or his decision.

CONTRAST FOI

An application under freedom of information legislation (in Qld the relevant legislation is the Right to Information Act 2009) will potentially allow you to look at copies of documents in the possession or control of the decision-maker, ie raw evidence.  The right to reasons under the ADJR Act/JR Act Part 4 and freedom of information rights under FOI legislation therefore complement each other.

CONTRAST OTHER COURT PROCEDURES

Once a person has commenced proceedings in a court (including ADJR Act and JR Act proceedings), the court has power to order disclosure/discovery and inspection of documents - see Rule 573 and more generally Chapter 7 of Queensland’s Uniform Civil Procedure Rules; and Part 20 of the Federal Court Rules.  These court processes again give access to raw evidence in the possession or power of the parties to the litigation (and potentially to documents in the possession of third parties).  Even in cases where a person has no right to reasons under the ADJR Act/JR Act Part 4 or its equivalent, the courts (provided judicial review proceedings are on foot) can still order that disclosure/discovery and inspection of documents go ahead.  The power to order disclosure/discovery and inspection of documents is, however, discretionary and courts will take into account presence or absence of rights under the JR Act Part 4 or its ADJR Act equivalent - see Australian Securities Commission v Somerville (1994) 128 ALR 132 at 141-142 and FCT v Nestle Australia Ltd (1986) 69 ALR 445 at 452-3.

The right to reasons under the ADJR Act/JR Act Part 4 has a number of advantages over general litigation mechanisms:

The right to reasons is available before any court proceedings have been commenced

The closest thing in conventional litigation to the ADJR Act/JR Act right to reasons is calling the decision-maker as a witness in the court proceedings and asking the decision-maker in the witness box “how did you reach your decision?”  This involves a much more expensive process than that available under the ADJR Act/JR Act Part 4;

The right to reasons (if available) does not depend on the exercise of any judicial discretion;

The right to reasons under the JR Act (although not the ADJR Act) is supported by a special costs rule in s50 JR Act.

One distinct advantage of the general litigation process of discovery/disclosure is that it potentially applies regardless of the status of the parties to litigation, ie regardless of whether they are governmental or not.  The right to reasons (and FOI) is only available vis-à-vis government bodies.

For more information on the relevant rules of civil court procedure, see B Cairns, Australian Civil Procedure, (13th ed, Thomson Reuters, 2024) Ch 11.  For criminal procedure, consult a standard criminal law text.

FEATURES OF A STATUTORY STATEMENT OF REASONS

See the discussion of relevant cases in EARC’s report at 123-125.

In the context of written statements of reasons under the ADJR Act, it has been observed that:

“... decision-maker[s] should set out ... [their] understanding of the relevant law, any findings of fact on which ... [their] conclusions depend (especially if those facts have been in dispute), and the reasoning processes which led ... [them] to those conclusions ... [They] should do so in clear and unambiguous language, not in vague generalities or the formal language of legislation.  The appropriate length of the statement covering such matters will depend upon considerations such as the nature and importance of the decision, its complexity and the time available to formulate the statement.  Often those factors may suggest a brief statement of one or two pages only” - see Ansett Transport Industries (Operations) Pty Ltd v Wraith (1983) 48 ALR 500 at 507.

Findings of fact set out in a statement of reasons need only be those which are material to decision.

Evidence relied upon need only be referred to and need not be set out in full in the statement.

Decision-makers must not simply list all of the documents that were before them.  Some attempt must be made to identify the evidence upon which decision was specifically based - see ARM Constructions Pty Ltd v DCT (1986) 65 ALR 343 at 349.  Decision-makers have been encouraged to express their reasons in a manner that reflects their own individualised consideration of matters that have come before them for decision rather than in formulaic terms - Roesner v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection [2015] FCAFC 132 at [40].

The courts are concerned not to be overly strict on decision-makers.  See, for example, Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Wu Shan Liang (1996) 185 CLR 259 at 271-2; ARM at 349; and Ansett Transport Industries (Operations) Ltd v Secretary, Department of Aviation (1987) 73 ALR 193 at 198-9 where Lockhart J acknowledged that in certain cases it may be difficult, if not impossible, for a decision-maker (in reasons) to assign varying degrees of weight to particular matters considered by the decision-maker to be relevant to the decision.  See also Ergon Energy Corporation Ltd v Rice-McDonald [2010] 1 QdR 516 at [14], [15] and [17], per McMurdo J.

In some cases, reasons need only include a brief account of the relevant law.  In other cases, detailed references may be necessary.  The decision-maker does not have to refer to the law in the same way that a barrister would advise her or his client - see Ansett v Secretary, Department of Aviation at 198.

APPLICATIONS FOR FURTHER STATEMENTS

If an applicant obtains reasons under Part 4 which are not as required by the JR Act, in certain circumstances, they can apply to court for a further statement of reasons - see s40 of the JR Act and ADJR Act s13(7).

EVIDENTIARY POINTS

The use of a statement of reasons in subsequent judicial proceedings raises complex rules of evidence.  A formal statement of reasons made under the ADJR Act/JR Act Part 4 may not be admitted by a court as evidence of what were the actual reasons for the decision-maker’s decision - see Taveli, where one member of Federal Court recognised that a decision-maker might try to draft a statement of reasons well after the making of the actual decision in such a way as to undermine the possibility of successful judicial review of the decision.  If court does not consider that the statement of reasons reflects what were the actual reasons of the decision-maker when the decision was made, the court is not bound to accept reasons as accurate and the statement may not even be admissible as evidence of the reasons - see Taveli at 167, 179 and 189.  See also EARC’s report at 109-110 and 115-120.  Compare S14/2002 v Refugee Review Tribunal [2004] FCAFC 171, [13], [15] and [27]-[35]; Braemer Power Projects Pty Ltd v Chief Executive, Dept of Mines and Energy [2010] 1 QdR 403 at [98]-[106]; and Alexander v Australian Community Pharmacy Authority (2010) 265 ALR 424 at [54]-[68], [72]-[78] and [84]-[91].

Taveli also raises the question of the cross-examination of the decision-maker during judicial review proceedings.  Whether a court allows cross-examination of a decision-maker may well be critical in cases where the statement of reasons undermines grounds of judicial review raised by the applicant - see Taveli at 165-167, 183 and 189-190.

If a statement of reasons made under the ADJR Act/JR Act Part 4 contains something which indicates that the decision was made unlawfully then the courts will readily place weight on such a statement following normal evidence rules, ie as an admission against interest - see Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Arslan (1984) 55 ALR 361 at 363.

If a statement of reasons made under the ADJR Act/JR Act Part 4 does not include a reference to the consideration of a certain factor during the decision-making process, a court may still infer (depending on other evidence) that the factor was, or was not, considered by the decision-maker - see ARM at 350.

A statement of reasons can only ever be evidence of views of the decision-maker when making the decision.  The statement is not direct evidence of the facts set out in the statement.  For eg, if the decision-maker’s reasons include the conclusion that X is person of bad character, that will not be evidence that X is in fact of bad character but only that that was the view of decision-maker - see Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Arslan at 364.

## RIGHT TO REASONS - PROCEDURAL POINTS

There are technical provisions in the ADJR Act/JR Act Part 4 which deal with time limits, court applications, and further exemptions.  The JR Act Part 4 also has special provisions regarding court costs associated with enforcing the right to reasons (there is no equivalent provision in the ADJR Act).  It is important to read carefully the relevant sections, eg JR Act ss 33, 35 - 40.

DUTY OF DECISION-MAKERS TO GIVE REASONS

A correlative duty on a decision-maker (ie correlative to the applicant’s right to reasons) is created, for eg, by JR Act s33, to give an appropriate statement of reasons.  The ADJR Act and Part 4 of the JR Act do not require decision-makers to give reasons automatically once they make a decision, ie the duty to give a statement of reasons arises only after a request for a statement of reasons has been made.  Non-compliance with a statutory duty to provide a written statement of reasons will constitute a non-jurisdictional error of law - Wingfoot Australia Partners Pty Ltd v Kocak (2013) 252 CLR 480, [24]-[30].

TO WHOM SHOULD AN APPLICATION FOR REASONS BE MADE?

An applicant for reasons should apply to the decision-maker for reasons or, if the decision-maker is State Cabinet or the Governor in Council, the applicant needs to apply to the relevant Minister - see s32(2) of the JR Act.

TIME LIMITS FOR APPLYING FOR REASONS

Two possible time limits are provided under the JR Act s33 (with the ADJR Act having similar provisions):

If the applicant has been given a written record of the decision which has been made, then they must apply within 28 days of receipt of that record - see JR Act s33(4)(a);

If there has been no written record of the decision given to the applicant, then they must apply for reasons within a reasonable time after the decision has been made - JR Act see s33(4)(b).

If the decision-maker intends to refuse to give reasons because of delay in applying for reasons, the decision-maker must give the applicant notice of refusal within fourteen days of receiving the request for reasons - see JR Act s33(5).  An applicant can challenge such a refusal relying on JR Act s33(6).

IF AN APPLICATION FOR REASONS IS MADE WITHIN TIME A RESPONSE IS REQUIRED WITHIN 28 DAYS

If the applicant’s request for reasons is received within the appropriate time limits, the decision-maker has 28 days within which to respond.  The decision-maker will either have to give a statement of reasons within that period or do one of two things, ie:

1.	Give the applicant notice that the decision-maker doesn’t think that the applicant is entitled to reasons - see JR Act s33(2)(a) [an applicant can then apply under JR Act s38 to the Supreme Court for an order that a statement of reasons be given.  Section 38 is broader than its ADJR Act equivalent.  The Queensland provision can also be used by an applicant who is simply ignored by a decision-maker, ie where decision-maker doesn’t even bother to respond to a request for reasons.  Under the ADJR Act, such an applicant would have to seek mandamus, or an order under s7 of the ADJR Act]; or

2.	The decision-maker can itself go to the Supreme Court and seek an order under JR Act s39 that the applicant is not entitled to reasons under JR Act s32.

OTHER EXCEPTIONS TO THE RIGHT TO REASONS

Sections 35, 36 and 37 of the JR Act create important exceptions to right to reasons.  The sections provide that the following types of information need not be divulged in a statement of reasons:

- Confidential information which relates to personal or business affairs of someone other than the applicant - see s35;

- Information, the disclosure of which would be contrary to the public interest, and which has been so declared by the Attorney-General - see s36.

Please read the sections carefully.  Six general points can be made in relation to these provisions (and the equivalent provision of the ADJR Act, ie s13A).

JR Act s35(2)(c) refers to government “competitive commercial activities”.  This expression is defined in s3. This renders information confidential if it relates to certain commercial activities of government.  This limitation works in conjunction with clause 14 in Schedule 2 of the JR Act.

JR Act s36(1)(a) sets out an exception for “deliberations or a decision of Cabinet or a Committee of Cabinet”.

JR Act s36(1) addresses the Attorney-General’s certificate.  Courts may go behind such certificates - see Haj-Ismail v Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs (1981) 36 ALR 516 at 526-527.

Under the JR Act s37, notices are to be given to applicants explaining why reasons aren’t being given.

Exempt information is normally removed from a statement of reasons, however JR Act s37 allows a decision-maker to refuse to give any statement in cases where removing exempted information from the statement would make the statement false or misleading.

JR Act s 37(5) preserves the court’s litigation related powers to order discovery of documents, to require evidence be given, or to order the production of documents.

THE RIGHT TO REASONS AND INTERLOCUTORY ORDERS

On the relationship between the right to reasons and obtaining a stay in relation to the decision in respect of which reasons are sought – see Capello v Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs (1980) 2 ALD 1014; and Re Ralkon Agricultural Company Pty Ltd and the Aboriginal Development Commission, unreported, 12 November 1986, affirmed on appeal (1987) 74 ALR 505.

COSTS

Note the operation of s50 of the JR Act which introduces a special rule for awarding costs in court applications relating to reasons.  Note also that there is no express reference in s50 to costs incurred in an application brought by a decision-maker under JR Act s39.  There is no ADJR Act equivalent to JR Act s50 - see Faulkner v Conwell (1989) 99 ALR 92 at 93.  See also s57 of the JR Act which precludes the imposition of a filing fee in respect of applications under the JR Act Part 4.

JR ACT - TIME FOR MAKING PART 3 APPLICATIONS; LIMITS ON REVIEW; AND POWERS OF COURT

Attention now turns to provisions of JR Act which allow Part 3 review to proceed.  Most of these provisions set out technical rules which can be understood by reading relevant statutory provisions.  We will need to look at:

- time limits for making Part 3 applications;

- other mechanisms for excluding decisions, conduct etc from judicial review; and

- the powers of the court when judicial review is sought.

## 2.	TIME LIMITS FOR JUDICIAL REVIEW APPLICATIONS UNDER PART 3 JR ACT/ADJR ACT

On time limits for judicial review applications generally, see Enid Campbell and Matthew Groves, ‘Time Limitations on Applications for Judicial Review’ (2004) 32 Federal Law Review 29.

In Qld, see the JR Act s26.  The equivalent ADJR Act provision (ie s11) operates in the same way.  There is complexity because the time limits run differently depending on the different ways people can be notified about decisions.  For a discussion of the general policy behind the different time limits - see Stergis v Boucher (1989) 86 ALR 179 at 180.

Essentially, the approach is different depending on whether the original decision-maker has given written notice to the applicant of the decision being challenged.  If written notice of the decision has been given to the applicant, then JR Act s26(1), (2) and (5) apply.

If no written notice of the decision has been given to the applicant, then JR Act s26 (3) and (4) apply.

WHERE AN APPLICANT HAS BEEN GIVEN WRITTEN NOTICE OF A DECISION

The basic rule in JR Act s26(2) and 26(5)(d) is that the time within which to make an application expires 28 days after the giving of written notice of the decision.

In cases where the applicant is given written reasons for the decision (whether through reliance on JR Act Part 4 or not), the time limits work in a different way - see s26(5).

EXTENSIONS OF TIME TO APPLY

Notice that the period within which an applicant must apply under s26(2) can be extended by Courts - see JR Act s26(1)(b).  Cases dealing with this discretion have established a number of points.

The starting point is that there is a prima facie rule that proceedings not commenced in time should not be allowed to proceed - see Fitzgerald J in Lucic v Nolan (1982) 45 ALR 411 at 416; endorsed by Moynihan J in Kuku Djungan Aboriginal Corporation v Christensen [1993] 2 QdR 663 at 665.

Generally, applicants must normally give an adequate explanation for their delay in applying for judicial review.  Thomas J acknowledged that this may not necessarily be a pre-condition for an extension.  The inability to give an explanation for the delay will, however, normally be a factor against extension - see Hoffman v Queensland Local Government Superannuation Board [1994] 1 QdR 369 at 372; compare Hakim v Minister for Immigration, Local Government and Ethnic Affairs (1992) 29 ALD 193 at 194-195.

Other factors relevant to granting an extension are usefully summarised by Wilcox J in the Federal Court decision in Hunter Valley Developments Pty Ltd v Minister for Home Affairs and Environment (1984) 58 ALR 305 at 310-312.

Among the factors which may be relevant are “notions of what is fair and equitable in the circumstances, whether any prejudice will be occasioned to the ... [government decision-maker], the public interest, and (where such a view is possible) the merits of the substantial application for review” - per Thomas J in Hoffman at 372.  See also Wirth v Mackay Hospital and Health Service [2016] QSC 39 at [123]-[129] (per Bond J).

NO WRITTEN NOTICE OF DECISION - SECTIONS 26(3) AND 26(4) JR ACT

Sections 26(3) and (4) of the JR Act are difficult to understand.  Essentially they apply in cases where JR Act s26(2) does not apply, ie where there is no document given to the person seeking review which sets out the terms of the decision.  In such cases, the applicant has a “reasonable time” within which to make an application.  See Stergis v Boucher (1989) 86 ALR 174 at 178-181; and Worthley v ASC (1993) 114 ALR 524 at 529.

## 3.	EXCLUSIONS FROM JUDICIAL REVIEW UNDER THE ADJR ACT/JR ACT

The ADJR Act equivalent of s4(a) of JR Act excludes review via Schedule 1 of ADJR Act.

The Queensland JR Act has a similar mechanism in s18(2).  JR Act s18(2) removes, from the scope of judicial review, Acts and decisions mentioned in Parts 1 and 2 of Schedule 1 of JR Act.

Note also s18A of the JR Act which excludes certain decisions of government owned corporations from review under the JR Act.  Section 18A operates via Schedule 6 of the JR Act – they remove avenues of judicial review to ensure that government-owned corporations can compete on even terms with non-governmental businesses.  See, for example, Waratah Coal Pty Ltd v Nicholls [2013] QSC 68 at [82]-[94]; and Chapter 7 of EARC’s report.

In 2000, the Commonwealth Parliament inserted s9A into the ADJR Act.  This section significantly restricts judicial review under the ADJR Act of administrative decisions made by lower courts in criminal proceedings.

NO EXCLUSIONS OF REVIEW BY REGULATIONS UNDER THE QLD JR ACT

ADJR Act allows decisions to be excluded from review by regulations - see sections 19 and 19A of the ADJR Act.  EARC recommended, however, and the Queensland Parliament accepted, that there should be no similar power to exclude, via regulations, judicial review under the Qld JR Act - see EARC’s report at [7.58].

OUSTER/PRIVATIVE CLAUSES

Section 18(1) of the JR Act disables ouster or privative clauses in Acts where the clause pre-dates the JR Act.  “Ouster” or “privative” clauses in statutory enactments purport to exclude judicial review of the legality of government decision-making.

The JR Act s18(1) does not disable post JR Act clauses - see generally South East Drainage Board v Savings Bank of South Australia (1939) 62 CLR 603.

In relation to new ouster clauses (and those preserved by Schedule 1 of JR Act), the question of their effect on avenues of judicial review critically depends on the actual wording of the particular ouster clause. Courts have generally sought to read down such clauses. It has often been held that clauses are not effective to exclude judicial review in cases where there has been jurisdictional error, and there are now constitutional prohibitions on ouster clauses that seek to exclude judicial review by the High Court, or State Supreme Courts, in respect of government decisions tainted by jurisdictional error.

(a)	Privative clauses and jurisdictional error

In order to fully understand the operation of privative clauses, it is necessary to briefly revisit the difficult notion of “jurisdictional error”. The concept of jurisdictional error is relevant to determining the scope of judicial review at common law and for the purposes of High Court’s constitutional jurisdiction under s75(v) of the Constitution. At common law in Australia an aggrieved individual can obtain a remedy (a prerogative writ) by demonstrating that a ‘jurisdictional error’ or a ‘non-jurisdictional error of law (on the face of the decision-maker’s record)’ has been made. Pursuant to the High Court’s original jurisdiction via s75(v) of the Constitution, aggrieved individuals must normally demonstrate that a ‘jurisdictional error’ has been made, in order to or obtain a remedy. According to the High Court, the constitutional remedies of prohibition and mandamus can only be obtained if a decision of a Commonwealth officer involves jurisdictional error.

So, taking either the common law path to judicial review (important for people in jurisdictions without a judicial review statute, such as South Australia and NSW) or taking the constitutional route (via s75(v)), may require applicants for review to satisfy one (or more) of the elements of a jurisdictional error. This is not a straightforward task insofar as, by contrast with the ADJR Act or the JRA 1991 (Qld) — which openly and transparently enumerate generic grounds of judicial review — there are no clues provided in s75(v) of the Constitution as to what a jurisdictional error means. The meaning of “jurisdictional error” can only be grasped by reading relevant cases, including *Craig v South Australia, *Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW and *LPDT v Minister for Immigration, Citizenship, Migrant Services and Multicultural Affairs (2024) 280 CLR 321 at [2]-[16].

Before embarking on the reading of these cases you should note that the meaning of jurisdictional error is unsettled: it remains a principle of judicial review which has not been defined exhaustively by the courts. Indeed, this may be deliberate on the part of the courts who may wish to avoid being “pinned down” on its precise content and application.

What constitutes a jurisdictional error?

Jurisdiction means having the authority to decide, and a “jurisdictional error” can be made by a decision-maker (either a court or administrative decision-maker) lacking authority or acting in excess of authority to decide.  The concepts of jurisdiction and jurisdictional error originate in respect of the exercise of judicial power but are now applicable to exercises of administrative power by government officials.

A decision-maker makes a “jurisdictional error” if they do something that they lack the power to do (want of jurisdiction) or if they make a decision outside of the limits of their functions and powers (excess of jurisdiction). Jurisdictional error reflects and expresses the gravity of an error made that warrants a remedy via the common law (prerogative writs) or s75(v) of the Constitution (constitutional writs) as the case may be.  Jurisdictional error is also now considered to often (but not always – ie not in cases of bias) require a causal link between a legal error and the decision being challenged, through the concept of materiality, ie “that there is a realistic possibility that the decision that was made in fact could have been different if the error had not occurred.” A realistic possibility is one that is more than “fanciful or improbable” - *LPDT at [7] and [14].

The scope of jurisdictional error was explored by the High Court in *Craig’s case (an appeal from the South Australian Supreme Court), and more recently in *Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW. Craig’s case demonstrates that jurisdictional error is a more limited concept when applied to (inferior) courts when compared with other administrative decision-makers (eg tribunals). In Craig, the High Court drew a distinction between inferior courts and other bodies exercising governmental power for the purposes of defining jurisdictional error. You should read carefully the judgment at 176-178 where the Court explains, broadly, when an inferior court falls into jurisdictional error, and then review the relevant (obvious and less obvious) examples given to illustrate that point. The examples are not exhaustive of the concept of jurisdictional error.

You should go on to consider why the “logic” of Anisminic v Foreign Compensation Commission [1969] 2 AC 147 (a decision of the UK House of Lords) — which led to the distinction between jurisdictional and non-jurisdictional errors collapsing under English law — was not fully transposed into Australian common law and applied to inferior courts in addition to administrative decision-makers.

The High Court in Craig discusses the concept of jurisdictional error as it applies to administrative bodies at 179. In short, what counts as a “jurisdictional error” in the context of an inferior court, is different (more limited) relative to the context of governmental (administrative) decision-making. The point here is that inferior courts are free to make some errors within their jurisdiction (not all legal errors made by the courts will be classified as “jurisdictional” and attract a remedy via judicial review).

What sort of errors can be made by an inferior court within jurisdiction? A court can make an “error of law” where it fails to identify relevant issues, or formulate the correct questions, or determine what is (and is not) relevant evidence. These “errors of law” cannot be remedied via judicial review relying on the writs of prohibition or mandamus. But that is not to say they go uncorrected. The High Court noted in Craig, that such errors may be corrected through an appeal (assuming there is a right to appeal a court’s decision) and the writ of certiorari may be available if the error of law is apparent on the face of the record of the inferior court.

What is important to appreciate is that there is a substantial (but not complete) overlap between the grounds of judicial review set out in the judicial review statutes (which govern review over administrative action) and the concept of jurisdictional error as it applies to administrative decision-makers. Accordingly, an administrative decision-maker who, for example, asks the wrong question (error of law), relies on irrelevant considerations, decides in a legally unreasonable manner, or fails to accord procedural fairness, makes a jurisdictional error (at common law/for constitutional (s 75) purposes) and these sorts of errors are also specified in the ADJR Act or JR Act.

But the ADJR Act and JR Act cast the net wider than the concept of jurisdictional error as it applies to administrative decision-makers: for example, “a breach of a procedure required by law to be observed” is enumerated in the ADJR Act (s 5(1)(b)) as a ground of review but it may not amount to a jurisdictional error because it might not be an essential precondition to the decision being made.

What are the consequences of jurisdictional error and how does this inter-relate with privative clauses?

Legal invalidity: A decision infected by a “jurisdictional error” is deemed, in law, to be no decision at all (it is a nullity). So, although there has been a decision made in fact, a decision that is made without legal authority is treated, retrospectively, by the court as invalid/void. Effectively it is as if the decision never existed. This may be contrasted with the position where a decision is deemed to be unlawful but not legally invalid. A decision made in “breach of procedures required by law to be observed” may be unlawful (and have legal consequences prospectively) but may not necessarily be deemed invalid (a consequence of a jurisdictional error) with retrospective legal effects. The distinction between legal invalidity and unlawfulness was explored by the High Court in the case of Project Blue Sky (1998) 194 CLR 355, which we looked at earlier in the course.

A finding of jurisdictional error, spelling invalidity, is significant when it comes to the operation of ouster (privative) clauses. These clauses attempt to limit the capacity of the courts to engage in judicial review over administrative decisions, by (inter alia) stipulating available grounds of review, or by forbidding the grant of remedies, or imposing strict time limits beyond which no review can be sought. Purporting to deny individuals judicial review over public decision-making gives rise to constitutional issues and tensions, which go to fundamental questions about the rule of law over administrative action, separation of powers (checks and balances) and access to justice.

A leading case dealing with (and perhaps reconciling) these matters, is Plaintiff S157 v Commonwealth (2003) 211 CLR 476, which you should read carefully before the workshop especially at [54]-[70] and [98]-[104]. This important case, which we have looked at in earlier classes, concerned the question of whether a decision, tainted by reason of procedural unfairness, could be insulated from judicial review (and remedy) by operation of a privative clause, in light of s75(v) of the Constitution.  According to Gaudron, McHugh, Gummow, Kirby and Hayne JJ (at [98]) the controversy over Federal ouster clauses reflected “two fundamental constitutional principles”:

“First, the jurisdiction of this Court to grant relief under s75(v) of the Constitution cannot be removed by or under a law made by the Parliament. Specifically, the jurisdiction to grant s75(v) relief where there has been jurisdictional error by an officer of the Commonwealth cannot be removed.  Secondly, the judicial power of the Commonwealth cannot be exercised otherwise than in accordance with Ch III. The Parliament cannot confer on a non-judicial body the power to conclusively determine the limits of its own jurisdiction.”

Prior to the High Court’s decision in Plaintiff S157/2002, it was common for Australian courts (State and Federal) interpreting ouster clauses to apply the rule of statutory construction identified in R v Hickman; Ex parte Fox and Clinton (1945) 70 CLR 598.  According to Dixon J (at 614-615) in Hickman, ouster clauses:

“… are not interpreted as meaning to set at large the courts or other judicial bodies to whose decision they relate.  Such a clause is interpreted as meaning that no decision which is in fact given by the body concerned shall be invalidated on the ground that it has not conformed to the requirements governing its proceedings or the exercise of its authority or has not confined its acts within the limits laid down by the instrument giving it authority, provided always that its decision is a bona fide attempt to exercise its power, that it relates to the subject matter of the legislation, and that it is reasonably capable of reference to the power given to the body.”

In Plaintiff S157/2002, however, Gaudron, McHugh, Gummow, Kirby and Hayne JJ observed that “… the so-called ‘Hickman principle’ is simply a rule of construction allowing for the reconciliation of apparently conflicting statutory provisions. Once this is accepted, as it must be, it follows that there can be no general rule as to the meaning or effect of privative clauses. Rather, the meaning of a privative clause must be ascertained from its terms; and if that meaning appears to conflict with the provision pursuant to which some action has been taken or some decision made, its effect will depend entirely on the outcome of its reconciliation with that other provision” – at [60].

In the State context, the position now appears to be essentially the same as the Federal position.  In Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW (2010) 239 CLR 531, French CJ, Gummow, Hayne, Crennan, Kiefel and Bell JJ, relying on the statement in Forge (2006) 228 CLR 45 at 76 [63] that “it is beyond the legislative power of a State so to alter the constitution or character of its Supreme Court that it ceases to meet the constitutional description” of “the Supreme Court of a State” in Chapter III of the Constitution, observed that:

“[t]he supervisory jurisdiction of the Supreme Courts was at federation, and remains, the mechanism for the determination and the enforcement of the limits on the exercise of State executive and judicial power by persons and bodies other than the Supreme Court.  That supervisory role of the Supreme Courts exercised through the grant of prohibition, certiorari and mandamus (and habeas corpus) was, and is, a defining characteristic of those courts. … [Such] observations made about the constitutional significance of the supervisory jurisdiction of the State Supreme Courts point to the continued need for, and utility of, the distinction between jurisdictional and non jurisdictional error in the Australian constitutional context.  The distinction marks the relevant limit on State legislative power.  Legislation which would take from a State Supreme Court power to grant relief on account of jurisdictional error is beyond State legislative power.  Legislation which denies the availability of relief for non jurisdictional error of law appearing on the face of the record is not beyond power” – [96], [98] and [100].

On Kirk, see also Public Service Association of South Australia Inc v Industrial Relations Commission (SA) (2012) 249 CLR 398 at [49]-[66] (regarding mandamus); JJ Spigelman, ‘The centrality of jurisdictional error’ (2010) 10 The Judicial Review 11; and C Finn, ‘Constitutionalising supervisory review at State level: The end of Hickman?’ (2010) 21 Public Law Review 92.

McMurdo P has suggested that the inherent jurisdiction to issue injunctive and declaratory relief may satisfy the requirements identified by the High Court in Kirk – see Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2012] 1 QdR 525, at [6]-[9].  See also Harvey v Commissioner of State Revenue [2014] QSC 183 at [104]-[108] (per Jackson J); and Berenyi v Maynard [2015] QSC 370 at [32] (per Philippides JA).  Contrast Kaldas v Barbour (2017) 107 NSWLR 341 at [195]-[196] (per Bathurst CJ), [347], [349] and [361] (per Basten JA), and [380] (per Macfarlan JA).  White JA in Northbuild, applying Kirk, read down schedule exclusions in the JR Act so as to allow review for jurisdictional error notwithstanding the provisions of Schedule 1 of the JR Act – see Northbuild at [78].

See also Deputy Commissioner of Taxation v Richard Walter Pty Ltd (1995) 183 CLR 168; Minister for Immigration and Multicultural Affairs v Yusuf (2001) 206 CLR 323; SAAP v Minister for Immigration and Multicultural and Indigenous Affairs (2005) 215 ALR 162.  For a discussion of the Hickman principle in a Federal and State context, see Mark Seymour, ‘Privative Clauses in Administrative Law: Recent Developments’ (2003) 77 Australian Law Journal 757.

## 4.	POWERS OF THE SUPERIOR COURTS HEARING APPLICATIONS UNDER THE ADJR ACT/JR ACT

Courts have various powers available at different stages of judicial review proceedings.

Orders can be made at interlocutory hearings;

Orders can be made at the final hearing of judicial review proceedings.

Remember also that appeals are possible from judicial review decisions made by the Supreme Court under the JR Act Part 3 (and from judicial review decisions made by a court under the ADJR Act).

INTERLOCUTORY ORDERS

Interlocutory orders are, relevantly, court orders made before a final determination is made by a court as to whether grounds of legality review under ADJR Act/JR Act Part 3 or Part 5 have been established.

STAYS OF ADMINISTRATIVE DECISIONS BEING REVIEWED UNDER THE ADJR ACT/PART 3 JR ACT

Consider the following scenario: an administrative decision-maker decides to cancel your licence to trade, and it is a criminal offence to trade without a licence.  If you believe that the decision cancelling your licence is unlawful and reviewable under the JR Act Part 3, you will want to have decision “stayed”, ie suspended, until judicial review proceedings in respect of the cancellation have run their course.  Otherwise you will be unable to trade in the meantime.  You can apply under s29 of JR Act for a stay of original decision.

It is clear from the JR Act s29(1) that simply applying for judicial review under Part 3 will not stop the administrative decision from operating.  To stop the decision having any effect until reviewed, you must specifically apply for a stay under JR Act s29.  The equivalent provisions of the ADJR Act are ss15 and 15A of the ADJR Act.

The Supreme Court’s power to order a stay under JR Act s29 is linked to the JR Act s20 (review of decisions).  If an applicant wants to stay Qld government conduct while seeking judicial review of the legality of that conduct, then an interlocutory injunction could be sought under s9(3) of the Civil Proceedings Act 2011 (Qld); see also s23 of Federal Court of Australia Act 1976.

STAYS - THE TEST

Generally, the standard interlocutory remedy equivalent to a JR Act s29 stay is an interlocutory injunction.  Interlocutory injunctions are designed to maintain status quo until judicial review is complete.  This remedy developed in a non-administrative law context.  To obtain an interlocutory injunction, the applicant must normally establish two things:

-	That there is a prima facie case/serious question to be tried raised by the court proceedings; and

-	That the balance of convenience favours the making of the interlocutory order.

Invariably, an applicant for such relief in non-administrative law litigation must also give an undertaking in respect of any damage that the interlocutory order may cause to the respondent.  The applicant will be required to compensate the respondent for any damage if the final hearing of the judicial review application goes against the applicant.

Can an applicant seeking a stay under the JR Act s29 be required to give an undertaking as to damages? For obiter comments suggesting no (based on differences between JR Act s29 and ADJR Act s15), see re Murphy and the Minister for Minerals and Energy (1995) 2 QAR 94 at 99.

There has been some apparent tension in applying the prima facie case/balance of convenience test in an administrative law context.  Some Federal Court decisions have cast doubt on the applicability of the requirements for interlocutory injunctions in the context of the ADJR Act s15 (ie equivalent of JR Act s29), suggesting that all an applicant need establish is that it is just to stay the decision.  Other cases (and probably the weight of authority) support application of the test for interlocutory injunctions to the ADJR Act/JR Act provisions - see discussion in Faingold v Zammit (1984) 1 FCR 87 at 91-92; and KC Park Safe (Brisbane) Pty Ltd v Cairns City Council [1997] 1 QdR 497 at 500-507.  See also Mossop, ‘Interlocutory Injunctions in Public Law’ (1993-4) 11 Australian Bar Review 59.

There may not be any real difference between the approaches to stays under the ADJR Act/JR Act and to applications for interlocutory injunctions.  Dawson J observed, in the context of an application for an interlocutory injunction, that “[t]he primary duty is to do complete justice in the cause” - see A v Hayden (No 1) (1984) 59 ALJR 1 at 5.  This statement was endorsed in context of ADJR Act in Re Aboriginal Development Commission and Ralkon Agricultural Co Pty Ltd (1987) 74 ALR 505 at 510.

Section 29 of the JR Act does not provide for the stay of a decision being reviewed under Part 5 of the JR Act.  A JR Act Part 5 applicant will therefore need to apply for a stay or an interlocutory injunction under the s9(3) of the Civil Proceedings Act 2011 or seek an order under s47(4) of the JR Act.

RELEVANCE OF OTHER REMEDIES

What should a review court do with JR Act Part 3 or Part 5 review applications where there are other remedies available to the applicant?  The existence of alternative remedies to those in the JR Act are dealt with by sections 10 - 15 of the JR Act.  For a discussion of the general issues that arise in this context, see Administrative Review Council, Report No 47, The Scope of Judicial Review, April 2006, 47-49.

Sections 10, 11 and 12 of the JR Act establish some basic propositions.  The comparable provision of the ADJR Act is s10.

Section 10 of the JR Act makes it clear that rights of review under the JR Act are in addition to any other rights of review that may be held by an applicant.

Section 11 makes it clear that the Supreme Court can effectively put an end to other forms of review (ie non-JR Act review) where review is also being sought under JR Act.

Section 12 of the JR Act allows the opposite.  Where other forms of review (by a court) are or were available to an applicant for judicial review under the JR Act, the Supreme Court can dismiss the JR Act application.  Instances of s12 being utilised include Stubberville v Webster [1996] 2 QdR 211; and Deng v Q-Comp [2011] QSC 191 at [41]-[54].  In Harrison v President of the Industrial Court of Queensland [2017] 1 QdR 515 at [76], Jackson J observed that “… the existence of a right of appeal from … [the District Court, Land Court, the Magistrates Court and QCAT] does not destroy the supervisory jurisdiction of this court.  However, in practice, a right of appeal will sound the death knell for most applications for judicial review for discretionary reasons.”

The ADJR Act equivalent of JR Act s12 does not require that an applicant seeking to rely on the ADJR Act demonstrate the existence of “special circumstances” justifying reliance on the ADJR Act over an alternative judicial remedy - Cremona v Administrative Appeals Tribunal (2015) 230 FCR 1 at [23], [46] and [52].

JR Act ss 13 and 14 are similar to s12.  Sections 13 and 14 do not have ADJR Act equivalents although the broad discretion available to the Federal Court to avoid abuses of process under ADJR Act would probably allow for the same result as is achieved via ss 13 and 14 of the JR Act.

JR Act ss 13 and 14 are clear legislative directions to the Supreme Court to not allow review of decisions or decision-making processes where it is not in the interests of justice for review to occur.

JR Act s13 provides that the Supreme Court must dismiss a review application under the JR Act where there is or was an alternative remedy available under a different law before a different body (not necessarily a court, so including merits review tribunals), where dismissal is in the interests of justice.  For a rare example where merits review was not considered a more appropriate remedy than judicial review, see CSL Australia Pty Ltd v Minister for Infrastructure and Transport (2014) 221 FCR 165, [236]-[237] (Allsop CJ) and [331] (Rares J).

JR Act s14 provides that the Supreme Court must dismiss an application under the JR Act which would interfere with proceedings which are currently being heard where those proceedings may be reviewed once a decision has been made.  The court is to have regard to the balance of convenience before making an order under JR Act s14.  The balance of convenience involves consideration of the interests of the parties and the public interest.  As with s13, the Supreme Court is only to dismiss the JR Act proceedings if dismissal is in the interests of justice.  Section 14 is designed to stop judicial review under the JR Act being used to fragment and hold up administrative decision-making, ie the mischief that the High Court was concerned to avoid in Bond.

JR Act s15 regulates the discretion in ss 11 - 14.  Note JR Act s15(1)(b) which provides that if an order under one of the sections is appropriate, the court “must try to ensure” that the order is made “at the earliest appropriate time”.  Note also Rule 577 of the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules.

SUMMARY DISMISSAL OF JUDICIAL REVIEW APPLICATIONS - SECTION 48 JR ACT

JR Act s48 operates in a way that is similar to ss 12 - 14.  It gives the Supreme Court a discretion to stay or dismiss judicial review applications at any stage if one of the grounds found in s48 exists.  These grounds are:

- that the claim is inappropriate;

- that there is no reasonable basis for the claim for judicial review;

- the claim is frivolous or vexatious; or

- the claim is an abuse of the process of the court.

JR Act s48 has been used by government decision-makers where they believe that the applicant for review has no basis upon which to seek review.  JR Act s48 allows the Supreme Court to summarily dismiss a review application.  As to the possible scope of s48(1)(a) [inappropriateness] – see Barrow v Chief Executive, Department of Corrective Services [2004] 1 QdR 485 at 487 [6]; and Intero Hospitality Projects Pty Ltd v Empire Interior (Australia) Pty Ltd [2008] QCA 83 at [37]-[57].  EARC’s report dealt with this ground of summary dismissal at [9.12]-[9.13], linking it to concerns about the appropriateness of judicial review of committal proceedings potentially fragmenting the criminal process.  Concerns were also raised about the potential impact of judicial review on decisions of a “prosecutorial nature.”

Under JR Act s48(2)(b), an order under s48 should be made at earliest appropriate time.  Note Rule 577 of the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules.  Federally, see s31A of the Federal Court of Australia Act 1976.

COSTS ORDERS UNDER SECTION 49 JR ACT

The Supreme Court of Qld has an important power under the JR Act which can be exercised prior to the final hearing of a judicial review application.  The Supreme Court can make orders under s49 of the JR Act in respect of court costs flowing from judicial review proceedings.

Section 49 is a major reform of the law of costs.  It has no equivalent in the ADJR Act.  It changes the law of costs in two major respects:

1.	It allows the court, in advance, to regulate how costs are going to be dealt with in the review proceedings.  Normally, costs orders are made after each particular hearing of the court;

2.	Most significantly, s49 changes the rule that costs should follow the event, ie that the loser should pay the winner’s court costs.  That general rule applies under the ADJR Act - see P Bayne, ‘Cost Orders on Review of Administrative Action’ (1994) 68 Australian Law Journal 816; and Roger Douglas, ‘Uses of Standing Rules 1980-2006’ (2006) 14 Australian Journal of Administrative Law 22 at 29-31.  The Federal Court has even indicated that in certain circumstances there will be a reluctance to award costs against administrative decision-makers, even when their decisions have been held to be bad in law - see Our Town FM Pty Ltd v Australian Broadcasting Tribunal (No 3) (1987) 77 ALR 609 at 612.  See also the Victorian Court of Appeal’s decision in Psychologists’ Registration Board of Victoria v Herald and Weekly Times Ltd (2000) 16 VAR 208.

In Queensland, 2 types of costs order can be made under s49:

a party (but not the administrative decision-maker) can apply for an order that another party indemnify it in respect of court costs properly incurred after the application for the indemnity costs order - see JR Act s49(1)(d). [Note that s49 provides that costs be awarded on a party and party basis.  Party and party costs are the standard basis for calculating court costs in most types of litigation.  Normally costs calculated on this basis are not as generous to the beneficiary of a costs order as solicitor and client costs - see generally Cairns, Australian Civil Procedure (13th ed, Thomson Reuters, 2024) ch 18; note also Fitzgerald P in re Minister for Transport and Anghel (1994) 1 QAR 453 at 455 where His Honour held that s49 authorised an award of costs on a solicitor and client basis.]

the Supreme Court can also order that a party only bear its own costs regardless of the outcome of the judicial review proceedings - see JR Act s49(1)(e).  This type of order can have retrospective effect.

Factors which the Supreme Court is to have regard to before making such orders, are set out in s49(2):

the financial resources available to the party (whether the party’s own resources or the resources of an associate with an interest in the outcome of the proceedings);

whether the proceedings involve an issue which affects or may affect the public interest; and

whether the party’s claim or involvement in the litigation has a reasonable basis.

Note that JR Act s49(2) may not set out an exhaustive list - see Anghel at 455; compare EARC’s report at [10.29].

The Supreme Court has a power to revoke or vary orders made under s49 if it considers that conduct of the party or a change in circumstances warrants revocation or variation - see JR Act s49(3).

It was originally envisaged that s49 would be utilised early in judicial review proceedings, but the Supreme Court has questioned whether this is a requirement of section’s application - see Anghel at 461 per Shepherdson J.  An eg of s49 being applied is Meizer v Chief Executive, Department of Corrective Services [2005] QSC 351 at [14].

Note the general power of the Supreme Court in respect of costs found in Chapter 17 of the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules.  There has been some controversy as to the relationship between s49 and Order 91 of the old Qld Supreme Court Rules.  One member of Qld Court of Appeal held that Order 91 applied in JR Act proceedings where no order can be made under s49 - per Shepherdson J in Anghel at 462 - see also s49(4); and White J in Barker v Qld Fire and Rescue Service [2000] QSC 395 (unreported 23 November 2000).  McPherson JA in Anghel suggested a different result at 458.

The most detailed consideration of s49 has come from the Qld Court of Appeal in the Anghel case - also reported at [1995] 2 QdR 454.  For general discussion, see chapter 10 of EARC’s report.
