REMEDIES PROBLEM QUESTION REVIEW

The Department of Minerals and Energy invites tenders for a dam
and hydroelectric project in North Queensland. Five companies
tender for the dam and hydroelectric contract. Mammoth
Constructions Pty Ltd (“Mammoth”) is amongst these tenderers.
Mammoth is selected by the Department to construct the dam and
hydroelectricity plant.

The dam will substantially affect the flow of a North Queensland
River. The dam will cause the creation of a large artificial lake up-
stream from the dam. Most of the land affected by the dam and the
lake is Crown land. The area to be flooded includes much of the last
known habitat of a rare species of wallaby. Following the
announcement by the Minister for Minerals and Energy of the
Government’s decision to go ahead with the dam and hydroelectric
project, the North Queensland River Wildlife Conservation Society
(“the Society”) writes to the Minister requesting reasons for the
Government’s decision. The Society is concerned about the threat
that the dam poses for the rare wallabies in the region.

The Government refuses to provide reasons and encourages
Mammoth to start construction as soon as possible.

The Society believes that the project contravenes the Fauna
Conservation Act (Qld) which imposes heavy penalties, inter alia,
upon those who kill or injure native species specified in the
Schedule to the Act. The wallaby species in question is included in
the Schedule.

Fines under the Fauna Conservation Act are to be imposed by the
District Court.

Mammoth begins construction work on the dam. The Society seeks
the Attorney-General’s approval to commence a relator action to
enforce the provisions of the Fauna Conservation Act. The Attorney
General refuses. The Attorney-General tells the President of the
Society that “it is politically impossible to stop the project. The
electorate in which the project is located is represented by the
Minister for Minerals and Energy and retaining this seat is critical
for the survival of the Government.”

The Society is a small but committed organisation of North
Queensland residents. It receives small amounts of State and
Federal Government funding. It has made submissions in relation to
river wildlife conservation to the State Government on two previous
occasions. One of these submissions followed an invitation from the
Government.

The Society’s constitution declares that the Society’s “fundamental
aim is to protect wildlife and the river systems that provide their
habitats in the North Queensland region.”

Students are to advise the Government, the Society and Mammoth
as to the prospects of any legal challenge that the Society may bring

in respect of the dam and hydroelectric project.

Questions

(a) What will the Society need to establish in order to seek reasons
in the Supreme Court in respect of the Government’s decision to go
ahead with the dam and hydroelectric project? What is the
relevance of section 50 of the JR Act?

(b) Assume that the Government, prior to the Supreme Court’s
determination on the application for reasons, agrees to provide
reasons for the decision to go ahead with the project. These reasons
disclose no legal irregularity. What are the prerequisites for the
Society to seek judicial review under Part 3 of the JR Act of the
Government’s decision to commence the dam and hydroelectric
project? What are the prerequisites for seeking remedies under Part
5?

(c) Can the Society seek a special costs order under Section 49 of
the JR Act?

(d) Can the government seek to have the Society’s Part 3
application dismissed prior to a full hearing by the Supreme Court
of the Society’s application for judicial review?

(e) Does the Society have standing under Part 3 of the JR Act to
seek review of the Government’s decision to go ahead with the dam
and hydroelectric project?

(f) Can the Society commence proceedings against Mammoth in
respect of the construction of the dam? What type of proceedings
might be commenced? Would the Society need to establish
standing? Would any decision on standing under Part 3 be identical
to a determination of standing in an action against Mammoth?

(g) Assume that the Supreme Court finds that the Society lacks
standing to commence proceedings against the Government or
against Mammoth. Mammoth constructs the dam and the area in
question is flooded. The Government loses the next election, and
the new government supports an action against Mammoth in the
District Court under the Fauna Conservation Act. The District Court
judge hearing the case imposes a heavy fine on Mammoth under the
Fauna Conservation Act. The District Court judge, in response to a
submission from counsel for Mammoth that Mammoth was acting in
accordance with Government instructions when constructing the
dam, responds by saying that it was possible for the dam to have
been constructed without killing or injuring any wallabies. The
judge notes that there was no evidence to suggest that the wallabies
could not have been relocated prior to the flooding. The District
Court judge concludes that the failure to relocate the wallabies
warrants the imposition of a fine under the Fauna Conservation Act.

Can Mammoth challenge the judge’s decision imposing the fine?
