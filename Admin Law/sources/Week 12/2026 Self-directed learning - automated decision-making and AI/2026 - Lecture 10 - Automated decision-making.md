# Slide 2

Automated Decision-making

Potential benefits
Access to justice – cheaper and quicker for applicants
Greater consistency in decision-making
Savings for government and taxpayers
Different ways in which automation can be used by governments
-	Template letters/emails automatically issued following human input of data into template [eg Pintarich v Federal Commissioner of Taxation (2018) 262 FCR 41]
Preliminary recommendations/draft decisions to assist human decision-makers
Automated/Predictive language model first tier decisions (and reasons), with appeals to human decision-makers
Different types of automation raising different issues
The automated application of “rules” created by human experts (eg Robo-debt)
Automation using predictive large language models that identify and “learn” from patterns in massive data-sets
Different types of public power raising different issues
Non-discretionary statutory powers
Broad statutory discretions requiring consideration of complex statutory and factual issues
Public powers impacting on fundamental common law rights (or, in light of s48 of the Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld), human rights enumerated in ss15-37 the Human Rights Act)

# Slide 6

Automated Decision-making

Administrative law legality
Potential grounds of legality review
Decision-making not authorised by legislation
Invalid sub-delegation – note express legislative provision for automated decision-making, for example, in section 66 of the Business Names registration Act 2011 (Cth), regarding the Australian Securities and Investments Commission
Robo-debt programme – the averaging of Australian Taxation Office annual income data in order to assess fortnightly welfare entitlements was not authorised under the relevant social security legislation; reversal of the onus of proof onto welfare recipients requiring them to disprove over-payments was not authorised by the relevant legislation

# Slide 7

Automated Decision-making

Applicability of legality review remedies
ABT v Bond and case law on so-called “self-executing” decisions and automated decision-making [see Creyke et al, 132-141; cf Pintarich v Federal Commissioner of Taxation (2018) 262 FCR 41]
Note that the ADJR Act/JR Act expressly apply to decisions “whether in the exercise of a discretion or not”
Common law and equitable remedies and automated decision-making
Statutory entitlements to reasons
Problems related to ABT v Bond
Programming expertise required to understand algorithmic decision-making and the inscrutability of self-learning algorithmic decision-making – “black boxes”?
Programming inadequacies are no justification for failure to comply with statutory duty to provide reasons
Confidentiality and reasons?  Note exceptions from obligation to give reasons for personal or other confidential information – would not appear to extend to coding underlying algorithmic decision-making (as in the sentencing soft-ware considered by the Wisconsin Supreme Court in Loomis)
