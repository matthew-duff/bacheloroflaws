Topic One

Law and Government: Introducing Administrative Law, Government Accountability and Administrative Justice

Introduction: what is administrative law and why do we need it?

Administrative law regulates the exercise of public powers (meaning ‘administrative’ and ‘delegated legislative’ powers) typically exercised by the government in respect of individuals or the wider community, and is one aspect of the broader field of public law.

Constitutional Law 								Human Rights Law

Constitutional law governs (inter alia) the powers and relationships between the three branches of government (legislature, judiciary and executive) and is closely associated with administrative law because (as we shall explore) it frames and informs the structure of administrative law and the development of many of its key principles. Administrative law closely interacts with human rights principles through access to justice – via ‘administrative/merits’ review and via ‘judicial review’ principles and remedies. Indeed, without government accountability (through administrative law mechanisms) the rule of law would be subverted and human rights imperilled. By contrast, constitutional law in Australia says only a little about people’s fundamental (or, basic) rights absent a Bill of Rights. The absence of a federal Bill of Rights (or, Charter of Fundamental Rights) has been, to a limited degree, overcome in some parts of Australia (including Queensland) via the introduction of statutory human rights regimes (to be discussed later in the course).

In this course we are, typically, concerned with the relationship between the state (typically government decision-makers (or ‘public entities’) exercising public powers) and the individual (citizen/non-citizen). Our task is to examine how that relationship is regulated, to explore the legal framework that confers rights upon people to challenge government actions that affect them and to review government processes. The framework for law and government in Australia is marked by the presence of a comprehensive system of administrative law (and administrative justice) that has mainly developed over the last 50 years or so.

The main purposes (or rationale) of administrative law may be said to be: to promote effective government administration; to promote accountability over the exercise of public powers; to ensure government action is open to challenge; and to check and constrain unlawful and unjust government ‘action’ (‘action’ means, decisions, conduct, or inaction). Administrative law supplies values and specific principles against which the government can be called to account by a range of mechanisms and different institutions, including but not limited to the courts and tribunals.

Key administrative law values are: legality, fairness, reasonableness/rationality, integrity, openness (or, transparency), efficiency and effectiveness. And these values find expression via express terms in certain statutes (e.g. those establishing statutory bodies, such as administrative review tribunals or Ombudsman offices) and through particular legal principles developed by the courts at common law. Some of these principles are called ‘grounds of judicial review’ as we shall explore later on. These ‘grounds of judicial review’ are used by the courts in the course of checking the legality of the exercise of public (administrative) powers usually conferred by statute on ‘the state’, by which we mean powers conferred on executive government. The ‘executive’ branch of government includes:

The Monarch (as figurehead) and his representatives (Commonwealth Governor-General, State governors, and NT Administrator who act on the advice of the executive branch of government);

Prime Minister/State Premiers;

Ministerial officeholders leading government departments (departments are administrative bureaucracies employing public servants (officials);

The Cabinet (senior ministers, such as Treasurer and Attorney-General);

Departmental officials/delegates (who perform day to day administration of the law and government policy); and,

Statutory authorities that perform many different public functions such as government regulators (e.g. Fair Work Australia and the Civil Aviation Safety Authority), security agencies (e.g. Australian Security and Intelligence Organisation (ASIO)) and independent oversight bodies (e.g. administrative review tribunals, Ombudsman offices and anti-corruption watchdogs).

It is important to just note (at this stage) that some important public powers and essential services are not exercised, or provided, by public officials or bodies (e.g. prison management and immigration detention). Instead, these types of roles and functions are outsourced to private companies under contracts with the government.  It is also essential to appreciate that not all public power is conferred by statute. Rather, some public powers stem from constitutional sources (e.g. s.61 Constitution – containing the ‘executive power of the Commonwealth’) and the common law, albeit these two sources of law ground relatively few powers as we live in an ‘age of statutes’ with much of the common law now ‘codified’ in legislation.

What do we mean by ‘public powers’?

There are, broadly, three types of public power: legislative, executive/administrative and judicial. As noted above, administrative powers (exercised at Commonwealth and State levels, respectively, by the executive branch) may be statutory or non-statutory.

There are several classes of Commonwealth (Federal) executive power covering:

the power to execute/administer any constitutional provisions conferring power on the executive branch of the state,

the power to execute/administer statutory provisions conferring power on the executive branch (the ‘bread and butter’ of administrative law)

the Crown’s non-statutory ‘prerogative powers’,

the Commonwealth executive’s ‘nationhood power’ and,

the Crown’s common law capacities (e.g. to enter into contracts with private entities, to hold and dispose of property).

In this course we are primarily interested in class (ii) administration (and oversight) of statutory powers, but we will examine classes (iii) and (v) on occasions.

The executive power of the States in Australia is sourced in: constitutional law (some of which is entrenched and requires a referendum to alter); the common law (‘prerogative powers’ and common law ‘capacities’); and, assorted statutes, including provisions contained in the Constitution Acts of states, e.g. Constitution of Queensland Act 2001, s.51 (‘Powers of the State’).

In short, the vast majority of executive powers is conferred by statute, but what actions and activities does this embrace?

Statutes may confer different types of public power (either administrative power or broader delegated legislative powers) on different public officials and entities. Accordingly, the Governor General (Cth), State Governors and Northern Territory Administrator enjoy certain statutory powers; for example, to appoint statutory officeholders (including federal court justices, Ombudsman officers and Directors of Public Prosecutions).

Additionally, statutes may confer personal powers on Federal and State/Territory ministers, with the consequence that they cannot lawfully be delegated to any other person or body (e.g. s 501(3) Migration Act 1958 (Cth) confers a broad personal ministerial power to refuse or cancel a person’s visa if it is in the ‘national interest’). But in practice statutes generally permit administrative powers to be exercised by ministerial delegates – meaning, public officials employed within a relevant department (such as the Department of Home Affairs) - through lawful delegation. Delegation of administrative power (through express or implied statutory terms) is critical in large volume areas of public administration, such as immigration, social security or taxation. Otherwise, the business of government would grind to a halt if ministers were required to personally exercise all statutory powers on a daily basis.

Additionally, some statutes directly confer administrative power on public officials, for instance, s 189 Migration Act 1958 (Cth) effectively enables authorised ‘officers’ (as defined in s 5) to detain a person (depriving them of their liberty) if they ‘reasonably suspect’ that a person in Australia is an unlawful non-citizen (i.e. without a valid visa). Moreover, police officers are given extensive statutory-based powers (e.g. Police Powers and Responsibilities Act 2000 (Qld)).

Statutory powers can also be vested in statutory authorities and they are usually found in the statute constituting the particular authority or body. As we shall see in the near future, the Administrative Review Tribunal Act 2024 (Cth) created the peak Commonwealth administrative review tribunal (which replaced the previous “Administrative Appeals Tribunal”). This Act governs the functioning, procedures and powers of an important ‘integrity’ body which provides for oversight (via an ‘appeal’ process) in relation to administrative decisions, taken by a wide range of Commonwealth officials and authorities, that individuals wish to challenge as wrong, substantively unfair or unjust. Another example of a statutory authority is the federal security agency (ASIO) which exists to protect those in Australia from serious threats to their security; its jurisdiction, powers and procedures to counter terrorism, espionage and border integrity are provided for in the ASIO Act 1979 (Cth).

Locally, there is the Queensland Human Rights Commission (which conciliates disputes arising between individuals and public entities under the Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld)). Another contemporary example, relating to public infrastructure, is the Cross River Rail Delivery Authority. This authority was established, and its functioning and powers guided, by the Cross River Rail Delivery Authority Act 2016 (Qld). It commenced its work, in 2017, to facilitate the efficient delivery of cross river rail in Brisbane. These are just a handful of examples of statutory authorities. We will encounter others as we progress through the study materials for this course.

Statutory powers can often confer considerable, discretionary, powers upon ministers and other public officials. This means that decision-makers have a degree of choice about whether and how to exercise a statutory power, necessitating a weighing up of competing considerations, public and private interests and/or values to determine what the best or ‘preferable’ decision is in the circumstances of the case at hand. Where discretionary powers are vested in public officials by statute, they are often given guidance (but not strictly dictated to) on how to exercise their administrative power through ‘soft law’ (not legislation) – meaning directions contained in policy guidelines that frame and inform the process of exercising a discretionary power. For example, if the Immigration Minister’s delegate is considering whether or not to cancel the visa of a felonious person of ‘bad character’ under s 501(2) Migration Act 1958 (Cth) their task of weighing up competing public and private/familial interests is informed by policy called ‘ministerial directions’, issued under s 499 of the Migration Act 1958 (Cth).

In this course we will delve into carefully selected cases covering a range of administrative powers and actions in diverse areas of public law, such as environmental and planning law, Aboriginal land rights, immigration and refugee law, tax administration, social security law and national security.

Elements of Accountability

The key elements of the system are judicial review over government decision-making by the courts, merits review over government decision-making - including by administrative tribunals, investigation of administrative action taken by public entities by the Ombudsman offices, anti-corruption watchdogs and human rights agencies, and the conferral of information and privacy rights, under freedom of information and privacy legislation, overseen by Information/Privacy Commissioners at Federal and State/Territory levels.

Administrative ‘disputes’ involve an individual (or corporate entity) as applicant (the aggrieved party), the ‘state’ as respondent, and the decision-making institution. The institutions that embody Australia’s commitment to administrative law (and administrative justice) are the courts (notably, High Court of Australia, Federal Court, Federal Circuit and Family Court, and Supreme Court in Qld), tribunals (such as the Administrative Review Tribunal (ART, which replaced the Administrative Appeals Tribunal, or AAT, in 2024), and in Queensland the Civil and Administrative Tribunal (QCAT)), Ombudsman offices (state/territory/federal) and human rights/anti-discrimination agencies (such as, the Australian Human Rights Commission and Queensland Human Rights Commission).

The institutions outlined above are some of the most important in Australia for resolving matters (disputes) arising between individual and the state (either the Federal government, or (for our purposes) public entities in Queensland).  The issues dealt with by courts (via ‘judicial review’) and by tribunals (via ‘merits review’) raise, respectively, questions of law, or mixed questions of fact/law. Democracy is enhanced when the actions of the executive branch (government decision-makers) are checked and evaluated by either judges or by independent tribunals to determine, respectively, if their actions are correct in law or, alternatively, the ‘best decision’ substantively on the merits of the particular case (meaning the ‘correct or preferable’ decision).

Ensuring that government decisions are ‘correct in law’ is an aspect of the “rule of law” doctrine in Australia. That doctrine means (in one sense) that decisions of the executive branch of the state must conform to the law as interpreted, declared and applied, by the courts. So the courts maintain the “rule of law” through judicial review proceedings, and the “rule of law” supplies the theoretical justification for having judicial review over executive action. We will explore the “rule of law” principle and (relatedly) the “separation of powers” (between the legislature, executive and judiciary) and system of “responsible government”, in workshop two when we examine the influence of the Commonwealth Constitution upon Administrative Law.

Foundations of administrative law

The origins of administrative law are in the English common law, supplemented in Australia by s. 75(v) of the Constitution - a very important provision which confers an ‘original jurisdiction’ upon the High Court of Australia to grant three remedies against federal government officials (‘officers of the Commonwealth’). Today, these common law and constitutional foundations of administrative law are supplemented by the various judicial review mechanisms created by parliaments, through legislation, since the late 1970s.

So, the protection of the public against administrative error or abuse by ministers, or other government officials or public bodies, now has a firm legislative foundation in the Administrative Decisions (Judicial Revew) Act 1977 (Cth) and, relevantly for our purposes, the Judicial Review Act 1991 (Qld).

Accordingly, it is important to appreciate that there are three, overlapping jurisdictional foundations for judicial review in Australia: (i) the common law, (ii) Commonwealth Constitution and (iii) statute. These matters will be explored and explained further in the workshops ahead. Additionally, ‘merits review’ tribunals, Ombudsman offices and human rights/anti-discrimination/integrity bodies are creatures of statute – meaning their jurisdiction is conferred, and functions, powers and procedures are governed, by statute.

In short, our framework of administrative law in Australia has, historically, been created by the courts (through the development of the common law), by those who drafted the Commonwealth Constitution (the separation of powers, and s 75 – the ‘original jurisdiction’ of the HCA) and the Federal and State/Territory legislatures, that (through statutes) create the legal structures within which disputes between individual and state arise, and which provide for the manner in which matters are to be resolved.

Administrative justice, government accountability and good administration

Our system of administrative law is underpinned and infused by three broad, overlapping, concepts: administrative justice; government accountability and good administration. Simply put, administrative justice is concerned with ensuring that when administrative action is taken, people’s substantive and procedural rights and interests are respected and protected through accessible, transparent, timely, and efficient decision-making processes.

Government (executive) accountability is concerned with ensuring that those exercising public powers are held to account: (i) legally, (ii) financially (through the Auditor-General, for example) and (iii) politically (through the convention of ‘responsible government’, parliamentary debates, parliamentary committee systems (for scrutinising new laws, the wider functioning of government and effect of government policies), and of course, periodic elections).

Principles of good public administration include: ensuring government agencies maintain accurate and accessible records; avoiding delays (‘administrative drift’) in decision-making; and managing the complexities of government decision-making efficiently through ongoing training and professional development. Further details on good administration principles can be accessed via the Commonwealth Ombudsman’s fact sheet on Ten Principles for Good Administration [go here]. These ten principles were developed after a series of Ombudsman’s reports into many cases of abuse of power in respect of immigration detention (the administrative detention of certain (‘visa-less’) non-citizens that identified legal, factual and administrative errors in many instances; with dreadful consequences for individuals’ (including Australian citizens’) personal liberty, health and well-being.

Government (executive) accountability

The underlying notion is that of the executive government giving an account or an explanation to a person or body to whom or to which it is responsible. That part of accountability is clear enough, however the form or process of accountability varies. So we need to be clear about the meaning and processes of democratic government accountability. What are the ways in which Ministers, public servants or government employees are accountable, and to whom are they giving an account or explanation?

There is accountability to the electorate; that is to say, Ministers, including the Prime Minister, may lose office at an election. This is a periodic, effectively collective, and rather distant form of accountability, whereas our principal concern is with accountability for day-to-day administrative decisions/conduct that touch people’s lives, often in profound ways. These actions (relating to social security, health, the environment, heritage protection, town planning, and administration of taxation, immigration, human rights or national security, for example) may affect an individual, group or the wider populace.

Administrative decisions taken by Ministers or public employees may be discussed and examined in Parliament, or by the media. But in addition there are relatively informal and formal retrospective administrative law mechanisms by which administrative action can be scrutinised and checked.  Judicial review by the courts is one integral constituent of our administrative law system that secures government accountability and legality, but it is only one component of a far more comprehensive liberal democratic system which includes scrutiny by other independent bodies. Administrative justice

This is a narrower concept than executive government accountability. It relates to normative standards of general application applicable to administrative decision-making. How did the concept arise and what is its meaning? Or, put differently, what is it that administrative justice delivers?

Questions about administrative justice can be answered by examining the objectives and values underpinning primary government decision making (e.g. the front line bureaucrats working in a government agency or department (such as Centrelink, or in Home Affairs), internal (departmental) review processes, and external institutions such as independent (‘merits review’) tribunals, human rights commissions or Ombudsman offices.  Additionally, there are elements of administrative justice that are closely aligned with principles developed by the courts (such as, procedural fairness or reasonableness/rationality) and understanding these principles can inform our understanding of administrative justice.

In short, administrative justice is a concept that seeks to reconcile an individual’s interest in securing substantive justice and legality on the one hand, with efficiency (timeliness and cost-effectiveness) concerns on the other hand. Executive government accountability: accounting for what, to whom, and against what standards will government action be considered?

It is a function of a lawyer to assist clients in protecting established legal rights/interests, and/or in asserting their legal rights and interests. This may involve representing groups of individuals or communities seeking to enforce public law (against the government or other private bodies) in the public interest.

Where a client approaches a lawyer with a grievance about the action(s) or proposed action(s) of government officials or agencies, then the task of the lawyer is to effectively communicate with the client, informing and advising them about what steps could be taken to redress their particular grievance.

The purpose of the following exercise is to develop practical legal skills (effective letter writing to a client) in addition to enabling you to demonstrate knowledge and understanding of key institutions, and processes, for redressing people’s grievances against the executive branch of government.

Consider the two scenarios below. You are required to draft a short letter (maximum two sides of A4, 12 point font, single spacing, standard margins) to each client to inform, advise and justify (i.e. evidence), the legal options available to them. You should write the advice using plain language and adopt a clear structure. This is so that the client can make an informed decision about how to go about redressing their grievances in the near future.

Scenario One

The Queensland government plans to scuttle a decommissioned navy vessel (which was gifted to them) 2000 metres off the coast near Caloundra to create an artificial reef for divers. The Federal Environment Minister has granted a permit to enable the scuttling of the vessel pursuant to the Environment Protection (Sea Dumping) Act 1981 (Cth).

A group of local residents are concerned about the environmental impact of the proposed sea dumping and form an action group “Stop the Scuttling Collective (SSC)”. Specifically the group is worried that the proposed scuttling would cause tidal problems leading to coastal erosion and pollute the marine environment. They approach a community legal centre – the Environmental Defenders Office (Qld) – for advice about how to stop the scuttling. The EDO is a non-profit body working in the area of public interest planning and environment law.  As the matter is about environmental protection and affects a large community on the Sunshine Coast the EDO agrees to represent the aggrieved local residents and appeal the Minister’s decision to grant an artificial reef permit.

As a solicitor at the EDO you must write to the action group’s campaign organiser, Ms Jesse Bell, to inform and advise her about the nature of the appeal (i.e. to what oversight body will the ‘appeal’ will be lodged, the type of ‘hearing’ that may held, the sort of ‘remedies’ that may be granted) to be pursued on behalf of the SSC.

**Scenario Two**

Ant and Scotty are serving prison sentences in a Queensland correctional facility for assorted public order misdemeanours; they got into an argument with two other prisoners, Peter and Adam, the argument ended in a physical altercation that left Peter and Adam in need of hospital treatment for minor injuries. After internal disciplinary proceedings, Ant and Scotty are placed in a special detention unit, isolated from other prisoners, with strictly limited contact with correctional staff, and without access to personal property, for five months. A member of the correctional staff at the prison has told them both that this form of detention is for the good order of the correctional facility. They complained to the manager of the prison in writing, but the complaint was rejected and now wish to complain to an external oversight body about the unfairness of the conditions imposed upon them and the effect it is having on their mental health.

Ant phones you, a caseworker at Prisoners Legal Service, from the prison seeking advice about what sort of external mechanisms are available so he and Scotty can lodge a complaint about their grievances. He’s heard about an ‘Ombudsman’ but has no idea what they do and whether they (or any other independent oversight bodies) can assist. Write an advice letter in simple, clear terms, setting out potential avenues of redress for Ant and Scotty with respect to their grievance.
