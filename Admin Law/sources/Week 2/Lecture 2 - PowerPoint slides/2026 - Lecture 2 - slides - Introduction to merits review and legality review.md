# Slide 43

Which courts generally undertake legality review?

Generally, it is only the superior courts that can undertake legality review.
In relation to Federal Government decision-making, this means:
The High Court of Australia – applicants for review rely principally on s 75(v) of the Constitution in relation to “officers of the Commonwealth” (although other provisions of the Constitution, such as s 75(iii), are also relevant);
The Federal Court of Australia – applicants for review rely on s 39B of the Judiciary Act 1903 (Cth) (which largely replicates, for the Federal Court, the High Court’s jurisdiction to undertake judicial review) and/or on the ADJR Act (re decisions and conduct under Federal enactments; the Federal Circuit and Family Court also has judicial review jurisdiction under the ADJR Act)
In relation to State and Territory government decision-making this means State or Territory Supreme Courts.  In Queensland, the Supreme Court has judicial review jurisdiction under the Judicial Review Act 1991 (Qld) and under the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction to issue declaratory and injunctive relief.  In Queensland, lower courts such as the District Court, the Magistrates Court and QCAT do not have jurisdiction to hear judicial review applications challenging the legality of Queensland government decisions.

# Slide 53

Historical development of the grounds of legality review and continuing remedial and constitutional complexity

Historically there appears to have been a link between the notion of separation of powers and the grounds of legality review
The ultra vires grounds of legality review developed in relation to review of actions of local authorities (eg city councils, including the making of subordinate legislation that such local authorities purported to enact such as bylaws) and ministerial decision-making
The jurisdictional error grounds of legality review (distinct from appeals) developed in relation to decision-making of lower courts and tribunals (or quasi-judicial decision-makers)
These classifications became less helpful as the grounds of legality review began to converge.  Consider, for example:
The supposed distinction between simple ultra vires and extended/abuse of power ultra vires; and
The supposed distinction between want of jurisdiction and excess of jurisdiction
The term “ultra vires” is still used in relation to unlawful/invalid subordinate legislation and “jurisdictional error” is used in relation to unlawful/invalid tribunal and court decisions
Continuing remedial and constitutional complexity
Statutory remedies in the ADJR Act (cf State and Territory legislation) are limited to Commonwealth decisions and conduct of an “administrative character” made “under an enactment” of the Commonwealth
The grounds of jurisdictional error vary in relation to tribunals and courts [for example, all errors of law by some tribunals appear to undermine their jurisdiction; whereas courts may make some errors of law that do not undermine their jurisdiction – see Craig v South Australia (1995) 184 CLR 163 at 176-180]
The entrenched constitutional remedies in section 75(v) (ie prohibition, mandamus and injunction) have focussed attention on jurisdictional error in the context of these constitutional remedies available against officers of the Commonwealth, with particular relevance to the operation of privative/ouster clauses; note also the importance of jurisdiction error in a State context in light of Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW (2010) 239 CLR 531

# Slide 54

Legality review - non-statutory executive branch powers and functions

Two categories of non-statutory executive branch powers and functions:
Prerogative powers – ie powers unique to the Crown (for example, in relation to entering into treaties and declaring war); and
Executive capacities (or liberties) which the Crown, as a legal person, appears to share with other legal persons (for example, to enter contracts)
“An act done in the execution of a prerogative executive power is an act which is capable of interfering with legal rights of others. An act done in the execution of a non-prerogative executive capacity, in contrast, involves nothing more than the utilisation of a bare capacity or permission, which can also be described as ability to act or as a ‘faculty’” - Plaintiff M68/2015 v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection (2016) 257 CLR 42 at [135], per Gageler J; although contrast Davis v Minister for Immigration, Citizenship, Migrant Services and Multicultural Affairs (2023) 279 CLR 1, at [127]-[137], per Edelman J
The English courts (reflecting the result of the English Civil War) have held that there can be no new prerogative powers
Prerogative powers can be displaced by Statute although prerogative and statutory powers can coexist
English and Australian courts have recognised that there is no blanket exclusion of legality review in relation to exercises of prerogative powers (see Creyke et al, Chapter 16):
