For the rest of the semester we will be looking at the context in which these grounds are applied by courts to review the exercise of government power.  We will be looking at the statutory and other remedies by which the grounds of review are applied.  For example, if you believe that a Commonwealth Government decision-maker has denied you natural justice, this part of the course will assist you in understanding how you actually bring your concerns before a court.  You may be able to rely on the ADJR Act or on a common law or equitable remedy (via s 75(v) of the Constitution or s 39B of the Judiciary Act) to challenge the government decision in question.

INTRODUCTION

LEGALITY REVIEW v MERITS REVIEW

Your recent classes have been analysing the various grounds of review in administrative law.  The grounds of review provide for what is often described as “legality review”.  This has been contrasted with “merits review”, ie review of the merits of decisions, etc, of those exercising government (generally executive) power.  Courts exercising powers of judicial review do not carry out merits review.

Where appeal rights are given from a particular decision, the body hearing the appeal may be authorised to carry out merits review.  The Administrative Review Tribunal (ART - which we looked at in earlier classes) is a Commonwealth tribunal which carries out merits review.  An understanding of the fundamental distinction between legality review and merits review is essential.  Merits review is normally more attractive to applicants because the body reviewing on the merits will make a decision dealing with all outstanding questions, whether legal or factual.  Legality review (whether relying on statutory/Constitutional or common law or equitable remedies) only involves a consideration of the legality of the government decision or conduct.  If a problem as to legality is found, then the court identifies that problem but can only refer the matter back to the original decision-maker for a decision on the merits.

It has, however, been estimated that up to 70% of successful judicial review applications may have led to favourable outcomes for applicants on the merits when the case is reconsidered by the original decision-maker – see Robin Creyke and John McMillan, ‘Judicial Review Outcomes – An Empirical Study’ (2004) 11 Australian Journal of Administrative Law 82 at 87.  For a perspective from the public service on legality review, see Michael D’Ascenzo, ‘Effectiveness of Administrative Law in the Australian Public Service’ (2008) 57 AIAL Forum 59.

On the potential for alternative dispute resolution techniques to be used in administrative law disputes – see Sophie Boyron, ‘The Rise of Mediation in Administrative Law Disputes: Experiences from England, France and Germany’ [2006] Public Law 320.  On alternative dispute resolution procedures under Australian administrative law – see Creyke et al, 243-246.

THE ADJR ACT - HISTORICAL BACKGROUND

THE GROUNDS OF REVIEW DEVELOPED IN THE CONTEXT OF COMMON LAW AND EQUITABLE REMEDIES

The various grounds of review developed in the context of certain common law and equitable remedies.  These remedies included the prerogative writs of certiorari, prohibition and mandamus and declarations and injunctions.  These remedies were (and still are in one form or another) available to review exercises of legislative, administrative and judicial power.  Essentially these remedies were the vehicles for raising before a superior court the grounds of legality review with which you are now familiar.

PROBLEMS WITH THE COMMON LAW REMEDIES

Unfortunately, the common law remedies (ie the prerogative writs) were plagued by technical difficulties which made it difficult for people to seek review of the exercise of government power.  We will look at some of these technical difficulties in more detail in a later class.  What happened was that in the early 1970s, a number of reports were published in Australia which advocated for reform of the procedures for judicial review and the codification of the grounds of review.  See Kerr Committee Report, Parliamentary Paper No 144 of 1971, at [251]-[274] and [390]; and The Ellicott Committee Report, Parliamentary Paper No 56 of 1973, at [19]. These reports are relevant to the interpretation of Commonwealth legislation – see s15AB of the Acts Interpretation Act 1901 (Cth).

ADJR ACT

The ADJR Act was the result of this call for reform.  The Act, though passed in 1977, commenced operation in 1980.  The Act gives to the Federal Court and the Federal Circuit and Family Court the power to review certain decisions and conduct under Commonwealth Government legislation.

## ADJR ACT – 3 MAJOR CHANGES

The ADJR Act made three major changes to the law that existed prior to its enactment.  The Act:

simplified procedures for seeking judicial review;

“codified” the common law grounds of review.

The ADJR Act basically allows review on the grounds that you are now familiar with from your classes to date.  It “codifies” the grounds of review (although note that two of the ‘codified’ grounds are broader than their common law equivalents – the error of law and no evidence grounds – see, for example, Minister for Immigration and Multicultural Affairs v Rajamanikkam (2002) 210 CLR 222 at [97]).  You have seen that these codified grounds are set out in sections 5 and 6 of the ADJR Act.  Note that Kirby J complained in his judgment in Re Minister for Immigration and Multicultural Affairs; Ex parte Applicant S20/2002 (2003) 77 ALJR 1165, [155]-[166], that “[t]o some extent the development of the common law of judicial review in Australia was retarded by the enactment of the ADJR Act in 1977” – at [157].  Professor Aronson responded to this claim in Aronson, ‘Is the ADJR Act hampering the development of Australian administrative law?’ (2004) Public Law Review 202, 214-216; and

provided a right to reasons in respect of certain government decisions.  The right to reasons is dealt with further below.

ADJR ACT – THREE BASIC POINTS

1.	The ADJR Act was not intended to provide merits review.  In the words of the Commonwealth Attorney-General at the time of passing the ADJR Act:

“Judicial Review by the Federal Court of Australia will not be concerned with the merits of the decision or action under review.  The only question for the court will be whether the action is lawful...”

Second reading speech on the ADJR Bill 1977 by the Hon RJ Ellicott, QC, Attorney-General (Parliamentary debate 30th parliament, second session at 1394-6, 28 April 1977 - House of Representatives); see also, for example, Turner v Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs (1981) 55 FLR 180 at 181-182.

2.	The ADJR Act allows legality review of the way certain Commonwealth Government powers are used.  The ADJR Act does not apply to exercises of power under State legislation - see the definition of “enactment” in s3 of the ADJR Act read with s38(1) of the Commonwealth Acts Interpretation Act 1901.  Basic constitutional principles are also relevant - see ss 76(ii), 77 and 122 of the Constitution; and Evans v Friemann (1981) 53 FLR 229 at 234-235.

The Federal Court and the Federal Circuit and Family Court generally do the reviewing under the ADJR Act - see ss 8 and 3 ADJR Act.  State courts do not have the power to review exercises of Commonwealth Government power - see s9 of the ADJR Act.  [But note section 3 and Schedule 3 of the ADJR Act.]

RELATIONSHIP WITH OTHER MODES OF REVIEW

The common law and equitable remedies were not removed by the ADJR Act - see s10 of the ADJR Act.

There are therefore different judicial review options before the Federal Court:

-	Review of the exercise of power under the ADJR Act, or

-	Review of the exercise of power relying on a prerogative writ, declaration or injunction. The Federal Court has jurisdiction to review exercises of Commonwealth government power using these remedies via s39B of the Judiciary Act 1903, s32(1) of the Federal Court Act 1976 and the Federal Court’s accrued jurisdiction - see R v Cook ex parte Twigg (1980) 147 CLR 15 at 25-26 and 32-34; cf R v Brown ex parte Federated Clerks’ Union of Australia (1984) 154 CLR 207 at 211; Optical Prescription Spectacle Makers Pty Ltd v Withers (1987) 13 FCR 594 at 595-596; and E Campbell, ‘The Accrued Jurisdiction of the Federal Court in Administrative Law Matters’ (1998) 17 Australian Bar Review 127.

RIGHT TO REASONS

The right to reasons is an important feature of the ADJR Act.

At common law there is no general legal right to reasons for administrative decisions or for an account of the facts upon which an administrative decision was based (the only alternative being to seek a discretionary order to access information and evidence related to a challenged decision once judicial review proceedings have been commenced, eg via discovery and inspection).

With the enactment of the ADJR Act, provided persons aggrieved by an administrative decision can fit themselves within terms of s 13 of the ADJR Act, they have a legally enforceable right to reasons and an account of material upon which a Commonwealth administrative decision was based (provided the decision was made under a Commonwealth enactment).  This statutory right to reasons is complemented by ss 24 and 27 of the Administrative Review Tribunal Act 2024 and the Commonwealth Freedom of Information Act 1982.

QUEENSLAND JUDICIAL REVIEW ACT

The JR Act entered into force in 1992.  Its enactment was a response to similar concerns with the traditional administrative law remedies that motivated the enactment of the ADJR Act.  See, eg, the Fitzgerald Royal Commission – ‘Report of a Commission of Enquiry Pursuant to Orders in Council’, 3 July 1989.

The Commission’s report noted that:

“… the judicial mechanisms for challenging ministerial and administrative decisions are quite limited and accordingly, of little practical effect. ... Reliance must be placed on extremely cumbersome judicial procedures to achieve any review of administrative action” - at 128.

The Royal Commission’s report recommended the establishment of the Electoral and Administrative Review Commission (EARC) - see 144 and 370.

The Commission’s report also recommended that EARC be directed to examine a number of priority issues including the establishment “of simpler procedures for obtaining a judicial review of administrative decisions” - at 144, 360 and 371.

EARC was established and published its ‘Report on the Judicial Review of Administrative Decisions and Actions’ in December 1990 - Serial No 90/R5.  EARC recommended that Queensland follow the Federal Government’s lead and pass an Act with provisions which were, in many respects, identical to the ADJR Act - see the EARC Report at 22 and 42.

EARC’s recommendations were generally endorsed by the Queensland Parliamentary Committee for Electoral and Administrative Review - see Parliamentary Committee’s report, ‘Judicial Review of Administrative Decisions and Actions’ - 14 June 1991 at 4.

These reports can be referred to by courts in interpreting the JR Act – see s 14B of Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld).

The Queensland Parliament followed EARC’s and the Parliamentary Committee’s recommendations and enacted the JR Act.  Parts 3 and 4 of the Act contain the same three fundamental features of the Federal Act.  The JR Act:

simplifies procedures for seeking judicial review;

“codifies” the common law grounds of legality review (in Part 3 of the Act); and

provides a right to reasons in respect of certain government decisions (in Part 4 of the Act).

Three additional points can be made in relation to the JR Act.

1.	The JR Act only authorises judicial review on grounds of illegality.  In the words of the Queensland Attorney-General, “... it is not the (Supreme) Court’s task to review the merits of an administrative decision” - see Hansard, 26 November 1991; see also Hoffman v Queensland Local Government Superannuation Board [1994] 1 QdR 369 at 370.

2.	Decisions of those exercising State government power (or spending State or local government money) can be reviewed under the JR Act - see s4, s3 and its definition of “enactment” and s14E of the Queensland Acts Interpretation Act 1954.

3.	The Supreme Court of Queensland does the reviewing under the JR Act - see s19 and definition of “Court” in s3 of the JR Act.

The Supreme Court, when applying the JR Act, can obtain guidance from decisions interpreting the ADJR Act - see EARC’s Report at 42.

Section 16(1) JR Act provides that:

-	if a provision of the AD(JR) Act expresses an idea in particular words; and

-	a provision of the JR Act appears to express same idea in different words because of different legislative drafting practice

-	the ideas must not be taken to be different merely because different words used.

See also section 16(2) and schedule 3 of the JR Act (which provides a comparative table of equivalent Federal sections and is reproduced below).

There are, however, differences between the Federal Act and the State Act.  The JR Act’s effect on the historical common law and equitable remedies is different.  The ADJR Act leaves these remedies in place.  The Queensland Act attempts to draw them into Part 5 of the JR Act.  Note that the JR Act Part 4 (the right to reasons) does not link in with Part 5.  The right to reasons is tied to reviewability under s20 (of Part 3) of the JR Act.

The relationship between the common law/equitable remedies and the statutory remedies in Queensland is complex.  There are two types of judicial review provided for in the JR Act.  An applicant can seek judicial review under Part 3 of the JR Act, known formally as an application for a “Statutory Order of Review” (see s3).  Alternatively, an application under Part 5, known formally as “Application for Review” (see s43), can be made.  The Rules of Queensland Supreme Court provide for the two types of application to be combined together - see Rule 568 of the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules.  Coordination of these different remedies is arguably more transparent in Queensland.  Federally, s39B of the Judiciary Act 1903 does not specifically refer to the prerogative writ of certiorari.  The Federal Court can issue the writs of prohibition and mandamus, and injunctions, against “officers of the Commonwealth” – see Vietnam Veterans’ Association of Australia NSW Branch Inc v Cohen (1996) 70 FCR 419 at 431-433.  Section 39B(1A) of the Judiciary Act expands the Federal Court’s jurisdiction to include the making of declarations and to matters arising under any laws made by the Commonwealth Parliament – see Transport Workers’ Union v Lee (1998) 84 FCR 60 at 64-67; compare McGowan v Migration Agents Registration Authority (2003) 129 FCR 118, [21]-[35].  A Commonwealth tribunal is an officer of the Commonwealth – see, for eg, SAAP v Minister for Immigration and Multicultural and Indigenous Affairs (2005) 228 CLR 294 at [43], [91], [153] and [180].  The High Court expressly left open whether “an independent contractor” might be “an officer of the Commonwealth” – Plaintiff M61/2010E v Commonwealth (2010) 243 CLR 319 at [51].

In Queensland there are no corresponding complexities regarding “officers” under Part 3 of the JR Act, or review by way of an order in nature of certiorari or declaration or any other remedy found in Part 5 of the JR Act.

THE OPERATION OF THE JUDICIAL REVIEW ACT PARTS 3 AND 4

Sections 20, 21 and 22 are the heart of the JR Act.

Section 20(1) provides that:

“A person who is aggrieved by a decision to which this Act applies may apply to the Court for a statutory order of review in relation to the decision.” (emphasis added)

Subsection 2 then goes on to set out the grounds of review.  Key expressions in s20(1) are:

aggrieved; and

decision to which this Act applies.

Section 21(1) applies to conduct engaged in for the purposes of making a decision to which the Act applies. Where such conduct has been proposed, is still being, or has been engaged in, then a person aggrieved can apply to the court for review.

Section 21(1) again includes the expression “a decision to which (the) Act applies”. Section 21(1) allows review of conduct, actual or proposed, which comes before a potential decision.

Subsections 22(1) and (2) of the JR Act provide that where a person has a duty to make a decision to which the Act applies, and where there is a failure to make such a decision, a person aggrieved by that failure to decide may apply to the court for review.

So a common phrase in sections 20, 21 and 22 is “decision to which (the) Act applies.”  If you can’t identify such a decision - whether as a reality, in terms of s20, or as a potentiality, under s21 (conduct) or s22 (failure to decide), then there is no review under Part 3 of the JR Act.

## “A DECISION TO WHICH [THE] ACT APPLIES”

The phrase is defined in s4 of the JR Act.  Section 4’s operation is critical to the scope of Part 3 review (and is also critical for the operation of Part 4). Review under JR Act Part 3 is only as wide as s4 allows it to be.  Section 4(a) provides that the phrase “decision to which [the] Act applies” means:

“a decision of an administrative character made, proposed to be made, or required to be made, under an enactment (whether or not in the exercise of a discretion).”

Important words in s4(a) are:

decision;

administrative character; and

under an enactment.

Note that each of the words in s4(a) “cannot be construed in isolation.  They are interrelated.  Each informs the meaning and content of the others” – Neat Domestic Trading Pty Ltd v AWB Ltd (2003) 216 CLR 277 at [116], per Kirby J; and Griffith University v Tang (2005) 221 CLR 99, [60], per Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ.

### (1)	“DECISION” (AND “CONDUCT”)

The word “decision” is not specifically defined anywhere in JR Act. There are, however, two sections which assist in working out scope of “decision”. The first is s5 which makes it clear that “decisions” would include:

making orders, awards or determinations;

giving certificates, directions, consents or permissions;

issuing licences, authorities or instruments;

imposing conditions or restrictions;

making declarations, demands or requirements;

retaining or refusing to deliver up an article.

The equivalent broad list of activities in the ADJR Act was seen by the Federal Court as a justification for a wide meaning for the word “decision” - see Lamb v Moss (1983) 49 ALR 533 at 555-556.  The High Court, however, has not been prepared to give the word “decision” as wide a meaning as the Federal Court gave it in Lamb v Moss. Nonetheless, JR Act s5 still helps in understanding the scope of the word “decision” (a point expressly acknowledged by the High Court).

See also s6 of JR Act, which states that:

“If provision is made by an enactment for the making of a report or recommendation before a decision is made, the making of the report or recommendation is itself taken, for the purposes of this Act, to be the making of a decision.”

This section was probably intended to overcome the common law rule which provided that the prerogative writ of certiorari was not available against reports or recommendations of public officials which did not and were not capable of affecting legal rights - see Ainsworth v The Criminal Justice Commission (1992) 175 CLR 564 at 580-581.

The Federal equivalent of s6 has been narrowly construed - see Edelsten v Health Insurance Commission (1990) 27 FCR 56 at 70 (following Ross v Costigan (1982) 59 FLR 184 at 198).  It was held that the enactment must specifically make the report or recommendation a condition precedent to making of a subsequent decision.  Note the different wording of the Queensland provision - see EARC’s report at [13.16]; and Administrative Review Council’s Report No 32 at [172]-[174].  The difference in wording, however, is not directed at overcoming the interpretation of the ADJR Act provision in Edelsten.  For a consideration of Queensland cases which appear to suggest a more liberal interpretation of the JR Act provision, see Wells v Carmody [2014] QSC 59 at [36]-[48]; although see Vega Vega v Hoyle [2015] QSC 111 at [112].

*Australian Broadcasting Tribunal v Bond (1990) 170 CLR 321 is the main High Court decision dealing with meaning of “decision” in the ADJR Act. Students must read (at minimum) 334-344 in the judgment of Mason CJ with whom Brennan and Deane JJ agreed.  Toohey and Gaudron JJ took a more liberal approach.

Facts

The case concerned an enquiry by the Australian Broadcasting Tribunal into whether or not to suspend or impose conditions on the radio and television licences held by companies which were effectively controlled by Alan Bond.  At the heart of the Tribunal’s enquiry were two major allegations.  The first was that Mr Bond caused a defamation action brought by the then Premier of Queensland against one of Mr Bond’s companies to be settled by paying a sum of $400,000 to the Premier, when it was alleged that the company’s likely liability would have been in the order of only $50,000.  It was also alleged that Mr Bond tried to conceal this arrangement from the Tribunal.  The other major allegation was that Mr Bond had personally threatened an executive of the AMP Society that if the Society did not stop acting contrary to the interests of Mr Bond in relation to the makeup of the Board of Directors of Bell Resources Limited, then Mr Bond would direct his TV reporters to gather damaging material on the AMP Society and broadcast it.

The main question under the Broadcasting Act 1942 for the Tribunal was whether to cancel or impose conditions on the corporate licences held by the Bond companies.  To make that determination the statute required the Tribunal to consider whether the companies were fit and proper “persons” to hold the licences.  The Tribunal’s determination on that question was actually reached by considering whether Mr Bond was a fit and proper person to hold media licences.  Having found that he was not, and having found that he controlled the company licensees, the Tribunal reached its determination that the companies were not fit and proper “persons”.

The logical sequence of the determinations therefore went something like this:

Mr Bond was not a fit and proper person

Mr Bond controlled the corporate licensees

The corporate licensees were therefore not fit and proper

The ultimate decision on whether to cancel or impose conditions

The ultimate decision to cancel had not yet been made by the Tribunal but appeared inevitable.

Mr Bond and the companies sought judicial review of the Tribunal’s behaviour under the ADJR Act.  Mr Bond had some success before the Federal Court [see (1989) 89 ALR 185] and the Tribunal appealed to the High Court.  The appeal was successful on a number of issues.  In the High Court, Sir Anthony Mason considered at length the term “decision” and attempted to distinguish a “decision” from “conduct”, which is reviewable (in Queensland under s21 of the JR Act).

A critical question was whether the Tribunal’s “decision” that Mr Bond was not a fit and proper person was a “decision” for the purposes of the ADJR Act.  Sir Anthony Mason’s conclusion was that it was not.

There were five steps in Sir Anthony Mason’s approach that warrant consideration.

1.	The word decision was not limited to a final decision disposing of a controversy between the parties.  Mason CJ made 3 observations:

- The ADJR Act was a remedial statute;

- The ADJR Act did not refer to “final” decisions;

- Declaratory relief extended beyond relief in respect of ultimate decisions.

2.	Despite this, the word “decision” had to be limited.  Reasons for limiting the word included the following:

-	The ADJR Act equivalent of JR Act s5 indicated that a decision must be something which has “the character or quality of finality.  It needed to be something like a determination of an application, enquiry or dispute ... ‘a determination effectively resolving an actual substantive issue’” - quoting from Deane J in Director-General of Social Services v Chaney (1980) 47 FLR 80 at 100;

-	The equivalent of JR Act s6 (which allows reports and recommendations to be classed as “decisions”) qualified the characteristic of finality.  Some sense of finality must be inherent in the term “decision” or else a section like JR Act s6 would not have been necessary;

-	The expression “conduct” was fleshed out in equivalent of JR Act s8.  Doing things which are preparatory to making a decision cannot themselves be decisions, otherwise there would be no point in providing for review of conduct.  If a generous interpretation was given to the word “decision”, then the ADJR Act equivalent of s8 would not have been necessary.

3.	Mason CJ saw the interpretation of “decision” as raising a competition between two policy considerations:

-	The policy of allowing persons aggrieved by administrative decision-making a convenient and effective means of redress; and

-	The policy of protecting “the efficient administration of government”.

According to Mason CJ, if “decision” is not linked to the quality of finality, then this will lead to fragmentation of processes of administrative decision-making and risk the efficiency of the administrative process.

4.	Mason CJ offered the following interpretation of the word “decision” in the ADJR Act:

“… a reviewable ‘decision’ is one for which provision is made by or under a statute.  That will generally, but not always, entail a decision which is final or operative and determinative, at least in a practical sense, of the issue of fact falling for consideration.  A conclusion reached as a step along the way in the course of reasoning leading to an ultimate decision would not ordinarily amount to a reviewable decision, unless the statute provided for the making of a finding or ruling on that point so that the decision, though an intermediate decision, might accurately be described as a decision under an enactment.  Another essential quality of a reviewable decision is that it be a substantive determination” - at 337.

For Mason CJ, a substantive determination involved the resolution of a substantive issue.  He contrasted matters of procedure which did not involve substantive issues.  An example of a procedural determination would be a determination to grant or refuse an adjournment during a hearing.

“[P]rovision … by or under a statute” appears to include implied “provision” – see Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Mayer (1985) 157 CLR 290 at 302-303.  In Mayer the majority in the High Court considered that the implicit source of the authority to make the determination in question was the enactment.  It appears that the determination must be expressly or impliedly required (and not simply authorised) in order for a court to conclude that it is “provided” for “by or under a statute” – Hutchins v Deputy Commissioner of Taxation (1996) 65 FCR 269, per Black CJ at 272 and Spender J at 278; see also Salerno v National Crime Authority (1997) 75 FCR 133 at 140-143; and Peek v CEO, Australian Government Solicitor (No 2)(2005) 226 ALR 268, per Edmonds J at [20] (which was decided after Tang and which therefore suggests that there is no inconsistency with the decision in Tang – see below).

Note that in Qld, the requirement in Bond that a decision must be provided for by or under an enactment must be modified to accommodate JR Act s4(b) because s4(b) expressly applies to “non-statutory schemes or programs” under which a decision has been made.

Applying this test to the facts of the case, Mason CJ held that the Tribunal’s determination that Mr Bond was not a fit and proper person to hold a licence was a conclusion reached as a step along the way in the course of reasoning leading to the decision as to the suitability of the companies to hold their licences.  For that reason, the determination regarding Mr Bond was not a “decision” for the purposes of the ADJR Act.  Note that the decision that the companies were not fit and proper “people” to hold licences was also a step in the course of reasoning leading to a decision whether or not to revoke the licences.  The decision regarding the companies being fit and proper persons was, however, caught by the ADJR Act because the Broadcasting Act specifically provided for this decision - see 339.

Even though the determination about Mr Bond was not reviewable as a “decision”, Mason CJ made it clear that if grounds of invalidity were present in the making of this determination, those grounds could be raised in review proceedings provided they were raised with respect to a reviewable decision.  For example, a breach of the rules of natural justice relating to an interim determination, to the extent that the breach was incorporated into a subsequent reviewable decision, could be dealt with by the court.  But the applicant had to wait.  An interim ruling could not be challenged.

5.	Mason CJ also considered the interpretation of the word “conduct” in the ADJR Act.  Mason CJ sets out limits on the interpretation of “conduct,” contrasting “decision” under ADJR Act with “conduct” which is reviewable under the Act.  Decisions must be substantive, final and operative. Conduct relates to something which is procedural and not substantive.  Conduct points to action taken rather than a decision made.

What about “procedural decisions”?  Mason CJ drew subtle distinctions in addressing this question:

“A challenge to conduct is an attack on proceedings engaged in before the making of the decision.  It is not a challenge to decisions made as part of the decision making process, except in the sense that if the decisions are procedural in character, they will precede the conduct which is under challenge” - at 342.

On the facts in Bond, the decision by the Tribunal that Mr Bond was not a fit and proper person to hold the licences in question was not “conduct” because it was not procedural (ie it was substantive) and did not amount to conduct (it being clearly a determination rather than “action taken”).

The High Court’s approach attempts to ensure that intermediate substantive determinations with no statutory foundation are neither “decisions” nor “conduct” for the purposes of ADJR Act review.

For details of what happened after the High Court’s decision - see The Bond Inquiry – Final Report, Australian Broadcasting Tribunal, Sydney 1990.

APPLICATION OF THE HIGH COURT’S APPROACH IN BOND

The High Court’s interpretation of the words “decision” and “conduct” in the ADJR Act is very technical.  Courts applying the High Court’s approach have had difficulty in applying it consistently - see Edelsten v Health Insurance Commission (1990) 27 FCR 56 at 66-70 and 72-73.

See also Harris v Bryce (1993) 41 FCR 388 at 395-396.  It was held in this case that a determination by the Commonwealth Sex Discrimination Commissioner to investigate a complaint of sexual discrimination contrary to s26 of the Sex Discrimination Act 1984, was not a “decision” for the purposes of the ADJR Act.  Bond’s case was specifically applied and it was noted that the Commissioner’s determination to investigate was not final or operative, or determinative of any issue.  Nor was the determination seen as substantive in any way, in the sense that it did not determine any person’s rights.  Note that the court held that, notwithstanding Bond, relief via s39B of the Judiciary Act 1903 (Cth) was potentially available.

Compare Re Excel Finance Corporation Ltd (1993) 41 FCR 346 at 375-376.  In this case an applicant was seeking review of the Australian Securities Commission’s determination to authorise a Receiver and Manager of a company to make an application under s597 of the Corporations Law.  Section 597 provided for public examinations of those involved in the affairs of companies.  The determination in this case was therefore similar to the one in Harris v Bryce in terms of the commencement of an investigation.  Without detailed consideration of Bond, O’Loughlin J [following an obiter statement made by Lockhart J in the decision in Mercantile Mutual Life Insurance Co Ltd v Australian Securities Commission (1993) 40 FCR 409 at 429-430] ruled that the determination by the ASC to allow a Receiver and Manager to apply for a public examination was a “decision” under the ADJR Act.  This aspect of the decision of O’Loughlin J was affirmed on appeal, see 42 FCR 578 at 580.

In Harris, the determination to investigate was not considered substantive, and yet in Excel Finance a decision to allow an investigation to proceed was considered substantive enough to qualify as a decision.

Decisions under JR Act which have applied Bond are State Bank of New South Wales Ltd v Commissioner of Stamp Duties [1994] 2 QdR 661 at 668 and Summerson v Commissioner of Stamp Duties (Qld) (1995) 95 ATC 4473.  Thomas J applied Bond to the JR Act in Redland Shire Council v Bushcliff Pty Ltd [1997] 2 QdR 97 at 99.  Thomas J noted, however, the difficulty in “directly transposing” the language of Mason CJ to “decisions” of a body that performs “non-adjudicative functions”.  Contrast the results in Harris and Excel with the decision of Crow J in A v Central Queensland Network Authorised Mental Health Service [2019] QSC 15 at [34]-[38].

For a review of the cases attempting to apply Bond, see Creyke R and Hill G, ‘A Wavy Line in the Sand: Bond and Jurisdictional Issues in Judicial and Administrative Review’ (1998) 26 Federal Law Review 15.

The judgement of Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ in Griffith University v Tang (2005) 221 CLR 99, while not expressly disapproving of the majority approach in Bond, nonetheless gave rise to doubts as to whether Mason CJ’s interpretation still commanded majority support on the High Court.  The requirement in Bond that a decision must be substantive was conspicuous in its absence in the joint judgement in Tang.  This absence appeared significant in light of the quotation in the joint judgment in Tang from the judgment of Toohey and Gaudron JJ in Bond.  Toohey and Gaudron JJ in Bond rejected the “substantive” requirement.  The position was not free from doubt, however, as the joint judgment in Tang used the relevant quotation from Bond to illustrate a different point.  The joint judgment in Tang also raised the possibility that there might be a “decision” for the purposes of the ADJR Act in cases where an enactment only implicitly authorises the making of a decision.  Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ in Tang appeared to suggest that this may have been sufficient (although note that their Honours refer to a “necessary implication” at [78]).  If determinations that are implicitly authorised by an enactment can be decisions for the purposes of the ADJR Act, then Mason CJ’s approach in relation to substantive steps in the decision-making process would be undermined.  Subsequent judicial decisions, however, suggest that the majority approach in Bond continues to represent the law in Australia.  For example, in 2024, the High Court in Fuller v Lawrence (2024) 99 ALJR 103 (at [16], in the joint judgment of Gageler CJ, Gordon, Edelman, Gleeson and Beech-Jones JJ) expressly recognised the continuing relevance of Mason CJ’s approach in ABT v Bond.

CONCLUDING REMARKS ON BOND

Underlying rationale of High Court’s decision in Bond seems to have been to avoid the situation of applicants being able to fragment and frustrate government decision-making processes by using ADJR Act to review substantive steps in a decision-maker’s reasoning process.

The Federal Court had (and continues to have) a general discretion to dismiss proceedings where the review application is premature.  The High Court appears to have doubted whether the existence of this discretion was enough.

In Queensland, a similar discretion to dismiss premature JR Act proceedings is enshrined in JR Act s14.

The interpretation of “decision” also impacts on right to reasons under Part 4 JR Act because a “decision” for the purposes of the JR Act Part 3 is subject to the right to reasons under Part 4.

The High Court in Bond opted for a very technical approach in identifying “decisions” and “conduct.”  Given that the historical legality review remedies, in particular declarations and the prerogative writ of prohibition, are not constrained by this technical approach, you can see why commentators have asked whether Bond was worth all the trouble.

### (2)	“ADMINISTRATIVE CHARACTER”

This expression is not further defined in the JR Act.

5 preliminary points can be made about the phrase “administrative character” (the first three points are particularly important).

The phrase maintains the trichotomy between legislative, executive (or administrative) and judicial acts and decisions - see *Resort Management Services Ltd v Noosa Shire Council [1995] 1 QdR 311 at 317; *Evans v Friemann (1981) 35 ALR 428 at 433-4; and *Minister for Industry and Commerce v Tooheys Limited (1982) 42 ALR 260 at 265.

Courts have generally sought to avoid giving the phrase “administrative character” a narrow or technical construction.  The ADJR Act and JR Act are remedial statutes and should therefore be given wide construction and application - see Evans v Friemann (1981) 35 ALR 428 at 435.  Characterising decisions for this purpose does not appear to involve a strictly analytical interpretation, with two factors being said to impel a broad construction:

-	It is the decision which must be of an administrative character, not the subject matter of decision.  An illustration of this factor is provided by the facts of Evans v Friemann.  Evans involved an attempt to review (among other things) decisions to fail a person in patent attorney exams.  Passes in these exams would have allowed the candidate to seek admission as a patent attorney.  Patent attorneys and the system of patents were governed by legislative instruments which included regulations dealing with exams.  If a strict analytical approach was taken, and only the subject matter of a decision was considered, then the decision to fail someone in an exam might be characterised as educational.  Fox ACJ in Evans rejected this approach.  One must look at the decision and characterise it.  According to Fox ACJ such a decision involves administering the patent system set up by legislative instruments.  The focus is not the subject matter of the decision.  The same point has been made by courts in relation to “commercial” decisions.  These can be of an administrative character - see 435;

-	Administration does not involve an isolated act; it is a process.  Hence, particular decisions need to be characterised in the context of any such process - see Evans at 435.

It is the character of the decision that is relevant, not generally the character of the decision-maker - cf Glenister v Dillon [1976] VR 550.  The point is illustrated in Queensland by QCAT. QCAT was held to be a court in Owen v Menzies [2013] 2 QdR 327, [7]-[20], [43]-[52] and [103], and yet s156 of the Queensland Civil and Administrative Tribunal Act 2009 envisages that Part 3 of the JR Act will apply to decisions made by QCAT that are of an administrative character.  Note, however, that the type of decision-maker is not completely irrelevant to characterisation of a decision - see Hamblin v Duffy (1981) 34 ALR 333 at 339.

“Decision of an administrative character” cannot be construed in isolation from other sections of the JR Act.  Consider, for eg, s5 of the JR Act.  The Federal Court in Evans v Friemann recognised that the ADJR Act equivalent of s5 would be relevant to interpreting the scope of the equivalent of s4(a) of the JR Act - see Evans at 431.  Thus, looking at JR Act s5, decisions granting or revoking licences, making or revoking an order, etc can be characterised as “administrative”.

It is difficult, if not impossible, to set out all encompassing definitions of “legislative”, “judicial” and “administrative power” - see Lockhart J in Hamblin at 338; Fox ACJ in Evans at 433-435.

THE “TESTS”

According to Lockhart J in Hamblin v Duffy:

“Legislative acts usually involve the formulation of new rules of law having general application”;

“Judicial acts generally entail determinations of questions of law and fact in relation to disputes susceptible of determination by reference to established rules or principles” - at 338;

“[A ‘decision of an administrative character’] ... includes at least the application of a general policy or rule to particular cases; the making of individual decisions” - at 339.

(emphasis added)

The Full Federal Court in Minister for Industry and Commerce v Tooheys Limited (1982) 42 ALR 260, when interpreting the ADJR Act, endorsed the following statement by Latham CJ in The Commonwealth v Grunseit (1943) 67 CLR 58 at 82-83 (obviously not made in context of ADJR Act):

“The distinction between legislation and the execution of legislation is that legislation determines the content of the law as a rule of conduct or a declaration as to power, right or duty, whereas executive authority applies the law in particular cases.”

This type of definition of administrative power has been criticised as being overly narrow, in particular, because it does not consider the full scope of administration which includes the creation of government policy.

In Queensland Medical Laboratory v Blewett (1988) 84 ALR 615 at 634, Gummow J (in the Federal Court) repeated the same quotation from Latham CJ with a warning that loose terms and unascertainable standards might end up as the product of trying to set down criteria for each of the 3 types of power.  Gummow J also noted that “the primary characteristic of the activities of administrators in relation to enactments of the legislature is to maintain and execute those laws ...” – at 634.

For a general discussion of factors relevant to distinguishing legislative decisions from administrative decisions – see *RG Capital Radio Ltd v Australian Broadcasting Authority (2001) 113 FCR 185 at [40]-[78]; and *Schwennesen v Minister for Environment and Resource Management [2010] QCA 340 at [8]-[37].

On the identification of legislative acts, see also Vietnam Veterans’ Affairs Association v Cohen (1996) 70 FCR 419 at 430-431.

For definition of judicial power in the context of the Constitution – see, for example, Love v Attorney-General (NSW) (1990) 169 CLR 307 at 319-320.

RULES OF THUMB:

Decisions enacting or amending subordinate legislation will not be administrative but will be legislative.  Thus a decision enacting a by-law will normally be legislative.  But note that there is no magic in the word “by-law”.  Courts will scrutinise a decision described as a by-law when they characterise the decision for the purposes of the ADJR Act/JR Act.  A decision described as a “by-law” that involved the application of a law to a particular set of circumstances and that did not change the law has been characterised as administrative - see Minister for Industry and Commerce v Tooheys Limited (1982) 42 ALR 260 at 265.  In this case the relevant Minister had the power to determine the duty payable on entry of goods into Australia according to general criteria.  Decisions regarding the entry of particular goods were referred to as “by-laws”.  Notwithstanding the use of the term “by-law” the nature of the decision was held to be administrative.

If a decision has actually changed the law then it will be legislative.

Contrast Queensland Medical Laboratory v Blewett (1988) 84 ALR 615.  In this case the Minister’s decision to adopt a new schedule of medical fees (which appeared at the end of a Commonwealth statute) had the effect of changing the law and thus was held to be legislative.

Steps preparatory to a change in the law will often be administrative - see *Resort Management Services Ltd v Noosa Shire Council [1995] 1 QdR 311 at 317-318.  In this case it was held that a decision by a local authority to propose an amendment to its town plan (which eventually could have led to a change in the law) was still administrative.  To propose a change to the plan was part of the process of administering the plan.  It is submitted that to actually change the town plan would involve an exercise of legislative power to the extent that it would result in a change in the law.

Unfortunately the case law in this area can be very technical.  For eg in Blewett Gummow J suggested that had the Minister, instead of endorsing a new schedule to the Act, decided not to change the schedule, then that decision would have been administrative.  See also Applied Medical Australia Pty Ltd v Minister for Health (2016) 246 FCR 555 at [35].

Contrast the decision of the Federal Court in Aerolineas Argentinas v Federal Airports Corporation (1995) 63 FCR 100; affirmed on appeal - (1997) 76 FCR 582 at 590-593.  In this case there was a decision made by the Federal Airports Corporation to impose a charge on all aircraft using certain airports to cover security expenses.  The imposition of the charge was, in a sense, general (ie it applied to all aircraft using those particular airports).  Nonetheless, the decision to impose the charge was held to be administrative.  One factor which led the judge at first instance (Beazley J) to conclude that the decision was administrative was the degree of executive control over the Corporation’s decision.  In particular, the decision of the Corporation was expressed to be governed by the Prices Surveillance Act.  This was a feature that suggested that the decision was not legislative, but administrative.

On appeal, the Full Court of the Federal Court began by characterising the nature of the Corporation’s undertaking.  A review of the Corporation’s statute indicated that it was, in a sense, a commercial undertaking.  The Court emphasised that cost recovery was the purpose of the decision.  The decision was therefore seen as part of the process of administering the Corporation’s commercial operation under the statute and was held to be administrative.  The reference in the decision to the Prices Surveillance Act was (in the view of the Full Court) only relevant to establishing the commercial character of the decision.

In RG Capital Radio Ltd v Australian Broadcasting Authority (2001) 113 FCR 185, [43]-[78], the Full Court of the Federal Court considered various factors indicative of legislative decisions.  These included that:

the decision in question determined the content of a general rule;

the decision was subject to parliamentary control;

the decision was not subject to executive variation or control; and

there was an absence of merits review in respect of the decision.

See also Roche Products Pty Ltd v National Drugs and Poisons Schedule Committee (2007) 163 FCR 451 at 458-461, where Branson J referred to factors including whether the decision determines the future lawfulness of conduct, whether public consultation was an important element in the process leading to the decision, and whether the decision was an important part of a national system of controls designed to ensure a uniform approach by the Commonwealth, states and territories; and Schwennesen v Minister for Environment & Resource Management [2010] QCA 340 at [8]-[37].  Note also s9 of the Legislation Act 2003 (Cth).

The requirement that a decision must be of an administrative character prevents the direct application of the ADJR Act or the JR Act Part 3 to decisions of a legislative character.  Note, however, the possibility of indirectly attacking the legality of a legislative instrument under the ADJR Act when seeking to review the legality of an administrative decision on the ground that it is invalid because the instrument it was purportedly made under was invalid – see, for example, the Administrative Review Council, Federal Judicial Review in Australia, Report No 50 (2012), [5.92].

Can a decision be both legislative and administrative?  See McWilliam v Civil Aviation Safety Authority (2004) 142 FCR 74 at [39]-[44].  Compare Schwennesen v Minister for Environment & Resource Management [2010] QCA 340 at [7]-[9] and [33]-[34].

Are decisions regarding policy or political issues administrative?  See Resort Management Case at 317.  The Queensland Court of Appeal in this case was emphatic that just because a decision involved the determination, implementation or application of policy or was influenced by political considerations, did not mean that the decision must be legislative.  In the view of the Court such features are commonly associated with decisions by executive government which are “quintessentially” administrative in character.  See also Uelese v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection (2016) 248 FCR 296 at [46].

The Queensland Supreme Court appears to have created a distinction between decisions of an administrative character and “managerial” decisions about prisoners – see, for eg, Bartz v Chief Executive, Department of Corrective Services [2002] 2 QdR 114 at 117-118.  Contrast Haque v Commissioner of Corrective Services (2008) 216 FLR 271 at [52]-[54].

What if a decision-maker is under a statutory duty to proceed judicially?  Administrative tribunals normally have a duty to proceed judicially.  Does this mean that their decisions must be characterised as judicial?  The answer given by the courts has been an emphatic “no”.  There is an important difference between a duty to proceed judicially (which one expects to find in most administrative tribunals subject to the rules of natural justice) and judicial power.  See, for example, Hamblin v Duffy at 339 and Deane J in Bond at 365-366.

Judicial power includes the capacity to conclusively determine facts and law in a dispute between two interests, or to decide that there has been a breach of the criminal law and to impose a criminal penalty – see, for example, Visnic v Australian Securities and Investments Commission (2007) 231 CLR 381 at [14]-[16] and Albarran v Members of the Companies Auditors and Liquidators Disciplinary Board (2007) 231 CLR 350 at [16]-[17].

Officials of courts, registrars, etc, may sometimes make decisions of an administrative character - see table of decisions below – although recall the exclusion of decisions from review under s9A of ADJR Act.

Contrast also Supreme Court and Federal Court rule making powers.  These apparently legislative powers (rules of courts are general and binding so they appear to be legislative in character) are incidental to judicial power and are treated as judicial - see Evans v Friemann at 433; compare Momcilovic v The Queen (2011) 245 CLR 1, [75]-[101], [172]-[189], [280], [457], [582]-[605] and [661].

### (3)	UNDER AN ENACTMENT

Section 4(a) of the JR Act provides that a decision made “under an enactment” can be reviewed under the JR Act Part 3.  Contrast JR Act s4(b) that provides for review of a decision under a “non-statutory scheme or program”.  There is no equivalent to JR Act s4(b) in the ADJR Act.

Decisions of non-government bodies are not normally made under an enactment.  Contrast the different judgments of members of the High Court on the applicability of the ADJR Act to decisions of a company incorporated under the Corporations Law of Victoria in *Neat Domestic Trading Pty Ltd v AWB Ltd (2003) 216 CLR 277 at [29], [47]-[65] and [114]-[134].  See also Mantziaris C, ‘A “Wrong Turn” On the Public/Private Distinction: NEAT Domestic Trading Pty Ltd v AWB Ltd’ (2003) 14 Public Law Review 197; Hill G, ‘The Administrative Decisions (Judicial Review) Act and “under an enactment”: Can NEAT Domestic be reconciled with Glasson?’ (2004) 11 Australian Journal of Administrative Law 135; Conde C, ‘Accountability for the Exercise of “Public” Power: A Defence of Neat Domestic’ (2005) 46 Australian Institute of Administrative Law Forum 1; and Aronson M, ‘Private Bodies, Public Power and Soft Law in the High Court’ (2007) 35 Federal Law Review 1.

There appear to be three overlapping issues related to the requirement that a decision be “under an enactment” in order for the ADJR Act and JR Act s4(a) to apply:

1.	Non-justiciability at common law

Courts will not engage in judicial review in certain circumstances where there is high level government decision-making involved, ie which involves a large number of interlocking interests and considerations (often referred to as “polycentric disputes”).  This appears to include judicial review under the ADJR Act and the JR Act.  One context often involving non-justiciability is the exercise of Crown prerogative power, ie decisions to relating to treaties and foreign relations more generally, and decisions relating to the defence and security of the country, etc.

2.	The interpretation of the words “under an enactment” in JR Act s4(a) and the ADJR Act adds an additional issue.  In ADJR Act cases, judges sometimes use the concept of Crown prerogative power in a different way.  Courts applying ADJR Act have held that where the source of power for a decision is the Crown’s prerogative, as opposed to some statute or statutory instrument, then the requirement of an enactment (under which the decision has been made) is not present, and review under ADJR Act is not available.  Similar situations have arisen where a decision-maker has made a decision to enter into, or exercise rights under, a contract.  The source of the power of Executive branch decision-makers to enter into contracts has been treated as not being any enactment, but has instead been considered to be the general law capacity held by decision-makers to enter into contracts.  The term “prerogative” power has sometimes been used to describe an Executive branch decision-maker’s power to enter contracts.  Prerogative power has thus been given broad and narrow meanings by judges.

Judges who give “prerogative power” a broad meaning appear to include every non-statutory power that the executive branch exercises, ie including the power to contract, within the term “prerogative power”.

Judges who use the term “prerogative power” in a narrow sense limit the term to a narrower set of powers unique to Crown, ie relating to treaties and foreign relations more generally, and the power to defend country.

In classes, the word “prerogative” will be used in its narrow sense to allow separate consideration of questions raised by government contracting.  The narrow sense of prerogative also allows non-justiciability issues to be seen in sharper relief.  On these issues - see Administrative Review Council’s Report No 32 at 19-21, and more generally F Wheeler, ‘Judicial Review of Prerogative Power in Australia: Issues and Prospects’ (1992) 14 Sydney Law Review 432.

3.	The third issue relates to the interpretation of s4(b) JR Act, which is unique to the JR Act and allows for review of decisions “under non statutory schemes and programs”.  This appears to expand upon review available under the ADJR Act, which requires that decisions (and conduct) be “under an enactment”.

Section 4(b) JR Act applies if:

-	a decision of an administrative character;

-	made by an officer or employee of the State, a State authority or a local government authority (or made by such bodies directly);

-	which has been made under a non-statutory scheme or program; and

-	which involves funds which have been appropriated by parliament or have been raised by a tax, fee, charge or levy authorised under an enactment.

Various issues are raised by JR Act s4(b): Does it allow review of exercises of prerogative power or decisions which relate to contracts?  How does it impact on the right to reasons?

We will now turn to consider the interpretation of the words “under an enactment” in the ADJR Act and the JR Act s4(a), before turning to consider JR Act s4(b) and related issues.

“UNDER AN ENACTMENT” AS A LIMITATION ON ADJR ACT REVIEW

A decision will still be “under an enactment” for the purposes of the ADJR Act despite the decision not being legally authorised by the enactment - see Blurton v Minister for Aboriginal Affairs (1991) 101 ALR 350 at 356-357; and Iannou v Fowell (1984) 156 CLR 328 at 335; but see Toohey and Gaudron JJ in Bond (1990) 170 CLR 321 at 378.  The word “decision” is therefore given a broader meaning in the context of conferring jurisdiction to engage in judicial review under the ADJR Act, than the word “decision” is normally given in an ouster clause (where judicial review is being excluded).

“Enactment” is defined in s3 JR Act and includes all subordinate legislation.  The expression “statutory instrument” is included in the s3 definition of “enactment”.  “Statutory instrument” is defined in s7 of Statutory Instruments Act 1992; see s7(3), read with s36 of Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld), Blizzard v O’Sullivan [1994] 1 QdR 112 at 121-2, and Chittick v Ackland (1984) 1 FCR 254 at 263-266.  “[U]nder” is also defined in s36 of Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld).

When will a decision be “under” an enactment?  In Evans v Friemann (1981) 35 ALR 428 at 436, Fox ACJ held that: “under” should be interpreted as “in pursuance of” or “under the authority of” a statute.  See also Glasson v Parkes Rural Distributions Pty Ltd (1984) 155 CLR 234 at 241.

Will a decision that can ultimately be traced back to an enactment be a decision made “under” that enactment?  *Australian National University v Burns (1982) 64 FLR 166 at 174, required a “direct” link with an enactment in order for a decision to be “under” that enactment.  Davies J in Post Office Agents Association Limited v Australian Postal Commission (1988) 84 ALR 563 at 571-572, asked whether an enactment was “the immediate or proximate source of the power rather than to an ultimate source”?

In *Griffith University v Tang (2005) 221 CLR 99, the joint judgment of Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ at [69], and Kirby J at [149] (in dissent in the case but not on this issue), question the value of applying a “proximity” approach when interpreting “under”.  According to Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ “[t]he search for ‘immediate’ and ‘proximate’ relationships between a statute and a decision deflects attention from the interpretation of the [Queensland Judicial] Review Act and the ADJR Act in the light of their subject, scope and purpose.”  Kirby J observed that “… the difficulty with the supposed distinction between ‘proximate’ and ‘remote’ sources of ‘decisions’ is obvious.  Essentially, the distinction is self-fulfilling.  Debates over ‘remote’ and ‘proximate’ sources are unhelpful because the words are descriptive of the outcome.  They are not prescriptive of the way in which that outcome is to be reached.”

An early High Court decision that addressed the words “under an enactment” in the ADJR Act was Glasson v Parks Rural Distributions Pty Ltd (1984) 155 CLR 234.  In this case, the High Court was considering a Commonwealth/State scheme under which petrol subsidies were administered.  There was a Federal statute and, relevant to this particular case, a New South Wales statute.  The applicant was seeking review under the ADJR Act of a decision to issue a certificate which required the applicant to repay a large sum of money.  The High Court concluded, despite the existence of the Federal statute and the scheme involving the Commonwealth, that the:

- source of the power to appoint the person who issued the certificate;

- power to issue the certificate; and

- source of the certificate’s legal effect -

were all to be found in the New South Wales statute.  On that basis, the Court could not say that the certificate was issued “under the authority, or in pursuance, of” the Commonwealth Act – at 241.  The certificate appears to have been issued under the New South Wales enactment, but the ADJR Act does not allow for judicial review of decisions made under State enactments.

The words “under an enactment” in the Qld JR Act were interpreted by the High Court in 2005 in Griffith University v Tang (2005) 221 CLR 99.  This case involved an application brought under Part 3 of the JR Act (surprisingly, given cases such as ANU v Burns (see below) and ANU v Lewins (1996) 68 FCR 87, the applicant did not appear to plead Part 5 of the JR Act in the alternative to her JR Act Part 3 application).  The applicant for review had been a PhD candidate at Griffith University.  A decision was made by a university committee to exclude the applicant from the PhD candidature programme on the ground that she had “undertaken research without regard to ethical and scientific standards”.  The applicant appealed this decision to another university committee.  Her appeal was unsuccessful.  She then sought judicial review of the decisions of both committees.

The University sought summary dismissal under s48 of the JR Act on the ground that there had been no decision “under an enactment” and thus review under Part 3 of the JR Act should be denied as the application disclosed “no reasonable basis” – see s48(1)(b) of the JR Act.  The applicant apparently did not raise s4(b) of the JR Act until the High Court proceedings (but no notice of contention was apparently filed in the High Court - see [140]).

Griffith University’s claim under s48 was unsuccessful before a single judge of the Supreme Court of Qld, and on appeal before the Qld Court of Appeal.  The University appealed to the High Court which, by majority, Kirby J dissenting, allowed the appeal.

On the central question of whether the decisions were under an enactment, the court examined the manner in which Griffith University undertook its activities.  The University was established by legislation.  The relevant Act regarding its establishment is now the Griffith University Act 1998 (Qld).  The Act provides not surprisingly that the University has the function of conferring higher degree awards (including PhDs).  It was noted by the High Court that under the Higher Education (General Provisions) Act 1998 (Qld), the University, along with the other established universities in Qld, has a monopoly (or more accurately an oligopoly) regarding the conferral of university degrees.

While the Griffith University Act created a council to govern the University with the power to make university statutes (specifically under s61(2)(c) of the Griffith University Act, statutes could be made on the “disciplining of students … undertaking courses at the university” - but no statutes relevant to the litigation had been made), the Act also conferred on the Council of the University the power to delegate most of its powers to University committees.  The committees relevant to this case had been effectively set up by the University Council but not under university statutes.  The committees were to apply, and in some cases to create, university policies.  The committees purported to apply university policies to the applicant when deciding to exclude her from the PhD candidature programme.

All members of the High Court noted that only Part 3 of the JR Act had been relied upon by the applicant and that a contractual claim had not been advanced by the applicant.  The majority was unable to characterise the decisions made by the committees under the relevant policies as having been made “under an enactment” as required by s4(a) of the JR Act.  Gleeson CJ appeared to require that for a decision to be “under an enactment” it must derive its “force or effect” from the enactment.  Gleeson CJ quoted an observation by Davies AJA in Scharer v New South Wales (2001) 53 NSWLR 299 at 313 that “a grant of authority to do that which under the general law a person has authority to do is not regarded as sufficient” – [18].

Gleeson CJ offered the following conclusion:

“The question in the present case turns upon the characterisation of the decision in question, and of its legal force or effect.  That question is answered in terms of the termination of the relationship between the appellant and the respondent.  That termination occurred under the general law and under the terms and conditions on which the appellant was willing to enter a relationship with the respondent.  The power to formulate those terms and conditions, to decide to enter the relationship, and to decide to end it, was conferred in general terms by the Griffith University Act, but the decision to end the relationship was not given legal force or effect by that Act” – [23].

Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ sought to derive assistance in interpreting the words “under an enactment” from the words “administrative character”.  Having noted that to be “under an enactment” it was required that the decision in question be required or authorised by the enactment, they observed that:

“[t]he decision so required or authorised must be ‘of an administrative character’.  This element of the definition casts some light on the force to be given by the phrase ‘under an enactment’.  What is it, in the course of administration, that flows from or arises out of the decision taken so as to give that significance which has merited the legislative conferral of a right of judicial review upon those aggrieved?

… The answer in general terms is the affecting of legal rights and obligations.  Do legal rights or duties owe in an immediate sense their existence to the decision, or depend upon the presence of the decision for their enforcement?  To adapt what was said by Lehane J in Lewins [(1996) 68 FCR 87 at 103], does the decision in question derive from the enactment the capacity to affect legal rights and obligations?  Are legal rights and obligations affected not under the general law but by virtue of the statute?” – [79]-[80], some footnotes not reproduced.

Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ later summarised their approach in the following terms:

“The determination of whether a decision is ‘made ... under an enactment’ involves two criteria:  first, the decision must be expressly or impliedly required or authorised by the enactment; and, secondly, the decision must itself confer, alter or otherwise affect legal rights or obligations, and in that sense the decision must derive from the enactment.  A decision will only be ‘made ... under an enactment’ if both these criteria are met.  It should be emphasised that this construction of the statutory definition does not require the relevant decision to affect or alter existing rights or obligations, and it will be sufficient that the enactment requires or authorises decisions from which new rights or obligations arise.  Similarly, it is not necessary that the relevantly affected legal rights owe their existence to the enactment in question.  Affection of rights or obligations derived from the general law or statute will suffice” – [89].

The relevant decisions of the University committees were held not to have been made under the Griffith University Act.  The decisions “had no impact upon matters to which the University Act gave legal force and effect.  [Ms Tang] enjoyed no relevant legal rights and the University had no obligations under the University Act with respect to the course of action the latter adopted towards the former.” – [96].   Contrast the views of Edmonds J in Guss v Commissioner of Taxation (2006) 152 FCR 88 at [41] where it is argued that the “obligation” referred to in Tang must be an obligation of the “person aggrieved … and not the decision-maker” which is conflict with the judgment of Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ in Tang at [96].

Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ in Tang also attempted to justify their restriction of statutory judicial review to decisions affecting rights or obligations (and thereby possibly excluding decisions affecting mere interests) by reference to the concept of a “matter” under the Commonwealth Constitution – see [90].  For a critique of this justification (which has the potential to limit more than just statutory judicial review under legislation based on the ADJR Act) see G Hill, ‘Griffith University v Tang: comparison with NEAT Domestic, and the relevance of constitutional factors’ (2005) 47 AIAL Forum 6; and C Mantziaris and L McDonald, ‘Federal judicial review jurisdiction after Griffith University v Tang’ (2006) 17 Public Law Review 22.  For a defence of the High Court’s approach, see PA Keane, ‘Judicial review: The courts and the academy’ (2008) 82 Australian Law Journal 623.  Compare M Aronson, ‘Private Bodies, Public Power and Soft Law in the High Court’ (2007) 35 Federal Law Review 1; and AE Cassimatis, ‘Judicial Attitudes to Judicial Review: A Comparative Examination of Justifications Offered for Restricting the Scope of Judicial Review in Australia, Canada and England’ (2010) 34 Melbourne University Law Review 1.  In White Industries Australia Ltd v Assistant Commissioner of Taxation (2007) 240 ALR 792, Lindren J applied Tang and rejected an attempt to rely on the ADJR Act but accepted the possibility of relying on s39B of the Judiciary Act – at [100]-[116].  See also Motor Trades Association of Australia Superannuation Fund Pty Ltd v Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (2008) 169 FCR 483 at [34]-[35], per Flick J; Minister for Health v Nicholl Holdings Pty Ltd (2015) 231 FCR 539 at [43], per Greenwood and Logan JJ; and Vega Vega v Hoyle [2015] QSC 111 at [124] and [127], per Lyons J, holding that an effect on reputational interests may be sufficient in order to satisfy the requirements identified in Tang. On the potential to avoid the outcome in Tang by seeking relief under Part 5 of the JR Act and the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction, see Y v University of Queensland [2019] QSC 282 at [10]-[12] and [71], per Lyons SJA (an appeal against the issuing of an injunction against UQ was dismissed – University of Queensland v Y (2020) 5 QR 686 [70], [92] and [93]); and Walters v Drummond [2019] QSC 97 at [49]-[50], per Applegarth J.

Kirby J in dissent in Tang rejected the approach advocated by Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ, describing it as an “untextual” limitation that contradicted the “remedial language, structure and purpose of the Review Act” – [167] and [164].  Kirby J offers a valuable summary of the various interpretative approaches to the phrase “under an enactment” and offers his own preferred interpretation – see [149].  He criticises Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ for constricting the jurisdictional requirements for JR Act review in a manner that stands in sharp contrast to the standing requirements under the JR Act (that expressly refers to “interests”) – [152].  The contrast between the position of the majority and Kirby J is perhaps most clearly evident when comparing the majority judgments with Kirby J’s observations at [161]-[163].  For an illustration of the restrictiveness of Tang, see King v Director of Housing (2013) 306 ALR 150 at [49]-[68].

Government contracting and ADJR Act/JR Act Part 3 Review?

Given the absence of an enactment, decisions made under the Crown’s prerogative powers or residual executive powers (or capacities) - will not be reviewable under ADJR Act/JR Act.

In relation to contracting and payments under contracts by the Commonwealth, see Williams v Commonwealth [No 1] (2012) 248 CLR 156, for eg [62]-[83] (French CJ), [121]-[159] (Gummow and Bell JJ), [201]-[218] (Hayne J), [508]-[534] (Crennan J), and [577]-[594] (Kiefel J); and Williams v Commonwealth [No 2] (2014) 252 CLR 416 at [76]-[83] (per French CJ and Hayne, Kiefel, Bell and Keane JJ).

Recall also the distinction between prerogative powers and contractual capacities discussed by members of the High Court:

“An act done in the execution of a prerogative executive power is an act which is capable of interfering with legal rights of others. An act done in the execution of a non-prerogative executive capacity, in contrast, involves nothing more than the utilisation of a bare capacity or permission, which can also be described as ability to act or as a ‘faculty’” - Plaintiff M68/2015 v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection (2016) 257 CLR 42 at [135], per Gageler J; although contrast Davis v Minister for Immigration, Citizenship, Migrant Services and Multicultural Affairs (2023) 279 CLR 1, at [127]-[137], per Edelman J.

Are government decisions to enter into contracts or to exercise rights under a contract, decisions which are under an enactment?

See *Australian National University v Burns (1982) 64 FLR 166.  Burns was a professor at ANU.  He was employed by the University in 1966.  The University was constituted by an Act of Parliament, the Australian National University Act 1946.  That Act, in s23, provided that the Council of the University could, from time to time, appoint professors.  The Act went on to confer on the University Council a power to make “statutes” relating, among other things, to the dismissal of professors.  No “statute” was adopted by the University Council in relation to dismissals.  Dismissal, however, was dealt with in the specific standard form contract of employment between Professor Burns and the University.  The contract provided that the professor could have his appointment terminated on the grounds of permanent incapacity.  It was on this basis, in 1981, armed with a Commonwealth medical officer’s report describing Professor Burns “as a seriously ill man”, that the University Council purported to terminate Professor Burns’ appointment.

Professor Burns sought reasons, under the ADJR Act, for the Council’s decision. The decision to dismiss Professor Burns was held to have been made directly under the employment contract and not under the University Act.  The decision was therefore held not to have been made “under an enactment” for the purposes of the ADJR Act.

Two additional points re Burns:

The Court did not limit the expression “under an enactment” to cases where decision-making powers in an enactment were precisely stated.  A broadly stated power in an enactment might provide for a decision, which, once made, would be held to be made under that enactment.

The Court acknowledged that you could have two effective sources of the power to make a decision - see 175-176.  Specific examples were given of statutory provisions which had special protection for public servants in relation to promotion, disciplinary action or dismissal, ie Public Service Act 1902 and Broadcasting and Television Act 1942.

ANU v Burns involved the exercise of a decision-making power after a contract had been entered into.

What about decisions by public bodies to enter into contracts?  In *Hawker Pacific Pty Ltd v Freeland (1982) 52 ALR 185, a government department had sought to purchase aircraft from a particular tenderer.  Another aircraft supplier which had unsuccessfully tendered for the contract sought judicial review under the ADJR Act of, among other things, the decision to enter the contract with its competitor.  There was no statutory provision that set out a power to contract.  The court held that the decision to enter the contract was not “under an enactment” but instead, the decision was held to be an exercise of residual executive power.  Hence, review was not available under the ADJR Act.  Note that there were particular regulations which had been made under the Audit Act 1901 which regulated the power to contract, essentially by setting out safeguards against improper tendering practices.  The company seeking review had argued that the regulations were an “enactment” under which the decision was made.  The Federal Court rejected this argument.  The judge considered the regulations, but concluded that compliance with the regulations was merely a step leading to the awarding of the contract.  Though the regulations regulated the power to contract, they were not the source of a “valid power to contract” - at 189.

See also Concord Data Solutions Pty Ltd v Director-General of Education [1994] 1 QdR 343, in which a similar result was reached; and Blizzard v O’Sullivan [1994] 1 QdR 112.

Contrast Australian Capital Territory Health Authority v Berkeley Cleaning Group Pty Ltd (1985) 7 FCR 575 at p 189.  The case concerned, like the Hawker case, a contract awarded at the end of a tender process.  The unsuccessful tenderer was seeking review of a decision to award a cleaning contract for a hospital and nursing home to another company.  The decision was made by an ACT statutory body (the Health Authority) which was established under an ACT Ordinance.  Section 8 of the Ordinance provided, among other things, that the authority had the power to “enter into contracts”.  Nowhere in the report is there any reference to statutory regulation of the tendering process.  The Full Federal Court decided that the decision to enter into the contract was, in fact, made under s8 and hence, ADJR Act review was available.  The Court distinguished Burns’ case on the grounds that this case involved the entry into a contract and not the exercise of rights under a contract, as was the position in Burns.  The Hawker Pacific decision was not mentioned by the Court although it is distinguishable given the existence of s8 of the relevant Ordinance.  Berkeley Cleaning was followed by Federal Court in James Richardson v Federal Airports Corporation (1992) 117 ALR 277 at 280-282.

It is important to emphasise that the approach in Berkeley Cleaning was expressly rejected by Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ in obiter in Tang (2005) 221 CLR 99, [82]-[83] (and was implicitly rejected by Gleeson CJ).  Another Federal Court decision that is useful in understanding the relevant principles is *General Newspapers Pty Ltd v Telstra (1993) 45 FCR 164 (Gummow J sat on the Federal Court in Telstra and the reasoning in Telstra also influenced Gleeson CJ’s approach in Tang).  In Telstra the Full Court of the Federal Court considered a claim by a company that had failed in its bid to secure the contract with Telstra to print the White and Yellow Pages phone directories.  The applicant company sought to have Telstra’s conduct scrutinised under both the ADJR Act and the Trade Practices Act (now renamed the Competition and Consumer Act 2010).

The facts of the case are complicated by the fact that they straddle the dissolution of the entity which was known as “Telecom” and the creation of a new entity known as “Telstra.”  General Newspapers had approached Telecom to see if it could be considered in future for the Yellow and White Pages printing contracts.  General Newspapers was told by a Telecom employee that they would be placed on a tender list.  There does not appear to have been any specific statutory provision dealing with the tendering process.  The statutory change from Telecom then occurred and “Telstra” was incorporated under the ACT’s Corporations Law.  After this change, Telstra decided not to go through a tender process after all, and decided to award the printing contract to the companies that were currently doing its printing work.  It was essentially these decisions that General Newspapers wanted judicially reviewed under the ADJR Act.

The old legislation which governed Telecom, did have a provision like those considered in Berkeley and James Richardson, ie a provision that stated that Telecom had the power to enter into contracts.  The new Act (ie Telecommunications Act 1991 (Cth)), which allowed for the incorporation of Telstra under the Corporations Law, had no such provision.  The Telecommunications Act did, however, have a provision in s238, which indirectly dealt with the production of telephone directories.  For some reason, General Newspapers did not rely on this provision in respect of their claim under the ADJR Act - see 166-167.  What was relied upon as “the enactment” under which the decision to enter the contract was made, was section 161 of the Corporations Law.  This was the general company provision that said that companies had all the powers of a natural person.  See now s124 of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth).

The Federal Court unanimously rejected General Newspapers’ claim that the ADJR Act applied.  Gummow J agreed with the joint judgment of Davies and Einfeld JJ on there being no reviewable decision to which the ADJR Act could be applied (at 194).  The Court’s reasoning as to why the ADJR Act did not apply makes it almost impossible to find that decisions made by those exercising government power which relate to contracts, are made “under an enactment.”

According to Davies and Einfeld JJ in Telstra, the ADJR Act was concerned with decisions which, being authorised or required by an enactment, are given force or effect by the enactment, or by a principle of law applicable to the enactment.  The ADJR Act will not apply to allow review of decisions made under the general law applicable in the community.

Davies and Einfeld JJ refused to follow Berkeley Cleaning and James Richardson.  To be under an enactment, they held that a decision must effectively derive its “force or effect” from empowering statute.  A statute which merely confers a capacity to contract, does not give the decision to contract its force or effect, which instead comes from ordinary laws of contract.

Davies and Einfeld JJ, however, left open two “exceptional cases where it may be proper to bring a proceeding under the ADJR Act” in a contractual context.  The second exceptional case related to a particular tender process specifically governed by a statutory scheme.  Davies and Einfeld JJ cited Gerah Imports Pty Ltd v Minister for Industry, Technology and Commerce (1987) 17 FCR 1 as authority that potentially supported review under the ADJR Act of a tender process “made by reference to the provisions of a statutory scheme” – at 173 of Telstra (45 FCR).  The first exception is difficult to understand.  What Davies and Einfeld JJ said was that exceptionally, where there has been a contract entered for an ulterior purpose, such as for private gain, and a challenge to the contract can be made by reference to an enactment under which the contract was entered, review under the ADJR Act may then be available - at 173.  It is not clear what the Federal Court meant by this.  Why single out ulterior purpose?  Why if other grounds of legality review found in the ADJR Act are present should there be no review?

There is a related policy question: “should every disappointed tenderer be able to run to the courts to seek judicial review of a government tendering decision?”  In responding to this question, it is relevant to consider that courts issuing the writs or making declarations or issuing injunctions have not given a consistent answer.  The Qld Supreme Court has made a declaration in respect of a contract entered contrary to statutory tender rules – Hunter Brothers v Brisbane City Council [1984] 1 QdR 328 [cf Tonkin v Cooma-Monaro Shire Council (2006) 145 LGERA 48; [2006] NSWCA 50; and MBA Land Holdings Pty Ltd v Gungahlin Development Authority (2000) 206 FLR 120].  There is English authority for the proposition that in the absence of a statutory tender regime, there is no administrative law review – see R v The Lord Chancellor, ex parte Hibbit & Sanders (Unreported 11 March 1993) noted in [1993] Public Law 214.  Arguments in favour of review under English law are collected in S Arrowsmith, ‘Judicial Review and the Contractual Powers of Public Authorities’ (1990) 106 Law Quarterly Review 277; and SH Bailey, ‘Judicial Review of Contracting Decisions’ [2007] Public Law 444.

The significance of General Newspapers is illustrated by CEA Technologies v Civil Aviation Authority (1994) 122 ALR 724.  In CEA, there was specific statutory authorisation for a public authority to enter into contracts but the Court, following Telstra, held that the contract in question did not derive its force or effect from the statute and was therefore not made “under an enactment”.

Telstra was cited without apparent disapproval by Thomas J in KC Park Safe v Cairns City Council [1997] 1 QdR 497 at 502; see also Thomas J, ‘Administrative Jurisdiction: The Jewel in the Crown’ (1998) 9 Public Law Review 43 at 50-51.

The approach in Telstra was also effectively endorsed in the context of the JR Act by Gleeson CJ in Griffith University v Tang (2005) 221 CLR 99, at [18], and Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ in Tang in essence endorse the approach at [80].  In Fuller v Lawrence (2024) 99 ALJR 103, at [10] and [19]-[21], the High Court (in a joint judgment of Gageler CJ, Gordon, Edelman, Gleeson and Beech-Jones JJ), implicitly recognised the close relationship between the approaches of Gleeson CJ and the plurality in Tang.

Contrast the criticism of Telstra in Allars, ‘Private Law but Public Power: Removing Administrative Law Review from Government Business Enterprises’ (1995) 6 Public Law Review 44, in particular at 56-75.

It is also relevant to note that government contracting can be scrutinised relying on non-administrative law causes of action, such as asserting the existence of a collateral contract.  Previously, scrutiny also occurred under the Trade Practices Act (see now the Competition and Consumer Act 2010).  See GA Flick, ‘Integrity in Government Tendering: Means of Review’ (1998) 14 Building and Construction Law 13, addressing the decision of Finn J in Hughes Aircraft Systems International v Air Services Australia (1997) 76 FCR 151; and L Alford and E Bird, ‘Tendering for government business: Process contracts, good faith, fair dealing, and probity’ (2011) 85 Australian Law Journal 678.

For discussion by Professor Aronson of questions posed by government privatisation and outsourcing - see his contribution to The Province of Administrative Law edited by M Taggart (Hart, 1997), at 40-70.  The Administrative Review Council issued a report on contracting out of Government Services in 1998.  The report is available at:

https://webarchive.nla.gov.au/awa/20190808004345/https://www.arc.ag.gov.au/Publications/Reports/Pages/Reportfiles/ReportNo42.aspx

The report is discussed in LM Zamprogno, ‘ARC Report on the Contracting Out of Government Services: Its context and Recommendations’ (1999) 6 Australian Journal of Administrative Law 75.  See also N Seddon, ‘Is Accountability a Cost of Contracting Out?’ in C Finn (ed), Sunrise or Sunset? Administrative Law in the New Millennium, Australian Institute of Administrative Law, Canberra, 2000, 37; C Koutsis, ‘Is it too Tough for Tendering?  Enhancing Accountability in Tendering Decisions for New South Wales Infrastructure Projects’ (2000) 8 Australian Journal of Administrative Law 5; C Saunders and KKF Yam, ‘Government Regulation by Contract: Implications for the Rule of Law’ (2004) 15 Public Law Review 51; and (from a US perspective) MJ Trebilcock and EM Iacobucci, ‘Privatization and Accountability’ (2003) 116 Harvard Law Review 1422; (from a New Zealand perspective) J Palmer and K Weaver, ‘Judicial review in a commercial context’ [2009] New Zealand Law Journal 14.  For a consideration of governmental responses to the global financial crisis from an administrative law perspective – see M Aronson, ‘The Great Depression, this Depression and Administrative Law’ (2009) 37 Federal Law Review 165.

### (4)	SECTION 4(b) JR ACT – UNDER A NON-STATUTORY SCHEME OR PROGRAM

The scope of s4(b) is uncertain.  For example, it is not clear the role of the principle of non-justiciability?

Sources of guidance on the interpretation of s4(b) include:

-	EARC’s report at 43-44 and 50-53;

-	Administrative Review Council’s Report No 32, Ch 4 and Report No 42, [6.43].

JR Act s4(b) provides that there will be a decision to which JR Act applies if

-	The decision is of administrative character;

-	The decision is made by officer or employee of State or State authority or local authority (or by such a body directly);

-	The decision is under non-statutory scheme or program; and

-	The scheme or program involves funds appropriated by parliament or obtained from taxes, charges, fees or levies authorised under an enactment.

For discussion of “decision of an administrative character”, see the earlier discussion.

The definition of “State authority” in s3 of the JR Act is also relevant.

Holmes CJ, writing extra-judicially, has observed that s4(b) “… has been a signal failure in the sense that no-one has ever made a successful application under it” – C Holmes, ‘Not making a difference: Queensland’s extension of statutory judicial review’ (2016) 85 AIAL Forum 1.

EARC’s report gave two egs of when what would become JR Act s4(b) might apply:

Where there is a decision which adversely affected a citizen, made under a scheme operated by a municipal or shire council funded by rate collections, but having no statutory basis; and

Where there is a decision “... of a primary producer body established under statute which operated a scheme that had no particular statutory basis but was funded by compulsory exactions from producers pursuant to statutory authority” - at 51.

Examples of ADJR Act cases that may have fallen within the terms of s4(b) had the cases involved a Qld government decision-maker are Ex-Christmas Islanders Association Inc v Attorney General’s Department (Commonwealth) (2005) 149 FCR 170, Macdonald Pty Ltd v Hammence (1984) 1 FCR 45, and Taranto Pty Ltd v Madigan (1988) 81 ALR 208 (the final 2 examples were provided by EARC at 43).

EARC rejected potential third limb to JR Act s4, which would have allowed review of “a decision of an administrative character otherwise operating in law to determine a question affecting the rights, interests or legitimate expectations of any person” - see [6.35].  EARC was concerned that if such a third limb of s4 was incorporated, it would allow review of the following types of decisions:

- decision on the outcome of a government tendering process;

- decision to enter into contract;

- decision made pursuant to rights of State of Queensland to manage and control its property;

- range of welfare type decisions, eg adoptions, placement with foster parents, etc;

- range of decisions made by prison administrators; and

- range of decisions made by police under common law powers.

EARC recognised that some of these decisions would be reviewable via the historical remedies (ie the writs and declarations and injunctions) and thus, may be reviewable under the JR Act Part 5, but EARC considered that it was not appropriate to allow review of such decisions under Part 3 of the JR Act or to allow a right to reasons for such decisions under JR Act Part 4.  Informal review by the Ombudsman (which is considered in LAWS1703 Principles of Public Law) was seen as a more appropriate remedy – [6.38]-[6.40].

Therefore it appears that EARC may not have envisaged s4(b) applying to contracts entered into, eg as a result of government tendering processes.  This conclusion gains some support from two decisions of Thomas J in Queensland.  In both cases, s4(b) not referred to and so the cases are not conclusive on the potential operation of s4(b) of the JR Act.

Concord Data Solutions Pty Ltd v Director-General of Education [1994] 1 QdR 343 involved a disappointed tenderer for the supply of school library software.  A decision by the Director-General to appoint a competitor as preferred supplier was held not to be a decision “under an enactment.”  Thomas J placed reliance on decisions such as Hawker Pacific.  Note also that Thomas J thought that the tendering process of local authorities in Queensland would involve decisions “under” enactments - see 353.

Blizzard v O’Sullivan [1994] 1 QdR 112 was similar to the case before the Federal Court in ANU v Burns and essentially the same result was reached [note, however, that there is a serious typographical error in the report at 117, line 27].  Although Blizzard does not specifically mention JR Act s4(b), Thomas J’s analysis of the scope of the word “instrument” may have some bearing on the scope of s4(b).  Contrast the potential application of the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction considered by Philippides JA in Berenyi v Maynard [2015] QSC 370 at [48]-[56].

The most important words in JR Act s4(b) appear to be “under a non-statutory scheme or program”.

If “non-statutory scheme or program” is given a broad interpretation, for eg, to include contracts, then JR Act Part 3 will potentially apply to government contracting, allowing judicial scrutiny of tendering processes, etc.

A narrow interpretation of “non-statutory scheme or program” would bring about the result suggested by EARC’s report.

*Anghel v Minister for Transport (No 1) [1995] 1 QdR 465, concerned an attempt to review decisions made by a Minister to give approval for the construction of an extra railway line through south-east Brisbane to service the Port of Brisbane.  The decision was made pursuant to a non-statutory Federal/State project which involved Queensland public funds.  A question that arose before the Supreme Court was whether such a project was a “scheme or program”?  It was argued on behalf of the Minister that the expression “scheme or program” implied repetition and had no application to a single project.  Derrington J rejected this interpretation.  The word “scheme” was held to encompass single projects and “program” was held to cover repetitive processes.

*Bituminous Products Pty Ltd v General Manager (Road Systems and Engineering), Department of Main Roads [2005] 2 QdR 344, involved a challenge under the JR Act to a government decision to change basic specifications for road building materials that had the effect of excluding materials sold by the applicant from consideration for road building projects in Queensland.  The relevant legislation obliged the Director General of the Department of Main Roads to develop roads implementation programs.  Funds were provided by appropriation legislation, both State and Federal.  The relevant decision to change the specifications was made by a departmental officer who was charged with administering an “approved product list”.  The applicant argued inter alia that this decision was made under a road implementation program that was a “non-statutory scheme or program” for the purposes of JR Act s4(b).  Holmes J rejected the applicant’s submissions.  Her Honour appeared reluctant to give the words “non-statutory scheme or program” a broad construction.  Holmes J noted the remedial nature of the legislation but also emphasised the policy concern regarding fragmentation of government decision-making.  Amongst the factors considered in determining whether there existed a “non-statutory scheme or program” was whether there were specific, as opposed to general, statutory appropriations, and whether the arrangements exhibited coherence or were systematic in their operation.  These factors did not support the applicant.  Holmes J also observed that to characterise the “Roads Implementation Program” as a non-statutory program, given that it was specifically required and regulated by statute, “would be an exercise in the absurd” - [2005] 2 QdR 344 at 351-353 [24]-[29].  Bituminous Products was cited with approval in JJ Richards & Sons Pty Ltd v Bowen Shire Council [2008] 2 QdR 342 at [23].

Does expression “scheme or program” extend to tendering processes or contracts?

Regarding the exercise of rights under contracts, there is analogous judicial authority and academic commentary that supports a narrow interpretation of JR Act s4(b), ie that decisions under non-statutory schemes or programs relate only to decisions which unilaterally affect rights or interests.  W Lane, Queensland Administrative Law (Looseleaf, Thomson Reuters, 2025) at [1.700] contrasts contracts, which have a consensual basis, which does not sit comfortably with the notion of a scheme or program which would appear to have a unilateral character.  See also Blizzard v O’Sullivan - at 121 - where Thomas J rejected the argument that a contract could be a statutory instrument.  Thomas J’s reasoning supports the idea that implicit in the notion of an instrument is something having effect through a unilateral exercise of power.  Compare section 7(3) of the Statutory Instruments Act.  This reasoning can readily be adapted to JR Act s4(b).  The distinction between unilateral and consensual exercises of power has been utilised to deny common law judicial review of government contractual practices.

Section 4(b) of the JR Act was also considered in Medtek v Chief Health Officer for Queensland (1998) 4 QAR 570 at 583-4; and Mikitis v Director General, Department of Justice and Attorney-General (1999) 5 QAR 123.

In relation to decisions to enter contracts, the reasoning in Telstra and Tang could be applied in the context of JR Act s4(b) which would restrict review.

Note Clause 13 of Schedule 2 of the JR Act which excludes the right to reasons for tendering decisions – this suggests that Part 4 (and therefore Part 3) of the JR Act would otherwise be applicable.  Alternatively, Clause 13 of Schedule 2 may have been inserted into the JR Act out of an abundance of caution.  This does not, however, explain why, if such a cautious approach was being taken, the draftsperson did not include a similar exclusion from review in Schedule 1 of the JR Act.  EARC expressly refused to recommend a Schedule 1 exclusion of tendering decisions and was of the view that certain tendering decisions would be reviewable via Part 3 of the JR Act – EARC’s report [7.24]-[7.32] and [12.24].

Section 4(b) of the JR Act is in need of further judicial exploration to test its full potential.

Another issue that arises is the relationship between JR Act s4(b) and the grounds for legality review because s4(b) is intended to operate when there is a non-statutory decision-making function or power, and most grounds of legality review set out in Part 3 of the JR Act depend on express or implied statutory limits on the scope of a decision-making power.  Grounds of legality review in the JR Act which refer to enactments therefore need to be adjusted if s4(b) type review is to be available.  This is achieved by s9 of the JR Act.  JR Act s9 allows review via s4(b) under sections 20(2)(d) and (e) and 21(2)(d) and (e).

### (5)	NON-JUSTICIABILITY AND THE ADJR ACT AND PART 3 OF THE JR ACT

For a general consideration of non-justiciability in Australia, see, for eg, Chris Finn, ‘The concept of “justiciability” in administrative law’ in Groves and Lee (eds), Australian Administrative Law, (Cambridge University Press, 2007), 143; and Chris Finn, The Justiciability of Administrative Decisions: A Redundant Concept? (2002) 30 Federal Law Review 239.  Non-justiciability, in the context of a report by a parliamentary committee, was considered by the High Court in Attorney-General (Tas) v Casimaty (2024) 98 ALJR 1139 at [31]-[42], in the joint judgment of Gageler CJ, Gordon, Steward, Gleeson, Jagot and Beech-Jones JJ, and by Edelman J at [99]-[104].

At common law, the position in Australia, in the context of administrative law, is well-illustrated by two decisions: South Australia v O’Shea (1987) 163 CLR 378 and Minister for Arts, Heritage and Environment v Peko Wallsend Ltd (1987) 75 ALR 218.  See also Hicks v Ruddock (2007) 156 FCR 574 at [14]-[34] and [57]-[86]; and Habib v Commonwealth (No 2) (2009) 175 FCR 350 at [50]-[68].

An important question from the perspective of the ADJR Act and the JR Act is whether, and, if so, to what extent, decisions of Cabinet can be judicially reviewed?

We have already seen (when considering natural justice) that at common law, at least one member of the High Court has suggested that a Cabinet decision which breaches the rules of natural justice may be open to some degree of judicial review - see Mason CJ in O’Shea at 387-388.

Under the JR Act s4(a), if a decision made by Cabinet is made “under an enactment”, then it would seem to be reviewable provided other the requirements of s4(a) are present - this conclusion is supported by reference to “Cabinet” in s32(2)(a) JR Act.  It is rare for statutes to specifically provide for Cabinet decisions.

What about decisions of Cabinet which are not made “under an enactment”?  Note again that s32(2)(a) JR Act suggests that such decisions of Cabinet could be reviewable via s4(b).

If Cabinet decisions are reviewable, what, if any, limits are there to review?  Does the notion of non-justiciability continue to apply following the passing of the JR Act?  EARC thought it would - see [6.18] of EARC’s report.  EARC also considered, however, that the right to reasons would still potentially apply in respect of unreviewable non-justiciable decisions – see [11.32].

But if non-justiciability does apply to potentially limit review, how far does it extend?  Lane, Queensland Administrative Law (Looseleaf, Thomson Reuters, 2025) suggests that review may be limited to Cabinet decisions “which directly affected rights or interests sufficient to invoke an obligation to afford procedural fairness” – at [1.730].  In relation to the prerogative of mercy, the Qld Court of Appeal has held in Holzinger v Attorney-General (2020) 5 QR 314 that a decision by the Governor, advised by the executive government, regarding the prerogative of mercy is not reviewable by the courts – [80]-[89] and [121].

Decisions of high governmental or political nature, or those which involve a large number of interlocking interests and considerations (“polycentric disputes”) appear to remain non-justiciable - see Chapter 9 of Administrative Review Council’s Report No 32.  Note, however, the arguments offered by C Finn against the view that polycentric disputes are non-justiciable – eg Finn, “The concept of ‘justiciability’ in administrative law” in Groves and Lee (eds), Australian Administrative Law (Cambridge University Press, 2007) 143 at 146-147.  On non-justiciability more generally (and on the related concept of deference) see also KM Hayne, ‘Deference – an Australian Perspective’ [2011] Public Law 75; A Kavanagh, ‘Defending Deference in Public Law and Constitutional Law Theory’ (2010) 126 Law Quarterly Review 222; and Daly P, ‘Justiciability and the “Political Question” Doctrine’ [2010] Public Law 160.

Contrast non-justiciability with the principle of Crown immunity from statute and from suit – see s64 of the Judiciary Act 1903 and the Crown Proceedings Act 1980 (Qld); McNamara (McGrath) v Consumer Trader and Tenancy Tribunal of NSW (2005) 221 CLR 646; Jarratt v Commissioner of Police (NSW) (2005) 224 CLR 44; Townsville Hospital Board v Townsville City Council (1982) 149 CLR 282, 291-292; Bropho v Western Australia (1990) 171 CLR 1; s13 Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld); Re Commissioner of Water Resources [1991] 1 Qd R 549, 551-552; Jacobsen v Rogers (1995) 182 CLR 572; NT Power Generation Pty Ltd v Power and Water Authority (2004) 219 CLR 40; and Australian Competition and Consumer Commission v Baxter Healthcare Ltd (2007) 232 CLR 1.

(6)	DECISIONS OF THE GOVERNOR-GENERAL AND STATE GOVERNORS

Prior to enactment of ADJR Act, the view prevailed that decisions of the Governor-General were non-justiciable.  For this reason, the ADJR Act equivalent of s4 expressly excludes from its scope decisions made by the Governor-General.

After the enactment of the ADJR Act, the common law developed suggesting the possibility of judicial review of certain decisions of the Governor-General - see R v Toohey ex parte Northern Land Council (1981) 151 CLR 170; FAI Insurance v Winneke (1982) 151 CLR 342.  The common law is therefore wider than the ADJR Act on this point.

Section 4(a) of the JR Act has the effect of potentially bringing review of the Governor in Council’s decisions under Part 3 JR Act, ie in line with the current position at common law.  JR Act Part 4 (ie the right to reasons) also now applies to decisions of the Governor in Council – see JR Act s32.  Note s53 JR Act.

(7)	SECTION 21 JR ACT - REVIEW OF CONDUCT

See section 8 of the JR Act. Recall the discussion of the review of conduct under the ADJR Act in Bond.

On whether the review of conduct should proceed once a reviewable decision has been made, see Minister for Immigration and Multicultural Affairs v Ozmanian (1996) 71 FCR 1 at 22-23 (per Sackville J) and 30 (per Kiefel J).

The words in brackets in s21(1) JR Act are not in the ADJR Act - see Gunaleela v Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs (1987) 15 FCR 543 at 556-557; Chan v Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs (1989) 169 CLR 379 at 386, per Mason CJ; and Margarula v Minister for Environment (1999) 92 FCR 35 at 44-46, per Sundberg J (reading s6 of the ADJR Act by reference to the Carltona principle).

### (8)	SECTION 22 JR ACT - REVIEW OF FAILURES TO DECIDE

Section 5 of the JR Act covers, for example:

- failure to make orders, awards or determinations;

- failure to give certificates, directions, approvals, consents or permissions, etc.

Note that in JR Act s22 there is no list of the grounds of legality review that we find in ss 20 and 21.

There are two types of review under JR Act s22

1.	Section 22(1), where:

- a person has a duty to make a decision to which the JR Act applies;

- no period is fixed for making this decision by any law; and

- there has been unreasonable delay in making the decision.

Duty to make a decision - The Federal Court has distinguished statutory powers from duties ie a duty to make a decision must exist for review under JR Act s22 - see Brownsville Nominees Pty Ltd v Commissioner of Taxation (1988) 19 FCR 169 at 173.  Note also the definition of “duty” in s3 of the JR Act.  Heydon J in Public Service Association of South Australia v Industrial Relations Commission of South Australia (2012) 249 CLR 398 at [91], observed that “[i]n the absence of statutory language to the contrary, a grant of jurisdiction ordinarily carries with it a duty to exercise it”; although contrast the Full Court of the Federal Court’s observation in Yasmin v Attorney-General (Cth) (2015) 236 FCR 169 at [113], that “[t]he question whether when Parliament reposes a discretionary power in a person, it intends to repose with it a duty to consider and determine whether to exercise the power (favourably or unfavourably) is not to be resolved by reference to any rule courts can assume Parliament and its legislative drafters are constructively, or actually, aware of …”.  An important factor appears to be whether the decision-maker has been given a power of an adjudicative nature (such as the powers invariably given to tribunals).  According to Aronson, Groves and Weeks “[a]n adjudicative body which is invested with jurisdiction to hear and determine a matter will almost always be under a positive duty to entertain matters within its jurisdiction. … Similarly, there are many situations in which powers of a non-adjudicative nature must be exercised.  However, there is no automatic implication that a non-adjudicative power carries with it a duty to consider whether to exercise it” – 951.

Unreasonable delay – is defined “objectively” as meaning that no reasonable person acting in good faith would, in the circumstances, have approved of the delay in making a decision.  The delay must not be capricious or irrational - see Thornton v Repatriation Commission (1981) 35 ALR 485 at 490-491 and CISC Pty Ltd v de Lacy [1996] 1 QdR 237 at 241-242.  For an eg of a finding of no unreasonable delay – see David v Superannuation Complaints Tribunal [2006] FCA 389.

2.	Section 22(2) of the JR Act, where:

- a person with a duty to make a decision;

- a law fixes a period within which a decision must be made; and

- a decision has not been made before the end of that period.

For consideration by the Qld Court of Appeal of when s22(2) does not apply – see Kruck v Qld Regional Parole Board [2009] 1 QdR 463 at [21]-[23].

Under JR Act s22 it is also a requirement that an applicant must be “aggrieved” by the failure to decide.
