REMEDIES

This study guide considers the various judicial review remedies available in administrative law — namely, statutory orders, the writs, and the equitable remedies of declarations and injunctions. The guide is designed to assist students gain a good understanding of the legal effect of the various remedies, which courts may grant them (and on what legal basis, ie in addition to the need to show some legal error), who they are directed at, and the circumstances in which they are available.

## 1. 	ORDERS THAT CAN BE MADE FOLLOWING SUCCESSFUL REVIEW UNDER PART 3 JR ACT/ADJR ACT

These powers are set out in s30 of JR Act.  Equivalent powers are set out in the ADJR Act in s16. These powers can only be exercised if there is a “decision to which … [the ADJR Act/JR Act Part 3] applies”, ie requirements that have already been considered in earlier classes.

JR Act s30 is divided into three major parts.  Sub-section 1 deals with Supreme Court’s power where there has been a decision by an administrative decision-maker.  Sub-section 2 deals with conduct.  Sub-section 3 deals with failures to decide.

Under the JR Act s30(1), the Supreme Court can quash or set aside the administrative decision effective from any date - see s30(1)(a).  It can refer the matter back to decision-maker for further consideration and can give directions as to how reconsideration should proceed including time limits for reconsideration - see s30(1)(b).  This power is used by courts to direct, for example, that a re-hearing be conducted by a differently constituted decision-maker - see Northern NSW FM Pty Ltd v Australian Broadcasting Tribunal (1990) 26 FCR 39 at 42-43.

The Supreme Court can make declarations as to rights of parties and can direct parties to do or to stop doing something - see JR Act s30(1)(c) and (d).

JR Act s30(2), which deals with conduct, includes similar powers to declare and direct - see s30(2)(a) and (b).

JR Act s30(3) is almost identical to sub-section 2, although, given that it applies to failures to decide, it includes the power to direct a decision-maker to make a decision - see JR Act s30(3)(a), (b) and (c).

POWER TO MAKE ORDERS UNDER JR ACT SECTION 30 DISCRETIONARY

Powers in JR Act s30 are discretionary.  An applicant under JR Act Part 3 can have established all of the requirements for Part 3 judicial review but may still not get order under s30 on discretionary grounds.  If there has been delay or misconduct, or if the error giving rise to a ground of review is only of a trivial nature, then the Supreme Court may not make an order under s30.  This discretion is a judicial discretion and will only be exercised in circumstances which are just.  On the relationship between the discretionary powers contained in the ADJR Act equivalents of s30 and ss12 and 13 of the JR Act – see Kamha v Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (2005) 147 FCR 516 at [87].

POWERS TO DECLARE AND DIRECT

Judicial powers to declare and direct under the ADJR Act/JR Act Part3 are very broad.  The High Court has held that these powers should not be restricted by undue technicality - see Park Oh Ho v Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs (1989) 167 CLR 637 at 644-645.  Compare Johns v Australian Securities Commission (1993) 178 CLR 408 at 433-434.

Note that the power to declare and direct is not to be used to force the original decision-maker to decide in a particular way upon reconsideration.  This would undermine distinction between merits and legality review.  Having made that general point, there have been some instances under the ADJR Act where the power to direct has been used to force a decision-maker to make a particular decision.  See, for eg, Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Conyngham (1986) 11 FCR 528; although see Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Guo (1997) 191 CLR 559 at 578-579.

NO RIGHT TO DAMAGES (AS YET)

Section 30 of the JR Act would not appear to allow the court to order that an administrative decision-maker pay damages in respect of an invalid administrative decision.  Damages are not generally available in administrative law proceedings - see Park Oh Ho at 645.

One can only seek damages against administrative decision-maker if there is some other cause of action, eg in tort or contract. See also s 59(3) of the Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld).  If interlocutory relief is sought, damages may, however, be payable in respect of that interlocutory relief (see below).

In relation to mandatory orders, see Enid Campbell, ‘Enforcement of Public Duties which are Impossible to Perform’ (2003) 10 Australian Journal of Administrative Law 201.

APPEALS FROM REVIEW DECISIONS UNDER JR ACT PART 3/ADJR ACT

Appeals appear to be governed by general rules for appeals – see, for eg, the Supreme Court Act 1991 (Qld), in particular s62.

Note the specific appeal provisions in ss 15, 48 and 49 of JR Act.

Re the ADJR Act - see ss 24 and 25 of Federal Court of Australia Act 1976 (Cth).

THE WRITS AND DECLARATIONS AND INJUNCTIONS AS JUDICIAL REVIEW REMEDIES

INTRODUCTION

Turning now from the new to the old, so far as administrative law remedies go.  We now need to consider 3 types of remedy which were the original vehicles for raising the grounds of legality review.  These remedies are the prerogative writs of certiorari, prohibition and mandamus, and declarations and injunctions.

Prior to the enactment of the ADJR Act and the JR Act these remedies were the only way to seek judicial review of exercises of government power.  It was through these remedies that the grounds of legality review developed.

HISTORY

The prerogative writs of certiorari, prohibition and mandamus have been around for centuries.  On their development - see Thomas J, ‘Administrative Review: The Jewel in the Crown’ (1998) 9 Public Law Review 43, in particular at 44-46; Aronson, ‘Retreating to the History of Judicial Review?’ (2019) 47 Federal Law Review 179 at 183-188; and (for a more detailed account) de Smith’s Judicial Review (9th ed, Sweet & Maxwell, 2024) chapter 15.  Declarations and injunctions (so far as administrative law is concerned) have come on the scene much later.

The JR Act Part 3 and the ADJR Act have “codified” the grounds of legality review and provide a simpler procedure for judicial review.  However, the JR Act Part 3 and the ADJR Act do not provide for review in every instance where the writs, and declarations and injunctions (also referred to as equitable remedies), are available.  In particular, decisions of a legislative character can be reviewed relying on declarations, the writs apply to decisions of a judicial character, and review relying on the writs or the equitable remedies does not appear to be limited to decisions that are made under an enactment.  Declarations and injunctions are available in an administrative law context against the conduct of non-governmental bodies.  More on this later.  Turning first to the prerogative and constitutional writs.

# 2.	THE PREROGATIVE WRITS

THE PREROGATIVE WRITS GENERALLY

The prerogative writs were the original remedy in judicial review proceedings.  The writs include certiorari, prohibition, mandamus, habeas corpus and quo warranto.

Some basic features:

The writs are only available against public bodies

The writs have been issued in cases of jurisdictional error (including breaches of the rules of procedural fairness) and fraud.

“PREROGATIVE WRIT”

A writ is a document issued in the name of and with the authority of the Crown.  A writ commands a person to do or to stop doing something.  A prerogative writ is directed to a public official exercising government power in relation to something the official has or hasn’t done with respect to that government power.

WHY PREROGATIVE?

There are a number of theories as to why these writs (which have been around for at least 5 centuries) are known as “prerogative writs”:

The term “prerogative” was applied to the writs “because they were conceived as being intimately connected with the rights of the Crown and to ensure that the prerogative was not encroached upon by disobedience to the prescribed structure for the administration of justice” – per Gaudron and Gummow JJ in re Refugee Review Tribunal; Ex parte Aala (2000) 204 CLR 82 at [20];

From a relatively early time the writs were awarded mainly by the Court of King’s Bench (and hence there may have been a link to the King’s prerogatives);

The writs were not issued as of course and usually lay only within the discretion of the court (again suggesting a link, via the discretion of the Court of King’s Bench, to the Crown’s discretionary prerogatives);

If the Crown was an applicant for certiorari, it had (and still has) certain pre-eminent rights (or prerogatives) which were not available to a normal applicant - see R v A Judge of the District Court and Shelley; Ex parte the Attorney General [1991] 1 QdR 170 at 177-179.  Cf McHugh J in Re McBain (2002) 209 CLR 372 at [106]-[112];

A political explanation?  Because the writs generally had a beneficial operation so far as citizens were concerned, royalist lawyers sought to emphasise their link to the Monarch to increase Royal prestige - see de Smith’s Judicial Review (7th ed, Sweet & Maxwell, 2013) at 858-860.

Note that the High Court has indicated that the writs available via section 75(v) of the Constitution should be referred to as “Constitutional Writs” rather than prerogative writs - see re Refugee Review Tribunal; Ex parte Aala (2000) 204 CLR 82 at [21] and [138].

## WHAT DO THE PREROGATIVE WRITS DO?

Prohibition and certiorari resemble each other in that they both attack parts of the decision-making process of public bodies.

Prohibition halts (or prohibits) the decision-making process in its tracks or prevents a decision from being implemented.

Certiorari quashes a decision once it is made.

By contrast, mandamus generally lies to direct the performance of a public duty, for example, where a decision-maker refuses to make a decision.

Comparing these 3 prerogative writs with review under Part 3 of the JR Act/ADJR Act, there is a rough equivalence between the writs and the 3 types of review available under Part 3 of the JR Act/ADJR Act:

-	certiorari corresponds to orders under JR Act s 20/ADJR Act s5 quashing a decision,

-	prohibition corresponds to orders under JR Act s 21/ADJR Act s6 applicable to conduct, and

-	mandamus corresponds to orders under JR Act s 22/ADJR Act s7 applicable to failures to decide.

Prohibition is only available before the decision-maker has completely finished his or her function.  If the decision has been made and the function is finished, then the decision-maker is said to be functus officio and the writ of prohibition is not available.

As the writ of certiorari quashes decisions, it is only available where a decision has in fact been made.  Otherwise there is nothing to quash.

For mandamus to issue there must be a public duty to make a decision.

There are 2 other prerogative writs which are not covered in classes and are not examinable:

The writ of habeas corpus is very important to the liberty of persons within Australia.

The writ of quo warranto – this is a prerogative writ of very limited application.  It is available against a person who acts in an office in which s/he was not entitled to act.  It was technically abolished in Queensland by s42 of the JR Act, although practically the remedy remains.  Section 42 allows the Supreme Court to make an order to the same effect as the old remedy of quo warranto.  This remedy is referred to in JR Act as a “prerogative injunction” - see s42 and s3 of the JR Act - see Mackie v Lui (1994) 85 LGERA 353.

## THE PLACE OF THE WRITS TODAY

With the enactment of the JR Act the Supreme Court can no longer issue the prerogative writs of certiorari, mandamus and prohibition.  If frequency of use is any indication, the most important administrative law remedies in Qld appear to be those found in Part 3 of the JR Act.  This therefore raises the question: How important are the prerogative writs today?  Four points need to be made:

Prohibition and mandamus are enshrined in the Commonwealth Constitution in s75(v).  This section invests the High Court with original jurisdiction in all matters in which the writ of mandamus or prohibition or an injunction is sought against an officer of the Commonwealth.

“[Section 75(v)] … secures a basic element of the rule of law” – per Gleeson CJ in Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth (2003) 211 CLR 476 at [5].

“Section 75(v) … may turn out to be our greatest guarantee of individual liberty” - Thomas J writing extra-judicially in ‘Administrative Jurisdiction: The Jewel in the Crown’ (1998) 9 Public Law Review 43 at 51.

Section 75(v) of the Constitution does not refer to certiorari.  Kirby J suggests a reason for this omission in Re McBain (2002) 209 CLR 372, at [165].  Callinan J offers another explanation in Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth (2003) 211 CLR 476, [120]-[123].  The High Court has given very broad scope to the writ of prohibition which to some extent makes up for the failure to refer to certiorari in s75(v).  The High Court has also held that it has the power to issue certiorari, despite the terms of s75 – see R v Cook; Ex parte Twigg (1980) 147 CLR 15 at 18, 25-26, 29, 30 and 32-34.  Certiorari was granted in part because prohibition applied for.  See also Bodruddaza v Minister for Immigration and Multicultural and Indigenous Affairs (2007) 228 CLR 651 at [61]-[64]; In re Student Assistance Review Tribunal; Ex parte Emery (1981) 55 ALJR 387 at 391-392 and 394; Edwards v Santos Ltd (2011) 242 CLR 421 at [53]; and Plaintiff S4/2014 v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection (2014) 253 CLR 219 at [61].  Certiorari can also be issued under ss75(iii) and 76(i) of the Constitution - Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth (2003) 211 CLR 476 at [80]; see also L Aitken (1986-7) 16 Federal Law Review 380; note in (1995) 69 ALJ 784; re Refugee Review Tribunal; Ex parte Aala (2000) 204 CLR 82 at [14]; and cf re Minister for Immigration and Multicultural Affairs; Ex parte Durairajasingham (2000) 74 ALJR 405 at [79].

On the reference to “an officer of the Commonwealth” – a Commonwealth tribunal is an officer of the Commonwealth – see, for eg, SAAP v Minister for Immigration and Multicultural and Indigenous Affairs (2005) 228 CLR 294 at [43], [91], [153] and [180].  The High Court expressly left open whether “an independent contractor” might be “an officer of the Commonwealth” – Plaintiff M61/2010E v Commonwealth (2010) 243 CLR 319 at [51].

The prerogative writs are still available at the level of Federal administrative law via the Federal Court.  The Federal Court has a similar jurisdiction to the High Court in respect of prohibition, mandamus and injunctions under s39B of the Judiciary Act 1903 (Cth). The Federal Court also has the power to issue certiorari, including for non-jurisdictional errors of law – see Baker v Patrick Projects Pty Ltd (2014) 226 FCR 302 at [28]-[29].  Section 10 of the ADJR Act indicates that rights of review under the ADJR Act do not take away from rights of review by way of prerogative writ.

The prerogative writs are still very important in States where statutory remedies following the pattern of the ADJR Act have not been introduced.  On the constitutional significance of the power of State Supreme Courts to issue the prerogative writs, see Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW (2010) 239 CLR 531 at [98].

In Queensland, by s41(1) of the JR Act, the Supreme Court is no longer to issue the writs of mandamus, prohibition and certiorari.  However, s41(2) goes on to provide that the Supreme Court of Queensland still has jurisdiction to grant any relief or remedy “in the nature of, and to the same effect as” the relief or remedy which was able to be granted by way of prerogative writ.  Section 43(1) of the JR Act links with s41(2), providing for applications for review for prerogative orders.  Prerogative orders are defined effectively to be orders in the nature of the writs of prohibition, certiorari and mandamus.  See Westpac Banking Corporation v Commissioner of Stamp Duties (1993) 93 ATC 4335 at 4337, where it was observed that JR Act s43 “preserves and simplifies in statutory form” the relief formerly available by way of prerogative writ.

The position in Qld therefore is that if an old prerogative writ would have been available, the equivalent prerogative order under JR Act Part 5 is now available.  EARC’s intention appears to have been that as the common law prerogative writs develop, so should the equivalent JR Act Part 5 orders develop.  The Queensland Parliamentary Committee for Electoral and Administrative Review specifically intended this form of “dynamic” development – see the Committee’s report, Judicial Review of Administrative Decisions and Actions, 14 June 1991, 11.  For the constitutional significance of the wording of s41 (compare s42) - see Thomas (1998) 9 Public Law Review at 48.  See also Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2012] 1 QdR 525 at [75]-[80], per White JA.

The JR Act Part 3 appears to be most frequently relied on in Queensland.

## WHY RETAIN ORDERS IN THE NATURE OF THE PREROGATIVE WRITS?

There were two main reasons why orders in the nature of the writs were retained in Qld:

To ensure that there was no reduction in the scope of administrative law review through the enactment of the JR Act.  Decisions under the ADJR Act are illustrative of the limits of that Act (see, in particular our consideration of the words “decision to which … [the ADJR Act/JR Act Part 3] applies”).  Decisions illustrating the limits of the ADJR Act are collected in EARC’s Report No 90/R5 at [6.2] – see also Kwiksnax Mobile Industrial and General Caterers Pty Ltd v Logan City Council [1994] 1 QdR 291 at 310-311.  The potential for JR Act Part 5 remedies to be available where Part 3 review is not available is expressly recognised in Rule 569 of the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules;

At a more fundamental level, the prerogative writs are an essential safeguard in our political system.  According to Thomas J, the writs are (along with the other judicial review remedies) “one of the lynch pins that secure the protection of civil rights in this country” - (1998) 9 Public Law Review at 46.  The High Court affirmed the constitutional role of the writs issued by State Supreme Courts in Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW (2010) 239 CLR 531 at [98].

In principle, and in practice, the prerogative writ style remedies were retained in Queensland as an effective safety net.

## IMPROVING THE WRITS?

The JR Act does away with some of the technical difficulties associated with the old prerogative writs.  In terms of procedure, things have improved.  O’Reilly v Mackman [1983] 2 AC 237 at 279-280 illustrates some of the procedural problems that went with the writs.  Most of the procedural difficulties associated with the prerogative writs have now been overcome.  In Qld see, for eg, Rule 573 and Chapter 7 of the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules.

Aside from points of procedure, however, a number of difficult technical problems remain.  These essentially relate to when the writs are available.  The JR Act leaves us with these problems.

Given that a prerogative order under JR Act Part 5 is generally available when the equivalent writ would have been available, principles drawn from prerogative writ cases going back hundreds of years are potentially relevant to the operation of JR Act.

## (a)	PROHIBITION AND CERTIORARI

## COMMON FEATURES

There are a number of important matters common to both certiorari and prohibition.

## GROUNDS FOR THE WRITS

The grounds for the writs of prohibition and certiorari are similar.  Both writs apply in cases of jurisdictional error, ie either want or excess of jurisdiction, including where there has been a denial of natural justice.  In relation to the link between the writs and jurisdictional error, recall the High Court’s decision in *Craig v South Australia (1994) 184 CLR 163 at 176-180.

In exceptional circumstances both writs may also apply where there has been non-jurisdictional error.  Only certiorari is available to correct non-jurisdictional errors of law apparent on the face of the record of the decision-maker.  There is authority that both certiorari and prohibition are available in the non-jurisdictional context in cases where fraud has been committed by one of the parties before the decision-maker - see Lord Reid in the Anisminic case [1969] 2 AC at 170-171; and Craig v South Australia (1995) 184 CLR 163 at 176, which approved of R v Wolverhampton Crown Court; ex parte Crofts [1983] 1 WLR 204 at 206.  In relation to whether the constitutional writ of prohibition, however, applies to non-jurisdictional errors, see Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth (2003) 211 CLR 476 at [82]-[83]; and SZFDE v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship (2007) 232 CLR 189 at [28]-[54].

## WHICH BODIES ARE SUBJECT TO THE WRITS? - COURTS

Historically, prohibition and certiorari were issued by superior courts of record, and they were not available against such superior courts.  The writs are clearly available against inferior courts and administrative tribunals.

When is a court a superior court of record to which the writs do not apply?  To answer that question you must look to the substance of the legislation creating the court or tribunal rather than the mere words used in that legislation.  Is the jurisdiction of the particular decision-maker to be found exclusively in the terms of a particular empowering statute of limited scope?  If so, then regardless of whether the body is declared by that statute to be a “superior court of record” which is given by the statute “all the powers and jurisdiction of the Supreme Court,” the body may still be a court which is subject to the writs.

If, however, the jurisdiction of the body rests on basic constitutional doctrine then it would appear to be a superior court of record to which the writs do not apply.

The High Court and State Supreme Courts therefore may not be subject to the writs – cf Kirby J in re McBain, (2002) 209 CLR 372 at [193]; French CJ, Gummow, Hayne, Crennan, Kiefel and Bell JJ in Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW (2010) 239 CLR 531 at [106]-[107]; and French CJ, Hayne, Crennan, Kiefel, Bell and Keane JJ in NSW v Kable (2013) 252 CLR 118 at [28]-[31].  Note that this does not mean that other judicial review remedies (ie injunctions and declarations) are inapplicable in respect of decisions of the High Court and State Supreme Courts.

The Queensland District Court is amenable to the prerogative writs of certiorari and prohibition - R v Loewenthal; Ex parte Blacklock (1974) 131 CLR 338 at 344.  Compare Ex parte Connell (1993) 10 WAR 414 at 417-421.  In Queensland, note s29 of the District Court Act 1967.

The Industrial Court of Queensland was held to be subject to the writ of prohibition in the Attorney-General of Queensland v Wilkinson (1958) 100 CLR 422 – despite being described in the relevant Queensland Act as a superior court of record having all the powers and jurisdiction of the Supreme Court.  See Fullagar J, at 431, who observed that “it would be absurd to suppose that the Industrial Court could ... try an indictment for murder.”  See generally Harrison v President of the Industrial Court of Queensland [2017] 1 QdR 515 at [47]-[60].  The Industrial Court of NSW was held subject to the writ of certiorari in Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW (2010) 239 CLR 531, [102] and [106]-[107].

The Family Court has been held to be subject to the writ of prohibition - R v Watson; Ex parte Armstrong (1976) 136 CLR 248 at 263 - despite a provisions of the Family Law Act which declared the Family Court to be a superior court of record.

The Federal Court appears to be subject to prohibition - see R v Federal Court ex parte Western Australian National Football League (1979) 143 CLR 190 at 215, 238 and 241.

## OTHER TYPES OF DECISION-MAKERS SUBJECT TO THE WRITS

Historically, the writs have only been available against certain types of decision-makers who made certain types of decisions.  In more recent times, availability has expanded dramatically, in particular, in the context of natural justice.  It is still useful, however, to consider the early 20th Century approach to determining the types of decision-makers and types of decisions to which the writs apply.

The classic statement of the approach was made by Atkin LJ in R v The Electricity Commissioners [1924] 1 KB 171 at 205.  The case involved the Electricity Commissioners, a statutory body with powers to reorganise the electricity industry in England.  The particular reorganisation scheme that the Commissioners proposed for one area was alleged to be not authorised by the relevant legislation.  The process for giving effect to the scheme included the holding of a public inquiry.  The legislation went on to provide that for the scheme to take effect it had to be confirmed by the relevant Minister and approved by both Houses of Parliament.  The Minister and the Houses of Parliament had the power to amend the scheme.  The Court of Appeal held that the writ of prohibition was available to restrain the Commissioners from holding the inquiry as the scheme being considered was not authorised by the legislation.

Atkin LJ noted that prohibition and certiorari originally restrained courts of inferior jurisdiction from exceeding their powers but that the operation of the writs had expanded to control the proceedings of bodies not claiming to be and not recognised as being courts of justice.  The following formula was then proposed:

“Wherever any body of persons having legal authority to determine questions affecting the rights of subjects, and having the duty to act judicially, act in excess of their legal authority, they are subject to the controlling jurisdiction of the King’s Bench Division exercised in these writs.” (emphasis added)

This formula has been consistently applied in Australia (see for example Kioa v West (1985) 159 CLR 550 at 563).  Lord Atkin’s formula operated in a similar way to the definition of “decision to which this Act applies” in s4 JR Act, ie, it marked out the prerequisites (in addition to illegality) for judicial review sought via certiorari and prohibition.

## LEGAL AUTHORITY?

The Atkin formula has been extended to control the conduct of bodies set up by the executive arm of government (ie without legislation) and not just to bodies set up by the legislature - R v Criminal Injuries Compensation Board; Ex parte Lain [1967] 2 QB 864 at 881, 889 and 891.  In this case certiorari was held to apply to the decision of a compensation board created by the executive arm of government.  The board was not created by a statute but was instead created by an exercise of Crown prerogative power.  The board was created by the government of the day to assess the amount of compensation that would be paid to the victims of crime.  Once the amount was assessed, the board would make a payment from money set aside by Parliament.  The system involved voluntary or ex gratia payments of money.  In this particular case the applicant for compensation was the spouse of a police officer who had been shot by a criminal and thus blinded in one eye.  Not long after this the police officer took his own life.  The officer’s spouse sought compensation for herself and their 3 children.  The board finally decided that she was not entitled to compensation under the ex gratia system as she was receiving assistance from other sources.  The English Divisional Court held that certiorari was potentially available in relation to a decision of the board despite the fact that the board did not derive its authority from a statute.  The legal authority for the Board’s decision came from the Crown’s prerogative powers, nonetheless certiorari was available.  Having decided that certiorari was potentially available, the court however refused relief on the basis that the applicant had not established a ground of review.

Non-governmental bodies would not normally have “legal authority” for the purposes of the Atkin formula.  However, where Parliament by statute has recognised a body and endorsed its functions, in particular its disciplinary functions, the body may be subject to prohibition and certiorari.  Compare R v Wadley; Ex parte Burton [1976] QdR 286 at 295 with R v Wilson; Ex parte Robinson [1982] QdR 642 at 645-646.

## DUTY TO ACT JUDICIALLY?

The wide scope of the Atkin formula was confirmed in Ridge v Baldwin [1964] AC 40.  In this case, Lord Reid rejected the notion that the words “duty to act judicially” imposed some super-added requirement which had to be expressly present in the empowering statute before the writs could be available.  The express presence of such a duty is not a pre-requisite for the writs to be available.  It could be deduced from the nature of the power given to the body.  The existence of a super-added duty appears now to have been rejected altogether in Australia – see Chase Oyster Bar Pty Ltd v Hamo Industries Pty Ltd (2010) 272 ALR 750 at [9]-[19], per Spigelman CJ; and Waratah Coal Pty Ltd v Coordinator-General, Department of State Development, Infrastructure and Planning [2014] QSC 036 at [28], per Applegarth J.

AFFECTING THE RIGHTS OF SUBJECTS?

Certiorari and prohibition may be available against a decision even though the decision has no effect until confirmed by Parliament or by the Governor in Council – see for eg Electricity Commissioners.

What about preliminary or recommendatory decisions?  The pattern, at least for certiorari, appears to be that the preliminary decision is subject to the writ where the “higher” body must at least take the preliminary decision into account, that is, where the higher body does not have the power to effectively ignore the preliminary decision.  This must be contrasted with the situation where there is merely a decision or recommendation which a higher body is not bound to consider.  See, for example, R v Collins; Ex parte ACTU Solo Enterprises (1976) 50 ALJR 471, where certiorari was held not to be available to quash a decision of a Royal Commission.  Stephen J acknowledged that the Royal Commission’s conclusions would inform the government.  Nonetheless the Royal Commission’s conclusions did not determine or affect the applicant’s rights – at 473-475.  Nor was the Commission’s report a condition precedent to some other decision which would affect rights or have some other legal consequence.

The requirements for certiorari in this context appear to be stricter than those for prohibition - see *Ainsworth v Criminal Justice Commission (1992) 175 CLR 564 at 581 and 595.  This difference illustrates the diminishing usefulness of the Atkin test as a guide to the availability of the writs.

For prohibition to be available the effect on rights necessary appears to be the same as the effect required for procedural fairness to apply.  This type of effect is not sufficient for certiorari - see *Hot Holdings Pty Ltd v Creasy (1996) 185 CLR 149 at 158-159.

The position in relation to Royal Commissions is not the same as that regarding company investigations under the Corporations Law.  See Annetts v McCann (1990) 170 CLR 596 at 599-600.  The decisions in Annetts and Collins were confirmed in Ainsworth v Criminal Justice Commission (1991) 175 CLR 564 at 576-577 and 581.

The High Court considered this area in *Hot Holdings Pty Ltd v Creasy (1996) 185 CLR 149, especially at 165.  By a 3/2 majority the court held that a preliminary decision of a West Australian Mining Warden was subject to certiorari where the making of the Mining Warden’s decision had to be taken into account by the ultimate decision-maker whose decision would clearly affect the applicant’s rights.  The majority held that for certiorari to issue you had to identify a discernible or apparent legal effect upon rights.  As the ultimate decision-maker was bound to consider the warden’s decision, certiorari was available.  This was so even though the decision-maker was not bound to follow the warden’s recommendation.

In light of the decision in Lain (involving the police widow’s compensation claim) it appears that the “right” which must be affected by the decision need not be an enforceable legal right.

## DECISIONS NOT SUBJECT TO THE PROHIBITION AND CERTIORARI

Exercises of delegated legislative power may not be amenable to the writs - see R v Wright; Ex parte Waterside Workers Federation of Australia (1955) 93 CLR 528 at 541-542.  The High Court has never declined to issue writs under s 75(v) of the Constitution with respect to exercises of legislative power when constitutional issues arise.  Compare the different positions in re Refugee Review Tribunal; Ex parte Aala (2000) 204 CLR 82 at [21], [138]-[139] and [161].

The decision of a Magistrate to commit an accused for trial on a charge of a criminal offence may not be subject to the writs.  Mason J in Sankey v Whitlam (1978) 142 CLR 1 at 83, in obiter, indicated his preference for the view that prohibition would lie against the Magistrate’s decision – see also R v Schwarten ex parte Wildschut [1965] QdR 276.  The opposite view is taken by the NSW Supreme Court - see Ex parte Cousens; re Blackett (1946) 47 SR (NSW) 145.  Recall the discretion to deny relief – in Qld ss12-14 and 48 of the JR Act.  Note that the position at the Federal level was altered in 2000 with amendments to section 39B of the Judiciary Act 1903 (Cth).  Committal proceedings before State Courts in relation to Commonwealth offences are not subject to judicial review by the Federal Court under s39B of the Judiciary Act.  Compare s9A of the ADJR Act.

## STANDING

The position at common law can be distinguished from the position in Qld under the JR Act.

## COMMON LAW STANDING – CERTIORARI AND PROHIBITION

The writs of certiorari and prohibition at common law were available to an applicant who was “a person aggrieved”.  See:

R v Liverpool Corporation; Ex parte Liverpool Taxi Fleet Operators Association [1972] 2 QB 299 at 308-309; and

R v Paddington Valuation Officers; Ex parte Peachey Property Corporation Ltd [1966] 1 QB 380 at 401.

A “person aggrieved” at common law did not have to be a party to the proceedings that produced the decision complained of.  The Courts could in their discretion confer standing on a “stranger” - that is a member of the public who seems no more affected by the decision than anyone else.  See R v Greater London Council; Ex parte Blackburn [1976] 1 WLR 550 at 559.  This view was confirmed in R v Inland Revenue Commissions; ex parte National Federation for Self Employment and Small Businesses Ltd [1982] AC 617 at 641.  See also Re Smith; Ex parte Rundle (1991) 5 WAR 295 at 305-306; and re McBain (2002) 209 CLR 372, [89]-[90] and [260].  McHugh J in re McBain, while accepting that certiorari and prohibition were available to “strangers” who were not aggrieved, nonetheless considered that “a stranger’s lack of standing will frequently result in the Court refusing to issue either writ on discretionary grounds” – [109].

STANDING UNDER PART 5 JR ACT

In Qld, with the enactment of the JR Act, standing for the orders in the nature of the prerogative writs is now governed by s44.  Under JR Act s44, a person seeking a Prerogative Order will need to show that their interests would be adversely affected by the action in question.  Recall that adverse effect is part of the definition of “aggrieved” in s7 of the JR Act.  Decisions on the scope of standing under the ADJR Act will therefore be relevant.  It appears, therefore, that the attempted simplification in the JR Act may have narrowed the scope of standing for orders in the nature of certiorari and prohibition.  However the common law standing test may not be as generous as it first appears – see Aronson Groves and Weeks, Judicial Review of Administrative Action and Government Liability (7th ed, Thomson Reuters, 2022) at 892 (“Aronson et al”).

## STAYS

The Queensland Supreme Court can stay proceedings of the original decision-maker while the review is carried out - see s47(4) of the JR Act.  For the constitutional writs, see Rule 25.11 of the High Court Rules 2004.

## ISSUES SPECIFIC TO PROHIBITION

Prohibition operates to halt the decision-making process in its tracks.  For the writ to be available, there must be something for the writ to prohibit.  If the decision-maker has completely performed its function, then the decision-maker is said to be functus officio and the writ will no longer be available.

The High Court has, however, been generous on the circumstances when a body is not Functus Officio - see R v Hibble; Ex parte Broken Hill Proprietary Company Ltd (1920) 28 CLR 456.  By the barest of majorities (the Chief Justice’s casting vote deciding the point), it was held by the High Court in Hibble that prohibition lay against a Tribunal’s decision where all that remained to be done was the enforcement of the decision.  The High Court so held even though the Tribunal itself was not responsible for enforcement.  Contrast the English position – see Estate and Trust Agencies (1927) Ltd v Singapore Improvement Trust [1937] AC 898 at 917-919.

The generous approach of the High Court in relation to prohibition perhaps arises due to the absence of certiorari in s75(v) of the Constitution.

Prohibition can also be sought too early – see Aronson et al, at 923-926.

## ISSUES SPECIFIC TO CERTIORARI

As noted above, certiorari and prohibition are normally available where there has been jurisdictional error.  Certiorari is also uniquely available in relation to non-jurisdictional errors of law apparent on the face of the record of the decision.

## NON-JURISDICTIONAL ERROR OF LAW ON THE FACE OF THE RECORD

In the normal use of certiorari against jurisdictional error, the normal range of evidence may be led, witnesses called, etc, to prove the jurisdictional error, for example, that a court has made a decision in excess of its jurisdiction.

In the non-jurisdictional application of certiorari, no outside evidence is allowed.  For the writ to apply in the non-jurisdictional context, there must have been an error of law apparent on the face of the record of the decision.  See Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW (2010) 239 CLR 531 at [78]-[87].

## LAW NOT FACT

There must have been an error of law as opposed to an error of fact.  Unfortunately, this distinction can be unclear.  An error of law may consist, for example, of a wrong interpretation of a statutory phrase (at least in cases where the statutory language has a technical meaning).

## FUNDAMENTAL/MATERIAL ERROR?

A further requirement for the non-jurisdictional application of certiorari was suggested in R v Tennant; Ex parte Woods [1962] QdR 241 at 258, per Wanstall J – ie that the error of law must also be fundamental to the decision. In Craig v South Australia (1995) 184 CLR 163 at 176, the High Court required that the decision be “affected” by an error of law.  In Hossain v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection (2018) 264 CLR 123 at [65], Edelman J (with whose reasons Nettle J substantially agreed) understood Craig as requiring that a non-jurisdictional error be “material in the sense that it ‘affected’ the decision”.

## THE RECORD?

However, the source of the greatest controversy and the main restriction on the non-jurisdictional application of certiorari has arisen in the context of what constitutes the record of the decision being reviewed.

It is important to understand what is at stake here.  For courts, an appeal is the normal mechanism for the correction of non-jurisdictional errors of law.

The scope of this exceptional use of certiorari critically depends on what constitutes the record.  The more that the reviewing Court can look at to find an error of law, the easier it will be to find such an error.  For example, an error of law may appear in the transcript of the proceedings of the lower court or tribunal.  However, if the record of the original decision-maker’s decision excludes the transcript and reasons of the decision-maker, and essentially only incorporates the order, ie “that the Tribunal finds in favour of X,” then it will be hard (if not impossible) to find an error of law.

In *Craig v South Australia (1995) 184 CLR 163 at 180-183 it was held that in the absence of statutory expansion, the record is a very narrow concept.  It will include the document initiating the proceedings, (ie that which grounds jurisdiction), the formal pleadings (if any, ie statement of claim, defence, counterclaim, etc) and the actual order or ruling of the lower court or tribunal.  The reasons, the transcript of the proceedings and the evidence before the decision-maker will not form part of the record.

The High Court acknowledged that sometimes parts of the reasons had to be incorporated into the order of the lower court or tribunal, for example, where the order involves an undertaking made by one party, the terms of which are set out in the reasons.  However, the High Court in Craig made it clear that the reviewing Court will only incorporate those parts of the reasons that must be incorporated.  Though the original decision-maker might have prefaced their order with expressions like “for these reasons” (ie referring back to the reasons) or “accordingly,” such expressions will not be enough to incorporate the reasons into the decision-maker’s order.

The High Court in Craig suggests that the same approach to the record will apply to tribunals as well as to lower courts - see 182.

In Queensland, Craig will generally have no impact on judicial review of tribunal (as opposed to court) decisions.  This is because it is a ground of review under the JR Act s20(2)(f) if the decision-maker has made an error of law (the equivalent ground under the ADJR Act is s5(1)(f)).  Section 20(2)(f) expressly provides that the error does not have to appear on the record of the decision.

Craig’s case will have impact in relation to review of decisions of inferior courts where there are no appeal rights.  Lower court decisions are not caught by the ADJR Act or Part 3 of the JR Act as they are not normally decisions of an administrative character.  The limiting impact of Craig’s case on lower court decisions has been removed in NSW – see s69(4) of the NSW Supreme Court Act 1970; and JW Shaw and FJ Gwynne, ‘Certiorari for Error on the Face of the Record’ (1997) 71 Australian Law Journal 356 at 367.  In Victoria, see s10 of the Administrative Law Act 1978.

## (b)	MANDAMUS

Mandamus commands the performance of a public duty.  The writ is similar in operation to s22 of the JR Act/ ADJR Act s7.

For mandamus to be available there must be an express or constructive refusal to perform a public duty.

The public duty does not need to be judicial or quasi-judicial.  Mandamus has, for example, been held to be available against the Police Force to ensure that they enforce the criminal law - see R v The Commissioner of Police; Ex parte Blackburn [1968] 2 QB 118 – although it is important to consider the limits identified on the Court’s power to review (at 136).

It is not enough that a decision-maker merely has a discretion.  There must also exist a duty to hear and determine the matter – ie a duty to consider whether to exercise the discretion.  Heydon J in Public Service Association of South Australia v Industrial Relations Commission of South Australia (2012) 249 CLR 398, observed that “[i]n the absence of statutory language to the contrary, a grant of jurisdiction ordinarily carries with it a duty to exercise it” – [91]; although contrast the observations of the Full Court of the Federal Court in Yasmin v Attorney-General (Cth) (2015) 236 FCR 169 at [113]. Mandamus is obviously available where there exists a non-discretionary public duty; and as noted when discussing s22 of the JR Act, where a power is adjudicative (such as the powers of a tribunal), a duty to exercise the power can be readily inferred, whereas for non-adjudicative powers, such an inference may not be warranted.

Note that mandamus has also issued where a court has found there has been a “constructive refusal” to perform the public duty, eg where the decision-maker in purporting to exercise the duty has acted in excess of their jurisdiction.  If, as a consequence of this breach, the decision is treated as void, then the courts have issued mandamus on the basis that a void purported exercise of duty is essentially a constructive refusal to exercise the duty.  The issuing of mandamus in cases of constructive refusal is akin to seeking certiorari and mandamus together – certiorari to quash the invalid decision, and mandamus to order the decision-maker to do the job lawfully second time round.

Mandamus is not available against the superior courts.  The Crown may still be immune from mandamus in certain circumstances.

Mandamus is generally available against public servants provided they owe a duty to members of the public.  In an exceptional case it was held that mandamus was not available against public servants who owed their duty to the Crown alone - see R v Lords Commissioners of the Treasury (1872) LR 7 QB 387.  One could reasonably doubt whether this authority would be followed today.  Contrast the scope of s22 of JR Act in light of the definition of “duty” in s3.

If a decision-maker fails to comply with a writ of mandamus, a peremptory writ of mandamus may be issued – see Plaintiff S297/2013 v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection (No 2) (2015) 255 CLR 231, per French CJ, and Hayne, Kiefel, Bell, Gageler and Keane JJ, at [37]-[47].

## STANDING

The applicants for mandamus need to show that they have standing to sue.

STANDING FOR MANDAMUS AT COMMON LAW

Historically, at common law, however, the standing rule for mandamus has been stricter than for the other two prerogative writs.  There must exist a specific legal right possessed by the applicant correlative to the duty he or she seeks to enforce.  See R v Arndel; Ex parte Freeman (1906) 3 CLR 557 at 566-567; and R v Commonwealth Court; Ex parte Ellis (1975) 132 CLR 473.

A more relaxed and wider view was taken in R v Police Commissioner; Ex parte Blackburn.  On the other hand, standing for the writ was denied in the case of R v Inland Revenue Commissioners; Ex parte National Federation of Self-Employed [1982] AC 617 at 639-642.  See also Re Exbea Pty Ltd [1989] 1 WAR 287 at 290; and R v Dainer; Ex parte Cooke (1986) 84 FLR 305 at 307.

STANDING UNDER PART 5 JR ACT

In Qld under the JR Act standing to seek mandamus orders is dealt with under s44 – ie interests adversely affected.

## DISCRETIONARY REMEDY

Mandamus is a discretionary remedy and will not generally be available if there is in existence another remedy of equally satisfactory character.  It was historically the administrative law remedy of last resort.

## STATUTORY MANDAMUS

In Queensland there is a remedy similar to a mandamus order under Part 5 of the JR Act.  The Supreme Court can issue statutory mandamus under the Civil Proceedings Act 2011.  This remedy exists alongside Part 5 of the JR Act.  It has its own standing requirements (s11 of the Civil Proceedings Act speaks of a person being “personally interested”).  Statutory mandamus may provide an alternative way of seeking review of government inaction.

In relation to the Queensland District Court, note s30 of the District Court Act 1967 and R v His Honour Judge Morley and Mellifont [1990] 1 QdR 54 at 61-63.

## (c)	MATTERS COMMON TO ALL PREROGATIVE WRITS

All the prerogative writs are discretionary in character and can generally be refused despite the fact that there are otherwise valid grounds in support of the writs - see R v Commonwealth Court of Conciliation and Arbitration (1949) 78 CLR 389; Aala (2000) 204 CLR 82 at [56]; SZBYR v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship (2007) 81 ALJR 1190 at [28]; and R v Cardwell Shire Council ex parte Solinas [1987] 2 QdR 583 at 603.  Of course, in cases where there is a clear absence of jurisdiction on the part of an inferior tribunal, a superior court is unlikely to refuse a prerogative writ - see Yirrell v Yirrell (1939) 62 CLR 287 at 297.  It is clear that the discretion may be exercised against relief via the prerogative writs where there has been fraud, concealment or lack of frankness.  An illustration of the discretion to refuse mandamus on the grounds of futility is R v Anderson; Ex parte Ipec-Air Proprietary Limited (1965) 113 CLR 177 at 201.  Delay will be relevant where a person seeks to rely on the prerogative writs.

Time - applications for prerogative orders under the JR Act must be brought as soon as possible, but in any event within 3 months of the relevant conduct or such longer period as the Supreme Court allows - s46 of the JR Act.  Section 46 applies to all the prerogative orders (ie prohibition, certiorari and mandamus orders) and also to declarations and injunctions sought under Part 5.  The High Court Rules set out time limits for mandamus and certiorari with a discretion to extend time.  Recall the discussion of the constitutionality of time limits in Plaintiff S157/2002 (2003) 211 CLR 476 at [84]-[91] and [173]-[175]; and Bodruddaza v Minister for Immigration and Multicultural and Indigenous Affairs (2007) 228 CLR 651 at [44]-[64].  See also Bham v The Determining Authority [2006] FCA 589, [60].

Other procedural provisions - there is also a common form application with the same standing test under s44.  Remember also that the Supreme Court’s powers under JR Act ss 12, 13, 14, 48 and 49 apply to applications for review under JR Act Part 5.

# 4.	EQUITABLE REMEDIES – DECLARATIONS/INJUNCTIONS

## (a)	DECLARATIONS AS REMEDIES IN ADMINISTRATIVE LAW

## INTRODUCTION

A declaration is a statement of the legal position of the parties to a dispute made by a superior court - that is, Supreme Court and upwards.  Declaratory relief has been developed by courts exercising equitable jurisdiction.

The courts have a wide discretion in relation to declarations.  Like an injunction, a plaintiff seeking a declaration must come before the court with clean hands and without undue delay, etc.  However, unlike an injunction, a declaration carries no immediate penalty if ignored.  A declaration is technically viewed as merely a judicial statement of what the law is.  If a defendant ignores a declaration, the plaintiff will generally have to come back before the court, prove that the declaration is being ignored and seek an injunction.  The declaration, like the injunction, is an important remedy outside of administrative law.

## DECLARATIONS IN ADMINISTRATIVE LAW

The case which heralded a new era for the declaration as an administrative law remedy was Dyson v The Attorney General [1911] 1 KB 410.  In this case, a form requiring information for taxation purposes was sent out by government officials to over 8 million people.  There was a criminal penalty for failure to provide all the information requested in the form.  The plaintiff, who received one of the forms, sought a declaration that certain of the questions asked on the form were unauthorised by the Act pursuant to which the forms were purportedly issued.  At an interlocutory stage, the Attorney-General (who was the defendant in the proceedings) sought to have the plaintiff’s action struck out.  The Attorney-General was successful at first and second instance.  The plaintiff subsequently appealed to the Court of Appeal which found in the plaintiff’s favour.

The Court held that:

There was power to make a declaration where the Crown was a party;

A declaration could be made regardless of whether or not any other relief was sought;

Declarations were a discretionary remedy and if inconvenience to the government caused by granting a declaration was at all a legitimate consideration, then so also was the convenience of the citizen.

## SOURCE OF POWER TO MAKE DECLARATIONS IN QUEENSLAND AND FEDERALLY

There are two main sources of power for the Qld Supreme Court to make declarations:

Part 5 of the JR Act - see JR Act s43(2) and (3) and s47(1) and (2).  Essentially these provisions allow the Supreme Court to make declarations (and issue injunctions) under JR Act Part 5 where appropriate “having regard to” the availability of orders in the nature of the prerogative writs.

The Supreme Court’s general or inherent power to make declarations - recognised in s43(3) of the JR Act and preserved by JR Act s10(1).  In relation to the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction, see Rules 262 and 585 of the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules, s58 of the Constitution of Queensland 2001, s10 of the Civil Proceedings Act 2011, ss10 and 11 of the Supreme Court of Queensland Act 1991 and re Solomon No 1 (1992) 1 QAR 158 at 161.

The Supreme Court has the power under JR Act s43(3) to allow proceedings commenced under the JR Act by way of application for review to continue as if they had been commenced as a normal action for a declaration or injunction within the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction.  See also Rule 585 of the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules and Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2012] 1 QdR 525 at [34]-[39].

Federal jurisdiction to issue declaratory relief arises under s31 of the Judiciary Act 1903 (Cth) and s21 of the Federal Court of Australia Act 1976 (Cth).  See generally NJ Young QC, ‘Declarations and Other Remedies in Administrative Law’ (2004) 12 Australian Journal of Admin Law 35.

## AVAILABILITY OF DECLARATIONS

The declaration is a remedy that may be granted on the grounds of ultra vires, jurisdictional error (including failure to accord procedural fairness) and perhaps also in relation to non-jurisdictional error – see below.

Street J in Sutherland Shire Council v Leyendekkers (1970) 91 WN (NSW) 250 at 258, rejected a restrictive approach to the discretionary power to grant a declaration.  According to Street J, “used with a proper sense of responsibility” declarations allowed for the quick, inexpensive and authoritative resolution of disputes, with an almost unlimited variety of disputes having been resolved with declarations, often when no other satisfactory form of relief was available.  French CJ, writing extra-judicially, has also observed that “[w]ell may we ask rhetorically of declarations as Homer Simpson asked of donuts – ‘is there anything they can’t do?’” – RS French, ‘Declarations – Homer Simpson’s Remedy – Is There Anything They Cannot Do?’ in K Dharmananda and A Papamatheos (eds), Perspectives on Declaratory Relief (Federation Press, 2009) 25 at 29.

Being an important remedy outside of administrative law, declarations are available to challenge the actions of non-governmental bodies or tribunals (for example, clubs may be subject to natural justice proceedings seeking declarations).  Declarations (along with injunctions) have also been sought against non-government bodies in defence of some public right or interest - eg Onus v Alcoa (1981) 149 CLR 27.

For additional judicial statements extolling the virtues of declarations in a public law context, see *Ainsworth v Criminal Justice Commission (1992) 175 CLR 564 at 581-2; and Forster v Jododex Australia Pty Ltd (1972) 127 CLR 421 at 435-439.  The following examples are provided merely to illustrate the breadth of the authority of superior courts to issue declarations:

1.	A declaration under Part 5 of the JR Act is available in respect of a decision which is an exercise of legislative power: Kwiksnax Mobile Industrial and General Caterers Pty Ltd v Logan City Council [1994] 1 QdR 291 at 310-311.

2.	Proceedings of the Governor in Council can be declared void, though certiorari would not lie against the Governor in Council: Banks v Transport Regulation Board (1968) 119 CLR 222 at 241; De Verteuil v Knaggs [1918] AC 557.

3.	An asylum seeker can obtain a declaration that an employee of Wizard People Pty Ltd, a company engaged by the Commonwealth to undertake “independent merits review” of a refugee status determination, has denied the asylum seeker natural justice when making a recommendation to the relevant minister – Plaintiff M61/2010E v Commonwealth (2010) 243 CLR 319; declaratory relief was issued in this case in circumstances where mandamus and certiorari were unavailable – see [99]-[104].

4.	Where a statutory authority has made a decision in a manner that is inconsistent with the requirements of legislation (eg where legislation requires decisions to be made in a manner that is consistent with Australia’s treaty obligations), a declaration can be issued that the decision is “unlawful” but not invalid – ie a declaration can be focussed on providing prospective correction of unlawful decision-making – Project Blue Sky (1998) 194 CLR 355 at [100].

5.	Ratepayers can challenge the validity of a rate in respect of their land by means of a declaration: Parramatta City Council v Pestell (1972) 128 CLR 305 at 310.

6.	A disappointed tenderer may seek a declaration that statutory tender requirements have not been complied with and that the resulting contract is void: Hunter Brothers v Brisbane City Council [1984] 1 QdR 328 at 341.

7.	A local authority vested with ownership of a public highway can obtain a declaration that a building erected within the boundaries of the highway is an obstruction of that highway: North Sydney MC v Murray (1969) 18 LGRA 60 at 64.

8.	The New Zealand Supreme Court declared invalid rulings by a mayor at a council meeting given in excess of the mayor’s powers: Wild v McGuire [1970] NZLR 489 at 493.

9.	A purported compulsory acquisition of land can be declared invalid: Reith v Town & Country Planning Board [1970] VR 192.

10.	A person wrongly deprived of a licence necessary for their work or business can sue the licensing authority for a declaration: Fagan v National Coursing Association of South Australia (1974) 8 SASR 546 at 561-562.

11.	Where a statutory authority has acted ultra vires any person who would have been affected by its act can have it set aside by certiorari, or declared void: Grunwick Processing Laboratories Ltd v ACAS [1978] AC 655 at 695, per Lord Diplock.  See also R v Secretary of State for Social Services; Ex parte Association of Metropolitan Authorities [1986] 1 All ER 164 at 175-176.

12.	A declaration can be granted as to whether a witness before a board of inquiry is compellable to answer a particular question (eg a police officer asked to give the name of an informant): Signorotto v Nicholson [1982] VR 413 at 415.

13.	A declaration can be made in relation to a Commission of Inquiry acting in breach of the rules of procedural fairness: Carruthers v Connolly [1998] 1 QdR 339 at 394; a declaration was made in relation to a Criminal Justice Commission report - Ainsworth v Criminal Justice Commission (1992) 175 CLR 564, cf Wells v Carmody [2014] QSC 59 at [13]-[32]; and a declaration was made in respect of a Crime and Corruption Commission report – Crime and Corruption Commission (Qld) v Carne (2023) 97 ALJR 737 at [21]-[27], per Kiefel CJ, Gageler and Jagot JJ, and [77] and [117], per Gordon and Edelman JJ.

As this list indicates, declarations are perhaps the most dynamic administrative law remedy.  They can be used in relation to exercises of legislative, executive and judicial power.  If sought via a superior court’s inherent jurisdiction they can be made with respect to non-governmental entities.  They are therefore available in cases where neither the prerogative writs nor statutory judicial review under the ADJR Act or the JR Act Part 3 are available.

Aronson et al have referred (at 1073-1081) to the “pre-emptive” use of declarations – ie to interpret legislation or declare rights.  This is an immensely important power which gives the declaration an edge over all other administrative law remedies.

## LIMITS ON AVAILABILITY OF DECLARATIONS

In Ainsworth v Criminal Justice Commission (1992) 175 CLR 564 at 581-582, the High Court (per Mason CJ, Dawson, Toohey and Gaudron JJ) made the following statement:

“It is now accepted that superior courts have inherent power to grant declaratory relief.  It is a discretionary power which ‘(i)t is neither possible nor desirable to fetter ... by laying down rules as to the manner of its exercise.’ … However, it is confined by the considerations which mark out the boundaries of judicial power.  Hence, declaratory relief must be directed to the determination of legal controversies and not to answering abstract or hypothetical questions. … The person seeking relief must have ‘a real interest’ … and relief will not be granted if the question ‘is purely hypothetical’, if relief is ‘claimed in relation to circumstances that (have) not occurred and might never happen’ … or if ‘the Court's declaration will produce no foreseeable consequences for the parties’.”

For further discussion of the various potential obstacles in the way of making declarations – see Aussie Airlines Pty Ltd v Australian Airline Ltd (1996) 139 ALR 663 at 670-671; and Bass v Permanent Trustee Co Ltd (1999) 198 CLR 334 at [49].

## DECLARATIONS AND HYPOTHETICAL QUESTIONS

Generally, courts will not answer hypothetical questions.  The rule is similar to the standing issue, and the High Court has addressed the issue under the heading of standing – see, eg, Plaintiff M68/2015 v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection (2016) 257 CLR 42 at [22], [235] and [348].

The High Court in Forster v Jododex at 437-438 adopted the Scottish rules which, as summarised by Lord Dunedin, provide as follows:

“There must be a real and not a theoretical question; the person raising it must have a real interest to raise it; the person must be able to secure a proper contradictor, that is to say, someone presently existing who has a true interest to oppose the declaration sought.”

See Russian Commercial and Industrial Bank v British Bank of Foreign Trade Ltd [1921] 2 AC 438 at 448.

A court will not make a declaration if the declaration will not achieve anything – see Gardner v Dairy Industry Authority of NSW (1977) 52 ALJR 180 (a case cited by the High Court in Ainsworth).  The applicants in this case were essentially seeking a declaration in respect of past government conduct with a view to placing political pressure on the government in the hope of extracting a better deal in the future.  Mason J (with Jacobs and Murphy JJ agreeing), observed that: “… it is one thing to say that declaratory relief will be granted against the Executive or a Statutory Authority in relation to existing rights and transactions.  It is quite another thing to say that it should be granted in respect of past transactions under legislation which has been repealed or amended when the Court’s declaration will produce no foreseeable consequence for the parties” – at 188.

A court will, however, be prepared to make a declaration in relation to the lawfulness of past government conduct where there is the possibility that the unlawful conduct may be repeated in the future – see, eg, Plaintiff M68/2015 v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection (2016) 257 CLR 42 at [23], [64], [235] and [349]-[350].

The Constitution does not authorise the offering of advisory opinions by the High Court - see North Ganalanja Aboriginal Corporation v Queensland (1996) 185 CLR 595 at 612.

In Momcilovic v The Queen (2011) 245 CLR 1, the Victorian Supreme Court’s power under s36 of the Victorian Charter of Human Rights and Responsibilities Act 2006 to issue “declarations of inconsistent interpretation” in relation to a statutory provision which in the opinion of the Court “cannot be interpreted consistently with a human right” was held by Gummow J (with whom Hayne J agreed) not to have dispositive effect and not to be a declaration of right but rather “it operates as a declaration of the absence of right”.  This description was based on s36(5) of the Charter which provides that:

“A declaration of inconsistent interpretation does not –

(a)	affect in any way the validity, operation or enforcement of the statutory provision in respect of which the declaration was made; or

(b)	create in any person any legal right or give rise to any civil cause of action.”

Gummow J therefore concluded that the role of the court under s36 lacked “the character of declaratory relief as ordinarily understood” – see Momcilovic (2011) 245 CLR 1 at [172]-[189].  Compare French CJ at [75]-[101]; Bell J at [661]; and Heydon J at [457].  Crennan and Kiefel JJ considered that the power in s36 of the Charter was incidental to judicial power and therefore judicial; it was not an advisory opinion - see [582]-[605].

For additional judicial consideration of declarations and hypothetical questions - see Edwards v Santos Ltd (2011) 242 CLR 421 at [31]-[46]; and Bass v Permanent Trustee Co Ltd (1999) 198 CLR 334 at [39]-[59].

## DECLARATIONS AND NON-JURISDICTIONAL ERRORS OF LAW

You will recall that while historically judicial review remedies did not generally apply to non-jurisdictional errors of law, certiorari did (and still does) apply to such errors where they are apparent on the face of the record of the original decision.  You will also recall that the High Court in Craig v South Australia limited the availability of certiorari in this context by narrowly defining the scope of the “record” (for example by excluding from the scope of the record the reasons of the original decision-maker).

Can a superior court issue a declaration that an executive decision-maker has made a non-jurisdictional error of law?  A relevant case in this regard is Punton v Ministry of Pensions and National Insurance (No 2) [1964] 1 WLR 226 at 234-237.  The case involved a claim for unemployment benefits by two workers in a shipyard.  The two employees had not worked for a period owing to a strike resulting from a demarcation dispute between two unions.  The two employees were members of a third union not involved in the dispute.  The National Insurance Commissioner, whose statutory function was to assess eligibility for unemployment benefits under the National Insurance Act, decided that, in accordance with the terms of the Act, the two employees did not qualify for unemployment benefits.  The two employees did not use the opportunity to challenge the decision using the writ of certiorari.  English law required that such a challenge be mounted within 6 months.  Instead, the employees commenced proceedings for a declaration that there had been a non-jurisdictional error of law.  They were unsuccessful both at first instance and before the Court of Appeal.  Lord Justice Sellers (with whom the other two members of the Court agreed), while accepting that the Court’s power to make a declaration was a valuable one which should not be fettered, did not consider that the Court had the power to make a declaration in this case.  At 234, his Lordship indicated that as a condition precedent to the payment of unemployment benefits under the Act, the Commissioner needed to make a decision as to an employee’s eligibility.  Sellers LJ, in particular, noted that the decision of the Commissioner could not be quashed by the Court of Appeal (as there had been no jurisdictional error) and that there did not appear to be any power to replace the decision so as to allow the employees to receive the benefit.  This would in effect mean that if the Court did make a declaration against the Commissioner’s decision, that “would leave outstanding two orders”, one of the Commissioner and the other of the Court, and the Court’s order would have no effect on the payment of the benefits claimed.  On this basis, Lord Justice Sellers held that the Court had no jurisdiction to make a declaration in this case – see 237.  His Lordship noted, however, that if he was wrong in this conclusion, he would still not make a declaration on discretionary grounds due to the employees’ delay in challenging the decision.

A similar result was reached in Healey v Minister of Health [1955] 1 QB 221 – the court would not countenance the use of a declaration as a back door appeal from the original decision.

The existence of two outstanding orders was accepted in R v Panel on Takeovers and Mergers; Ex parte Datafin Plc [1987] 2 WLR 699 at 717-718 ie eroding the basis of Lord Justice Sellers LJ’s argument in Punton (No 2) that there was no such power.

The reason for emphasising these cases is that if a declaration is available in such cases, then the restrictions in Craig (limiting the availability of certiorari for non-jurisdictional errors of law to those legal errors apparent on the face of the original decision-maker’s record) might be avoided by seeking a declaration.  The scope of the “record” is not relevant to the availability of declarations.  On the application of declaratory relief to non-jurisdictional error, see Aronson et al, at 1072-1073.  Compare the position of injunctions which appear to be available to cure non-jurisdictional errors of law – Abebe v Commonwealth (1999) 197 CLR 519 at [103]-[105]; and Commissioner of Taxation v Futuris Corp (2008) 237 CLR 146 at [47], per Gummow, Hayne, Heydon and Crennan JJ.  See also Project Blue Sky (1998) 194 CLR 355 at 393; and Plaintiff S157/2002 (2003) 211 CLR 476 at [82]; but see Callinan J in Plaintiff S157/2002 (2003) 211 CLR 476, [120]-[123].

Declaratory relief for non-jurisdictional errors of law might be denied on discretionary grounds in such cases where other judicial review remedies have been removed by an ouster clause – see, for example, Harvey v Commissioner of State Revenue [2014] QSC 183 [104]-[108] (per Jackson J).

It is important to emphasise, however, that the limitation regarding review for non-jurisdictional error of law will not be an issue if review of the decision in question is possible under Part 3 of the JR Act or the ADJR Act.  In jurisdictions that have ADJR Act inspired legislation, the availability of a declaration for non-jurisdictional error of law will probably only be significant when review is sought of court decisions where there are no appeal rights.

STANDING UNDER THE JR ACT

For declarations sought under JR Act Part 5, standing is provided for in JR Act s44, which confers standing on persons whose interests are “adversely affected”.  Remember that under s7 of the JR Act, an applicant for statutory judicial review under Part 3 of the JR Act, must show that they are a person aggrieved.  Section 7 of the JR Act provides that a person aggrieved includes a person whose interests are adversely affected creating a link between standing under Parts 3 and 5 of the JR Act.

STANDING AND DECLARATIONS SOUGHT UNDER THE INHERENT JURISDICTION OF SUPERIOR COURTS

In relation to the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction, the standing requirements for declarations and injunctions considered earlier are of direct relevance.  The High Court’s decisions in ACF v the Commonwealth, Onus v Alcoa, and Bateman’s Bay will be directly applicable.  If the view of Gaudron, Gummow and Kirby JJ expressed in Bateman’s Bay prevails then the Court’s inherent jurisdiction to grant declarations and injunctions will be an attractive way for public interest bodies to come before a superior court to seek judicial review of executive decisions.

## TIME LIMITS

An application for a declaration under JR Act Part 5 must be brought within the terms of s46 of the JR Act (ie as soon as possible but in any event not later than 3 months or as ordered by the Court).  Declarations sought via the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction to issue a declaration are not subject to same time limitation, but the Supreme Court is not likely to take kindly to an attempt to avoid the consequences of tardiness by attempting to use its inherent jurisdiction to issue declaratory relief.  See M Wait, ‘Pursuing Declaratory Relief to Evade Time Limits Applicable to Judicial review: The Emergence of an Australian Alternative to the Rule of Procedural Exclusivity’ (2010) 64 AIAL Forum 10.

## STAYS

Where final relief is sought in the form of a declaration under JR Act Part 5 and there is a need to maintain the status quo pending judicial review, an interlocutory injunction (under the JR Act or the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction - see below) would normally be the appropriate remedy to maintain the status quo pending a decision on final relief.

DECLARATIONS ARE DISCRETIONARY REMEDIES - THE EXISTENCE OF OTHER REMEDIES

Declarations, in common with the prerogative writs and injunctions, are a discretionary remedy.  As a discretionary remedy, the presence or absence of another remedy may influence the Court in exercising its discretion to make a declaration.  Recall ss11, 12, 13 and 14 of JR Act.

## DISCRETION – DECLARATIONS AND THE PREROGATIVE WRITS

It is clear today that despite what may have been the case in the past, the availability of a prerogative writ is no jurisdictional bar to the granting of a declaration, but merely a factor for the court to weigh in the exercise of its discretion.  Given the flexibility of declarations, it is unlikely that a court will refuse to issue a declaration because a prerogative writ may potentially be available.  See Gibbs J in Forster v Jododex at 438: “... a declaration would be a more satisfactory remedy than prohibition, being quicker, simpler and attended by less doubts.”  See also Barnard v National Dock Labour Board [1953] 2 QB 18 at 41-43.

DISCRETION - STATUTORY REMEDIES AND THE AVAILABILITY OF DECLARATIONS

Remember that the declaration is unique among administrative law remedies in that it can be used in 2 different ways:

As a standard judicial review remedy, eg, once a government decision is made, a Court can make a declaration that there has been some form of jurisdictional error or that the decision is ultra vires;

As a means of interpreting a statute or declaring rights, sometimes before a government decision-maker has made a decision.  This is part of what Aronson et al (at 1073-1081) refer to as the pre-emptive use of declarations.

When using a declaration in a pre-emptive manner the courts have developed rules controlling the making of declarations.  Essentially, if a statute creates a right and also creates a remedy before a tribunal, a court won’t normally intervene via a declaration but will leave the parties concerned to follow the statutory procedure - see Barraclough v Brown [1897] AC 615; contrast Pyx Granite Co Ltd v Ministry of Housing and Local Government [1960] AC 258 at 286 and 302 and 303.

Aronson et al observe that this rule does not apply to a court using a declaration in the context of review for jurisdictional error (at 1075).  The High Court’s approach in Kirk appears to support this qualification.

## (b)	INJUNCTIONS AS REMEDIES IN ADMINISTRATIVE LAW

An injunction is a court order enforceable by imprisonment for contempt of Court, requiring a party to civil proceedings to do something or, more commonly, to refrain from doing something.

Notice that injunctions are enforceable by contempt proceedings - a person who does not obey an injunction can be imprisoned until they purge their contempt.  Notice also that injunctions are sought by civil action: there is therefore a civil standard of proof.

An injunction is an equitable remedy and is therefore discretionary.  The party seeking it must come before the court with “clean hands”, etc.

## SOURCE OF THE POWER TO ISSUE INJUNCTIONS IN QUEENSLAND AND FEDERALLY

In common with declarations, there are two sources of power for the Qld Supreme Court to issue injunctions in an administrative law context – Part 5 of the JR Act and the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction.  An applicant can seek an injunction via:

Sections 43 and 47 of the JR Act where it is appropriate to issue an injunction “having regard to” the availability of orders in the nature of the prerogative writs.

The Qld Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction to issue injunctions which is enshrined in the statutes which create the Supreme Court.  The Supreme Court’s jurisdiction to grant interlocutory injunctions was recognised in the Judicature Act reforms of the 19th Century - see now s58 of the Constitution of Queensland 2001, s9 of the Civil Proceedings Act 2011, and ss10 and 11 of the Supreme Court of Queensland Act 1991.

The Supreme Court will entertain administrative law applications for injunctions sought relying on the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction to grant injunctions – see, for eg, the decision of Thomas J in Carruthers v Connolly [1998] 1 QdR 339.

Sources of power to issue injunctions in a Federal context include s75(v) of the Constitution and s39B of the Judiciary Act 1903 (Cth).

## INJUNCTIONS AND ‘PRIVATE’ LAW

Injunctions are a “private law” remedy.  They are commonly sought in all manner of non-administrative law legal disputes whether contractual, tortious or equitable.

## THE AVAILABILITY OF INJUNCTIONS IN ADMINISTRATIVE LAW

In the administrative law context an injunction is available:

1.	To prevent public authorities exercising administrative functions in an ultra vires manner: see A-G v Manchester Corporation [1906] 1 Ch 643 where a local authority, that was empowered to run a tramway company, was not permitted to provide a parcels delivery service unconnected with its trams.

2.	To prevent enforcement of invalid subordinate legislation.  See Australian Alliance Assurance Co Ltd v A-G for Queensland [1916] St R Qd 135 (invalid regulations).  The enforcement of an invalid council resolution has also been prevented by injunction: see Howes v Gosford SC (1961) 7 LGRA 2 at 144 and 157 (New Brunswick Court of Queen’s Bench); and a planning consent (Steeples v Derbyshire County Council [1984] 3 All ER 468 (the English Court of Queen’s Bench)).

3.	To correct jurisdictional errors committed by judicial and quasi-judicial bodies including a breach of the rules of procedural fairness – see Carruthers v Connolly [1998] 1 QdR 339.  It has been suggested that injunctions are available to cure non-jurisdictional errors of law – Abebe v Commonwealth (1999) 197 CLR 519 at 551-2 [103]-[105], per Gaudron J; and Commissioner of Taxation v Futuris Corp (2008) 237 CLR 146 at [47], per Gummow, Hayne, Heydon and Crennan JJ; cf Project Blue Sky (1998) 194 CLR 355 at 398; but see Callinan J in Plaintiff S157/2002 (2003) 211 CLR 476, [120]-[123].

4.	An injunction appears available to interfere with an invalid attempt to exercise delegated legislative powers by subordinate bodies deriving their powers from Parliament, with controversy instead focusing on reliance on injunctions directly against parliamentary processes – see Aronson et al, at 1103-1105.

It is clear from the above list that injunctions (in common with declarations) are available in circumstances where the ADJR Act/Part 3 JR Act would not apply.

## CLASSIFICATION OF INJUNCTIONS

Injunctions are classified both according to the stage of the dispute in which they are sought, and according to the effect they have on the legal person to whom they are directed.  In relation to the stage of the dispute, therefore, you have interim, interlocutory and permanent injunctions.

In relation to the effect of injunctions, injunctions may be either mandatory - that is, making a person do some positive act - or prohibitory - that is restraining a person from engaging in certain conduct.

## INTERIM INJUNCTIONS

The focus is here on injunctions issued in circumstances of extreme urgency and often ex parte (ie without all of the relevant parties before the court).  Unfortunately, there is inconsistency in the case law in the use of the terms “interim” and “interlocutory”.  This guide uses “interim” to refer to injunctions sought ex parte.  The object of an interim injunction is normally to maintain the status quo until the other party can be brought to the Court for an interlocutory hearing.  The duration of such an interim injunction is generally only for a couple of days at most.

As with all ex parte applications, the person seeking the interim injunction is under a duty to inform the court of all relevant circumstances and to keep the court fully advised of any change of circumstances before the injunction has been issued by the Supreme Court Registry.  A failure to disclose relevant facts on an ex parte application will result in the court dissolving the injunction even though the facts not disclosed, had they been put to the court, would not have resulted in a different order at the time of the ex parte hearing – see Worsley Timber Pty Ltd v Shire of Mullewa (1987) 64 LGRA 287 at 290.

The High Court issued an interim injunction in circumstances of extreme urgency (on a Sunday night when forcible removal of asylum seekers was scheduled for 11.30am the following morning) in the Matter of – A proposed application of Shah v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship [2011] HCATrans 195 (these proceeding were eventually designated Plaintiff M70/2011 v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship (ie the Malaysian Declaration case that we considered in Workshop 5) – the High Court’s judgment on the substantive challenges being reported at (2011) 244 CLR 144).  The interim injunction was here sought prior to the filing of the substantive application for judicial review.  The Commonwealth was represented at the hearing but, without a substantive application having yet been filed, was not in a position to effectively respond to the substantive arguments that were proposed to be put by the prospective applicants.  Hayne J issued the interim injunction on the basis that there appeared to be a serious question to be tried that would be defeated if interim relief was not granted (as the applicants would be forcibly removed to Malaysia prior to the High Court’s determination of the substance of their challenge).  Interim relief was ordered despite financial consequences for the Commonwealth arising from the travel arrangements that had already been made.  The interim injunction was to operate until the High Court could consider whether to issue an interlocutory injunction.  The hearing for an interlocutory injunction was held the following day and interlocutory relief was issued – see Shah v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship [2011] HCATrans 196.

## INTERLOCUTORY INJUNCTIONS

An interlocutory injunction is used to maintain the status quo until the final hearing.  It is not granted automatically.

Recall s47(4) of the JR Act which gives the Supreme Court power to stay a decision being challenged by an applicant for prohibition or certiorari orders.  A similar power to stay a decision being challenged (under JR Act s20 in Part 3) can be found in s29 of the JR Act.  In effect, a stay under s29, a stay under s47(4), and an interlocutory injunction are identical, ie they’re designed to achieve the same purpose; to keep things as they are – to stop the complained of decision being implemented until a court has the chance to consider the legality of the government decision.  On the inherent power to grant a stay or an injunction, see the Civil Proceedings Act 2011, s9(3), ss10 and 11 of the Supreme Court of Queensland Act 1991 and s58 of the Constitution of Queensland 2001.

The courts when granting interlocutory injunctions have developed a specific approach to deciding whether to grant such injunctions.  A similar approach has been applied to JR Act s29 (although this is not without controversy, see our earlier discussion of s29) and would probably be applied to JR Act s47(4).  It is therefore important to consider the circumstances where a court might issue an interlocutory injunction.

## THE TEST

Two slightly different approaches or tests have been applied in determining whether to issue an interlocutory injunction.

See Beecham Group Ltd v Bristol Laboratories Pty Ltd (1968) 118 CLR 618 at 622-623 where the High Court accepted a test involving 2 enquiries:

“[W]hether the plaintiff has made out a prima facie case, in the sense that if the evidence remains as it is there is a probability that at the trial of the action, the plaintiff will be entitled to relief.” [Emphasis added.]

“[W]hether the inconvenience or injury which the plaintiff would be likely to suffer if an injunction were refused outweighs or is outweighed by the injury which the defendant would suffer if an injunction were granted.”  This second enquiry is referred to as the balance of convenience.

## PRIMA FACIE CASE?

In American Cyanamid Ltd v Ethicon Ltd [1975] AC 396 at 407-408, the House of Lords overturned a Court of Appeal decision refusing an interlocutory injunction and proposed a different approach.  The Court of Appeal had effectively conducted a mini-trial when determining whether the plaintiff had established a prima facie case.

Note that at the interlocutory stage, all evidence is given by affidavit and is not normally tested by cross-examination.  This stage is therefore totally unsuited to a mini-trial.  Lord Diplock, with whom the other members of the House of Lords agreed, considered that the Court of Appeal had applied the wrong approach.  Lord Diplock noted that expressions like “a probability”, “a prima facie case” or “a strong prima facie case” caused confusion as to what interlocutory injunctions were meant to achieve.

In the place of this prima facie case test, Lord Diplock offered the following: “The Court, no doubt, must be satisfied that the claim is not frivolous or vexatious; in other words, that there is a serious question to be tried.”  His Lordship considered that courts at an interlocutory hearing should not try to resolve conflicts of evidence on affidavit as to facts on which the claims of either party may ultimately depend.  Nor should courts decide, at an interlocutory hearing, difficult questions of law which called for detailed argument and mature consideration.  These were matters to be dealt with at trial.

The High Court, for a period, appeared to embrace the “serious question to be tried test” - see Murphy v Lush (1986) 60 ALJR 523 at 524 and Patrick Stevedores Operations No 2 Pty Ltd v Maritime Union of Australia (1998) 195 CLR 1 at [21] footnote 14, in the majority judgment of Brennan CJ, McHugh, Gummow, Kirby and Hayne JJ.

In 2006, however, in *Australian Broadcasting Corporation v O’Neill (2006) 227 CLR 57 (a defamation case) Gummow and Hayne JJ (with whom Gleeson CJ and Crennan J agreed) favoured the prima facie case test – at [65]-[71] and [19].  Gummow and Hayne JJ explained the prima facie case test in the following manner:

“By using the phrase ‘prima facie case’, [the High Court in Beecham] did not mean that the plaintiff must show that it is more probable than not that at trial the plaintiff will succeed; it is sufficient that the plaintiff show a sufficient likelihood of success to justify in the circumstances the preservation of the status quo pending the trial” – at [65].

Gummow and Hayne JJ suggest that what will be “a sufficient likelihood” will vary depending on the circumstances of each case.  Gummow and Hayne JJ accepted that the phrase “serious question to be tried” could still be used in Australia but rejected Lord Diplock’s view that this element could be satisfied by simply showing that the ultimate legal claim was not frivolous or vexatious – at [70]-[71].  Hayne J, for example, in Shah v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship [2011] HCATrans 196 at 25 referred to the need to establish “a sufficiently serious question to be tried” to warrant the issue of an interlocutory injunction.  For an application of the approach in ABC v O’Neill in an administrative law context in Qld, see Australian Leisure and Hospitality Group Ltd v Chief Executive, Department of Employment, Economic Development and Innovation [2009] QSC 354 at [22]-[25], per Applegarth J.

## A PUBLIC LAW ADJUSTMENT TO THE APPROACH TO INTERLOCUTORY INJUNCTIONS?

In Castlemaine Tooheys Ltd v South Australia (1986) 161 CLR 148 at 153, Mason ACJ, put forward a 3-point test for interlocutory injunctions in public law litigation (referred to without disapproval in Patrick Stevedores Operations No 2 Pty Ltd v Maritime Union of Australia - see [21] footnote 14):

“[T]hat the plaintiff has made out a prima facie case, in the sense that if the evidence remains as it is there is a probability that at the trial of the action the plaintiff will be held entitled to relief”;

That the plaintiff will suffer irreparable injury for which damages will not be adequate compensation unless an injunction is granted.

The issue of whether irreparable injury will be suffered is normally considered as part of the balance of convenience.  In relation to this second point made by Mason ACJ in Castlemaine Tooheys, his Honour was not too rigid about it.  See Richardson v Forestry Commission (1987) 164 CLR 261 at 275 where Mason CJ considered that it was enough that the plaintiff show that irreparable injury was a possibility in the sense that there was a real, as distinct from a bare, possibility of an adverse effect if no injunction was granted.  One difficulty in applying this approach in an administrative law context is that there is no right to damages resulting from a successful administrative law challenge;

The plaintiff must show that the balance of convenience favours granting of an interlocutory injunction.

## BALANCE OF CONVENIENCE

This element is discussed in the Ethicon decision at 408-410.  According to Lord Diplock, it is unwise to attempt to list all the various matters which may need to be taken into consideration in deciding where the balance lies, let alone to suggest the relative weight to be attached to them.  This will vary from case to case.  Lord Diplock considered 3 factors in Ethicon:

“[T]he extent to which the disadvantages to each party would be incapable of being compensated in damages” in the event of one of them succeeding at trial.  Again, note the absence of a right to damages in administrative law;

“[I]f the extent of uncompensatable disadvantage to each party was comparable, then the Court might take into account the relative strengths of each party’s case as revealed by the affidavit evidence.”  His Lordship warned however that this should be done only if the evidence to be relied upon for this weighing up was not subject to any real doubts.  The Court was not justified in embarking upon anything resembling a preliminary trial of the action upon conflicting affidavits in order to assess the strengths of each party’s case;

The effect of granting an injunction on third parties.  See also Silktone v Devreal Capital Pty Ltd (1990) 21 NSWLR 317 at 323-324.  The impact of the order on the public generally can and should be taken into account - Patrick Stevedores Operations No 2 Pty Ltd v Maritime Union of Australia at [65]; and Australian Leisure and Hospitality Group Ltd v Chief Executive, Department of Employment, Economic Development and Innovation [2009] QSC 354 at [30], per Applegarth J.

## UNDERTAKING AS TO DAMAGES

In non-administrative law litigation, undertakings as to damages are invariably required of applicants seeking interlocutory injunctions.

The need for an undertaking in commercial litigation is readily apparent.  In cases where government power is being exercised or “public” rights are involved, however, requiring an undertaking as to damages becomes more complicated.

The position with respect to interlocutory injunctions in Queensland is specifically governed by Chapter 8 Part 2 of the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules which provides that interlocutory injunctions must not be issued without an undertaking to pay damages given by the party obtaining the interlocutory injunction except where there is “good reason” – Rule 264.  There is clear potential, when there is “good reason”, to dispense with the requirement of an undertaking in administrative law litigation.

## INTERLOCUTORY INJUNCTIONS IN QUEENSLAND

An illustrative Qld case is Central Qld Speleological Society Incorporated v Central Queensland Cement Pty Ltd [1989] 2 QdR 512.  This decision involved an attempt by the Speleological Society to restrain, by interlocutory injunction, a corporation (ie a non-governmental entity) from blasting of a particular cave within Mount Etna.  The Society alleged that blasting would breach the Queensland Fauna Conservation Act.

The Society was unsuccessful at first instance, and on appeal (by majority) the Full Court held that the Society did not have standing to appear before the Court - Thomas J dissented on standing.  The Court however considered (by way of obiter) whether an interlocutory injunction would have been appropriate.  On the issue of balance of convenience, you will find the relevant discussions at 532 in the judgment of Derrington J and at 525-527 in the judgment of Thomas J.  de Jersey J did not consider that there was a serious question to be tried and consequently did not deal with the balance of convenience.

Thomas J noted that on a pecuniary level, the likely damage to the cement company from the granting of an interlocutory injunction would be much greater than any damage suffered by the Society if the injunction was refused.  His Honour observed that the Speleological Society’s undertaking to pay damages was virtually worthless and that had the action been of a commercial nature, then such a factor would normally be decisive.

Evidence was tendered that the interlocutory injunction would have caused losses to the cement company in the order of $41,800 per week.  The Society could only effectively give an undertaking in the sum of $15,000.  The Society’s gross earnings from the sale of T-shirts and stickers over 5 years was $3,575 and $434.50 was earned from the operation of a camp site.

However, given the fact that the case involved a wide range of issues and given the fact that matters of public interest could be taken into account in the balance of convenience, Thomas J (reaching the opposite conclusion to Derrington J) considered that it was appropriate that an interlocutory injunction should be granted preventing the cement company from blasting pending the Court’s decision regarding alleged breaches of the Fauna Conservation Act.

Derrington J expressed sympathy for the society, recognising that in terms of preserving the status quo it would be impossible to restore the cave if an injunction were not granted and blasting occurred.  However, his Honour made it very clear that, in his view, in order to avoid abuse of the system of justice, an interlocutory injunction would only be granted if the Society provided security sufficient to meet the undertaking that ought to have been given in an amount determined by proper enquiry.

Compare the approach taken by Thomas J in the Speleological Society case at 525-527 with the approach advocated by Mossop, ‘Interlocutory Injunctions in Public Law’ (1993-4) 11 Australian Bar Review 59.  Mossop argues that in “public law” litigation the ability to give an undertaking should be among the issues weighed in the balance of convenience.  The author contrasts this approach with that to be taken in “private” litigation.  In such litigation the capacity to give an undertaking should be a separate requirement for obtaining an interlocutory injunction.  If an adequate undertaking could not be given then an injunction would not normally be granted in private litigation.  This approach is consistent with that taken by Thomas J.  On the giving of undertakings by public authorities under English law, see JNE Varuhas and PG Turner, ‘Injunctions, Undertakings in Damages and the Public-Private Divide’ (2014) 130 Law Quarterly Review 33.

## PERMANENT INJUNCTIONS

Asking whether there is a serious question to be tried; and what is the balance of convenience relates only to interlocutory injunctions.  These issues are not relevant to the issuing of a permanent injunction.

Before a permanent injunction will be issued at the final hearing of a legal dispute, the plaintiff must have established his or her legal case.  In an administrative law context this would normally involve showing that one of the grounds of legality review has been established in relation to the government decision being judicially reviewed.  The plaintiff must then persuade the court that as a matter of discretion, a permanent injunction should be issued.  Because of the serious consequences for a respondent who fails to abide by an injunction, the terms of the court’s injunction need to be precisely and carefully worded.

## MANDATORY v PROHIBITORY INJUNCTIONS

There are theoretically 6 types of injunctions: mandatory and prohibitory versions of each of the interim, interlocutory and permanent injunctions.

## MANDATORY INTERIM INJUNCTIONS

The rarest type of injunction is the mandatory interim injunction - see R v Secretary of State for the Home Department; Ex parte Herbage [1986] 3 WLR 504 at 514.  Only in exceptional circumstances would a court grant mandatory interim relief.  There would have to be shown great urgency to prevent the danger of serious injury or damage occurring.

## MANDATORY INTERLOCUTORY INJUNCTIONS

Mandatory interlocutory injunctions are not commonly issued.  In Shepherd Homes Ltd v Sandham [1971] Ch 340 at 348-349, it was observed that “the seriousness of the order remains as an important factor.”  If a mandatory interlocutory injunction is granted it may have a devastating impact on the respondent and there will often be no need to grant a further mandatory injunction at the final hearing.  The granting of a mandatory injunction at an interlocutory stage will often effectively decide the case.  Normally when a prohibitory interlocutory injunction is granted there will still be a question at the final hearing whether the injunction should be dissolved or continued.  The granting of a mandatory interlocutory injunction may often cause the party bound by it to capitulate.  For this reason it will only be in an unusually strong and clear case that a mandatory interlocutory injunction will be issued.  Megarry J gives an example (at 349) when it might be appropriate to order a mandatory interlocutory injunction: If a defendant has rushed to finish what is being done in breach of the plaintiff’s rights, then provided the plaintiff is prompt in seeking the Court’s assistance, a mandatory interlocutory injunction may be appropriate.  This will effectively restore the status quo.

Megarry J concluded that before a mandatory interlocutory injunction would be granted: “the Court must, ... (amongst other things) feel a high degree of assurance that at the trial it will appear that the injunction was rightly granted.”  This approach was expressly approved in Queensland in the Active Leisure (Sports) Pty Ltd v Sportsman’s Australia Ltd [1991] 1 QdR 301 at 304 and 314.  See also Queensland v Australian Telecommunications Commission (1985) 59 ALR 243 at 245; but note Gummow J’s judgment in Business World Computers Pty Ltd v Australian Telecommunications Commission (1988) 82 ALR 499 at 501-504.

## MANDATORY PERMANENT INJUNCTIONS

The grant of a mandatory permanent injunction is unlike a prohibitory permanent injunction in that mandatory injunctions can never be granted “as of course” - see Redland Bricks Ltd v Morris [1970] AC 652 at 665-6.  Every case must depend essentially upon its own particular circumstances.  The courts essentially require a stronger case than required for a prohibitory permanent injunction.

## PROHIBITORY PERMANENT INJUNCTIONS

Contrast the approach a court takes to the request for the grant of a prohibitory permanent injunction - see York Brothers (Trading) Pty Ltd v Commissioner of Main Roads [1983] 1 NSWLR 391 at 399-400.  In this case (which involved the law of nuisance), Powell J acknowledged that once a plaintiff has established his or her case and where what is sought is a prohibitory permanent injunction, then prima facie the plaintiff is entitled to an injunction.

## INJUNCTIONS - STANDING

If an injunction is sought under Part 5 of the JR Act, then the applicant must show that its interests are adversely affected by the decision (JR Act s44).  This links in with the JR Act Part 3 standing requirements.  Standing cases considered earlier apply to applications for injunctions relying on the Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction.  Recall, also, the Bateman’s Bay decision.

## TIME LIMITS

Applications for injunctions under JR Act Part 5 must be made in accordance with s46 of the JR Act - ie as soon as possible but in any event not later than 3 months or as ordered by the Court.  Injunctions sought via Supreme Court’s inherent jurisdiction are not subject to same limitation, but it is difficult to imagine the Supreme Court granting an injunction in its inherent jurisdiction to an applicant who has been slack and who has missed the time limit in the JR Act Part 5.

## INJUNCTIONS TO ENFORCE THE LAW

Public officials or citizens may seek to use injunctions to make other citizens comply with the law.  The Attorney-General at common law is the one who has standing to sue to enforce the law.  Normally, the criminal law is enforced through the criminal courts.

There are circumstances, however, where civil proceedings can be brought to enforce the law (including criminal law).  These circumstances were considered by the House of Lords in Gouriet v the Union of Post Office Workers [1978] AC 435.  In that case, a private individual sought unsuccessfully to enforce provisions of 2 English statutes which made it criminal offences to interfere with postal and telegraphic services.  A trade union was proposing to disrupt communications between Britain and the Republic of South Africa in protest over apartheid.  The Attorney-General had refused to act against the union.  At 481, Lord Wilberforce considered the power of the Attorney-General to invoke the assistance of the civil courts in aid of the criminal law.  His Lordship stated: “It is an exceptional power confined in practice, to cases where an offence is frequently repeated in disregard of a usually inadequate penalty ... or in cases of emergency ... . [I]t is a ... [power] not without its difficulties ... .”

Lord Wilberforce described the jurisdiction as one of great delicacy.  His Lordship indicated two areas of delicacy.  The first was the problem of a civil court granting an injunction (breach of which could potentially lead to imprisonment) where “Parliament [had only] imposed a sanction (eg a fine of £1), without an increase in severity for repeated offences.”  Secondly there was the problem of a civil court without a jury and applying a civil standard of proof effectively convicting a citizen “where Parliament [had] ... provided for trial of offence by indictment before a jury”.

The High Court has indicated that the position set out in Gouriet’s case reflects the law in Australia.  See Mason J in The Commonwealth v John Fairfax & Sons (1980) 147 CLR 39 at 49-50 and Gibbs CJ in the Attorney-General (Qld) (Ex rel Kerr) v T (1983) 57 ALJR 285 at 286.  Note the comments of Gaudron, Gummow and Kirby JJ in Bateman’s Bay 194 CLR 247 at [41].

Two final points on injunctions and enforcing the law.

An important feature of the claims in Onus v Alcoa and the Qld Speleological Society case was that both cases involved members of the public (individually and collectively) alleging that other entities were breaking or were about to break statutory rules which existed to protect a public interest.  As Allars observes, in such cases it is obviously better that the matter be dealt with early via an injunction than to leave things until the statute had been breached, particularly when the statutory penalties cannot bring back the destroyed relics or bat habitat - Introduction to Australian Administrative Law (Butterworths, 1990) 294.

In all the types of case considered under this heading, notice that the ADJR Act/JR Act would have been inapplicable.  The defendants in these cases (Alcoa and Central Qld Cement) were not government decision-makers exercising government power.  Injunctions therefore had to be sought via the inherent jurisdiction of the relevant court.

## EQUITABLE NATURE OF INJUNCTIONS

Injunctions are equitable remedies.  A plaintiff must come to the court with clean hands and equitable defences such as delay or acquiescence may be available to the defendant.  Finally, as with all other JR Act remedies, an application for an injunction under Part 5 of the JR Act will be subject to the Court’s powers under sections 11, 12, 13, 14, 48 and 49.

# 4.	COLLATERAL CHALLENGE

The final topic to briefly mention is that of “Collateral Challenge”.  The remedies so far considered all involve a direct challenge to the lawfulness/validity of the exercise of government power.  However a challenge to the lawfulness/validity of the exercise of such power may be made indirectly in non-administrative law proceedings.  In tort claims for damages or in criminal proceedings, plaintiffs or defendants may find themselves challenging the legality of government conduct.  This is known as collateral challenge.  See Lehane J in Federal Airports Corporation v Aerolineas Argentinas (1997) 76 FCR 582, 593-600; M Aronson, ‘Criteria for Restricting Collateral Challenge’ (1998) 9 Public Law Review 237; E Campbell, ‘Collateral Challenge of the Validity of Governmental Action’ (1998) 24 Monash University Law Review 272; and R Douglas, ‘Collateral Attacks on Administrative Decisions: Anomalous But Efficient’ (2006) 51 AIAL Forum 71.
