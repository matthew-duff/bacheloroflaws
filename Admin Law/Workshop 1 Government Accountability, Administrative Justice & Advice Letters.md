# Question 1(a): Non-Judicial Institutions for Administrative Justice

The following non-judicial institutions review and redress individual grievances about executive government action:

### **Administrative Review Tribunal (ART) / QCAT**
Administrative Review Tribunal (ART)
The ART (which replaced the AAT in 2024) conducts *merits review* at the Commonwealth level, stepping into the shoes of the original decision-maker to determine the "correct or preferable" decision (*Shi v MARA*; Groves & Boughey). It is a creature of statute operating within the executive branch, and its jurisdiction extends across 400+ Commonwealth Acts. 

Queensland Civil and Administrative Tribunal
QCAT performs an equivalent function in Queensland. Merits review bodies secure administrative justice by ensuring individual cases result in the substantively *best* decision, not merely a legally permissible one — they are the most direct mechanism for correcting administrative errors affecting individuals.

### Ombudsman Offices (Commonwealth and Queensland)
The Commonwealth Ombudsman (established under the *Ombudsman Act 1976 (Cth)*) and the Queensland Ombudsman investigate "matters of administration" — complaints about government action, conduct, or inaction. The Ombudsman is independent, non-adversarial, free to access, and can investigate on their own motion. Creyke describes the Ombudsman as "highly effective at identifying systemic flaws and providing accessible, non-adversarial review." Importantly, the Ombudsman makes *recommendations* rather than binding orders, though these carry significant weight and are generally complied with.

### Human Rights Commissions
The Australian Human Rights Commission (federal) and Queensland Human Rights Commission (under the *Human Rights Act 2019* (Qld)) conciliate disputes between individuals and public entities involving alleged breaches of human rights obligations. The QHRC can refer unresolved matters to QCAT for adjudication.

### Anti-Corruption Watchdogs
Bodies such as the Crime and Corruption Commission (Qld) investigate corrupt or improper conduct by public officials. While not primarily focused on individual redress, they can investigate complaints and refer matters for prosecution or recommend disciplinary action.

# Question 1(b): Courts and Administrative Justice

Yes. Courts play a fundamental role in securing administrative justice, though their role is distinct from merits review bodies.

Courts exercise *legality review* (judicial review): they ask not *what* the best decision is, but whether the decision was made *according to law*. 

This jurisdiction flows from three overlapping sources: 
	(i) the common law; 
	(ii) s 75(v) of the Constitution (conferring original jurisdiction on the HCA to issue writs against "officers of the Commonwealth"); and 
	(iii) statute — primarily the *Administrative Decisions (Judicial Review) Act 1977* (Cth) in the Federal Court and the *Judicial Review Act 1991* (Qld) in the Supreme Court.

Courts secure administrative justice by:
- Enforcing the rule of law: requiring executive action to conform to legal authority (Dixon J in *Communist Party Case*; *Church of Scientology v Woodward*)
- Reviewing decisions for errors of law, breach of procedural fairness, jurisdictional error, and other grounds codified in the ADJR Act ss 5–6
- Granting powerful remedies: certiorari (quashing a decision), mandamus (compelling action), prohibition (restraining unlawful action), and declarations
- Protecting a constitutional *minimum* of judicial review that Parliament cannot remove through privative clauses (*Plaintiff S157/2002*)

Crucially, courts cannot substitute their own view on the merits — they cannot enter the "forbidden field" of merits (*AG (NSW) v Quin*). This is the fundamental boundary between legality and merits review, maintained by the strict separation of powers under the Constitution (Groves & Boughey; Lecture 1 Summary).

French CJ confirms that judicial review anchors administrative justice standards in the Constitution and the rule of law itself, giving those values their ultimate legal enforceability (French, 2010).

# Question 2: Political and Legal Means of Systemic Accountability

Where the concern is not an individual grievance but the *system* of government processes itself, accountability operates through:

## **Political mechanisms:**
- **Responsible government**: Ministers are members of Parliament and must answer to it for the conduct of their departments. Parliament can debate, censure, and — ultimately — remove a government.
- **Parliamentary questions and debates**: Direct scrutiny of executive conduct on the floor of Parliament.
- **Parliamentary committee systems**: Committees scrutinise the passage of legislation, government expenditure (through estimates committees), and the broader functioning of government agencies and policies.
- **Periodic elections**: The ultimate collective accountability — the executive is accountable to the electorate (Workshop 1 Learning Guide; Lecture 2 Slides Summary).
- **Media scrutiny**: Public disclosure of government misconduct and maladministration.
## **Legal Accountability:**
- **Courts:** Courts conducting judicial review
- **Merits review:** Merits reviewers who are able to substitute their decision as the "correct and preferable decision". Looking for something more than lawfulness. Still includes lawfulness as a minimum criteria though.
## Financial accountability
- **s 83 Constitution**: "No money shall be drawn from the Treasury of the Commonwealth except under appropriation made by law."

## Integrity / investigatory bodies
- **Ombudsman (systemic investigation)**: Beyond individual complaints, the Ombudsman can conduct own-motion investigations into systemic maladministration and publish reports to Parliament. This is the most important legal tool for systemic accountability.
- **Auditor-General**: Financial accountability — auditing government expenditure and reporting to Parliament on compliance and efficiency.
- **Freedom of Information (FOI)**: The *FOI Act 1982* (Cth) (significantly reformed in 2010 to promote a pro-disclosure culture) provides enforceable access to government-held information, enabling civil society and the media to scrutinise systemic issues.
- **Information/Privacy Commissioners**: Oversee FOI and privacy rights at Federal and State/Territory levels.
- **Anti-corruption watchdogs**: Bodies like the CCC (Qld) investigate systemic corruption and conduct in public life.

Creyke's "bird's nest" model captures this well: the systemic health of administrative justice depends on the *collective* strength of these institutions mutually reinforcing each other, even if each has weaknesses individually.

### Question 3: Administrative Justice Values

**French CJ's "skeleton" (French, 2010, pp 10–17)** identifies four core minimum standards:
1. **Lawfulness** — decisions must accord with the rules prescribed by law
2. **Rationality** — decisions must be logically open on the available information
3. **Fairness** — procedural fairness is a central, non-optional requirement
4. **Intelligibility** — reasons must be given so the affected person understands why the decision was made

Beyond this core, French identifies *broader* administrative justice values: efficiency, timeliness, accessibility, and affordability — without which the formal legal rights are practically hollow.

**Creyke (2007)** frames administrative justice around "integrity in government": institutions must exercise power for their intended public purposes and observe "proper practice." Her analysis emphasises that justice requires reconciling the individual's interest in substantive fairness and legality with the needs of public administration (efficiency and consistency).

**The Workshop Study Guide** distils the core administrative law values as: *legality, fairness, reasonableness/rationality, integrity, openness (transparency), efficiency, and effectiveness.*

**Lecture 1** adds the Aronson, Groves & Weeks formulation: *openness, fairness, participation, accountability, consistency, rationality, accessibility, legality, and impartiality.*

All of these values find expression in the grounds of judicial review (e.g., the duty to accord procedural fairness; the rationality/reasonableness grounds) and in the objectives of merits review bodies (e.g., the ART's statutory objective to provide review that is "fair, just, economical, informal, and quick": ART Act s 9).

### Question 4: Creyke's Analysis — Normative Effect of External Review
What does Creyke’s analysis tell us about the normative effect (or, impact) of external review, over executive government action, by tribunals and courts?

Creyke's analysis reveals that external review by tribunals and courts has a meaningful *upstream* normative effect on executive government, beyond the resolution of individual cases:

- Makes for better, more consistent decisions
	- A more coherent overall system of public decision making
- Shapes the attitude of public decision makers.
- Increases the public's confidence in the system

**1. Compliance culture**: Empirical studies cited by Creyke show a high degree of executive acceptance of administrative law values. There is a genuine culture of compliance among public officials — judicial review outcomes are not merely symbolic but actually shape decision-making practice.

**2. Systemic improvement through the Ombudsman**: The Ombudsman's ability to identify *systemic* flaws across agencies means external review can drive improvements to administrative practice at an institutional level, not just in the individual case under review.

**3. Guidance function of the ART/AAT**: The ART has become, in Creyke's words, a "premier institution" providing interpretive guidance on hundreds of statutes. Its decisions inform and set standards for front-line administrative decision-making, creating an indirect normative influence far beyond the cases that reach it.

**4. Diffusion of executive power**: Independent external review by tribunals (outside the original decision-making agency) diffuses executive power, acting as a structural check against arbitrariness and self-interest in administration.

**5. Acknowledged weaknesses**: Creyke is clear-eyed about the limits — underfunding, jurisdictional traps in judicial review, and the risk that efficiency concerns override justice. These structural weaknesses diminish the normative impact in practice.

**Overall**, Creyke argues for viewing the administrative law system holistically: it is the *collective* coherence of the "integrity system" — courts, tribunals, Ombudsman, FOI, and human rights bodies mutually reinforcing each other — rather than any single institution, that produces the normative effect of securing integrity in government.

**Dear Ms Bell,**

**Re: Appeal of the Federal Environment Minister's Artificial Reef Permit — *Environment Protection (Sea Dumping) Act 1981* (Cth)**

We act on behalf of the Stop the Scuttling Collective and write to advise you about the options available to challenge the Federal Environment Minister's decision to grant a sea dumping permit under the *Environment Protection (Sea Dumping) Act 1981* (Cth).

**1. The Minister's Decision and Your Grounds for Challenge**

The Minister has granted a permit authorising the scuttling of a decommissioned navy vessel 2,000 metres off Caloundra. Your members are concerned this will cause coastal erosion and marine pollution. There are two primary avenues through which we can challenge this decision on your behalf.

**2. Merits Review — Administrative Review Tribunal (ART)**

If the *Sea Dumping Act* or associated legislation confers a right of review on the ART, you may seek *merits review*. This is a hearing before an independent tribunal (the ART, which replaced the former Administrative Appeals Tribunal in 2024) in which the Tribunal "steps into the shoes" of the Minister and considers all the evidence afresh.

The ART is not restricted to legal errors — it can weigh the environmental evidence you raise and determine what the *correct or preferable* decision should have been. Hearings are less formal than a court proceeding: the parties present evidence and submissions, expert witnesses can be called, and the process is designed to be fair, efficient, and accessible.

Following a hearing, the ART can:
- **Affirm** the Minister's decision (uphold the permit as it stands)
- **Vary** the decision (for example, impose stricter environmental conditions on the permit)
- **Set aside** the decision and remit it to the Minister for reconsideration with guidance
- **Substitute** its own decision (for example, refuse the permit outright)

**3. Judicial Review — Federal Court under the *ADJR Act 1977* (Cth)**

Separately or alternatively, the SSC may apply to the Federal Court for *judicial review* under the *Administrative Decisions (Judicial Review) Act 1977* (Cth). Judicial review is concerned with the *lawfulness* of the Minister's decision, not whether it was the best decision on the environmental merits.

Relevant grounds may include: the Minister's failure to consider relevant environmental considerations; taking into account irrelevant matters; a breach of procedural fairness; or acting outside the scope of the statutory power.

If judicial review succeeds, the Court can quash the permit decision and order the Minister to make the decision again, this time lawfully. The Court cannot, however, directly substitute its own decision on whether the permit should be granted.

**4. Our Recommendation and Next Steps**

We will investigate whether the *Sea Dumping Act* provides a specific right of merits review before the ART, as this would allow the Tribunal to directly address your environmental concerns. Judicial review remains available in any event as a constitutional backstop.

**Please note that strict time limits apply to both forms of review and it is important that we act promptly.** We will be in contact shortly with further advice on the applicable time limits and standing requirements.

Do not hesitate to contact me if you have any questions in the meantime.

### Scenario Two — Letter to Ant (and Scotty)

**Dear Ant,**

**Re: Your Grievance About Conditions in the Special Detention Unit**

Thank you for calling us. You asked what external mechanisms are available for you and Scotty to challenge the conditions of your current detention. We write to explain your options in plain terms.

**1. The Situation**

You and Scotty have been placed in a special detention unit for five months following disciplinary proceedings. You are isolated from other prisoners, have strictly limited contact with staff, and have no access to personal property. Your written complaint to the prison manager was rejected. You are concerned about the fairness of these conditions and their effect on your mental health.

**2. The Queensland Ombudsman**

The Queensland Ombudsman is an independent officer who investigates complaints about the administrative actions of Queensland government agencies - which includes the Department of Corrective Services and the management of correctional facilities.

They have a free line available for prisoners.

The Ombudsman can investigate whether:
- The decision to place you in the special detention unit was reached through a fair and proper process
- The prescribed procedures were actually followed during the disciplinary proceedings
- The conditions imposed (isolation, no property, limited staff contact for five months) are unreasonable, excessive, or disproportionate
- The prison's actions are in line with good public administration principles

To make a complaint, you can write to the Queensland Ombudsman. We can help you prepare and send the complaint letter.

There is also a Queensland Ombudsman for health in addition to the ones mentioned above.

**3. The Queensland Human Rights Commission**

Under the *Human Rights Act 2019* (Qld), you have rights that public entities - including prisons - must respect and actively consider. Relevant rights include:
- The right to humane treatment in detention (s 30 HRA), which requires that you be treated with humanity and respect for your inherent dignity
- The right to protection against cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment (s 17 HRA)

You may lodge a complaint with the Queensland Human Rights Commission (QHRC), which will attempt to conciliate the dispute between you, Scotty, and the prison. If conciliation fails, the matter can be referred to QCAT (the Queensland Civil and Administrative Tribunal) for a formal hearing.

**4. The official visitor**
An external official who provides investigations into Queensland corrective services. Low bar to entry, informal, and regular visits.

Potential drawback is the lack of authority - these are not legally binding.

**5. QCAT**
There is no conferral of jurisdiction to QCAT.

**5. What We Recommend**

We recommend you and Scotty **lodge a complaint to the Queensland Ombudsman** as the most immediate and accessible step. We will also assess whether a human rights complaint to the QHRC is appropriate given the documented impact on your mental health — the mental health concerns may be particularly important under the HRA.

We will call you again shortly to discuss which option is best suited to your circumstances.

**Please note that time limits may apply** — it is important that you contact us as soon as possible so we can act quickly on your behalf.

Yours sincerely,

[Caseworker]
Prisoners Legal Service
