# Workshop 4: Grounds of Judicial Review – Natural Justice (Procedural Fairness)

## Overview of Judicial Review (JR) vs Merits Review
- **Merits Review**: Reconsideration of the substantive fairness/justice of a decision (*de novo*). Focuses on the "correct or preferable" decision.
- **Judicial Review**: Concerned with the **legality** of the decision-making process, not the substance. Ensures decisions are lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair.
- **Constitutional Protection**: JR is protected by **s 75 of the Constitution** (federal) and is an inherent power of State Supreme Courts.

## Sources of JR Jurisdiction
1. **Common Law**: Inherent power of superior courts to remedy "jurisdictional errors."
2. **Constitutional**: 
   - **s 75(v)**: Entrenched jurisdiction against "officers of the Commonwealth" (mandamus, prohibition, injunction).
   - **s 75(iii)**: Matters where the Commonwealth is a party.
   - **s 39B Judiciary Act (Cth)**: Replicates s 75(v) for the Federal Court.
3. **Statutory Schemes**: 
   - **AD(JR)A 1977 (Cth)**: Codifies grounds (e.g., s 5(1)(a) natural justice).
   - **JRA 1991 (Qld)**: Equivalent for Queensland.

## Natural Justice (Procedural Fairness)

### 1. The Threshold Question: When does the duty arise?
The common law presumes a duty to accord procedural fairness when a decision affects **rights, interests, or legitimate expectations** in a "direct and immediate way" (*Kioa v West*).

#### Key Cases on Threshold:
- **Ridge v Baldwin (1964)**: Extended natural justice to administrative bodies (not just quasi-judicial ones) when dismissing a public official (affecting livelihood/pension).
- **FAI v Winneke (1982)**: Natural justice applies to the **renewal of a licence** (legitimate expectation of renewal).
- **Kioa v West (1985)**: The seminal Australian case. Mason J: Presumption of PF applies unless clearly excluded by statute. It covers personal liberty, status, livelihood, and reputation.
- **Plaintiff M61/2010E (Offshore Processing Case)**: Even "non-statutory" or highly discretionary processes (like refugee status assessments) attract PF if they inform a statutory power and affect liberty.
- **Isbester v Knox City Council (2015)**: PF applies to decisions affecting "important" interests, such as the destruction of a pet dog.

#### Exclusions/Limitations:
- **Legitimate Expectations**: No longer a separate touchstone for PF in Australia (*WZARH*). The focus is now on whether the decision affects an interest and what fairness requires in the circumstances.
- **Direct vs. Indirect Effect**: Only "direct and immediate" effects attract PF. 
    - *Example*: *Saraceni v ASIC* – A decision to authorize an investigation only indirectly affects the potential examinee at the early stage.
- **Public at Large**: Decisions affecting the community generally (e.g., airport noise policy) usually do not attract individual PF rights.

### 2. Statutory Exclusion
- **Principle of Legality**: Statutes are construed on the assumption that they do not overthrow fundamental common law principles (like PF) unless using **"plain words of necessary intendment"** (*Annetts v McCann*, *Saeed*).
- **Procedural Codes**: A statutory code (e.g., in the *Migration Act*) does not necessarily exclude common law PF unless it is explicitly "exhaustive" (*Ex parte Miah*).

### 3. The Content Question: What does fairness require?
The content of PF is **flexible** and depends on the context (*Kioa*, *Mobil Oil*).

#### Components of a Fair Hearing:
1. **Prior Notice**:
   - **Substantive**: Must disclose the "case to be met," specifically adverse information that is **"credible, relevant and significant"** (*Kioa*, *VEAL*).
   - **Temporal**: Must allow adequate time to prepare (*Dunghutti Elders Council*).
2. **Disclosure of Adverse Material**:
   - Even if the decision-maker claims they didn't rely on it, if it is "credible, relevant and significant," it may need to be disclosed so the party can rebut it (*VEAL*).
3. **Reasonable Opportunity to Respond**:
   - Does not always require an oral hearing (*Heatley*, *WZARH*).
   - **Oral hearings** are necessary if there are inconsistencies in evidence or credibility issues.
4. **Legal Representation**: No absolute right, but may be required for complex matters or where the party lacks capacity (*Cains v Jenkins*).
5. **Cross-examination**: Not a general right in administrative law, but may be required if credibility is central.

### 4. Materiality and Jurisdictional Error
- **Jurisdictional Error**: A breach of PF generally constitutes a jurisdictional error, making the decision void.
- **Materiality Threshold**: A breach is only jurisdictional if it is **material**—meaning there is a **"realistic possibility"** that the outcome *could* have been different if the error had not occurred (*SZMTA*, *LPDT v Minister for Immigration*).
- **Onus**: The applicant bears the onus of proving materiality, but it is "not demanding or onerous" (*LPDT*).

## The Rule Against Bias
Bias is the absence of impartiality.

1. **Actual Bias**: The decision-maker actually had a closed mind (very hard to prove).
2. **Apprehended Bias**: Whether a **"fair-minded informed observer"** might conclude that the decision-maker **might not** bring an impartial mind to the resolution of the question (*Ebner*).

### The Ebner Test (Two-fold):
1. Identify the factor that might lead the decision-maker to decide the case other than on its legal and factual merits.
2. Articulate a "logical connection" between that factor and the feared departure from impartiality.

#### Key Points on Bias:
- **No Automatic Disqualification**: Unlike English law (*Pinochet*), Australia has no automatic disqualification for pecuniary interests; it always depends on the *Ebner* test.
- **Contextual Application**: The standard of impartiality expected of a judge is higher than that of a government minister (*Jia Legeng*).
- **Irrelevant Prejudicial Material**: Placing highly prejudicial and irrelevant material before a reviewer can give rise to apprehended bias (*CNY17*).

## Workshop Task: Summerton v VRT
*Potential PF Issues to Identify:*
1. **Refusal of short rest**: May breach the requirement for a "reasonable opportunity to respond," especially given Summerton's anxiety and PTSD.
2. **Denial of cross-examination**: Since the retired officer's evidence directly contradicted Summerton's (a credibility issue), denying cross-examination might be unfair.
3. **Failure to disclose the 'blog' material**: Even if the Member gave it "little weight," if it was used to find Summerton's testimony "unreliable," it was "credible, relevant and significant" and should have been disclosed (*VEAL*).
4. **Materiality**: Would disclosing the blog or allowing cross-examination have realistically changed the outcome? (Per *LPDT*).
