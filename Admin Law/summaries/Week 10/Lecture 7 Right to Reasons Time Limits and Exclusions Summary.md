# Lecture 7 Summary

## 1. Right to reasons

### No general common law right
- There is **no general common law duty** requiring administrative decision-makers to give reasons: *Osmond*.
- But failure to give reasons can sometimes amount to a **failure to exercise jurisdiction**: *Kirk* at [83].

### Statutory right under the ADJR Act and JR Act
- The right to reasons is **statutory and remedial**, so it should not be construed narrowly or technically: *Australian Institute of Marine and Power Engineers*.
- The right does **not depend on proving the decision is unlawful**. An applicant only needs to show, in substance, that there is a decision to which the Act applies and that they are a person entitled to seek review/reasons.
- Reasons matter strategically because they can expose whether a review ground exists. Failing to seek reasons may leave an applicant unable to prove illegality: *RP Data*.

### What a proper statement of reasons should contain
- Per *Ansett v Wraith*, reasons should identify:
  - the decision-maker's understanding of the relevant law
  - the key findings of fact
  - the reasoning process connecting the facts and law to the result
- Reasons should be **clear and unambiguous**, not just vague legislative formulae.
- Courts will not read administrative reasons with over-zealous scrutiny: *Wu Shan Liang*.

### Limits and exceptions
- Under the JR Act, the right to reasons is excluded for some matters, including:
  - **Schedule 2 exclusions**
  - **confidential personal or business information**
  - material whose disclosure would be **contrary to the public interest**, where certified by the Attorney-General

### Reasons vs FOI vs discovery
- **Reasons**: focused explanation of why the decision was made; no need to commence litigation; does **not** give access to the underlying documents.
- **FOI / right to information**: access to actual documents; broader documentary access.
- **Discovery**: available only once litigation is on foot and is discretionary.

## 2. Time limits

### Applying for reasons
- If the applicant was **not given a written record of the terms of the decision**, they have a **reasonable time** after the decision to seek reasons.
- If they **were given a written record**, they have **28 days** from receiving it to apply for reasons.

### Applying for review
- For **Part 3 JR Act review**:
  - if there was **no written record**, the applicant has a **reasonable time** after the decision
  - if there **was** a written record of the terms of the decision, including a statement of reasons, the applicant has **28 days** from receiving it
- For **Part 5 JR Act review**, s 46 gives a **3 month** limit.

### Exam point
- Always ask whether the applicant received a **written record** and whether they first sought **reasons**, because the time-limit analysis turns on that sequence.

## 3. Stays, dismissal, alternative remedies and costs

### Stays and summary dismissal
- A stay under **JR Act s 29** is functionally similar to an **interlocutory injunction** preserving the status quo.
- The ADJR Act has analogous stay provisions in **ss 15 and 15A**.
- **JR Act s 48(1)(b)** allows summary dismissal; the slides note it was used successfully by Griffith University against Ms Tang.
- The ADJR Act has **no equivalent summary dismissal provision**.

### Alternative review pathways
- Both the Federal Court and the Supreme Court may refuse statutory judicial review where there is **adequate alternative review**, such as tribunal review.
- The existence of an appeal right does not eliminate supervisory jurisdiction, but in practice it may strongly weigh against judicial review as a matter of discretion: *Harrison*.

### Costs
- **JR Act s 50** gives the Supreme Court a special costs power in proceedings brought to vindicate the statutory right to reasons.
- **JR Act s 49** gives special costs powers in judicial review proceedings, including prospective indemnity-style protection if sought early.
- The slides emphasise that these special JR Act costs provisions have **no equivalent in the ADJR Act**.

## 4. Exclusions from review and jurisdictional error

### Statutory exclusions and ouster clauses
- **JR Act s 18(1)** disables ouster clauses that pre-date the commencement of the JR Act.
- Later ouster clauses remain subject to ordinary principles of statutory interpretation and to the constitutional limits explained in *Kirk*.
- The slides also point to **Schedule 1 JR Act exclusions** and the exclusions from the right to reasons under **s 31 and Schedule 2**.

### Why jurisdictional error still matters
- The ADJR Act and Part 3 JR Act grounds are **broader than jurisdictional error**, but exclusions from those statutory schemes do not necessarily end the matter.
- At common law, the distinction between **jurisdictional error** and **error within jurisdiction** remains important: *Craig*.
- Tribunals can make errors of law **within jurisdiction** as well as jurisdictional errors: *Hossain*.
- *Kirk* confirms that State Supreme Courts retain constitutionally protected supervisory review for **jurisdictional error**.
- So a statute may exclude ordinary statutory review without excluding the Court's supervisory jurisdiction for jurisdictional error.

### Privative clauses
- In the Commonwealth context, *Plaintiff S157* shows that privative clauses are read down so they do not protect decisions affected by jurisdictional error.
- In Queensland, *Northbuild* indicates that **Schedule 1 JR Act exclusions are limited to decisions not infected by jurisdictional error**, or at minimum that inherent supervisory review remains available.

## 5. Exam use

### Short problem-question checklist
1. Is there a **decision to which the ADJR Act or JR Act applies**?
2. Is the applicant a **person aggrieved / entitled to seek review**?
3. Has the applicant sought **reasons**, and if not, would doing so help prove a review ground?
4. Was there a **written record of the terms of the decision**? If yes, calculate the **28-day** limit from that date.
5. Is there a **Schedule 1 or Schedule 2 exclusion**, or another statutory limit on reasons/review?
6. Even if statutory review is excluded, is **supervisory review for jurisdictional error** still available?
7. Is there an **adequate alternative remedy** such as an appeal or tribunal review that may lead the court to refuse relief?

### One-line takeaway
- For exam purposes, treat **reasons, time limits, exclusions, and jurisdictional error** as a linked sequence: reasons help identify grounds, written notice affects timing, exclusions may narrow statutory review, and jurisdictional error may keep supervisory review alive.
