# Australian Broadcasting Tribunal v Bond

![Case image](/assets/pixabay/australian_broadcasting_tribunal_v_bond_pixabay_2803366.jpg)

*Image credit: [Pixabay](https://pixabay.com/photos/vitoria-celebration-soccer-field-2803366/) by [Fotorech](https://pixabay.com/users/Fotorech-5554393/). Query: `Commodory companies dispute`*


## Why this case matters
- Leading High Court authority on the meaning of a `decision` and `conduct` under the `Administrative Decisions (Judicial Review) Act 1977 (Cth)`.
- Useful whenever an exam question asks whether an intermediate ruling, finding, or conclusion in an administrative process is itself reviewable.

## Facts
- The Australian Broadcasting Tribunal investigated whether companies associated with Alan Bond remained fit and proper to hold commercial broadcasting licences.
- The Tribunal concluded that Mr Bond was not a fit and proper person and, because of his control of the licensees, that the licensee companies were no longer fit and proper persons to hold their licences.
- The respondents sought review under the ADJR Act of various findings, rulings, and conclusions made during that process.

## Core holdings

### 1. Reviewable `decision`
- A reviewable decision will generally be one that is final, operative, or determinative in a practical sense.
- A conclusion reached merely as a step in the reasoning toward an ultimate outcome is ordinarily **not** a reviewable decision.
- An intermediate conclusion can still be reviewable if the statute itself requires or authorizes that determination as an essential preliminary matter.
- Here, the finding that a **licensee company** was no longer a fit and proper person under `s 88(2)(b)(i)` was reviewable because it was a substantive statutory determination.
- By contrast, the conclusion that **Mr Bond personally** was not a fit and proper person was not reviewable: it was only part of the reasoning leading to the statutory conclusion about the licensees.

### 2. Reviewable `conduct`
- `Conduct` in `s 6` refers to procedural steps taken for the purpose of making a reviewable decision, such as holding an inquiry or taking evidence.
- It does **not** extend to substantive findings, conclusions, or inferences reached along the way.
- So a party cannot recast non-reviewable substantive reasoning as reviewable `conduct`.

### 3. Error of law and `no evidence`
- An error of law only grounds review if it is **material** to the decision: the decision would have been, or might have been, different without it.
- The `no evidence` ground is limited by `s 5(3)` and does not turn every contested factual finding into a reviewable legal error.
- Bond therefore narrows attempts to challenge factual reasoning under the ADJR Act unless the statutory conditions for review are met.

### 4. Procedural fairness
- There was no denial of procedural fairness where the parties had a fair opportunity to call evidence and address the case actually being advanced against them.
- Bond is therefore more useful for the structure of ADJR review than for expanding procedural fairness doctrine.

## Authorities the reading points to
- `Lamb v Moss`: not every conclusion in an administrative process is a separately reviewable decision.
- `Ross v Costigan`: helps explain the distinction between ultimate determinations and steps in reasoning.
- `Kioa v West`: used as a comparison point for orthodox procedural fairness, especially the need for a reasonable opportunity to respond.

## Exam use
- Cite Bond when distinguishing a reviewable statutory determination from mere findings of fact, credibility assessments, or reasoning steps.
- Use it to explain why `conduct` under the ADJR Act is procedural, not substantive.
- Use it to support the proposition that not every error or arguable factual mistake is reviewable under the ADJR Act.

## Bottom line
- Under the ADJR Act, substantive statutory determinations are generally reviewable decisions, procedural steps may be reviewable conduct, but findings and conclusions along the way usually are not.
