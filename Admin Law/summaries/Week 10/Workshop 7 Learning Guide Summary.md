# Workshop 7: Access to Justice I - Jurisdiction to Review

## Core point
This topic is about threshold jurisdiction, not grounds. Before arguing natural justice, improper purpose, or unreasonableness, ask whether the court can review the matter under the statutory pathway relied on.

## Legality vs merits
- Judicial review is legality review only.
- Courts do not remake the decision on the merits.
- If illegality is established, the usual result is remittal to the original decision-maker.
- Merits review is for bodies such as the Commonwealth ART, not the reviewing court.

## Why the ADJR Act matters
The source identifies three major reforms made by the ADJR Act:
1. Simpler review procedure.
2. Codification of major grounds of review.
3. A statutory right to reasons.

Queensland's `JR Act` broadly mirrors those features in Parts 3 and 4, but with important local differences, especially `s 4(b)` and the interaction with Part 5.

## Jurisdiction roadmap
Under the `JR Act`, the key entry points are:
- `s 20`: review of a decision.
- `s 21`: review of conduct engaged in for the purpose of making a decision.
- `s 22`: review of failure to decide.

For all three, the critical threshold is whether there is a **"decision to which the Act applies"**.

## 1. "Decision" and "conduct"

### Basic structure
- `s 5 JR Act` gives examples of decisions: orders, determinations, licences, permissions, declarations, demands, and refusals to deliver articles.
- `s 6 JR Act` deems some reports or recommendations to be decisions where an enactment provides for them before a decision is made.

### The main authority: `ABT v Bond`
`Bond` is the leading case on the distinction between reviewable decisions and reviewable conduct.

High-yield points:
- A reviewable decision is usually final, operative, and determinative in a practical sense.
- A substantive intermediate step in reasoning is usually not itself a reviewable decision unless the statute specifically provides for that determination.
- Conduct is generally procedural action taken in the course of making a decision, not a substantive ruling.
- The policy concern is to prevent fragmentation of administrative processes and protect efficient government administration.

### Exam takeaway from `Bond`
Ask:
1. Is the impugned act the operative determination, or just a step along the way?
2. Does the statute expressly or impliedly require that step to be made as a distinct determination?
3. Is the complaint really about procedural conduct before the final decision?

If the act is only an intermediate substantive conclusion, statutory review may fail even though the point can later be raised when challenging a reviewable final decision.

## 2. "Administrative character"

### Core idea
The decision must be administrative, not legislative or judicial.

### Main principles
- Characterise the **decision**, not merely its subject matter.
- The character of the decision-maker is relevant but not decisive.
- Administrative law statutes are remedial and generally construed broadly.
- Administration is a process, so the decision should be viewed in context.

### Working distinctions
From `Hamblin v Duffy`, `Evans v Friemann`, and `Tooheys`:
- **Legislative** acts create or alter general rules of law.
- **Judicial** acts conclusively determine disputes by applying established law.
- **Administrative** acts typically apply law or policy to particular cases, or otherwise administer a statutory regime.

### Useful rules of thumb
- Making or amending subordinate legislation is usually legislative.
- A step preparatory to changing the law may still be administrative.
- Decisions called a "by-law" are not automatically legislative; substance controls.
- A decision can still be administrative even if it is commercial, policy-laden, or made by a tribunal or court officer.

## 3. "Under an enactment"

### Why this matters
For `s 4(a) JR Act` and the ADJR Act, the decision must be made under an enactment. This is often the hardest threshold issue.

### High Court test: `Griffith University v Tang`
A decision is made **under an enactment** only if both criteria are satisfied:
1. The decision is expressly or impliedly required or authorised by the enactment.
2. The decision itself confers, alters, or otherwise affects legal rights or obligations, and in that sense derives from the enactment.

This is the key exam formula.

### What `Tang` excludes
Even where a statutory body exists because of legislation, not every decision it makes is under an enactment.

The question is not whether the statute forms the historical background. The question is whether the impugned decision gets its legal force or effect from the enactment itself rather than from:
- general law,
- contract,
- internal policy, or
- some other non-statutory source.

### Contracts and tendering
The source strongly indicates that contract-related decisions often fail the `under an enactment` test:
- `ANU v Burns`: dismissal under employment contract, not under enactment.
- `Hawker Pacific`: decision to enter a contract was an exercise of residual executive capacity, not statutory power.
- `General Newspapers v Telstra`: a statutory capacity to contract does not itself give the contract decision legal force or effect.

Exam answer:
`Corporations Act s 124` or a general statutory power to contract is usually not enough. The force of the contract comes from ordinary contract law, not the empowering statute.

## 4. `JR Act s 4(b)`: non-statutory schemes or programs

### Why it matters
Queensland goes beyond the federal ADJR model by extending review to some decisions under **non-statutory schemes or programs** involving public funds.

### Elements of `s 4(b)`
The decision must be:
1. of an administrative character;
2. made by a State or local government officer, employee, or authority;
3. under a non-statutory scheme or program; and
4. connected with funds appropriated by Parliament or raised through statutory taxes, fees, charges, or levies.

### Limits and uncertainty
- The scope of `s 4(b)` remains uncertain.
- The case law suggests courts have not given it a broad, settled operation.
- Holmes CJ has observed extra-judicially that no successful application has yet been made under it.

### Key cases
- `Anghel`: "scheme" can include a single project; "program" can include repetitive processes.
- `Bituminous Products`: narrow approach; coherence and specificity matter, and a scheme expressly structured by statute may not be "non-statutory".

### Likely exam approach
If `s 4(a)` fails, ask whether Queensland `s 4(b)` could capture the decision. But be cautious:
- government contracts and tendering are still contestable;
- courts may resist an interpretation that fragments administrative processes too early;
- `s 9 JR Act` matters because some grounds need modification for `s 4(b)` review.

## 5. Non-justiciability

### Core idea
Some disputes remain inappropriate for judicial review because they involve high-level political judgment, prerogative power, national security, foreign affairs, or polycentric balancing.

### High-yield points
- Non-justiciability remains relevant despite the statutory schemes.
- Cabinet decisions may be reviewable if made under an enactment, but difficulties remain where the matter is highly political or polycentric.
- Decisions of the Governor-General were expressly excluded from the ADJR framework.
- Queensland is broader regarding Governors and Governor in Council decisions than the Commonwealth ADJR position.

## 6. Conduct and failure to decide

### `s 21 JR Act`: conduct
- Covers conduct engaged in for the purpose of making a reviewable decision.
- Usually procedural steps, not substantive intermediate rulings.
- Use `Bond` to distinguish conduct from an unreviewable substantive step in reasoning.

### `s 22 JR Act`: failure to decide
Two situations:
1. No statutory time limit, but there is unreasonable delay.
2. A statutory time limit exists and expires without decision.

High-yield requirements:
- there must be a **duty** to make the decision, not merely a power;
- the applicant must be aggrieved;
- unreasonable delay is assessed objectively.

## Right to reasons
- A major advantage of statutory review is the right to reasons.
- Under the Queensland scheme, Part 4 is tied to the concept of a reviewable decision.
- So if there is no decision to which the Act applies, the reasons right may also fail.

## Problem-question checklist
Use this order in exams:
1. Identify the review pathway: ADJR Act, `JR Act` Part 3, `JR Act` Part 5, `s 39B Judiciary Act`, or common law/constitutional review.
2. Identify the impugned act: decision, conduct, or failure to decide.
3. Ask whether the applicant is "aggrieved".
4. Is there a reviewable **decision** under `Bond`, or merely an intermediate step?
5. Is the act of an **administrative character**, rather than legislative or judicial?
6. Is it **under an enactment** under the `Tang` two-limb test?
7. If Queensland and `s 4(a)` fails, consider `s 4(b)`.
8. Consider exclusions, non-justiciability, Governor/Cabinet issues, and tendering/contract limitations.
9. If statutory review fails, consider fallback review via `s 39B`, declarations, injunctions, prohibition, mandamus, certiorari, or `JR Act` Part 5.
10. Only after jurisdiction is established, turn to the substantive grounds of review.

## Short-answer points for the workshop questions

### What policy factors explain `Bond`?
- Access to convenient review for aggrieved persons.
- But also protection of efficient administration.
- The High Court preferred a line that avoids fragmenting ongoing decision-making by challenges to every intermediate ruling.

### How do courts distinguish legislative, administrative, and judicial acts?
- Legislative acts make or alter general rules.
- Judicial acts conclusively determine disputes by applying law.
- Administrative acts administer a statutory regime, often by applying law or policy to particular facts.

### What is the `Tang` test?
- The enactment must require or authorise the decision.
- The decision must derive from the enactment the capacity to affect legal rights or obligations.

### Why is a contract decision not automatically made under an enactment?
- Because a statute may only provide a capacity to contract.
- The legal force of the contract generally comes from ordinary contract law, not the statute itself.

## Bottom line
Most jurisdiction problems in this area can be reduced to four questions:
1. Is there a reviewable decision, conduct, or failure to decide?
2. Is it administrative?
3. Is it under an enactment, or in Queensland under `s 4(b)`?
4. If not, is there another review pathway outside the statutory scheme?
