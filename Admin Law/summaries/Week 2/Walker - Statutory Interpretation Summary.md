[[Walker, The role of statutory interpretation in administrative law (2023) 109 AIAL Forum 33]]

# Thesis
Walker argues that modern Australian administrative law is shaped by a "statutory turn": judicial review is now chiefly explained as enforcing statutory limits on executive power, even where those limits are implied using common law techniques of interpretation.

# Key Arguments
- Judicial review of statutory power has moved away from a purely common law explanation toward Brennan J's view in `Kioa v West` that review is concerned with enforcing legislative limits on power.
- Even so, the common law still matters because courts use common law interpretive techniques, together with interpretation statutes, to identify the text, context and purpose of legislation.
- Many major grounds of review are now framed as implied statutory limits rather than free-standing common law doctrines. Walker uses four examples:
  - `Anthony Hordern`: a specific statutory power with conditions may confine a broader general power covering the same subject matter.
  - Reasonableness or rationality: Parliament is presumed not to authorise unreasonable exercises of power, with `Li` showing a less rigid approach than old `Wednesbury` language and opening space for proportionality reasoning.
  - Procedural fairness: where power affects rights, interests or duties, Parliament is presumed to intend observance of natural justice unless displaced.
  - Materiality: an error is jurisdictional only if it could realistically have made a difference to the decision; Walker discusses `SZMTA`, `MZAPC` and `Nathanson`.
- Review of non-statutory executive action is harder because there is no detailed statute to construe, but legality still turns on identifying legal limits from constitutional structure, common law, the nature of the power, and any statute that regulates or displaces residual executive power.
- `Davis` shows that non-statutory executive instructions cannot be used to bypass statutory limits. Where statute gives a power personally to the Minister, departmental processes cannot effectively exercise that power for the Minister.

# Doctrinal Significance
- The article is a clear judicial statement that statutory interpretation is now central to Australian administrative law.
- It shows why judicial review can remain legitimate without collapsing into merits review: courts present themselves as enforcing Parliament's limits, not substituting their own views.
- It also rejects any simple statute/common law binary. Statute supplies the source and limits of power, but common law reasoning is still the method by which implied limits are identified and applied.
- Walker links this framework to rule-of-law values and public trust: legality review enhances confidence in government because it disciplines executive action through legally ascertainable limits.

# Exam-Use Takeaways
- If asked where grounds of judicial review come from, say many are best understood as implied statutory conditions on valid exercise of power, identified through statutory construction.
- For a statutory power question, structure the analysis around text, context and purpose, then ask whether Parliament is taken to have implied limits such as fairness, reasonableness, or materiality.
- Use `Anthony Hordern` where a decision-maker relies on a broad power to avoid conditions attached to a more specific power.
- Use `Li`, `SZMTA`, `MZAPC`, `Nathanson`, and `Davis` as modern illustrations of how interpretation shapes review doctrines.
- For non-statutory action, note that review is still possible, but the source and content of the legal limits are more contestable and may depend on constitutional structure, common law, and statutory displacement.
