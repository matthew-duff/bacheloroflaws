# Robin Creyke, "Administrative Justice - Towards Integrity in Government"

## Thesis
Creyke argues that "administrative justice" is a core objective of Australian administrative law but remains difficult to define. Instead of chasing a single abstract definition, she proposes assessing administrative justice through the institutions, values, and performance indicators that make up the administrative law system, using the National Integrity Systems Assessment (`NISA`) methodology as an evaluative model.

## Key Arguments
- Administrative justice sits within administrative law because it is delivered through administrative law institutions such as courts, tribunals, ombudsmen, reasons obligations, and information-access regimes.
- The central tension is between justice to the individual and the demands of administration, especially efficiency, consistency, policy implementation, and budget constraints.
- Australian materials do not yield a settled definition. Some accounts emphasise fairness and protection of individual interests; others emphasise balancing those interests against broader public administration needs.
- The better view is broad and systemic: administrative justice is not confined to one institution or one doctrine, but depends on the interaction of multiple review and accountability mechanisms.
- Measuring administrative justice requires benchmarks, not rhetoric. Broad ideals like fairness are too vague unless translated into concrete indicators such as accessibility, timeliness, affordability, rationality, intelligibility, independence, and accountability.
- Administrative justice also depends on culture. Formal institutions are insufficient unless agencies, tribunals, and courts internalise values of legality, fairness, and accountability in day-to-day decision-making.

## Integrity Framework
- Creyke links administrative justice to the "integrity branch" of government associated with Spigelman CJ and later McMillan.
- On this view, integrity means more than absence of corruption. It includes government institutions exercising power for proper purposes, according to law, and in accordance with accepted public values and procedural standards.
- That connection matters because administrative law institutions are part of the machinery that secures integrity in government.
- The `NISA` "bird's nest" metaphor is central: no single institution secures integrity or administrative justice on its own, but a network of mutually supporting institutions can.

## Strengths and Weaknesses Identified
### Strengths
- Australia has a comparatively rich administrative justice framework: judicial review, merits review, ombudsmen, reasons, `FOI`, privacy protections, internal review, and related accountability bodies.
- Ombudsmen are highlighted as especially effective because they are accessible, investigative, capable of identifying systemic defects, and able to secure practical corrections.
- Tribunals and internal review are valuable because they are cheaper and more accessible than courts, and can correct errors efficiently.
- Empirical studies cited by Creyke suggest a significant compliance culture within the executive, with agencies generally accepting the legitimacy of external review.

### Weaknesses
- Underfunding can distort the system by privileging efficiency over justice.
- Judicial review contains access barriers, including jurisdictional complexity and dual entry points between statutory and common law review.
- Some areas, especially `FOI` and privacy, remain incomplete or unsatisfactory from an administrative justice perspective.
- The system is not perfectly coherent: in practice, ombudsmen and tribunals may now matter more to citizens than courts, despite the original Kerr vision of an integrated package.

## Doctrinal Significance
- The article is important because it reframes administrative law as a system of institutions and values, not just a set of judicial review doctrines.
- It explains why administrative justice cannot be reduced to legality alone: lawful administration also requires fairness, accessibility, reasons, accountability, and effective review structures.
- It provides a useful bridge between doctrinal topics that are often studied separately, including judicial review, merits review, ombudsmen, `FOI`, privacy, and institutional design.
- It supports exam arguments that administrative law should be evaluated by whether it produces both lawful and practically just outcomes, while recognising legitimate administrative constraints.

## Exam Use
- Use this article when asked to explain what "administrative justice" means in Australian law: the concept is contested, but always involves balancing individual justice with administrative effectiveness.
- Use it to support a systemic answer: administrative justice is achieved through an interlocking set of institutions rather than by courts alone.
- Use the article to explain why ombudsmen, tribunals, reasons, and `FOI` matter doctrinally: they are part of the accountability framework that makes administrative justice real in practice.
- Use Creyke to argue for reform through benchmarks, resourcing, and institutional coherence rather than through abstract definitions alone.
- If an exam question asks whether Australian administrative law is effective, Creyke's position is broadly positive: the core is sound, but weaknesses remain at the margins.
