[[Creyke's article on integrity in government]]

# Reference
Robin Creyke, 'Administrative Justice – Towards Integrity in Government' (2007) 31 *Melbourne University Law Review* 705.

# Overview / Key Points
Professor Creyke examines the elusive concept of "administrative justice" and proposes a methodology for measuring its achievement by linking it to "integrity in government."

*   **Defining Administrative Justice**: The concept is a philosophy that the rights and interests of individuals should be properly safeguarded in administrative decision-making, tempered by the needs of public administration (efficiency/consistency).
*   **The "Integrity Branch"**: Creyke links administrative justice to the emerging "integrity branch" of government (a concept promoted by Spigelman CJ). Integrity involves observing "proper practice" and ensuring institutions exercise power for their intended public purposes.
*   **The "Bird's Nest" Model (NISA Study)**: Using the National Integrity Systems Assessment (NISA) methodology, Creyke views the administrative law system as a "bird's nest"—a collection of individually weak but collectively strong institutions (Ombudsman, AAT, Courts, FOI, etc.) that provide mutual accountability.
*   **Strengths of the Australian System**:
    *   **Ombudsman**: Highly effective at identifying systemic flaws and providing accessible, non-adversarial review.
    *   **AAT**: Has become a "premier" institution providing guidance on hundreds of statutes.
    *   **Compliance Culture**: Empirical studies show a high degree of executive acceptance of administrative law values and a culture of compliance with judicial review outcomes.
*   **Weaknesses**: Underfunding, jurisdictional traps in judicial review, and the "efficiency" element sometimes predominating over "justice."

# Significance
This article provides a systemic view of administrative law, moving beyond individual cases to look at how the entire "integrity system" functions. It offers a way to measure the "health" of administrative justice through institutional strengths, weaknesses, and coherence, rather than just legal doctrine.
