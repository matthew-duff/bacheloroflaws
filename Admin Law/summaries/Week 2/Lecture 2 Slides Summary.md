# Lecture 2 - Introduction to merits review and legality review

## Core distinction
- **Merits review** asks what the correct or preferable decision is.
- **Legality review** asks whether the decision-maker acted within legal limits.
- This distinction matters for remedies, forum, and the kinds of arguments available in an exam answer.

## Merits review
### Federal level: ART
- The `ART` replaced the `AAT` in 2024. Because many ART provisions mirror the old AAT Act, AAT case law often remains relevant.
- ART jurisdiction always depends on at least two enactments:
  - the `ART Act`; and
  - the specific enactment conferring review jurisdiction.
- The enabling Act is critical because it may expand, confine, or alter the tribunal's powers, including whether the tribunal may substitute a decision or only recommend.

### Nature and powers of merits review
- In `Shi v Migration Agents Registration Authority`, the tribunal's role is to step into the shoes of the original decision-maker and make the decision that ought to have been made on the merits.
- Merits review is directed to the **correct or preferable** decision.
- New facts may be relevant on review, but not always. The scope of review still depends on the statute.
- The ART's broad statutory objectives are important, but `Minister for Immigration and Citizenship v Li` indicates those objectives are facultative rather than jurisdiction-limiting.

### Government policy
- If legislation requires compliance with government policy, that policy is binding on the tribunal: `Williams v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection`.
- If policy is not made binding by legislation, the tribunal must weigh consistency against fairness in the individual case.
- `Drake` is the key authority:
  - `Drake v Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs` warns against excessive tribunal deference to policy.
  - `Re Drake (No 2)` accepts that cautious and sparing departures from ministerial policy may be appropriate, especially where Parliament has scrutinized the policy.

### Procedure, evidence, and fairness
- ART procedure is not purely adversarial. It is at least partly inquisitorial or hybrid.
- `Bushell v Repatriation Commission` states there is no strict onus of proof in tribunal merits review, although practical proof problems still arise.
- The ART is not bound by the rules of evidence, but `Pochi v Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs` requires logically probative material.
- Mere suspicion is not enough, except in special contexts such as national security.
- Practical onus issues still arise: `McDonald v Director-General of Social Security`; `Sun v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection`.

### Standing and appeals
- Standing to seek merits review depends on the applicable statutory scheme.
- ART decisions may be reviewed internally in some contexts, through the guidance and appeals panel in some cases, and on questions of law in the Federal Court.

## Queensland merits review
- Queensland does not maintain separation of powers as strictly as the Commonwealth.
- `QCAT` can exercise both judicial and administrative powers and has been held to be a court: `Owen v Menzies`.
- As with the ART, QCAT jurisdiction depends on:
  - the `QCAT Act`; and
  - the statute conferring review jurisdiction.
- The conferring Act must always be checked because it may alter the scope of review or available powers.

## Legality review
### Why courts do it
- The lecture identifies both private law and public law justifications for legality review.
- Private law concerns protection of individual rights and interests.
- Public law concerns the rule of law and ensuring public power stays within legal limits.
- Authorities mentioned include `Griffith University v Tang`, `Corporation of the City of Enfield v Development Assessment Commission`, and `Ainsworth v Criminal Justice Commission`.

### Which courts?
- Generally only superior courts undertake legality review.
- For Commonwealth decisions this usually means:
  - the High Court under `s 75(v)` of the Constitution; and
  - the Federal Court under `s 39B` of the `Judiciary Act 1903 (Cth)` and the `ADJR Act`.
- For State decisions this generally means State Supreme Courts.
- In Queensland, judicial review lies in the Supreme Court under the `Judicial Review Act 1991 (Qld)` and inherent supervisory jurisdiction.

### Grounds of review
- The `ADJR Act` largely codifies common law grounds of legality review.
- Slide 62 lists classic grounds, including:
  - denial of natural justice;
  - failure to observe required procedures;
  - lack of jurisdiction;
  - lack of statutory authorization;
  - error of law;
  - fraud;
  - no evidence;
  - irrelevant and relevant considerations;
  - improper purpose;
  - bad faith;
  - acting under dictation;
  - inflexible application of policy;
  - unreasonableness;
  - uncertainty; and
  - other abuse of power.
- The grounds overlap and are not always neatly separated.
- The lecture notes possible unifying labels such as Lord Diplock's `illegality`, `irrationality`, and `procedural impropriety`, and Kirby J's `lawfulness, fairness and reasonableness`.

## Statutory interpretation and legality
- Statutory interpretation is central to legality review because legal limits depend on what the statute properly authorizes or requires.
- `Project Blue Sky` is the key authority:
  - read the provision in light of the text, context, and purpose of the statute as a whole;
  - legal meaning is not always the same as literal meaning.
- Interpretation statutes and, where appropriate, extrinsic materials may assist, but extrinsic materials cannot displace clear statutory text: `Catlow v Accident Compensation Commission`.

## Principle of legality
- The principle of legality presumes Parliament does not intend to curtail fundamental common law rights and freedoms without clear language.
- Core authorities:
  - `Potter v Minahan`;
  - `Coco v The Queen`;
  - `R v Secretary of State for the Home Department; Ex parte Simms`.
- The lecture also flags limits and cautions, including concerns about overuse of the principle in `ICAC v Cunneen`.
- It also notes a possible link between the principle of legality and `s 48` of the `Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld)`, with reference to `Momcilovic v The Queen`.

## Non-statutory executive power
- The lecture distinguishes:
  - **prerogative powers**, which are unique to the Crown; and
  - **executive capacities**, which resemble capacities shared with other legal persons.
- There is no blanket exclusion of legality review for prerogative powers.
- Key authorities include:
  - `Council of Civil Service Unions v Minister for the Civil Service`;
  - `R (Miller) v The Prime Minister`;
  - `R v Toohey; Ex parte Northern Land Council`;
  - `Attorney-General (NSW) v Quin`.
- Some matters may still be non-justiciable where they involve high-level polycentric decisions: `Peko-Wallsend`.

## Subjective states of mind and broad discretions
- Review becomes harder where legislation turns on what the decision-maker `believes` or on broad standards such as the `national interest`.
- Harder does not mean impossible:
  - `Wu Shan Liang` shows subjective formulations can still be reviewed;
  - `Plaintiff S156/2013` notes that national interest questions are often political;
  - `Plaintiff M79/2012` shows review is difficult but not impossible even where broad discretion and subjective satisfaction are combined.
- The lecture also notes that a discretion may be drafted so broadly as to raise constitutional issues: `Plaintiff S157/2002`; compare `Bodruddaza`.

## Presumption of regularity
- Public officials are presumed to act lawfully unless the challenger proves otherwise.
- This presumption places the onus of proving illegality on the applicant.
- Authorities mentioned include `McLean Bros & Rigg Ltd v Grice`, `Wednesbury`, and `Plaintiff M64/2015`.

## Exam use
- Start by identifying whether the complaint is about:
  - the **correct or preferable decision**; or
  - the **lawfulness** of the decision-making process or outcome.
- If it is a merits problem, identify:
  - the tribunal;
  - the enabling Act;
  - whether the tribunal can substitute, vary, affirm, or only recommend.
- If it is a legality problem, identify:
  - the source of power;
  - the relevant court and review avenue;
  - the best ground or grounds of review;
  - the statutory interpretation issue that defines the legal limit.
- Use authorities selectively:
  - `Shi` for the nature of merits review;
  - `Drake` for tribunal use of policy;
  - `Bushell` and `Pochi` for procedure and evidence in tribunal review;
  - `Project Blue Sky` for interpretive method;
  - `Coco` for the principle of legality;
  - `Quin` as a reminder that courts do legality, not merits.
