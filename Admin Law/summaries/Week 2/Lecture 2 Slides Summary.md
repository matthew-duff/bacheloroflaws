This lecture explores the two primary mechanisms for challenging administrative decisions in Australia: **Merits Review** (Executive branch/Tribunals) and **Legality Review** (Judicial branch/Courts).

## 1. Merits Review (Tribunals)
Merits review involves "stepping into the shoes" of the original decision-maker to determine the **"correct or preferable"** decision (*Shi v MARA*).

### Federal Level: The Administrative Review Tribunal (ART)
- **Establishment (2024):** Replaced the AAT. Provisions are similar, so AAT case law remains relevant.
- **Jurisdiction:** Dependent on both the *ART Act* and the specific enactment conferring jurisdiction (400+ acts).
- **Objective:** To provide review that is fair, just, economical, informal, and quick (*ART Act* s 9; *Minister v Li*).
- **Nature of Proceedings:** 
    - Hybrid (adversarial/inquisitorial). 
    - No formal "onus of proof" (*Bushell v Repatriation Commission*), though practical issues of proof remain (*McDonald v DG of Social Security*).
    - Not bound by rules of evidence but must rely on "logically probative evidence" (*Pochi v Minister for Immigration*).
- **Government Policy:** 
    - Binding if the statute requires compliance (*Williams*).
    - If non-binding, the ART should generally follow it but may depart "cautiously" to ensure individual justice (*Drake No 2*).

### Queensland Level: QCAT
- **QCAT:** Exercises both judicial and administrative powers; held to be a "court" (*Owen v Menzies*).
- **Jurisdiction:** Broad (civil disputes, occupational regulation, etc.), dependent on the *QCAT Act* and enabling statutes.

## 2. Legality Review (Courts)
Legality review (Judicial Review) ensures the government acts within the boundaries of its legal power.

### Justification and Jurisdiction
- **Justification:** Private law (protecting rights) and public law (maintaining the rule of law) (*Griffith University v Tang*; *Enfield*).
- **Courts:** Generally limited to superior courts (HCA s 75(v); Federal Court s 39B/ADJR Act; State Supreme Courts).

### Grounds of Review
- **Codification:** ADJR Act ss 5 & 6 codify common law grounds (e.g., natural justice, irrelevant considerations, unreasonableness).
- **Unifying Principles:** Often grouped as "lawfulness, fairness, and reasonableness" (*Guo*; *Li*).
- **Statutory Interpretation:** Central to review. The goal is to find the "legal meaning" consistent with the language and purpose of the whole statute (*Project Blue Sky*).

### Key Legal Doctrines
- **Principle of Legality:** A presumption that Parliament does not intend to interfere with fundamental common law rights unless expressed in "unmistakable and unambiguous" language (*Coco v The Queen*; *Potter v Minahan*).
- **Subjective Discretions:** Provisions using "if the Minister believes" make review difficult but not impossible (*Wu Shan Liang*); broad "national interest" questions are often political (*Plaintiff S156/2013*).
- **Presumption of Regularity:** Public officials are presumed to act lawfully; the onus is on the applicant to prove illegality (*McLean Bros & Rigg v Grice*).

## 3. Non-Statutory Executive Power
- **Prerogative Powers:** Unique Crown powers (e.g., treaties, war).
- **Executive Capacities:** Powers shared with legal persons (e.g., contracting).
- **Reviewability:** No blanket exclusion for prerogative powers (*CCSU*; *Miller*), though high-level "polycentric" decisions may be non-justiciable (*Peko-Wallsend*).
