# Workshop 1 Guide Summary

## Core idea
Administrative law regulates the exercise of public power, mainly administrative and delegated legislative power exercised by the executive. Its central purposes are to promote effective administration, secure accountability, and allow unlawful or unjust government action to be challenged.

## What administrative law is for
- Promotes effective government administration.
- Holds public decision-makers accountable.
- Keeps government action open to challenge.
- Checks unlawful and unjust decisions, conduct, and inaction.

## Key values
- Legality
- Fairness
- Reasonableness / rationality
- Integrity
- Openness / transparency
- Efficiency
- Effectiveness

These values appear both in statutes and in common law principles, especially the grounds of judicial review.

## Public power and the executive
The course is mainly concerned with the relationship between the state and the individual, and with how public powers are supervised when exercised by executive government.

### Main categories of public power
- Legislative
- Executive / administrative
- Judicial

### Commonwealth executive power mentioned in the guide
- Power to execute and administer constitutional provisions.
- Power to execute and administer statutes.
- Prerogative powers.
- Nationhood power.
- Common law capacities, such as contracting and holding property.

Exam point: the "bread and butter" of administrative law is the administration of statutory power.

## Who exercises public power
- Ministers
- Departmental officials and delegates
- Statutory authorities
- Governors / Governor-General in some statutory contexts

The guide stresses that many statutory powers are delegated in practice, especially in high-volume fields such as migration, taxation, and social security.

## Accountability structure
Australia's administrative justice system is broader than courts alone.

### 1. Judicial review
- Performed by courts.
- Concerned with legality, not merits.
- Supports the rule of law by ensuring executive action conforms to law.

### 2. Merits review
- Performed by tribunals such as the Administrative Review Tribunal and QCAT.
- Concerned with the "correct or preferable" decision.
- Reconsiders facts, law, and discretion.

### 3. Integrity and oversight bodies
- Ombudsman offices
- Human rights / anti-discrimination bodies
- Anti-corruption watchdogs
- Information and privacy commissioners

### 4. Political and systemic accountability
- Responsible government
- Parliament and committees
- Auditor-General
- Media scrutiny
- Elections

Exam distinction: individual grievance mechanisms and systemic accountability mechanisms are related but not identical.

## Three foundational concepts

### Administrative justice
Concerned with ensuring administrative action respects substantive and procedural rights through accessible, transparent, timely, and efficient processes.

### Government accountability
Concerned with ensuring those who exercise public power can be called to account legally, financially, and politically.

### Good administration
Concerned with proper record-keeping, avoiding delay, and maintaining efficient and professional decision-making systems.

## Foundations of judicial review
The guide identifies three overlapping foundations:
- Common law
- Constitution
- Statute

Authorities to cite:
- `Constitution s 75(v)` as a constitutional foundation of judicial review against officers of the Commonwealth.
- `Administrative Decisions (Judicial Review) Act 1977 (Cth)` as the major Commonwealth statutory review framework.
- `Judicial Review Act 1991 (Qld)` as the relevant Queensland statutory review framework.

## Institutions and authorities worth naming
- High Court of Australia
- Federal Court
- Federal Circuit and Family Court
- State Supreme Courts
- Administrative Review Tribunal
- QCAT
- Ombudsman offices
- Australian Human Rights Commission
- Queensland Human Rights Commission

## Workshop question structure

### Part A: theory questions
Ask:
- What institution is reviewing the action?
- Is the issue legality, merits, maladministration, rights, corruption, privacy, or systemic failure?
- Is the problem about an individual's grievance or broader government accountability?
- What value is being protected: legality, fairness, rationality, openness, efficiency, or integrity?

### Part B: client advice questions
Your answer should identify:
- The decision or conduct being challenged.
- The decision-maker or public body involved.
- The most suitable external review or complaint avenue.
- Whether the body can conduct merits review, legality review, investigation, or informal resolution.
- The likely procedure, hearing style, and possible remedies.
- Why that avenue is appropriate for the client's objective.

## Compact problem-question checklist
- Identify the source of power: statute, Constitution, or non-statutory source.
- Identify the decision-maker: minister, delegate, official, or statutory authority.
- Classify the issue: legality, merits, maladministration, rights impact, or systemic accountability.
- Match the forum:
  - court for legality
  - tribunal for merits
  - Ombudsman / commission / watchdog for complaints, investigation, or integrity issues
- State the governing standards: legality, fairness, rationality, transparency, efficiency, accountability.
- State the available outcome: set aside, remit, remake, investigate, recommend, report, or otherwise provide redress.

## Scenario prompts from the guide

### Scenario 1: environmental permit challenge
Focus on:
- identifying the external oversight body for the permit decision
- whether the avenue is an appeal / merits review process or a legality challenge
- the nature of any hearing
- the remedies that might follow

### Scenario 2: prison detention complaint
Focus on:
- external complaint bodies rather than internal prison processes
- Ombudsman-style investigation and other independent oversight mechanisms
- practical redress for unfair conditions and mental health impacts

## Bottom line
For Week 2, remember the big picture: administrative law is about controlling public power through a network of courts, tribunals, and integrity bodies, with legality, fairness, rationality, openness, and accountability as the recurring standards.
