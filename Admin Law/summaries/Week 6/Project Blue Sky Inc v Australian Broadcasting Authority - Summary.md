# Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority

## Citation
*Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority* (1998) 194 CLR 355; [1998] HCA 28 (HCA).

## Facts
- The Australian Broadcasting Authority (`ABA`) made an Australian Content Standard under `s 122` of the `Broadcasting Services Act 1992 (Cth)`.
- Clause 9 required at least 50% of programming broadcast between 6 am and midnight to be Australian programs, rising to 55%.
- Several New Zealand companies challenged the standard, arguing it discriminated against New Zealand programs contrary to the Australia-New Zealand Closer Economic Relations Trade Agreement and its Trade in Services Protocol.
- The key statutory provisions were `s 122(4)`, which said standards must not be inconsistent with the Act, and `s 160(d)`, which required the ABA to perform its functions consistently with Australia's treaty obligations.

## Issues
1. Was a transmission quota a valid "standard" relating to the Australian content of programs under `s 122`?
2. Did the phrase "Australian content of programs" authorise a standard framed by reference to Australian provenance or participation?
3. Was cl 9 inconsistent with Australia's obligations under Arts 4 and 5 of the Protocol?
4. If cl 9 was made in breach of `s 160(d)`, did that breach make the standard invalid?

## Held
1. Yes. A transmission quota could be a standard relating to the Australian content of programs.
2. Yes. The phrase was broad and flexible enough to include matter reflecting Australian identity, character, and culture, including participation by Australians.
3. Yes. Clause 9 gave Australian programs more favourable market access and treatment than New Zealand programs, contrary to the Protocol.
4. No, in the majority's view. Breach of `s 160(d)` made the act unlawful, but not automatically invalid.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- The majority rejected any rigid reading of "Australian content." The phrase extended beyond narrow subject matter and could include Australian settings, issues, participants, creators, and producers.
- Reading `s 122` in context, the ABA's power had to be exercised consistently with `s 160(d)` and Australia's treaty obligations. Parliament had not given the ABA two inconsistent commands; rather, it required Australian-content standards to be framed lawfully within those limits.
- Clause 9 breached Arts 4 and 5 of the Protocol because it guaranteed Australian programs a protected share of broadcasting time while New Zealand programs had to compete for the remainder.
- The Court said the old mandatory-directory distinction distracts from the real question. The correct test is whether it was a purpose of the legislation that an act done in breach of the provision should be invalid.
- Legislative purpose is determined from the language of the provision, the scope and objects of the statute, and the practical consequences of invalidity.
- Applying that test, `s 160(d)` regulated how the ABA should perform already-conferred functions; it was not an essential condition upon the existence of power. Because invalidity would create major uncertainty and inconvenience across ABA functions, Parliament was not taken to intend every breach of `s 160(d)` to void the resulting act.
- Even so, a breach of `s 160(d)` remained unlawful. A person with sufficient interest could seek declaratory or injunctive relief.

## Significance
- Leading High Court authority on statutory invalidity in Australian administrative law.
- Replaced the older "mandatory vs directory" formula with a legislative-purpose inquiry.
- Frequently cited for the proposition that not every breach of statute invalidates administrative action.
- Also illustrates that courts construe statutory powers in light of the whole Act, including provisions requiring consistency with international obligations.

## Exam Use
- Cite `Project Blue Sky` when asked whether non-compliance with a statutory requirement makes an administrative act invalid.
- State the rule: ask whether Parliament intended breach of the provision to invalidate the act, assessed by text, subject matter, statutory purpose, and consequences.
- Use it to distinguish between:
  - provisions that condition the existence or scope of power; and
  - provisions that regulate the manner of exercising an existing power.
- Apply it in procedural ultra vires problems where a statute has been breached but invalidity is not obvious.
- Pair it with cases like `Tickner v Chapman` or `Plaintiff M70` to compare breaches that do invalidate decisions with breaches that only make conduct unlawful.
