# Bourke - *Women's Business: Sex, Secrets and the Hindmarsh Island Affair*

## Thesis
Bourke's central claim is that the Hindmarsh Island affair shows how administrative law can distort and delegitimise Aboriginal sacred knowledge when it insists that such knowledge be translated into the language of objective proof, procedural regularity, and public scrutiny. She argues that Ngarrindjeri women's claims were not simply rejected on neutral legal grounds; they were filtered through legal categories that privileged developers, experts, and procedurally framed objections over Indigenous women's privacy, authority, and cultural survival.

## Key Arguments

### 1. Administrative law's idea of objectivity is culturally loaded
- Bourke argues that Australian administrative law presents itself as neutral and universal, but in practice it privileges forms of knowledge that are rationalised, public, and easily verifiable.
- Aboriginal sacred knowledge, especially secret knowledge, is disadvantaged because it is oral, contextual, restricted by gender and status, and not easily reducible to evidentiary forms comfortable to courts and officials.
- This helps explain why heritage disputes become preoccupied with whether beliefs are "true" or "fabricated", rather than with the substantive harm that disclosure and desecration may cause.

### 2. Procedural fairness can operate as a weapon against heritage protection
- A major theme is that procedural fairness arguments were used to quash protection orders under the *Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Heritage Protection Act 1984* (Cth).
- Bourke treats *Chapman v Tickner* and related litigation as examples of Aboriginal interests being "trumped" by the legally legible claims of developers framed in the language of notice, hearing, and procedural defect.
- Her critique is not that procedural fairness is unimportant, but that in this context it was elevated above privacy, confidentiality, and religious/cultural protection.

### 3. The "public interest" framing exposes sacred knowledge to destructive scrutiny
- Bourke criticises the assumption that Aboriginal heritage protection automatically becomes a matter of "public interest" and therefore open to public examination.
- She argues that this framing ignores the colonial history of state control over Aboriginal life and wrongly treats intimate spiritual knowledge as public property.
- The article suggests that self-determination may sometimes require the law to protect secrecy rather than demand disclosure.

### 4. Hindmarsh Island reveals the intersection of race and gender
- Bourke uses intersectional feminist analysis to argue that the controversy cannot be understood solely as a race issue or solely as a women's issue.
- Aboriginal women were historically excluded from legal, political, bureaucratic, and anthropological processes, making their sacred knowledge especially vulnerable to disbelief.
- She contrasts the intense scrutiny of Ngarrindjeri women's claims with the relatively different treatment of comparable "men's business" claims, arguing that gender shaped both media fascination and legal response.

### 5. Credibility hierarchies determine whose account counts as truth
- Bourke argues that the case exposed hierarchies of credibility: Aboriginal women were treated as least believable, while developers, male dissenters, and experts were given greater authority.
- She draws on feminist and post-structural accounts of knowledge to show that official truth-finding is shaped by power, not merely by neutral fact-finding.
- On her account, the law's treatment of Ngarrindjeri women reflected racist and sexist assumptions about who can be a credible witness.

## Doctrinal Significance
- The article is a critique of the doctrinal environment surrounding judicial review under the *Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Heritage Protection Act 1984* (Cth), especially the dominance of procedural fairness reasoning in *Chapman v Tickner* and related cases.
- It is useful for understanding why heritage-protection litigation may formally turn on procedure while substantively reproducing unequal power relations.
- Bourke also highlights the tension between:
  - confidentiality and disclosure;
  - cultural/religious protection and procedural fairness;
  - substantive justice and legally recognised rights;
  - Indigenous knowledge and expert-mediated legal proof.
- The article therefore helps explain why black-letter administrative law can produce outcomes that appear procedurally orthodox yet substantively unjust.

## Exam Use Takeaways
- Use this article to add critical depth to a procedural fairness answer involving Aboriginal heritage claims or other contexts where disclosure itself may cause harm.
- Cite it to argue that apparently neutral administrative law standards can privilege certain forms of knowledge and disadvantage culturally distinct claimants.
- Pair it with *Chapman v Tickner* when explaining how statutory notice and hearing requirements can invalidate decisions even where the underlying purpose of the scheme is protective.
- Use it in problem questions to discuss why confidentiality, cultural difference, and the public/private divide matter when assessing what fairness requires.
- It is especially helpful if you want to show that "fair procedure" is not always substantively fair for all affected groups.

## Bottom Line
Bourke's article is best read as a sustained critique of the epistemological assumptions built into administrative law. For exam purposes, its main payoff is the argument that the Hindmarsh Island litigation was not just about defective procedure; it showed how legal procedure, public-interest discourse, and credibility standards can systematically marginalise Indigenous women's claims.
