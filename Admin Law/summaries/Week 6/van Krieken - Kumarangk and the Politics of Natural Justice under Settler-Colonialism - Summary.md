# van Krieken - *Kumarangk (Hindmarsh Island) and the Politics of Natural Justice under Settler-Colonialism*

## Thesis
van Krieken's central claim is that the Kumarangk/Hindmarsh Island litigation exposes a structural problem in administrative law: procedural fairness is presented as neutral and universal, but in practice it can privilege developers and other powerful actors over Aboriginal communities whose interests depend on protective state action. The article argues that natural justice doctrine often assumes government is a threat to rights, when in cases like Kumarangk the state was attempting to create or preserve Aboriginal protection against private development.

## Key Arguments

### 1. The article reframes Kumarangk as a problem of administrative law, not just cultural conflict
- van Krieken rejects the idea that the cases were mainly about whether Ngarrindjeri women's beliefs were "true".
- He argues that this question was close to legally irrelevant because the protection measures were set aside primarily for procedural reasons under the *Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Heritage Protection Act 1984* (Cth).
- The deeper issue is how judicial review structured participation, notice, disclosure, and ministerial consideration in a contest between Aboriginal heritage protection and development interests.

### 2. Liberal judicial review is better at restraining government than protecting state-created rights
- A core theme is that administrative law foregrounds state interference with private interests and backgrounds the fact that law also creates and protects rights.
- In Kumarangk, this meant procedural fairness principles were readily mobilised for developers whose commercial interests were impeded by ministerial protection of Aboriginal heritage.
- van Krieken argues that this "restraining government" model makes it harder for courts to recognise how judicial review can defeat fragile protective rights created for colonised peoples.

### 3. Abstract fairness principles can entrench unequal power
- The article draws on the idea that apparently neutral principles can produce political outcomes when detached from social context.
- Requiring fuller notice, disclosure, and opportunities to respond sounds fair in the abstract, but in this setting it advantaged parties with legal resources and developmental interests while placing Aboriginal sacred knowledge under pressure of exposure and scepticism.
- The article therefore treats procedural fairness as a site where settler-colonial power can be reproduced rather than merely checked.

### 4. Confidentiality is central, and full disclosure is not the only fair model
- van Krieken emphasises the tension between disclosure and the protection of restricted Aboriginal knowledge.
- He notes that common law and public interest immunity already recognise situations where confidentiality can outweigh ordinary disclosure expectations.
- On his reading, the law did have room to accommodate secret women's knowledge; the problem was not an unavoidable clash between Aboriginal belief and Australian law, but the way fairness doctrine was framed and deployed.

### 5. The jurisprudence contains a contradiction about participation
- Courts insisted on participation and informed submissions by affected non-Aboriginal parties, but this form of participation could itself damage Aboriginal interests the statute was designed to protect.
- The article argues that participation rights are not politically neutral because access to legal processes is socially uneven.
- As a result, procedural fairness can become the mechanism through which powerful actors defeat protective legislation.

### 6. "Whitefella law" describes law operating with a contracted view of social relations
- van Krieken does not say Australian law is inherently incapable of accommodating Aboriginal interests.
- Instead, he argues that law looks like "whitefella law" when it treats parties as formally equal while ignoring the background reality of settler-colonial inequality.
- The article contrasts an abstract universalism with a more context-sensitive approach that takes existing power relations seriously.

## Doctrinal Significance
- The article is most useful as a critique of procedural fairness doctrine in the heritage-protection context.
- It explains why *Chapman v Tickner* and *Norvill v Chapman* are not just notice-and-consideration cases, but examples of how ultra vires and natural justice reasoning can displace the protective purpose of a statute.
- It highlights the doctrinal tension between:
  - disclosure and confidentiality;
  - meaningful participation and cultural secrecy;
  - formal equality and substantive inequality;
  - restraining government and preserving rights created by government action.
- van Krieken also suggests that reforms like those proposed by Elizabeth Evatt, including clearer confidentiality rules, more time, and less adversarial procedures, are doctrinally significant ways to reduce the destructive effects of ordinary fairness doctrine in this setting.

## Exam Use Takeaways
- Use this article to argue that what fairness requires depends on statutory purpose and social context, not just abstract participation rights.
- Pair it with *Tickner v Chapman* or *Chapman v Tickner* to show that strict procedural compliance may be orthodox in doctrinal terms yet still undermine the protective object of the legislation.
- Cite it in essay questions about Indigenous heritage, confidentiality, or procedural fairness to show that judicial review can sometimes protect the already powerful rather than the vulnerable.
- Use it to complicate any simple claim that administrative law is politically neutral.
- It is especially helpful where a problem question involves confidential cultural material, competing claims to land, or a statute aimed at urgent protective intervention.

## Bottom Line
van Krieken's article is a sophisticated critique of the politics behind procedural fairness. For exam purposes, its main payoff is the argument that the Kumarangk cases demonstrate how natural justice can operate as a vehicle for settler-colonial power when courts apply universal fairness principles without sufficient regard to statutory purpose, confidentiality, and unequal social relations.
