# Pattenden v Federal Commissioner of Taxation

## Court and citation
- Federal Court of Australia, Logan J
- `[2008] FCA 1590; 175 FCR 1`

## Facts
- The ATO was auditing Mr Pattenden and considering large tax liabilities against him.
- An authorised ATO officer, Mr Benson, decided on 21 April 2008 that a departure prohibition order (`DPO`) should be made under `s 14S` of the `Taxation Administration Act 1953 (Cth)`.
- After that decision, other ATO officers inserted additional liability details and credits into the order and affixed an electronic signature of the Deputy Commissioner.
- Those later changes meant the issued DPO referred to a taxation liability that Mr Benson had not considered when he made his decision.
- Mr Pattenden challenged the DPO under `s 14V`.

## Issues
- Was the DPO invalid because substantive aspects of the decision were carried out by unauthorised subordinates?
- Did reasonable grounds exist for the belief that it was desirable to stop Mr Pattenden leaving Australia to protect tax recovery?

## Held
- The DPO was set aside.
- Main ground: the issued DPO did not reflect the decision actually made by the authorised officer.
- Alternative ground: there were no reasonable grounds for the requisite belief under `s 14S(1)(b)`.

## Key reasoning

### 1. Limits of authorised agency under the Carltona principle
- Logan J accepted that, in principle, an authorised officer may act in the delegate's name and may use subordinates for procedural or clerical steps.
- But that does **not** permit unauthorised subordinates to make substantive choices that belong to the authorised decision-maker.
- Here, inserting a different tax liability into the DPO was not merely clerical. It changed the substance of the order.
- Because Mr Benson never decided to make a DPO in respect of the liability ultimately stated in the issued order, the purported DPO was not his decision "in all key respects".
- The later affixing of the Deputy Commissioner's signature to that altered order was therefore also unauthorised.

## Principle on delegation
- A statute may permit internal administrative acting under the `Carltona` principle, especially in large departments.
- But the authorised repository of power must still make the substantive decision.
- Subordinates may handle consequential, ministerial, or clerical implementation only.
- If unauthorised officers determine a substantive element of the order, the decision is invalid for unlawful delegation/sub-delegation.

### 2. Reasonable grounds under `s 14S`
- The court treated the question as objective: were there facts sufficient to induce a reasonable person to hold the required belief?
- A DPO is not justified simply because a person owes tax and travels overseas.
- The statutory purpose is protection of revenue, meaning departure must threaten recovery of the debt.
- On the evidence, that standard was not met:
- Mr Pattenden had substantial assets in Australia and New Zealand.
- He had attended ATO interviews and Australian court proceedings rather than fleeing.
- His travel history and asset movements had innocent explanations.
- Recovery in New Zealand was legally available, weakening the claim that departure would impair enforcement.
- Accordingly, even if the delegation point failed, the DPO should still have been set aside.

## Ratio / exam takeaway
- `Pattenden` is authority that unlawful delegation occurs where an authorised officer does not personally make the substantive decision embodied in the final instrument.
- It is especially useful for distinguishing:
- lawful internal agency or clerical implementation; from
- unlawful sub-delegation of the actual evaluative decision.
- In an exam, pair `Pattenden` with `O'Reilly`:
- `O'Reilly` shows when departmental acting in a principal's name may be acceptable;
- `Pattenden` shows the boundary, where subordinates go beyond execution and effectively make part of the decision themselves.

## Short rule statement
- An authorised officer can rely on subordinates for implementation, but not for substantive alteration of the decision actually made. If the final order reflects the judgment of unauthorised officers rather than the authorised repository of power, it is invalid.
