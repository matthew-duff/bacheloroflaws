# Workshop 5 Learning Guide Summary

## Core frame
- This workshop covers "simple" forms of ultra vires concerned with whether power exists, who may exercise it, and whether statutory preconditions have been met.
- The source places these topics within Lord Diplock's `CCSU` classification of judicial review grounds:
  - illegality
  - irrationality
  - procedural impropriety
- The key method is statutory interpretation. In administrative law, courts ask:
  - who is the lawful repository of power?
  - for what purpose was the power conferred?
  - what considerations are express or implied?
  - does the statute displace natural justice?

## ADJR structure to cite
- `ADJR Act 1977 (Cth) ss 5(1)(b)-(d), 6(1)(b)-(d)` codify the main grounds covered here.
- `s 5(1)(b) / s 6(1)(b)`: procedures required by law were not observed.
- `s 5(1)(c) / s 6(1)(c)`: the person who purported to make the decision did not have jurisdiction.
- `s 5(1)(d) / s 6(1)(d)`: the decision was not authorised by the enactment.

## 1. Procedural ultra vires

### Rule
- A decision may be invalid where a statutory procedure is a condition precedent to the exercise of power and the procedure has not been lawfully followed.
- The modern question is not whether a requirement is "mandatory" or "directory".
- The controlling test from `Project Blue Sky` is:
  - was it a purpose of the legislation that an act done in breach of the provision should be invalid?

### `Project Blue Sky`
- Use `Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority` for the modern invalidity test.
- Ask:
  - what does the text say?
  - what are the statute's subject matter and objects?
  - what are the consequences of treating the act as void?
- Not every statutory breach invalidates the decision. Invalidity depends on legislative purpose.

### Planning-law illustration from the source
- The source uses a defective public-notification example involving the redevelopment of an old police station.
- Even if one street address in the notice is wrong, invalidity does not automatically follow.
- If the notice still gives enough information, such as the correct lot number and a recognisable site description, a court may conclude the statutory purpose of notification was still achieved.
- Exam point: identify the statutory purpose of the procedure before concluding that breach equals invalidity.

### `Tickner v Chapman`
- `Tickner v Chapman` is the leading workshop authority on procedural ultra vires.
- Under the `Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Heritage Protection Act 1985 (Cth)`, the notice and reporting steps were important preconditions to a valid ministerial declaration.
- The Full Court held the public notice was defective because it did not give sufficient notice of the matters to be dealt with in the report.
- The case shows that where a statutory notice procedure is designed to enable meaningful public participation, inadequate notice can invalidate the decision.
- The source also flags a further issue in `Tickner`: whether the Minister personally considered the relevant confidential material appended to the report.

## 2. Improper delegation

### Rule
- Power must be exercised by the official or body authorised by statute.
- A person cannot decide under a statute merely because they work within the same department.
- The issue is whether the final decision was truly that of the authorised repository of power.

### `O'Reilly` and the `Carltona` idea
- The source uses `O'Reilly` as the main illustration.
- In large administrative systems, practical necessity may allow authorised officers to act through properly authorised departmental agents.
- The reasoning draws on `Carltona Ltd v Commissioner of Works`.
- Exam point: internal departmental acting may be lawful where the statute, properly construed, allows it and where the authorised officer remains legally responsible.

### `Pattenden`
- Pair `Pattenden v Federal Commissioner of Taxation` with `O'Reilly`.
- `Pattenden` is useful for the limit of permissible internal acting.
- It shows that subordinates may assist with implementation, but they cannot make substantive changes that the authorised decision-maker never made.
- Use it to argue unlawful sub-delegation where the final instrument reflects another officer's judgment rather than the authorised officer's decision.

## 3. Unauthorised decisions

### Rule
- A decision is unauthorised where the decision-maker misconceives the scope or limits of the statutory power.
- This includes cases where the statute does not permit the kind of decision made, or where a required statutory state of mind was not lawfully formed.

### `Swan Hill v Bradbury`
- Use `Swan Hill v Bradbury` for the proposition that a power to regulate or restrain does not necessarily authorise prohibition.
- The case is a reminder that courts construe statutory language closely when deciding whether a decision falls within power.

### `Goldie v Commonwealth`
- `Goldie` illustrates unauthorised action where a statutory precondition is a required state of mind, here a reasonable suspicion.
- If a statute says an officer may act only when they "reasonably suspect", "reasonably believe", or are "reasonably satisfied", that state of mind must be supported by objectively justifiable material.
- The source stresses that the officer must make reasonable search and inquiry, especially where liberty is at stake.
- Ignoring contrary material can make detention or arrest unauthorised.

### `Love` and `Montgomery`
- The source links `Goldie` to later detention cases after `Love v Commonwealth`.
- If information suggests a person may be an Aboriginal Australian and therefore not an "alien", it may be unreasonable to suspect that the person is an unlawful non-citizen.
- `Montgomery v Minister for Immigration` is the workshop example of that application.

## 4. Jurisdictional facts

### Rule
- Jurisdictional facts are objective facts or conditions that must exist before power is enlivened.
- If the fact does not exist, the official lacks jurisdiction and the decision is invalid.
- These are an important exception to the general idea that ordinary factual questions are for the executive, not the court.

### `Plaintiff M70`
- `Plaintiff M70 v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship` is the key workshop case.
- The issue was whether the criteria in `s 198A(3)` of the `Migration Act 1958 (Cth)` were objective preconditions to the Minister's declaration of Malaysia as a receiving country.
- The source presents the majority approach as a relatively intense level of scrutiny because the statute was closely connected to protection obligations and human-rights concerns.
- Exam use:
  - argue that the statutory criteria were not merely matters of ministerial opinion
  - characterise them as jurisdictional facts that had to exist in reality
  - conclude the declaration was invalid because the criteria were not met on the facts

## Authority map
- `CCSU v Minister for the Civil Service`: taxonomy of JR grounds.
- `Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Guo`: lawfulness, fairness, and reasonableness as core review values.
- `Project Blue Sky`: legislative-purpose test for invalidity after breach of statute.
- `Tickner v Chapman`: defective statutory notice can invalidate a decision.
- `O'Reilly` and `Carltona`: when departmental agency may be lawful.
- `Pattenden`: limit on internal acting; substantive sub-delegation is invalid.
- `Swan Hill v Bradbury`: decision outside the true scope of statutory power.
- `Goldie`: required state of mind must be objectively supportable.
- `Love` and `Montgomery`: detention power constrained where Aboriginality undermines suspicion of alienage.
- `Plaintiff M70`: objective jurisdictional facts as preconditions to power.

## Compact problem-question checklist
1. Identify the exact statutory source of power.
2. Ask whether the challenge is really about procedure, repository of power, scope of power, or a jurisdictional fact.
3. For procedure:
   - what statutory step was required?
   - what purpose did that step serve?
   - applying `Project Blue Sky`, did Parliament intend breach to invalidate the act?
4. For delegation:
   - who was authorised by statute?
   - was the decision made by that person, or merely implemented by staff?
   - did subordinates make any substantive evaluative choice?
5. For unauthorised decisions:
   - did the statute authorise this kind of decision at all?
   - was a required belief, opinion, suspicion, or satisfaction actually and reasonably formed?
6. For jurisdictional facts:
   - is the statutory criterion an objective precondition to power?
   - did that fact exist in reality, not just in the decision-maker's view?
7. Finish with remedy:
   - if the condition was not met, argue jurisdictional error or invalidity and link back to the statutory ground under the `ADJR Act`.

## Bottom line
- The workshop's central theme is that judicial review of "simple" ultra vires is still driven by close statutory construction.
- In exams, anchor each argument in the statute first, then use the leading authority that best matches the error: `Project Blue Sky`, `Tickner`, `O'Reilly`, `Pattenden`, `Goldie`, or `Plaintiff M70`.
