# Plaintiff M70/2011 v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship; Plaintiff M106/2011 v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship

## Citation
*Plaintiff M70/2011 v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship; Plaintiff M106/2011 v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship* (2011) 244 CLR 144; [2011] HCA 32 (HCA).

## Facts
Australia and Malaysia entered into an arrangement under which Australia would transfer up to 800 asylum seekers who arrived irregularly by sea to Malaysia for refugee status assessment. On the same day, the Minister made a declaration under s 198A(3) of the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth) that Malaysia met the statutory criteria for receiving offshore entry persons.

M70 and M106, both Afghan citizens, then arrived at Christmas Island without visas and were detained as unlawful non-citizens. M70 was an adult. M106 was an unaccompanied minor. Departmental officers determined that M70 should be taken to Malaysia and that M106 could also be taken once support services were in place.

The critical background fact was that Malaysia was not a party to the Refugees Convention or Protocol, had not undertaken any legally binding obligation to Australia to provide Convention-level protections, and did not recognise refugee status in its domestic law.

## Issues
1. Whether s 198A was the only statutory source of power to take offshore entry persons to another country for refugee status determination.
2. Whether the criteria in s 198A(3)(a) were objective jurisdictional facts or merely matters for the Minister's practical assessment.
3. Whether those criteria required the receiving country to be under binding legal obligations, not just practical or political assurances, to provide effective procedures and protection.
4. Whether Malaysia validly satisfied the criteria in s 198A(3)(a).
5. Whether M106, as an unaccompanied minor, could be taken from Australia without the Minister's written consent under the *Immigration (Guardianship of Children) Act 1946* (Cth).

## Held
1. Yes. Section 198A was the only source of power to take asylum seekers to another country for the determination of refugee status.
2. Yes. The criteria in s 198A(3)(a) were objective jurisdictional facts that had to exist before the declaration power was enlivened.
3. Yes, in substance. The majority held that the statutory criteria required legally enforceable protection obligations under the receiving country's domestic law or international commitments, not merely non-binding assurances about what might occur in practice.
4. No. Malaysia did not satisfy those criteria, so the Minister's declaration was ultra vires and invalid.
5. No. M106 could not be taken from Australia without the Minister's written consent as guardian under s 6A of the *Immigration (Guardianship of Children) Act 1946* (Cth).

## Reasoning / Ratio
- The structure of the *Migration Act* showed that, for offshore entry persons, transfer to another country for status determination had to occur under s 198A, not under a broader, free-standing executive or removal power.
- The text of s 198A(3)(a) was directed to the existence of objective conditions in the receiving country. The section spoke in terms of what the country "provides", not what the Minister merely believes may happen in practice.
- Because fundamental rights and Australia's protection obligations were at stake, the Court treated the statutory preconditions as jurisdictional facts capable of judicial determination.
- For the majority, the required "protection" and "effective procedures" had to rest on binding legal obligations. A non-binding arrangement, or hopes that a country would treat asylum seekers appropriately, was insufficient.
- Malaysia failed the statutory test because it was not legally bound to provide Convention-level assessment and protection, and its domestic law did not secure refugee status or the required protections.
- As to M106, the Guardianship Act operated alongside the *Migration Act*. Taking a non-citizen child out of Australia required the Minister's written consent, which had not been given.

## Significance
- Leading High Court authority on jurisdictional facts in administrative law.
- Shows that where Parliament makes the exercise of power conditional on objective statutory preconditions, courts can determine for themselves whether those conditions actually existed.
- Important migration-law authority that non-binding executive arrangements cannot substitute for the legal protections required by statute.
- Also illustrates the overlap between migration powers and other statutory constraints, here the Minister's guardianship obligations toward non-citizen children.

## Exam Use
- Cite it when arguing that a statutory precondition is a jurisdictional fact reviewable by a court.
- Use it to distinguish legally enforceable criteria from mere practical satisfaction, political assurances, or factual predictions by the decision-maker.
- Apply it where legislation conditions power on the existence of protective legal frameworks, especially in migration or rights-sensitive contexts.
- Pair it with other jurisdictional error authorities when explaining how misconstruing statutory criteria can invalidate the exercise of power.
