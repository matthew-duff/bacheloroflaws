# Chapman v Luminis Pty Ltd (No 4)

## Citation
- *Chapman v Luminis Pty Ltd (No 4)* [2001] FCA 1106; (2001) 123 FCR 62.
- Federal Court of Australia, von Doussa J.

## Facts
- The case arose out of the Hindmarsh Island bridge dispute in South Australia.
- Developers associated with Binalong sought to build the bridge. Aboriginal objectors sought protection under `s 10` of the `Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Heritage Protection Act 1984 (Cth)` (`HPA`).
- The Minister appointed Professor Saunders to prepare the `s 10(4)` report. Part of the claimed Aboriginal tradition involved confidential "women's business", some of which was placed in sealed or secret material.
- The Minister made a `s 10` declaration preventing construction for 25 years.
- In earlier ADJR litigation, that declaration was set aside because the notice was defective and the Minister had not considered the representations in the way the Full Court later required.
- The present proceeding was not the judicial review challenge itself. Instead, the developers and related parties sued the Minister, the reporter, consultants and others in negligence, statutory duty, misfeasance, and misleading or deceptive conduct.

## Issues
- Was procedural fairness denied because the developers were not given the confidential details of the women's business claim?
- Did `s 10` require the Minister personally to read every representation before making the declaration?
- Did the invalid declaration expose the Minister or reporter to private law liability?

## Held
- All claims failed.
- Procedural fairness was afforded to the developers despite the non-disclosure of the confidential material.
- The Minister was not required to read each and every representation personally.
- The invalidity of the declaration in earlier public law proceedings did not establish negligence, misfeasance, breach of statutory duty, misleading conduct, or recoverable loss in this case.

## Key reasoning

### 1. Procedural fairness is flexible
- von Doussa J treated procedural fairness as a flexible obligation shaped by the circumstances, citing `Kioa v West`.
- Here, the confidential material concerned restricted Aboriginal women's knowledge. Full disclosure could itself conflict with the cultural claim being asserted.
- The decisive question was whether the developers had enough information about the substance of the claim to make an answer.
- The court held they did. They were told the general nature of the cultural objection, were given Professor Saunders' report, and made submissions in response.
- On that basis, the court held that procedural fairness had been afforded even though the secret material itself was not disclosed.

### 2. General disclosure can be enough where confidentiality matters
- The judgment is important because it rejects the idea that fairness always requires disclosure of all underlying material.
- Where highly confidential information is involved, fairness may be satisfied by giving affected parties the general nature of the issues and a meaningful opportunity to respond at that level of generality.
- The court accepted Professor Saunders' evidence that she disclosed as much as was reasonably possible without frustrating the statutory purpose or breaching the confidentiality attached to the tradition.

### 3. The Minister did not need to read every representation personally
- Although earlier judicial review authority required the Minister personally to consider the report and attached representations, von Doussa J held that `s 10(1)` did not mean the Minister had to read each and every one of more than 400 representations himself.
- The court distinguished between:
- a personal, non-delegable obligation to consider the material; and
- a literal requirement to read every individual document personally.
- The Minister had misunderstood the legal standard later articulated by the Full Court, but the court held his conduct was not reckless or wilfully blind for the purposes of the pleaded private law claims.

### 4. Invalid administrative action does not automatically create damages liability
- Even though the declaration had been quashed in earlier public law proceedings, that did not mean the Minister or reporter owed a private law duty of care to the developers.
- The powers exercised under `ss 9` and `10` were treated as public and political in character, making private law duties inappropriate.
- The court also rejected the broader civil claims and held that the applicants had not proved recoverable loss caused by the declaration.

## Significance
- `Chapman` is a useful authority on procedural fairness where confidentiality limits disclosure.
- It supports the proposition that fairness is context-sensitive and may be met by disclosure of the gist, rather than the full confidential detail, of adverse material.
- It is also useful to separate two ideas:
- public law invalidity of a decision; from
- private law liability for damages flowing from that invalidity.

## Exam takeaway
- Use `Chapman` when a decision-maker withholds confidential material but gives the affected person the general substance of the case against them.
- The case supports the argument that procedural fairness does not always require full disclosure if that would undermine the statutory scheme or the protected confidentiality itself.
- Pair it with `Kioa v West` for the flexible-content point, and with the earlier `Tickner v Chapman` litigation to show that the content of a statutory "consideration" obligation may still be enforced strictly on judicial review.

## Short rule statement
- Procedural fairness is flexible. Where confidential cultural material cannot fairly be disclosed in full, fairness may still be satisfied if affected parties are told the general nature of the claim and are given a real opportunity to respond.
