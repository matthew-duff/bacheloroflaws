# Tickner v Chapman

## Citation
*Tickner v Chapman* (1995) 57 FCR 451 (FCFCA).

## Facts
- The case arose from the Hindmarsh Island bridge controversy.
- An application was made under s 10 of the *Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Heritage Protection Act 1984* (Cth) seeking protection of Aboriginal areas around Goolwa and Hindmarsh Island from injury or desecration.
- After receiving Professor Saunders' report, the Minister made a 25-year declaration prohibiting acts connected with construction of the proposed bridge in the protected area.
- Opponents challenged both the report and the Minister's declaration.

## Issues
- Did the notice published under s 10(3) validly state the purpose of the application?
- Did the Minister satisfy s 10(1)(c) by "considering" the report and the attached representations?

## Held
- No. The notice was defective because it did not sufficiently identify the specified area to be protected and did not adequately identify the threatened harm and protection sought.
- No. The Minister had not fulfilled the statutory requirement to consider the attached representations.
- The Full Court dismissed the appeal and left in place the orders quashing the report and the Minister's declaration.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- Section 10 is structured so that interested persons have an effective opportunity to make informed representations. The notice requirement is part of that protective process, not a mere formality.
- Because s 10(1)(a) requires an application for preservation or protection of a "specified area", the notice stating the purpose of the application must identify that area with reasonable clarity. Vague generalities are insufficient.
- The notice must also communicate the nature of the apprehended injury or desecration, so that interested persons can understand what is in issue and provide useful responses.
- Under s 10(1)(c), the Minister must consider both the report and the attached representations. "Consider" requires an active intellectual process directed to the representations themselves.
- Staff assistance is permissible, but the Minister cannot satisfy the section merely by relying on a staff member's view that the report adequately reflects the representations.
- On the facts, the Minister's process was inadequate: the representations were not meaningfully considered by the Minister before the declaration was made.

## Significance
- This is a leading procedural ultra vires case.
- It shows that statutory notice requirements will be enforced strictly where they are designed to secure meaningful participation by affected or interested persons.
- It also illustrates that where Parliament requires ministerial consideration personally, the duty cannot be reduced to a superficial briefing or effective delegation.

## Exam Use
- Use this case where a statute requires public notice before a decision: argue that notice must contain enough detail to let affected persons make meaningful representations.
- Use it on procedural ultra vires questions involving non-compliance with statutory preconditions to power.
- Use it where a statute requires a Minister to "consider" submissions or representations: the decision-maker must personally engage with the substance, even if staff help organise or summarise material.
