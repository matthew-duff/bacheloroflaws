# Workshop 5: Judicial Review Grounds II – Procedural Ultra Vires and Simple Ultra Vires

## 1. Classifying Judicial Review Grounds
Lord Diplock in *CCSU v Minister for the Civil Service* (1985) classified JR grounds into three heads:
1. **Illegality**: Whether the decision-maker has the authority and correctly understands/applies the law.
2. **Irrationality**: "Wednesbury unreasonableness."
3. **Procedural Impropriety**: Breach of natural justice (procedural fairness) and failure to follow statutory procedural rules (**procedural ultra vires**).

## 2. Statutory Interpretation in Administrative Law
Administrative law is often an exercise in statutory interpretation to determine:
- Who is the **lawful repository** of power?
- What is the **purpose** of the power?
- What are the **relevant considerations** (express or implied)?
- Does the statute displace **natural justice**?

## 3. Simple Ultra Vires (Illegality)
Codified in **AD(JR)A 1977 (Cth) s 5(1)(b)-(d)** and **s 6(1)(b)-(d)**:
- **s 5(1)(b)**: Procedures required by law were not observed (**Procedural Ultra Vires**).
- **s 5(1)(c)**: Person who made the decision did not have jurisdiction (**Unlawful Delegation**).
- **s 5(1)(d)**: Decision was not authorized by the enactment (**Unauthorised Decision**).

---

## 4. Procedural Ultra Vires
Failure to follow statutory procedures (e.g., public notification in planning law).

### The Test for Validity: *Project Blue Sky* (1998)
The High Court replaced the old "mandatory vs. directory" distinction.
- **The Modern Test**: "Whether it was a **purpose of the legislation** that an act done in breach of the provision should be invalid."
- **Factors**: Language of the statute, subject matter, objects, and consequences of holding the act void.

### Case Study: *Tickner v Chapman* (1995)
- **Context**: The "Hindmarsh Island Bridge Affair" involving Ngarrindjeri heritage.
- **Issue**: Defective public notice under the *Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Heritage Protection Act 1984* (HPA).
- **Outcome**: The Minister's declaration was invalidated because the public notice was insufficient to allow for meaningful representations.
- **Key Principle**: Statutory notice must provide enough detail for interested parties to understand the "matters to be dealt with."

---

## 5. Unlawful Delegation of Power
Power must be exercised by the person nominated by Parliament.

### Agency vs. Delegation: *O’Reilly v State Bank of Victoria* (1983)
- **Carltona Principle**: In large government departments, it is a **practical necessity** that powers be exercised by authorised agents, even if not formally delegated.
- **Outcome**: The Commissioner of Taxation could act through agents because of the "myriad of cases" involved (millions of taxpayers).

---

## 6. Unauthorised Decisions & State of Mind
Decisions are unauthorised if a statutory precondition (like a specific state of mind) is not met.

### Reasonable Suspicion: *Goldie v Commonwealth* (2002)
- **Issue**: Detention under s 189 *Migration Act* requires the officer to "reasonably suspect" the person is an unlawful non-citizen.
- **Outcome**: Suspicion must be **justifiable upon objective examination** of relevant material, including reasonable search and inquiry.

### Aboriginality and "Non-Alien Non-Citizens": *Love v Commonwealth* (2020)
- **Rule**: Aboriginal Australians (meeting the *Mabo [No 2]* tripartite test) are not "aliens" under s 51(xix) of the Constitution.
- **Consequence**: They cannot be detained or deported under the *Migration Act*.
- **Application**: *Montgomery v Minister for Immigration* (2021) – If there is evidence of Aboriginality, an officer's suspicion that the person is an alien may not be "reasonable."

---

## 7. Jurisdictional Facts
Essential preconditions that must exist *in fact* before a power can be exercised.
- **Example**: A person must *actually* be an "alien" before deportation powers apply.
- **Scrutiny**: Courts apply an intense level of scrutiny to jurisdictional facts, especially when human rights are at stake.

### Case Study: *Plaintiff M70* (Malaysian Declaration Case) (2011)
- **Issue**: The Minister declared Malaysia a safe third country under s 198A *Migration Act*.
- **Jurisdictional Fact**: The criteria in s 198A(3) (e.g., providing access to effective procedures and protection) were held to be **jurisdictional facts** that must be met in reality (legally binding protections), not just as a matter of the Minister's opinion.
- **Outcome**: Declaration was invalid because Malaysia did not provide the legally binding protections required by the statute.
