# Workshop 10: Remedies

## Core point
Part 3 of the `JR Act` and the `ADJR Act` give simplified statutory review remedies, but they do not cover the whole field. Part 5 of the `JR Act`, the constitutional writs, and equitable remedies remain the safety net where statutory review is unavailable or too narrow.

## 1. Statutory remedies under Part 3 `JR Act` / `ADJR Act`
- `JR Act s 30` (`ADJR Act s 16`) lets the court quash or set aside a decision, remit it with directions, make declarations, and direct parties to do or stop doing something.
- These powers only arise if there is a reviewable decision, conduct, or failure to decide under Part 3.
- Relief is discretionary even after a ground of review is made out. Delay, misconduct, or trivial error can justify refusal.
- Courts should not use directions to force the decision-maker to reach a particular merits outcome on reconsideration.
- Damages are generally unavailable in administrative law review unless some separate cause of action exists.

### Authorities to cite
- `JR Act s 30`; `ADJR Act s 16`
- `Northern NSW FM Pty Ltd v Australian Broadcasting Tribunal`: remitter can include directions, including rehearing by a differently constituted body
- `Kamha v Australian Prudential Regulation Authority`: remedial discretion under the statutory scheme
- `Park Oh Ho v Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs`: declaration/direction powers should not be restricted by undue technicality
- `Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Conyngham`; `Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Guo`: caution against using directions to collapse legality into merits

## 2. Prerogative and constitutional writs
### What each writ does
- `Certiorari`: quashes a decision already made.
- `Prohibition`: stops a decision-maker from continuing or implementing an unlawful process before the function is complete.
- `Mandamus`: compels performance of a public duty where there is express or constructive refusal.

### Why they still matter
- `s 75(v)` of the Constitution entrenches `mandamus`, `prohibition`, and `injunction` against officers of the Commonwealth.
- In Queensland, the Supreme Court no longer issues the old writs directly, but Part 5 preserves equivalent relief "in the nature of" the writs.
- These remedies fill gaps left by Part 3 review, especially where the decision is not one to which the statutory review Acts apply.

### Availability rules to remember
- `Certiorari` and `prohibition` usually respond to jurisdictional error, including denial of procedural fairness.
- `Certiorari` also uniquely reaches non-jurisdictional error of law on the face of the record.
- `Prohibition` fails if the body is truly `functus officio`, though the High Court has taken a generous view of when a process is still on foot.
- `Mandamus` needs a duty, not merely a power; a void purported exercise can amount to constructive refusal.
- At common law, standing is broader for `certiorari` and `prohibition` than for `mandamus`; in Queensland Part 5 standing is now governed by `JR Act s 44` (`interests adversely affected`).
- All prerogative-style remedies are discretionary and subject to time limits under `JR Act s 46`.

### Technical limits most likely to be examined
- `Electricity Commissioners` / Atkin formula: legal authority, rights affected, and historically a duty to act judicially.
- The "duty to act judicially" idea has been relaxed; modern focus is functional.
- For preliminary decisions, ask whether the later decision-maker must at least take the impugned step into account.
- For non-jurisdictional certiorari, the record is narrow: initiating process, pleadings, and formal order, not reasons or transcript unless incorporated.

### Authorities to cite
- `Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth`: `s 75(v)` as a basic rule-of-law protection
- `R v Cook; Ex parte Twigg`: certiorari may accompany constitutional relief
- `Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW`: constitutional significance of supervisory review and record discussion
- `Westpac Banking Corporation v Commissioner of Stamp Duties`: Part 5 preserves prerogative-style relief in statutory form
- `Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd`: significance of the Queensland Part 5 structure
- `R v Electricity Commissioners; Ex parte Electricity Joint Committee Co`: classic availability formula
- `Ainsworth v Criminal Justice Commission`; `Hot Holdings Pty Ltd v Creasy`: effect on rights and preliminary decisions
- `R v Hibble; Ex parte BHP`: generous approach to prohibition and `functus officio`
- `Public Service Association of South Australia v Industrial Relations Commission of South Australia`: jurisdiction may carry a duty to exercise it
- `Craig v South Australia`: jurisdictional error and narrow conception of the record

## 3. Declarations
### Why declarations matter
- Declarations are the most flexible public law remedy.
- They can be used where Part 3 review or the prerogative writs are unavailable, including legislative acts, some executive processes, and even some non-governmental bodies via inherent jurisdiction.
- They can also be used in a pre-emptive way to declare legal rights or statutory meaning.

### Source of power
- In Queensland: Part 5 of the `JR Act` plus the Supreme Court's inherent/general jurisdiction.
- Federally: `Judiciary Act s 31` and `Federal Court of Australia Act s 21`.

### Limits
- A declaration must resolve a real legal controversy.
- The applicant needs a real interest.
- Courts will not answer abstract or hypothetical questions or grant declarations with no foreseeable practical consequence.
- Availability of another remedy is relevant to discretion, but not a jurisdictional bar.
- For declarations under Part 5, `JR Act s 46` applies; inherent-jurisdiction declarations are not formally subject to the same limit, but delay still matters.

### Authorities to cite
- `Dyson v Attorney-General`: modern administrative law importance of declarations
- `Ainsworth v Criminal Justice Commission`: real controversy, real interest, no hypothetical questions
- `Forster v Jododex Australia Pty Ltd`: flexibility and utility of declaratory relief
- `Kwiksnax Mobile Industrial and General Caterers Pty Ltd v Logan City Council`: declarations can reach legislative action
- `Plaintiff M61/2010E v Commonwealth`: declaration used where mandamus/certiorari were unavailable
- `Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority`: declaration of unlawfulness without necessarily invalidating the act
- `Punton v Ministry of Pensions`; `Datafin`: caution around using declarations as a back-door appeal for non-jurisdictional legal error

## 4. Injunctions
### What injunctions do
- Injunctions restrain unlawful conduct or compel action.
- In public law they can prevent ultra vires administrative action, stop enforcement of invalid delegated legislation, and respond to jurisdictional error.
- They can also be used where the defendant is not a government decision-maker and the case must proceed through the court's inherent jurisdiction.

### Types
- `Interim`: urgent, often ex parte, very short-term relief until the other side can be heard.
- `Interlocutory`: preserves the status quo pending final hearing.
- `Permanent`: final relief after the plaintiff proves the substantive legal case.
- `Mandatory` injunctions are harder to get than prohibitory ones, especially at the interlocutory stage.

### Test for interlocutory relief
- The current Australian approach is `ABC v O'Neill`: a sufficient likelihood of success to justify preserving the status quo, plus balance of convenience.
- Public interest and effects on third parties can matter in the balance.
- Undertaking as to damages is normally required, but Queensland rules allow dispensation for good reason.

### Authorities to cite
- `ABC v O'Neill`: interlocutory test in Australia
- `Beecham Group Ltd v Bristol Laboratories Pty Ltd`: classical prima facie case and balance of convenience
- `American Cyanamid Co v Ethicon Ltd`: serious question formulation
- `Castlemaine Tooheys Ltd v South Australia`: public law framing
- `Patrick Stevedores Operations No 2 Pty Ltd v MUA`: public interest and interlocutory relief
- `Worsley Timber Pty Ltd v Shire of Mullewa`: strict disclosure duty on ex parte applications
- `Central Qld Speleological Society Inc v Central Queensland Cement Pty Ltd`: public interest and undertaking issues
- `Shepherd Homes Ltd v Sandham`; `Active Leisure (Sports) Pty Ltd v Sportsman's Australia Ltd`: stronger case needed for mandatory interlocutory injunctions

## Problem-question checklist
### Remedy selection
- Is Part 3 review available first, or is this a Part 5 / writ / declaration / injunction problem?
- Is the complaint about a completed decision, an ongoing process, or a failure to decide?
- Is the objective to quash, stop, compel, declare rights, or preserve the status quo?

### Writs
- `Certiorari`: has a decision already been made, and is there jurisdictional error or error of law on the face of the record?
- `Prohibition`: is the process still alive, and does the impugned step have a sufficient legal effect?
- `Mandamus`: is there a public duty to decide or act, rather than a bare discretion or a merely private obligation?

### Declarations
- Is there a real controversy now, not a hypothetical or advisory question?
- Will the declaration have practical utility?
- Is a declaration being used because other remedies are unavailable, or improperly as a substitute appeal?

### Injunctions
- Do you need urgent interim relief or ordinary interlocutory preservation?
- Can you show a sufficient likelihood of success plus favourable balance of convenience?
- Are public interest effects, third-party impacts, and undertaking as to damages important?

## Fast answers to the workshop questions
- `Part 3` gives the simplified statutory review path; `Part 5` preserves prerogative-style orders plus declarations/injunctions as a safety net.
- The `ADJR Act` has no direct equivalent to Queensland `Part 5`.
- For a committee report or recommendation, ask whether it has enough legal effect for `certiorari`, and whether `prohibition` can stop later reliance on it.
- For mandamus, distinguish a duty to decide from a mere power, and public duties from contractual obligations.
- Appeal is usually preferable to `certiorari` because appeal is the ordinary mechanism for correcting non-jurisdictional legal error and is not confined by the narrow "record" limits.
- A hypothetical request for a declaration about a power not yet exercised will usually fail for want of a real controversy.
- If `Schedule 1` excludes Part 3 review, shift immediately to Part 5 remedies, declarations, injunctions, or constitutional relief where available.
