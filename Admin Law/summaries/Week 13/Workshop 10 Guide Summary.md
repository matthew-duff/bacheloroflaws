# Workshop 10: Remedies

## Overview
This guide examines the judicial review remedies available in administrative law, covering statutory orders under the *Judicial Review Act 1991* (Qld) (JR Act) and the *Administrative Decisions (Judicial Review) Act 1977* (Cth) (ADJR Act), the prerogative/constitutional writs, and equitable remedies (declarations and injunctions). It explores the legal effect, jurisdictional basis, and availability of each remedy.

## Key Points

### 1. Statutory Remedies (JR Act s 30 / ADJR Act s 16)
- **Powers**: Courts can quash/set aside decisions (s 30(1)(a)), refer matters back with directions (s 30(1)(b)), make declarations (s 30(1)(c)), and direct parties to act/refrain from acting (s 30(1)(d)).
- **Discretionary**: Relief is not automatic; courts may refuse orders based on delay, misconduct, or triviality of error.
- **Damages**: Generally not available in administrative law proceedings unless a separate cause of action (e.g., tort/contract) exists.

### 2. Prerogative and Constitutional Writs
- **Certiorari**: Quashes an invalid decision already made. Available for jurisdictional error and non-jurisdictional error of law on the face of the record.
- **Prohibition**: Prevents a decision-maker from proceeding or implementing a decision. Only available before the body is *functus officio*.
- **Mandamus**: Commands the performance of a public duty (express or constructive refusal).
- **Constitutional Basis**: s 75(v) of the Constitution enshrines mandamus, prohibition, and injunctions against "officers of the Commonwealth," securing the rule of law.
- **Queensland Context**: Under JR Act s 41, the Supreme Court no longer issues the writs themselves but grants "prerogative orders" with the same effect (Part 5).

### 3. Equitable Remedies
- **Declarations**: A formal statement of the legal position. Highly flexible and "dynamic"; available against legislative acts, Governor in Council decisions, and non-governmental bodies (via inherent jurisdiction).
    - *Limit*: Must involve a "real" legal controversy, not abstract or hypothetical questions (*Ainsworth v CJC*).
- **Injunctions**: Court orders to do (mandatory) or refrain from (prohibitory) an act.
    - **Interim/Interlocutory**: Used to maintain the status quo pending final hearing.
    - **Test (*ABC v O'Neill*)**: Requires a "prima facie case" (sufficient likelihood of success) and that the "balance of convenience" favours the injunction.

### 4. Technical Limitations
- **The Record**: For non-jurisdictional certiorari, the "record" is narrowly defined (*Craig v South Australia*) to exclude reasons and transcripts unless expressly incorporated.
- **Standing**: Generally requires the applicant to be a "person aggrieved" or have "interests adversely affected" (JR Act s 44).

## Significance
Remedies are the "lynch pins" of civil rights, ensuring government accountability. While statutory review (Part 3 JR Act) is most common, the retention of prerogative-style orders (Part 5) and equitable remedies provides a vital "safety net" for cases where statutory review is unavailable (e.g., decisions not made "under an enactment" or legislative acts). The constitutional entrenchment of these remedies in s 75(v) prevents Parliament from entirely ousting judicial review.
