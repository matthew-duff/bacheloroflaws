# Week 13 Overview Summary

## Overview
This overview covers the final topics of administrative law remedies, focusing on the transition from statutory remedies to common law and equitable relief. It bridges the theoretical grounds of review with the practical outcomes available to applicants in both federal (ADJR Act) and Queensland (JR Act) jurisdictions.

## Key points
- **Statutory Remedies**: Found in ADJR Act s 16 and JR Act Part 3. These codify the grounds and provide flexible powers to quash decisions, refer matters back with directions, and declare rights.
- **Common Law (Prerogative) Writs**:
    - **Certiorari**: Quashes decisions for jurisdictional error or non-jurisdictional error of law on the face of the record.
    - **Prohibition**: Prevents a decision-maker from proceeding where there is jurisdictional error.
    - **Mandamus**: Commands the performance of a public duty.
    - In Queensland, these are incorporated into **Part 5 of the JR Act** as "orders in the nature of" the writs.
- **Equitable Remedies**:
    - **Declarations**: A flexible statement of the legal position. Highly effective in public law as public entities generally comply without the need for coercive orders.
    - **Injunctions**: Coercive orders (prohibitory or mandatory). Interlocutory injunctions require a *prima facie* case and a balance of convenience (*ABC v O'Neill*).
- **Key Case Principles**:
    - *Ainsworth v CJC*: Certiorari cannot quash a report with no legal effect, but a declaration may still be available.
    - *Craig v South Australia*: Defines the "record" narrowly for inferior courts regarding certiorari.
    - *Project Blue Sky*: Distinction between mandatory/directory requirements and the validity of acts done in breach.

## Significance
Understanding remedies is crucial because the availability of a remedy often dictates the strategy of a judicial review application. While statutory remedies are the primary path, the inherent jurisdiction of superior courts to grant declarations and the specific requirements for prerogative relief (like the "record" for certiorari) remain vital for cases falling outside statutory schemes or requiring flexible declaratory relief.
