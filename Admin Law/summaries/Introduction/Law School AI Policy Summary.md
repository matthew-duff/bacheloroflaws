[[Law School - Use of AI by Students in Law (20Feb26)]]

Reference: TC Beirne School of Law, *The Use of Generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) By Students in Law* (20 February 2026).

## Overview
This policy outlines the TC Beirne School of Law's expectations and professional standards regarding the use of Generative AI (GenAI) by law students. It emphasizes that while GenAI is a tool in modern legal practice, it is not a substitute for rigorous legal reasoning, research, and ethical judgment. The policy aims to guide students in making informed, ethical decisions about AI use while defining the boundaries between permitted support and academic misconduct.

## Key points
- **Permitted Use**: Unless prohibited by a specific task, GenAI may be used for:
	- Clarifying instructions or criteria.
	- Planning and structuring work.
	- Improving grammar and expression of original drafts.
	- Identifying research issues or generating prompts.
	- Explaining general legal concepts (subject to independent verification).
- **Non-Negotiable Rules**:
	- **Independent Verification**: Students must verify all legal authorities (cases, legislation, quotes).
	- **Authorship**: All analysis, arguments, and conclusions must reflect the student's own reasoning.
	- **Transparency**: AI use must be disclosed and referenced where required.
- **Academic Misconduct**: Prohibited actions include submitting AI-generated substantive analysis as one's own, using AI to fabricate sources, or using AI to evade detection of copied material.
- **Poor Academic Practice**: Over-reliance on AI, superficial analysis, or failure to verify outputs may not be misconduct but will result in lower marks and reflect poorly on professional standards.

## Significance
This policy is significant for Administrative Law and legal education as it aligns academic standards with evolving professional expectations. It reinforces the principle of personal accountability—a cornerstone of legal practice—by holding students responsible for the accuracy and integrity of their work, regardless of the tools used. It explicitly warns that reliance on unverified AI content undermines the administration of justice and may be regarded as professional misconduct in a legal career.
