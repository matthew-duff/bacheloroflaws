This lecture provides an introduction to the scope, values, and constitutional context of administrative law in Australia, specifically focusing on the accountability of bodies wielding public power.

### Key Concepts
- **Definition**: Administrative law is concerned with the legal accountability of bodies wielding public power and ensuring rights/interests are respected in dealings with government.
- **Values**: Openness, fairness, participation, accountability, consistency, rationality, accessibility, legality, and impartiality (Aronson, Groves and Weeks).
- **Constitutional Context**:
    - **Rule of Law**: Acts as an "assumption" of the Constitution (Dixon J in *Communist Party Case*). Judicial review is the enforcement of the rule of law over executive action (*Church of Scientology v Woodward*).
    - **Responsible Government**: Ministers are members of Parliament and accountable to it, though practical limitations exist (e.g., size of departments).
    - **Separation of Powers**: Strict separation at the Federal level between judicial and executive power (*Boilermakers' Case* logic). This distinction underpins the difference between merits and legality review.
- **Merits vs. Legality Review**:
    - **Merits Review**: Concerned with the "correct or preferable" decision. Conducted by tribunals (e.g., Administrative Review Tribunal - ART) within the Executive branch.
    - **Legality Review**: Concerned with whether a decision was made according to law. Conducted by Courts (High Court s 75(v), Federal Court via ADJR Act). Courts cannot substitute their own decision on the merits (*AG (NSW) v Quin*).
- **Justiciability**: Some decisions (e.g., high-level policy, international relations) are "non-justiciable" and not suitable for judicial review (*Peko-Wallsend*).

## Significance
This lecture establishes the foundational framework for the course, highlighting that administrative law is the primary mechanism for maintaining the balance between executive power and individual rights under the Australian Constitution. It introduces the critical distinction between merits and legality review which dictates which bodies (tribunals vs courts) can hear specific types of administrative disputes.
