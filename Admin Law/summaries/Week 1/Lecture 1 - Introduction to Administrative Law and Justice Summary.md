# Lecture 1 - Introduction to Administrative Law and Justice

## Core points
- Administrative law is about the accountability of bodies exercising public power and protecting people affected by government decision-making.
- The values emphasized are openness, fairness, participation, accountability, consistency, rationality, accessibility, legality, and impartiality.
- The lecture is introductory but doctrinally important because it sets up the constitutional foundations of judicial review.

## Federal administrative law structure
- **Legislative power**: exercised by Parliament and delegated legislators making regulations and by-laws.
- **Administrative power**: exercised by ministers, cabinet, departments, officers, and tribunals through policy-making and individual decision-making.
- **Judicial power**: exercised by federal courts.
- Review mechanisms differ by institution:
  - legislation may be challenged through constitutional and administrative law judicial review;
  - executive decisions may be reviewed internally, externally on the merits, or judicially for legality.

## Constitutional context
### Rule of law
- The rule of law is treated as an assumption of the Constitution.
- Judicial review enforces the rule of law by keeping executive action within statutory limits.
- Key authorities mentioned:
  - *Australian Communist Party v Commonwealth* for the constitutional importance of rule of law;
  - *Church of Scientology Inc v Woodward* for the proposition that judicial review enforces the rule of law over executive action;
  - *Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth* for the idea that `s 75(v)` secures a basic element of the rule of law;
  - *Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW* for the concern against "islands of power" immune from supervision.

### Responsible government
- Ministers sit in Parliament and are politically accountable for departments through questioning and committee scrutiny.
- In practice, ministerial responsibility is imperfect because departments are large and party discipline reduces scrutiny.
- The lecture contrasts political accountability with legal accountability: political controls matter, but they are not a complete substitute for judicial review.

### Separation of powers
- At the federal level, legislative, executive, and judicial powers are constitutionally distinguished.
- A central distinction is that legislation creates general rules, while executive power commonly applies those rules in particular cases.
- Strict federal separation of judicial and executive power helps explain why courts do legality review rather than merits review.

## Merits review v legality review
### Merits review
- Usually conducted by executive tribunals, especially the Administrative Review Tribunal.
- Focuses on the "correct or preferable" decision.
- The reviewer may substitute a new decision without first identifying legal error.
- Often involves policy and broad discretionary judgment.

### Legality review
- Conducted by the High Court, Federal Court, and Federal Circuit and Family Court.
- Focuses on whether the decision-maker acted according to law, not whether the decision was preferable.
- If unlawfulness is found, the usual response is to set aside and remit, not to substitute the court's own merits decision.
- *Attorney-General (NSW) v Quin* is the key authority in the lecture for the proposition that courts do not cure mere administrative injustice by re-making executive decisions.

### Federal legal bases mentioned
- `s 75(v)` of the Constitution entrenches High Court review against officers of the Commonwealth.
- The `ADJR Act` codifies important grounds of legality review, including natural justice, jurisdiction, legal error, no evidence, and improper exercise of power.

## State and Territory differences
- Federal and State/Territory systems are separate.
- Separation of powers constraints are less strict at State and Territory level.
- This helps explain why State bodies such as tribunals may combine functions in ways that would be more difficult federally.
- State Supreme Courts undertake legality review of State executive action, and *Kirk* confirms the entrenched importance of supervisory jurisdiction for jurisdictional error.

## Boundary and theory issues
- The lecture flags recurring boundary questions between public and private law, especially where power is exercised by statutory bodies, contractors, universities, or government business enterprises.
- It also introduces formalism versus functionalism and debates about whether some matters are non-justiciable because they are committed to political rather than legal accountability.

## Exam use
- Start any admin law problem by identifying:
  - the source of power;
  - who exercised it;
  - whether the complaint is about merits or legality;
  - which review body has jurisdiction.
- Use the constitutional frame:
  - rule of law explains why judicial review exists;
  - responsible government explains political accountability but also its limits;
  - separation of powers explains why federal courts do not conduct merits review.
- For federal questions, distinguish clearly between:
  - tribunal reconsideration of the correct or preferable decision; and
  - court supervision for legal error under `s 75(v)` and statutory review avenues such as the `ADJR Act`.
