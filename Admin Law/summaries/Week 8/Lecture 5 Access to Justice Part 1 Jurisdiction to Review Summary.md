# Lecture 5 Summary

## 1. Big picture: access to legality review

- This lecture is about **threshold jurisdiction**, not the substantive grounds of review.
- Before arguing procedural fairness, improper purpose, or unreasonableness, first ask whether the applicant can get into the **ADJR Act** or **Part 3 of the JR Act**.
- The statutory schemes were enacted to improve access to judicial review by:
  - simplifying procedure
  - codifying legality grounds
  - creating a right to reasons

## 2. Jurisdictional structure

### Commonwealth position

- The `ADJR Act` is concerned with **legality, not merits**.
- It is directed to review of **Commonwealth government power**.
- Review is undertaken in the **Federal Court** and the **Federal Circuit and Family Court**.
- Historically, the ADJR Act was intended to improve on the cumbersome prerogative writ system.
- The lecture notes that the Act was initially highly successful, but later litigants often pleaded **`Judiciary Act s 39B`** as well because it is not constrained by the ADJR Act's narrower gateway requirements and offers broader remedies.

### Queensland position

- The `JR Act` performs a similar reforming role for **State government power**.
- Part 3 deals with statutory judicial review; Part 5 preserves the historical remedies.
- Review is undertaken by the **Supreme Court of Queensland**.
- Queensland differs from the Commonwealth scheme in several important respects, especially:
  - `s 4(b)`: review of decisions under a **non-statutory scheme or program**
  - reviewability of decisions of the **Governor in Queensland**
  - `s 14`: power to dismiss **premature** applications
  - `ss 49-50`: **special costs provisions**

### Relationship with other remedies

- At the Commonwealth level, legality review can be sought through:
  - ADJR Act review
  - historical common law and equitable remedies
  - combined pleading of both
- In Queensland, the lecture identifies four possibilities:
  - Part 3 JR Act review
  - Part 5 JR Act review
  - combined Part 3 and Part 5 review
  - declarations and injunctions outside the JR Act

## 3. Core threshold question

- The key entry points are:
  - `ADJR Act ss 5, 6, 7`
  - `JR Act ss 20, 21, 22`
- The recurring threshold is whether there is a **"decision to which this Act applies"**.
- The lecture presents three linked elements:
  - **decision** or **conduct**
  - of an **administrative character**
  - made **under an enactment**, or in Queensland, under a **non-statutory scheme or program**

### Interrelation of the elements

- *Griffith University v Tang* warns against treating these elements as wholly separate.
- The better approach is to treat them as **interrelated parts of one statutory formula**.

## 4. "Decision" and "conduct"

### Main authority: `Australian Broadcasting Tribunal v Bond`

- *Bond* is the leading case on the difference between a reviewable **decision** and reviewable **conduct**.
- Mason CJ accepted the ADJR Act is a **remedial statute** and that reviewable decisions are not limited to final decisions.
- But the concept of decision still has a **limited field of operation**.

### Meaning of "decision"

- A reviewable decision is generally one:
  - provided for by or under statute
  - that is **final or operative and determinative**, at least in a practical sense
  - and is a **substantive determination**
- An intermediate conclusion reached as part of reasoning is **usually not reviewable** unless the statute specifically provides for that conclusion as a distinct ruling or finding.

### Meaning of "conduct"

- "Conduct" means **action taken**, rather than a decision made.
- It is generally **procedural rather than substantive**.
- So procedural steps taken for the purpose of making a decision may be reviewable as conduct even when substantive intermediate reasoning steps are not.

### Why `Bond` matters

- The case balances two competing concerns:
  - allowing redress for unlawful administrative action
  - avoiding fragmentation and inefficiency in government decision-making
- The lecture also notes that the interpretation of "decision" affects the **right to reasons**, because every reviewable decision may potentially attract an obligation to give written reasons.

### Queensland modification

- The lecture flags that `Bond` must be applied **in a modified manner in Queensland** because `JR Act s 4(b)` extends review to decisions under a **non-statutory scheme or program**.
- Queensland also addresses premature challenges through **`JR Act s 14`**.

## 5. "Administrative character"

### Core distinction

- The act must be administrative, not legislative or judicial.

### `Hamblin v Duffy`

- Lockhart J explained:
  - **legislative** acts formulate new rules of law of general application
  - **judicial** acts determine disputes by applying established rules and principles
  - **administrative** acts include the application of policy or legal rules to particular cases and the making of individual decisions

### `RG Capital Radio`

- This case illustrates how courts characterise the impugned act.
- Features tending to indicate a **legislative** act include:
  - determining the content of a general rule rather than applying a rule to facts
  - parliamentary control
  - wide public consultation and broad policy considerations
  - absence of executive variation/control and absence of merits review

### Exam point

- Always characterise the **nature of the impugned act**, not just the identity of the decision-maker.
- Ask whether the act creates a general rule or instead applies law or policy to a particular person or situation.

## 6. "Under an enactment"

### Leading authority: `Griffith University v Tang`

- `Tang` is the leading High Court authority on when a decision is made **under an enactment**.
- The lecture highlights two closely related formulations:
  - Gleeson CJ asked whether the **legal force or effect** of the decision derives from an enactment rather than the general law.
  - Gummow, Callinan and Heydon JJ asked whether the decision derives from the enactment the **capacity to affect legal rights or obligations**.

### Working test

- A strong exam formulation is:
  1. The decision must be expressly or impliedly authorised by the enactment.
  2. The decision must derive from the enactment the capacity to affect legal rights or obligations.

### `Fuller v Lawrence`

- The lecture notes `Fuller v Lawrence` as confirming the continuing relevance of `Bond` and the close relationship between the different approaches in `Tang`.
- The key emphasis is that the second limb is satisfied where the enactment gives the decision capacity to affect **rights or obligations**.

### Exam point

- Do not stop after identifying statutory authority for the decision-maker to act.
- The harder question is often whether the particular decision has **legal force because of the statute**, rather than because of contract, consent, or the general law.

## 7. Queensland extension: non-statutory schemes or programs

### Why this matters

- `JR Act s 4(b)` extends review beyond decisions under an enactment.
- The lecture also points to `JR Act s 9`, which confirms that Part 3 grounds can apply to exercises of powers or functions under a **non-statutory scheme or program**.

### `Anghel v Minister for Transport (No 1)`

- Derrington J suggested:
  - a **scheme** carries a quality of singleness or structured coherence
  - a **program** suggests a repeated series of events, such as repeated grants or aid over time

### `Bituminous Products`

- Holmes J adopted a **narrow interpretation** of decisions under a non-statutory scheme or program.
- A scheme or program heavily required, structured, and governed by statute may not sensibly be described as **non-statutory**.

### Exam point

- In Queensland, ask:
  1. Is the matter truly non-statutory?
  2. Is there a coherent scheme or recurring program?
  3. Does the challenge fit within `s 4(b)` and `s 9`, or is ordinary `under an enactment` analysis the better route?

## 8. Authorities to cite fast

- `ABT v Bond`: distinction between reviewable decisions, unreviewable substantive reasoning steps, and reviewable procedural conduct.
- `Griffith University v Tang`: two-limb approach to whether a decision is under an enactment.
- `Hamblin v Duffy`: distinguishes administrative from legislative and judicial acts.
- `RG Capital Radio`: practical indicators for characterising an act as legislative or administrative.
- `Anghel`: meaning of scheme/program in `JR Act s 4(b)`.
- `Bituminous Products`: narrow approach to what counts as a non-statutory scheme or program.
- `Fuller v Lawrence`: recent High Court confirmation of the continuing significance of `Tang` and enactment-based effect on rights or obligations.

## 9. Exam use

### Short problem-question checklist

1. What review pathway is being invoked: `ADJR Act`, `JR Act Part 3`, `JR Act Part 5`, or supervisory/common law relief?
2. Is the impugned act a **decision**, **conduct**, or **failure to decide**?
3. If it is a "decision", is it a substantive operative determination, or only a step in reasoning under `Bond`?
4. Is the act of an **administrative character**, rather than legislative or judicial?
5. Does the decision derive its legal force from an **enactment** under `Tang`?
6. In Queensland, is there instead a decision under a **non-statutory scheme or program**?
7. Is the application premature, making `JR Act s 14` relevant?
8. If the statutory pathway fails, is some **alternative review remedy** still available?

### One-line takeaway

- For exam purposes, treat jurisdiction as a sequence: identify the review pathway, classify the impugned act, apply `Bond` and `Tang`, then check whether Queensland's wider statutory language or alternative remedies keep review available.
