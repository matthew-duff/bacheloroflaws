# Jurisdictional Error Without the Tears

## Core thesis
- Aronson says `jurisdictional error` is not a precise organising concept. It is mainly a **conclusory label** used once a court decides an error is serious enough to make an administrative act or decision **legally invalid**.
- The doctrine still matters because it signals a common legal consequence: **nullity**. A decision affected by jurisdictional error is usually treated as if it had no legal force from the start.
- The real analytical question is not whether an error can be given the label, but **why this particular error should attract invalidity in this statutory context**.

## Why the concept still matters
- **Remedies**: prohibition and mandamus generally require jurisdictional error; certiorari may also extend to non-jurisdictional error of law on the face of the record.
- **Privative clauses**: the concept explains why clauses protecting "decisions" often fail to protect decisions affected by jurisdictional error, because such acts are only purported decisions.
- **Collateral attack**: if an administrative act is a nullity, its validity can sometimes be challenged indirectly in later proceedings.
- **Remaking decisions**: if the first decision was invalid, the decision-maker may be able to decide the matter again because legally there was no effective first decision.
- **ADJR Act review**: some statutory grounds reflect or overlap with jurisdictional error, but some review rights also go beyond it.

## Exam takeaways
- Treat `jurisdictional error` as a **conclusion about invalidity**, not a self-defining test.
- The usual consequence is **nullity**, but nullity is not absolute: relief can still be refused for reasons such as standing, delay, or discretionary considerations.
- Whether breach of a statutory condition causes invalidity is ultimately a question of **statutory construction**.
- The High Court's entrenched jurisdiction under `s 75(v)` gives jurisdictional error special constitutional importance, especially against Commonwealth officers and in defeating privative clauses.

## Modern catalogue of jurisdictional error
Aronson's summary of `Craig v South Australia` identifies the main categories:

1. Mistakenly asserting or denying the existence of jurisdiction.
2. Misunderstanding the nature or limits of the decision-maker's functions or powers.
3. Acting outside the general area of authority by deciding forbidden matters or making forbidden orders.
4. Proceeding on an incorrect assumption about a jurisdictional fact or other objectively required precondition.
5. Ignoring a mandatory relevant consideration or relying on a prohibited irrelevant consideration where validity depends on compliance.
6. Misconstruing the statute in a way that distorts the function being performed or the extent of the available power.

Additional well-established categories drawn from `Anisminic` and confirmed in Australia include:
- **Bad faith**
- **Breach of natural justice / procedural fairness**

## Key authorities and why they matter
- `Craig v South Australia`: leading Australian catalogue of jurisdictional error; also draws a distinction between inferior courts and tribunals, leaving more room for non-jurisdictional error by inferior courts.
- `Anisminic Ltd v Foreign Compensation Commission`: foundational English authority showing that serious legal errors within an inquiry can still invalidate the decision; helps explain the expansion of jurisdictional error beyond its older narrow meaning.
- `Re Refugee Review Tribunal; Ex parte Aala`: confirms that breach of natural justice can amount to jurisdictional error for constitutional writ purposes; strong authority that procedural fairness can ground nullity.
- `Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth`: privative clauses cannot shield jurisdictional error from the High Court's constitutional review; a purported decision infected by jurisdictional error is not truly a decision under the Act.
- `Minister for Immigration and Multicultural Affairs v Bhardwaj`: if the original decision was invalid, a tribunal may lawfully remake it because there was no legally effective first decision.
- `Project Blue Sky`: reinforces that invalidity depends on statutory construction rather than empty labels like mandatory/directory; crucial for explaining when breach of a condition leads to nullity.
- `NAIS v Minister for Immigration and Multicultural and Indigenous Affairs`: illustrates how serious procedural unfairness, including delay undermining credibility assessment, can amount to jurisdictional error.

## High Court's special position
- Aronson stresses that the High Court's `s 75(v)` jurisdiction is not perfectly explained by ordinary common law ideas of excess of jurisdiction.
- Constitutional writs can control Commonwealth officers even where the issue is better described as breach of constitutional limits rather than classic excess of jurisdiction.
- There are hints in the cases that `jurisdictional error` may be construed more narrowly in the constitutional writ context, but Aronson treats this as doctrinally uncertain and potentially obscuring.

## Nullity, privative clauses, and context
- Aronson's central unifying idea is that **nullity** explains much of the doctrine's practical work.
- If an error produces nullity, courts can:
  - bypass many privative clauses;
  - permit collateral challenge;
  - treat the matter as still open for lawful redetermination.
- But nullity is not mechanically applied in every case. Courts may refuse relief, and Parliament can create review or appeal rights that operate even where the original act was invalid.

## Going beyond jurisdictional error
- Aronson notes that some remedies and statutory review grounds extend beyond jurisdictional error.
- In particular, **certiorari, injunctions, declarations, and parts of the ADJR Act** may reach some non-jurisdictional legal errors.
- This means modern judicial review is increasingly **context-sensitive**: not every successful challenge depends on showing jurisdictional error, and not every successful challenge requires treating the decision as void from inception.

## Bottom line for exams
- Best short definition: **jurisdictional error is a conclusory label for an error that usually renders the decision invalid**.
- Best analytical move: explain **what error occurred, why the statute makes it important, and why that should or should not lead to invalidity**.
- Best constitutional point: the doctrine is central because it preserves minimum judicial supervision under `s 75(v)` and limits the effectiveness of privative clauses.
