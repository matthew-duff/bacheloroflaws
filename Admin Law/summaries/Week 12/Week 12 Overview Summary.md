# Week 12 Overview Summary

## Overview
This week focuses on the intersection of administrative law with modern technology, specifically **Automated Decision-Making (ADM)** and **Artificial Intelligence (AI)**. It explores how traditional administrative law principles—such as legality, procedural fairness, and the right to reasons—apply to decisions made by algorithms rather than human officials. A significant case study is the **Robodebt** program, which serves as a cautionary tale of the legal and social risks associated with automated government power.

## Key points
- **Benefits and Risks of ADM**: While automation offers consistency and efficiency, it poses risks to administrative justice, including "black box" inscrutability and the potential for embedded bias in data.
- **Legality and Authorization**: ADM must be expressly authorized by legislation. The Robodebt program was found illegal because the "income averaging" method used was not authorized by the relevant social security statutes.
- **Natural Justice**: Automation challenges the **hearing rule** (opportunity to respond to adverse material) and the **bias rule** (if algorithms reflect historical biases).
- **The "Decision" Requirement**: The case of *Pintarich v Federal Commissioner of Taxation (2018)* highlights the legal complexity of what constitutes a "decision" when a computer automatically generates a letter without human mental processes.
- **Right to Reasons**: Programming inadequacies or "black box" complexity do not justify a failure to comply with statutory duties to provide reasons.
- **Academic Standards**: Students are expected to use AI ethically, ensuring all legal research and reasoning remain their own, with independent verification of all AI-generated citations.

## Significance
As government agencies increasingly adopt AI and ADM, understanding the legal constraints on these technologies is critical for administrative law practitioners. The shift from human to algorithmic decision-making requires a re-evaluation of how accountability is maintained. The Robodebt Royal Commission (2023) underscores that technological efficiency cannot override the fundamental requirement that public power be exercised lawfully, fairly, and rationally.
