# Mammoth Problem Questions Summary

## Core answer structure
- The Society should analyse the problem in sequence: `reasons -> Part 3 JR Act review -> Part 5 / declaration / injunction -> proceedings against Mammoth -> later challenge by Mammoth if fined`.
- The recurring threshold questions are: is there a `decision to which the Act applies`, is the Society a `person aggrieved` / sufficiently interested, is urgent interlocutory relief needed, and is this an exceptional case for civil enforcement of a criminal statute.

## Issues matrix

| Issue | What the Society must show | Likely authorities | Best exam direction |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| (a) Reasons | A reviewable decision under the `JR Act` and entitlement as a person able to seek review/reasons | `Osmond`; `Australian Institute of Marine and Power Engineers`; `RP Data`; `JR Act s 32` | Emphasise that reasons are statutory, not common law, and strategically important because they may expose a review ground |
| Relevance of `s 50` | Proceedings are brought to vindicate the statutory right to reasons | `JR Act s 50` | Mention special costs power for reasons litigation; it softens the ordinary rule that costs follow the event |
| (b) Part 3 review | A decision under an enactment, standing as a person aggrieved, and compliance with time limits | `JR Act Part 3`, especially `s 20`; time-limit note from `s 26`/written record logic in course materials | Do not assume success on the merits just because reasons disclose no irregularity; identify any review ground separately |
| Part 5 relief | Standing under `s 44`, a suitable public law remedy, and a real controversy warranting relief | `JR Act Part 5`; `ss 43-44`; remedies notes | Present Part 5 as the fallback/safety-net route if Part 3 is unavailable or too narrow |
| (c) Special costs for review | Proceedings must be judicial review proceedings and the order should be sought early | `JR Act s 49` | Strong point for the Society if litigation is genuinely public-interest and resources are limited |
| (d) Early dismissal | Government may argue no reviewable decision, no standing, or no reasonable basis | `JR Act s 48(1)(b)` | Flag as a real threshold risk before a full merits-style hearing |
| (e) Standing under Part 3 | Interests adversely affected in a real and practical way | `ACF`; `Onus`; `Argos`; `North Coast` line discussed in standing notes | Society has a better argument than a mere busybody because of its objects, prior submissions, local membership, and geographic connection to the affected habitat |
| (f) Suit against Mammoth | A basis for declaration/injunction or other proceedings restraining unlawful conduct under the Fauna legislation | `Onus`; `Bateman's Bay`; `Gouriet`; `ABC v O'Neill` | Possible, but exceptional because civil process would be used to restrain conduct that also attracts statutory penalties |
| Standing against Mammoth | `Special interest`, not merely ideological concern | `ACF`; `Onus`; `Bateman's Bay` | Standing analysis is formally distinct from Part 3 standing, though in practice the tests often converge |
| (g) Mammoth after a fine | Identify appeal or supervisory challenge based on legal error, not simply disagreement with facts | Part 5 / supervisory review principles | Mammoth's better path is to allege error of law or jurisdictional error; a mere complaint about the judge's factual conclusion is weak |

## Party-by-party planning points

### Society
- Start with `s 32` reasons because the reasons request is low-risk, may reveal review grounds, and supports later costs arguments under `s 50`.
- For Part 3, identify the operative government decision precisely: the decision to go ahead with the project, approve the project, or authorise commencement.
- Build standing cumulatively: environmental protection object, North Queensland membership, prior government-facing submissions, and direct connection to the affected wallaby habitat.
- If construction is underway, ask for urgent interim relief: stay/injunction logic turns on `prima facie case` and `balance of convenience`.
- If suing Mammoth directly, acknowledge the `Gouriet` / `Onus` warning that injunctions enforcing criminal law are exceptional.

### Government
- Attack the case at the threshold: no reviewable decision, no standing, no basis for interlocutory relief, and summary dismissal under `s 48(1)(b)`.
- Resist any attempt to convert broad political disagreement about the dam into an administratively reviewable legal error.
- Argue that prosecution under the Fauna legislation is the ordinary statutory enforcement pathway, making private civil restraint inappropriate unless the case is truly exceptional.

### Mammoth
- Oppose standing and interlocutory relief, especially if the Society cannot show a concrete legal interest or provide a satisfactory undertaking position.
- Emphasise the court's caution about using injunctions to police conduct already governed by a penal statute.
- If later fined, challenge only on orthodox appellate/public law grounds such as legal error, misconstruction of the offence, or jurisdictional error.

## Compact exam framework
1. Identify the exact decision or conduct being challenged.
2. Choose the pathway: `reasons`, `Part 3`, `Part 5`, `declaration`, `injunction`, or later appeal/challenge.
3. Deal with standing immediately.
4. State the threshold statutory hook.
5. Apply the likely ground or remedy.
6. Address interlocutory issues: urgency, `balance of convenience`, costs, summary dismissal.
7. Conclude separately for the Society, Government, and Mammoth.

## One-line takeaway
- The Society's strongest route is an early reasons request followed by a standing-focused JR analysis; its weakest point is any attempt to use civil proceedings to enforce the Fauna legislation directly against Mammoth without showing a genuinely special interest and exceptional circumstances.
