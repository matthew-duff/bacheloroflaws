# Workshop 9: Right to Reasons, Time Limits, and Exclusions

## Overview
This guide covers the statutory right to reasons under the *Administrative Decisions (Judicial Review) Act 1977* (Cth) ('ADJR Act') and the *Judicial Review Act 1991* (Qld) ('JR Act'). it also explores time limits for seeking review, the impact of privative (ouster) clauses, the concept of jurisdictional error, and the interlocutory powers of the court (including stays and costs).

## Key points

### The Right to Reasons
- **Common Law**: There is no general common law right to reasons for administrative decisions (*Osmond*).
- **Statutory Reform**: The ADJR Act (s 13) and JR Act (s 32) created a remedial right to written statements of reasons for decisions to which the Acts apply.
- **Entitlement**: Requires a "decision to which the Act applies" and that the applicant be a "person aggrieved" (entitled to seek review under s 20 JR Act / s 5 ADJR Act).
- **Content of Statement**: Must include findings on material questions of fact, reference to the evidence relied upon, and the reasons for the decision (s 3 JR Act; s 13 ADJR Act).
- **Exceptions**: Reasons can be refused for specific categories (e.g., Schedule 2 exclusions, confidential personal/business affairs, or public interest immunity via Attorney-General certificate).

### Jurisdictional Error and Privative Clauses
- **Jurisdictional Error**: A decision-maker acts outside their authority. It is a central concept for constitutional review (s 75(v)) and overcoming privative clauses.
- **Inferior Courts vs Tribunals**: *Craig v South Australia* establishes that jurisdictional error is narrower for inferior courts than for administrative tribunals.
- **Privative Clauses**: Statutory provisions that purport to exclude judicial review.
    - **Constitutional Limit**: Parliament cannot exclude review for jurisdictional error by the High Court (*Plaintiff S157*) or State Supreme Courts (*Kirk*).
    - **Invalidity**: A decision infected by jurisdictional error is a "nullity" (it never legally existed), which often allows courts to bypass privative clauses that only protect "decisions made under the Act".

### Procedural Matters
- **Time Limits**: Generally 28 days from receiving written notice of the decision or reasons (s 26 JR Act; s 11 ADJR Act). Courts have discretion to grant extensions based on the adequacy of explanation and prejudice to the respondent (*Hunter Valley Developments*).
- **Stays**: Applicants can seek to suspend the operation of a decision pending review (s 29 JR Act; s 15 ADJR Act). The test usually involves establishing a "serious question to be tried" and that the "balance of convenience" favours a stay.
- **Costs**: The JR Act (s 49) allows for unique "protective costs orders" or indemnities early in proceedings, departing from the usual rule that costs follow the event.

## Significance
This topic is critical because the right to reasons is the "third pillar" of administrative law reform, enabling transparency and allowing applicants to determine if a decision is worth challenging. The interplay between jurisdictional error and privative clauses represents the fundamental constitutional tension between legislative intent to limit review and the judiciary's role in maintaining the rule of law.
