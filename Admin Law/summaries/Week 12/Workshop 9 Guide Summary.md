# Workshop 9: Right to Reasons, Time Limits, Exclusions, and Court Powers

## Core point
This workshop is about access to and control of judicial review. It asks:
- when reasons can be compelled;
- when review must be brought;
- when legislation tries to exclude review; and
- what powers the court has before and during review proceedings.

## 1. Right to reasons under the ADJR Act and JR Act

### Common law starting point
- There is **no general common law right** to reasons for administrative decisions: `Osmond`.
- But failure to give reasons can still matter in particular contexts, and in some cases may amount to jurisdictional failure or failure to exercise jurisdiction: compare `Edwards v Giudice`, `Kennedy Miller`, and `Kirk`.

### Why the statutory right matters
The source emphasises four recurring points:
- The right is **remedial**, so courts avoid grudging or overly technical construction: `Australian Institute of Marine and Power Engineers`.
- Reasons let an aggrieved person understand the decision, assess whether to challenge it, and decide how best to challenge it: `Burns v ANU`, `Taveli`.
- Requiring reasons can improve administrative decision-making quality.
- The right must still be balanced against the need for efficient administration: `Ansett Transport Industries`.

### Who is entitled?
Under `JR Act s 32` and the ADJR equivalent (`s 13`), the applicant must show:
1. a **decision to which Part 4 applies**; and
2. that they are a person entitled to seek review under `JR Act s 20` / the ADJR Act.

For Queensland, that means the applicant must usually establish:
- a **decision** rather than mere conduct or failure to decide;
- of an **administrative character**;
- made under an **enactment** or within the `JR Act` extension for a relevant non-statutory scheme/program; and
- that they are **aggrieved**.

### What must the statement contain?
Under `JR Act s 34` and the definition of "reasons" in `s 3`, the statement must contain:
- findings on material questions of fact;
- reference to the evidence or other material supporting those findings; and
- the reasons for the decision itself.

High-yield authority:
- `Ansett v Wraith`: reasons should explain the relevant law, material findings, and reasoning process in clear language.
- `ARM Constructions`: do not merely dump every document; identify the evidence actually relied on.
- `Roesner`: formulaic reasons are disfavoured; the statement should reflect genuine individualised consideration.

### Time limits and enforcement for reasons
- If the applicant receives a written record of the decision, the request for reasons must be made within **28 days**: `JR Act s 33(4)(a)`.
- If no written record is given, the request must be made within a **reasonable time**: `s 33(4)(b)`.
- If the request is in time, the decision-maker has **28 days** to respond: `s 33(2)`.
- If delay is relied on as a basis for refusing reasons, notice of refusal must be given within **14 days** of the request: `s 33(5)`.
- If the decision-maker says the applicant is not entitled, or simply ignores the request, the applicant can seek relief under `s 38`.
- The decision-maker may instead seek a declaration under `s 39` that reasons are not required.
- If the statement given is inadequate, the applicant may seek a **further statement** under `s 40`.

### Main limits and exceptions
A right to reasons can be excluded or cut back by:
- `JR Act s 31` and **Schedule 2** exclusions;
- prior provision of an adequate statement of reasons;
- confidential personal or business information of someone else: `s 35`;
- information contrary to the public interest and certified by the Attorney-General: `s 36`;
- cases where deleting exempt material would make the statement false or misleading: `s 37`.

Exam point:
- exclusions from the right to reasons are generally construed **strictly**;
- but always check the schedule carefully, especially tendering and government commercial activity exclusions.

### Reasons compared with FOI and litigation processes
- The statutory reasons right gives the applicant the **decision-maker's explanation**, not the underlying documents.
- FOI / RTI can provide access to the **raw materials and documents**.
- Discovery/disclosure in litigation can also expose underlying materials, but it is discretionary and available only once proceedings exist.

Best comparison:
- reasons are cheaper, earlier, and non-discretionary if the statute applies;
- FOI and discovery are broader for documents, but do different work.

## 2. Time limits for judicial review applications

### Basic rule
Under `JR Act s 26` and ADJR `s 11`:
- where written notice of the decision is given, the ordinary period is **28 days** after notice;
- where written reasons are later provided, the statutory provisions alter how time runs;
- where no written notice is given, the application must be brought within a **reasonable time**.

### Extensions of time
The source treats extension as discretionary, with a prima facie expectation that out-of-time cases do not proceed unless there is good reason.

Authorities to cite:
- `Lucic v Nolan`: late proceedings are prima facie not to be allowed.
- `Hoffman`: an adequate explanation for delay is usually important, though not always strictly essential.
- `Hunter Valley`: fairness, prejudice, public interest, and apparent merits are relevant.
- `Wirth`: modern Queensland example applying those considerations.

### Alice problem pattern
For a problem like the workshop taxi-licence question:
1. identify the date of written notice of the decision;
2. identify the date reasons were requested and then supplied;
3. apply `s 26(5)` carefully if reasons were provided;
4. if timing is tight, discuss extension power under `s 26(1)(b)`;
5. for immediate practical relief, consider a **stay** under `s 29`.

## 3. Exclusions from review, privative clauses, and jurisdictional error

### Schedule exclusions
- Under `JR Act s 18(2)`, matters in Parts 1 and 2 of **Schedule 1** are excluded from statutory review.
- `JR Act s 18A` separately excludes certain decisions of government-owned corporations.
- Unlike the ADJR Act, the Queensland `JR Act` does **not** permit review exclusions by regulation.

### Ouster / privative clauses
- `JR Act s 18(1)` disables pre-`JR Act` ouster clauses.
- Post-`JR Act` clauses depend on their actual wording and are usually read down if possible.

### Why jurisdictional error matters
Privative clauses cannot conclusively shield a decision affected by **jurisdictional error**.

High-yield authorities:
- `Craig`: jurisdictional error is narrower for inferior courts than for administrative decision-makers.
- `LPDT`: jurisdictional error often now includes a **materiality** inquiry, except in some categories such as bias.
- `Plaintiff S157`: Parliament cannot remove the High Court's entrenched `s 75(v)` jurisdiction for jurisdictional error.
- `Kirk`: State Parliaments likewise cannot strip State Supreme Courts of their supervisory jurisdiction for jurisdictional error.

Exam position:
- statutory exclusions may block the statutory pathway;
- but they do **not necessarily** eliminate constitutional or supervisory review for jurisdictional error.

### Quick definition to use
A jurisdictional error arises where the decision-maker:
- lacks authority to decide;
- exceeds the limits of the authority given;
- or commits an error going to the exercise of power in a way that makes the decision legally invalid.

For administrative decision-makers, many classic judicial review grounds can amount to jurisdictional error, including:
- asking the wrong question;
- taking irrelevant considerations into account;
- legal unreasonableness;
- denial of procedural fairness.

## 4. Court powers and discretionary controls

### Stay power
If the decision needs to be suspended pending review, use `JR Act s 29` (or ADJR `ss 15, 15A` federally).

The source links the stay question to ordinary interlocutory injunction ideas:
- serious question / prima facie case; and
- balance of convenience.

Key exam use:
- a stay preserves the status quo while legality is tested;
- merely filing review proceedings does **not** itself suspend the decision.

### Alternative remedies and fragmentation
The court may refuse or dismiss review where another avenue is more appropriate:
- `ss 10-15 JR Act` regulate overlap with appeals, merits review, and other proceedings.
- `s 14` is especially aimed at preventing fragmentation and interruption of ongoing processes, echoing the policy concern in `Bond`.

### Summary dismissal
Under `s 48`, the court may stay or dismiss a judicial review claim if it is:
- inappropriate;
- without reasonable basis;
- frivolous or vexatious; or
- an abuse of process.

### Costs
Two distinct Queensland costs provisions matter:
- `s 49`: flexible costs orders in judicial review proceedings, including no-costs or indemnity-style arrangements in appropriate cases;
- `s 50`: special costs rule for proceedings brought to vindicate the statutory right to reasons.

Exam point:
- Queensland is more generous than the federal scheme in using costs rules to support public law review and reasons litigation.

## Authorities to cite fast
- `Osmond`: no general common law right to reasons.
- `Australian Institute of Marine and Power Engineers`: reasons provisions are remedial; no grudging construction.
- `Burns v ANU`, `Taveli`: reasons help applicants understand and challenge decisions.
- `Ansett v Wraith`: content and quality of an adequate statement of reasons.
- `Craig`: inferior courts versus administrative bodies on jurisdictional error.
- `Plaintiff S157` and `Kirk`: privative clauses cannot destroy review for jurisdictional error.
- `LPDT`: modern High Court statement of jurisdictional error and materiality.
- `Hunter Valley`: extension-of-time factors.

## Problem-question checklist
1. Identify the pathway: `JR Act` Part 3, Part 4, Part 5, ADJR Act, common law, or `s 75(v)`.
2. Ask whether there is a reviewable **decision** and whether the applicant is **aggrieved**.
3. If reasons are sought, check `s 31` / `s 32`, the request timing under `s 33`, and any Schedule 2 or `ss 35-37` exception.
4. If review timing is in issue, work through `s 26` carefully and then discuss extension factors.
5. If review appears excluded, identify whether the exclusion is merely statutory or whether jurisdictional-error review still survives.
6. If urgent practical relief is needed, consider a **stay** under `s 29`.
7. If the claim seems weak or procedurally problematic, consider `s 48`, alternative remedies, and costs consequences.

## Short answers to the workshop themes
- **What must an applicant establish for reasons?** A reviewable decision to which Part 4 applies, plus entitlement to seek review as an aggrieved person.
- **When may reasons be refused?** Delay, lack of entitlement, Schedule 2 exclusion, or the exemptions in `ss 35-37`.
- **How do reasons compare with FOI/discovery?** Reasons explain the decision; FOI and discovery expose underlying documents.
- **How do privative clauses interact with review?** They may restrict statutory review, but not entrenched review for jurisdictional error.
- **What should be raised in an urgent practical problem?** Time limits, stay power, extension of time, and any risk of summary dismissal.
