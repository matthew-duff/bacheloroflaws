[[2025 Self-directed learning - automated decision-making and AI]]

# Reference
Jonathan H. Choi, Amy B. Monahan & Daniel Schwarcz, 'Lawyering in the Age of Artificial Intelligence' (2024) 109(1) *Minnesota Law Review* 147.

# Overview
This article presents the results of the first randomized controlled trial studying the impact of generative AI (specifically GPT-4) on human legal analysis. The study involved 60 law students completing four realistic legal tasks (drafting a complaint, contract, employee handbook section, and client memo) with and without AI assistance. The research aims to shift the focus from AI's autonomous capabilities to its role as a supportive tool for legal professionals, addressing ethical obligations and the normative nature of law.

# Key points
- **Efficiency Gains:** AI assistance led to significant and consistent increases in speed, reducing task completion time by 11.8% to 32.1% across different tasks.
- **Quality Improvements:** The impact on the quality of legal analysis was slight and inconsistent. While some tasks saw minor improvements, others showed negligible change or even slight decreases.
- **Equalizing Effect:** AI assistance was most beneficial for lower-skilled participants in terms of quality improvement, suggesting an "equalizing effect" that could promote greater equality within the legal profession.
- **Time Savings Consistency:** Time savings were relatively uniform regardless of the participant's baseline speed, indicating broad productivity benefits.
- **User Perception:** Participants generally correctly identified which tasks AI was most helpful for and reported increased satisfaction when using the tool for those tasks.
- **Lower-Bound Estimate:** The authors argue these results represent a lower-bound estimate of AI's potential, as specialized legal AI tools and increased user proficiency will likely yield even greater benefits.

# Significance
- **Productivity and Satisfaction:** The findings suggest that AI can significantly enhance legal productivity and improve lawyer satisfaction by reducing the time spent on routine analytical tasks.
- **Legal Education and Practice:** Law schools, firms, and judges are urged to thoughtfully incorporate AI tools into their workflows and curricula, planning for a future where AI is widespread.
- **Access to Justice:** The equalizing effect of AI may lower the barriers to entry for legal services and improve the consistency of legal work product.
- **Normative Implications:** The study highlights the need for a reassessment of billing models, in-house vs. outside counsel decisions, and the ethical integration of AI in judicial and legal decision-making.

# Administrative Law Context
While focused on "lawyering" generally, the study's findings on **automated decision-making (ADM)** and AI assistance are directly relevant to administrative law, particularly regarding:
- The efficiency and consistency of merits review and administrative drafting.
- The potential for AI to assist (or complicate) the exercise of discretionary powers.
- The evolving standard of "competence" and "due diligence" for legal practitioners and decision-makers in an automated environment.
