[[LPDT v Minister for Immigration, Citizenship, Migrant Services and Multicultural Affairs (2024) 280 CLR 321]]

**Citation**: *LPDT v Minister for Immigration, Citizenship, Migrant Services and Multicultural Affairs* (2024) 280 CLR 321; [2024] HCA 12

### Facts
LPDT, a Vietnamese national, had his visa cancelled under s 501(3A) of the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth) following several criminal convictions, including drug trafficking. He applied for revocation of the cancellation under s 501CA(4). The Administrative Appeals Tribunal (AAT) affirmed the decision not to revoke, relying on Primary Consideration 1 (protection of the Australian community) and Primary Consideration 4 (community expectations) from Ministerial Direction No 90. The AAT made several errors in applying Direction 90, including:
1. Finding his conduct "very serious" under a paragraph where his crimes did not fit the criteria.
2. Finding another paragraph "militated" towards a "very serious" finding when it only concerned "serious" conduct.
3. Finding a paragraph regarding formal warnings was "directly relevant" despite no evidence of such warnings.

The Full Federal Court found these were errors but held they were not "material" because the outcome would likely have been the same.

### Issue
1. Whether the AAT's failure to comply with Direction 90 (as required by s 499(2A)) constituted jurisdictional error.
2. The correct test for "materiality" in judicial review of administrative decisions.

### Held
The High Court unanimously allowed the appeal, finding the AAT's decision was affected by jurisdictional error.

### Reasoning
1. **Materiality Test**: The Court clarified that an error is material if there is a **"realistic possibility"** that the decision **could** have been different had the error not occurred. It is not necessary to show the decision *would* have been different.
2. **"Realistic" vs "Fanciful"**: "Realistic" distinguishes a possibility from one that is "fanciful or improbable." The threshold is not demanding or onerous.
3. **Single Error**: The Court noted that while there were multiple "particulars" of non-compliance with Direction 90, they constituted a single error: failure to comply with s 499(2A).
4. **Evaluative Assessment**: Because the decision involved an evaluative weighing of multiple factors, the errors in assessing the seriousness of the conduct (Primary Consideration 1) necessarily infected the final balancing exercise.
5. **No Speculation**: It is improper for a reviewing court to speculate on how a decision-maker *would* have reasoned without the error. To do so risks crossing the line from judicial review into merits review.

### Significance
*LPDT* is a landmark case for administrative law as it provides authoritative guidance on the **threshold of materiality** for jurisdictional error. It confirms that the "realistic possibility" test is the standard and emphasizes that courts must avoid "merits review" when conducting the counterfactual inquiry into materiality. It also clarifies that multiple failures to follow a Ministerial Direction should be viewed as a single jurisdictional error of failing to comply with the statutory mandate to follow such directions.
