# Applicant VEAL v Minister for Immigration (2005) 225 CLR 88

## Citation

*Applicant VEAL of 2002 v Minister for Immigration and Multicultural and Indigenous Affairs* [2005] HCA 72; (2005) 225 CLR 88.

## Facts

- VEAL, an Eritrean national, applied for a protection visa claiming a fear of persecution for political reasons.
- After the delegate refused the application, the Refugee Review Tribunal received an unsolicited confidential letter alleging that VEAL had admitted involvement in killing a prominent person and was actually a supporter of the Eritrean government.
- The Tribunal did not tell VEAL about the letter or its allegations, did not invite any response, and affirmed the refusal of the visa.
- In its reasons, the Tribunal said it had been unable to test the letter and therefore gave it "no weight".

## Issues

- Did procedural fairness require the Tribunal to disclose the existence or substance of the adverse allegations in the confidential letter?
- If so, did fairness require disclosure of the actual letter or the identity of the informer?
- Could the Tribunal avoid the obligation by later saying it gave the material no weight?

## Held

- Yes. Procedural fairness required the Tribunal to tell VEAL the substance of the allegations and give him an opportunity to respond before deciding the review.
- No. Procedural fairness did not require the Tribunal to provide the letter itself or reveal the identity of the informer.
- The Tribunal could not escape the duty of disclosure by later asserting that it gave the letter no weight.

## Reasoning / Ratio

- Procedural fairness is concerned with the fairness of the decision-making process, not with whether the material in fact changed the outcome.
- Information is "credible, relevant and significant" if, before the decision is made, it cannot simply be dismissed as obviously not credible, irrelevant, or trivial.
- That assessment is made prospectively during the process, not retrospectively by reference to how the decision-maker later describes the material in its reasons.
- The letter here directly bore on whether VEAL genuinely feared persecution for a Convention reason, so it was adverse material of a kind that had to be put to him.
- A real risk of prejudice exists even if the influence may be subconscious; the law does not require proof that the material actually affected the result.
- The public interest in protecting confidential informants could be accommodated by disclosing the substance of the allegations without disclosing the letter or the informer's identity.

## Significance

- This is a leading Australian authority on procedural fairness and adverse information in administrative decision-making.
- It confirms that the hearing rule applies to adverse material that is capable of influencing the decision, not merely to material expressly relied on in the final reasons.
- It is commonly cited with *Kioa v West* for the proposition that adverse information that is credible, relevant and significant must be put to the affected person.
- It also shows that fairness is flexible: the substance of allegations may need to be disclosed even where confidentiality concerns justify withholding the source.

## Exam Use

- Use `VEAL` where a decision-maker has received adverse third-party information but says it did not rely on it.
- Argue that the key question is whether the material was "credible, relevant and significant" at the time of decision-making, not whether the final reasons disclaim reliance.
- If confidentiality is raised, use `VEAL` to say fairness may be satisfied by disclosing the substance of the allegation rather than the document or source.
