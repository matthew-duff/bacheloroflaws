# Stowers v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection (2018) 265 FCR 177

## Citation

*Stowers v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection* [2018] FCAFC 174; (2018) 265 FCR 177.

## Facts

- Stowers, a New Zealand citizen, held a Subclass 444 visa and had it mandatorily cancelled under s 501(3A) of the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth) because he did not pass the character test.
- He was first invited under s 501CA(3) to make representations for revocation and was given Direction 65 plus a State Certificate setting out some convictions.
- Months later, the Department sent additional material, including a National Police Certificate and sentencing remarks from the Local Court and District Court, and invited comment.
- Unlike the treatment of the incoming passenger cards, the Department did not explain what inference might be drawn from most of that additional material.
- The Assistant Minister refused revocation under s 501CA(4), finding that Stowers' convictions for breaches of judicial orders and non-custodial dispositions showed a "disregard for judicial orders" and increased concern about reoffending.

## Issues

1. Did procedural fairness require the Assistant Minister to give more specific notice of the way the additional material might be used against Stowers?
2. Was the conclusion that Stowers displayed a disregard for judicial orders obvious enough from the material already provided?
3. If notice was inadequate, was there practical injustice sufficient to amount to jurisdictional error?

## Held

1. Yes. Procedural fairness required more specific notice of the basis on which the additional material might support the adverse finding.
2. No. It was not reasonably apparent that the National Police Certificate and sentencing remarks would be used to conclude that Stowers had displayed a disregard for judicial orders.
3. Yes. The denial of an opportunity to respond to that critical adverse basis constituted practical injustice.
4. The appeal was allowed, the primary judge's orders were set aside, and the revocation request was remitted for reconsideration according to law.

## Reasoning / Ratio

- Procedural fairness required Stowers to be informed of the critical issues and adverse material that would likely matter to the revocation decision.
- The court drew on *Kioa*, *VEAL*, *SZBEL* and *NBNB* to emphasise that fairness is practical and context-specific, and aims to avoid practical injustice.
- Although Direction 65 identified protection of the community and "serious conduct" as relevant, the first material given to Stowers did not indicate that breaches of judicial orders or non-custodial dispositions would be treated as showing contempt or disregard for the law.
- When the Department later sent additional material, it did not tie that material back to Direction 65 or identify which parts might be relied on to support the adverse finding, except in relation to the incoming passenger cards.
- The adverse inference was not obvious on the face of the new material. The Local Court remarks were critical of compliance, but the District Court remarks were materially more favourable and suggested some optimism about future compliance.
- The Assistant Minister also failed to particularise which convictions for breaches of judicial orders and non-custodial dispositions were actually being relied on.
- That mattered because, had proper notice been given, Stowers could have made submissions about the circumstances of those convictions and sought to reduce their significance.

## Significance

- This is an important procedural fairness case in the migration context, especially for s 501 cancellation and revocation decisions.
- It shows that merely handing over adverse documents is not always enough: the person may need practical, direct and non-misleading notice of the critical way those documents might be used.
- The case is especially useful where the decision-maker relies on an evaluative character finding that is not clearly signposted by the disclosed material.
- It reinforces that the content of fairness depends on context, but the core concern is whether the person had a real opportunity to answer the case against them.

## Exam Use

- Use `Stowers` where a decision-maker discloses documents but fails to identify the critical adverse inference it may draw from them.
- Cite it with `Kioa` and `VEAL` for the proposition that the hearing rule requires notice of adverse material or critical issues, not just a formal opportunity to comment in the abstract.
- Use `Stowers` with `NBNB` to argue that procedural fairness may require practical, direct and non-misleading notice of the factors critical to the decision.
- Distinguish cases where the adverse significance of material is obvious from those where the inference is evaluative, contestable, or dependent on unspecified parts of the record.
