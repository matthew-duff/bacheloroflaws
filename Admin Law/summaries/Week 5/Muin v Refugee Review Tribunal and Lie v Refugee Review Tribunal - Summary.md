# Muin v Refugee Review Tribunal; Lie v Refugee Review Tribunal [2002] HCA 30

## Citation
*Muin v Refugee Review Tribunal; Lie v Refugee Review Tribunal* [2002] HCA 30; (2002) 76 ALJR 966; 190 ALR 601.

## Facts
- Mr Muin and Ms Lie each applied for protection visas and were refused by a delegate of the Minister.
- In each matter, the delegate's reasons referred to numerous items of "Part B" material, including country and departmental material considered relevant to the application.
- On review, the Refugee Review Tribunal wrote in terms indicating that it had asked for the Department's documents and had looked at "all the material" or "all the papers" relating to the application.
- In fact, the agreed facts supported the conclusion that the Tribunal did not have all of the Pt B material that had been before the delegate, including material favourable to each applicant's case.
- It was agreed that each applicant believed the Tribunal already had that material and would have taken steps to supply or identify it if they had known otherwise.
- In Mr Muin's case, the Tribunal also took into account an adverse DFAT cable attached to departmental submissions without telling him its substance or giving him a chance to respond.

## Issues
- Whether the Tribunal denied procedural fairness by misleading the applicants into thinking all relevant departmental material was already before it.
- Whether Mr Muin was separately denied procedural fairness because adverse material was considered without disclosure or an opportunity to respond.
- Whether any failure by the Secretary to comply with `s 418(3)` or by the Tribunal to act under `s 424(1)` independently invalidated the decisions.

## Held
- By majority, both applicants were denied procedural fairness because the Tribunal's communications would have misled a reasonable review applicant into thinking it was unnecessary to draw the favourable Pt B material to the Tribunal's attention.
- Mr Muin was also denied procedural fairness because the Tribunal considered adverse material in the DFAT cable without giving him a reasonable opportunity to answer it.
- The Court did not treat the statutory arguments about `ss 418(3)` and `424(1)` as independently establishing invalidity in the way contended by the plaintiffs.
- Relief granted included prohibition, certiorari and mandamus requiring the review applications to be determined according to law.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- Procedural fairness in this setting required a reasonable opportunity to present the case for refugee status and to answer adverse material actually in the Tribunal's possession.
- The problem was not merely that documents were missing. The Tribunal's letters said, in substance, that it had asked for the Department's documents and had looked at all the relevant material. In context, that would lead a reasonable applicant to think there was no need to re-identify or resupply favourable Pt B material.
- Because the applicants were misled in that way, they lost a fair opportunity to put part of their case before the Tribunal.
- In Mr Muin's case, fairness was separately breached because the Tribunal took adverse country information into account without disclosing its substance and inviting a response.
- Several judges rejected the plaintiffs' attempt to derive invalidity directly from `s 418(3)` and `s 424(1)`. Even if there had been non-compliance with the document-transfer requirement, that did not automatically mean the Tribunal lacked power or that relief followed absent a demonstrated effect on the fairness or legality of the review process.

## Significance
- `Muin` is a leading authority that procedural fairness can be denied by misleading process communications, not only by an outright refusal to hear a party.
- It shows that a review body must not create the impression that it already has and has considered all relevant material if that is not true.
- It also reinforces the orthodox hearing-rule point that adverse material actually considered by the decision-maker must be put to the affected person in substance.
- The case is useful in separating a fairness breach from a bare statutory-processing breach: the decisive point was the practical denial of a fair opportunity, not simply administrative mishandling of documents.

## Exam Use
- Cite `Muin` where an administrator or tribunal's correspondence or procedure misleads a person into not putting favourable material before the decision-maker.
- Use it with `Kioa v West` and `Applicant VEAL` for the proposition that fairness requires a reasonable opportunity both to advance one's case and to answer adverse material.
- Distinguish statutory non-compliance from jurisdictional error: `Muin` helps show that a breach of an information-transfer provision matters most when it affects the fairness or conduct of the review.
