# Plaintiff S10/2011 v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship (2012) 246 CLR 636

## Pinpoint Principle

- The key point in `[39]-[53]` is that procedural fairness is not presumed just because a departmental process may influence whether a request ever reaches the Minister.
- The threshold question is whether the statute, properly construed, makes the impugned step one that attracts a duty to hear the affected person.
- For these personal ministerial intervention powers, the answer was no at the pre-consideration stage.

## Issue

- Did the departmental and ministerial processes surrounding possible intervention under the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth) require the applicants to be given procedural fairness before requests under provisions such as `ss 48B`, `351` and `417` were screened out or not considered?

## Holding

- No. French CJ and Kiefel J held that the relevant provisions did not imply an obligation to accord procedural fairness at that stage.
- The powers were exceptional, personal to the Minister, and expressly framed so the Minister had no duty to consider whether to exercise them.
- The departmental assessment process operated only as an administrative filter or briefing mechanism to assist the Minister, not as an exercise of statutory power determining rights.

## Why These Paragraphs Matter

- These paragraphs are important because they deal with the **threshold** question: whether natural justice applies at all, before asking what fairness would require.
- They show that procedural fairness turns on statutory construction, not on broad appeals to "legitimate expectation" or the mere practical importance of the decision to the applicant.
- They are routinely used to distinguish between:
  - a statutory process that itself affects rights or interests and therefore attracts fairness; and
  - a merely preparatory or facilitative process for a non-compellable personal power, which may not.

## Exam Use

- Use `Plaintiff S10/2011` where a problem question involves a **personal ministerial discretion** and an argument that a claimant should have been heard before a request was screened out.
- Start with the statute: ask whether the decision-maker had a duty to consider the request, whether the power is personal and non-delegable, and whether the impugned step is truly statutory or only preparatory.
- Distinguish `Plaintiff M61/2010E`: that case involved a different statutory setting where the process had a stronger legal connection to the exercise of power and the applicants' interests.
- Pair this case with `WZARH` to show that once a fairness obligation is engaged, "legitimate expectation" is not the controlling test; but `Plaintiff S10/2011` may stop the argument earlier by showing no fairness duty arose in the first place.
