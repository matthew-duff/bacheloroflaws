# Groves - Rules on Bias

## Thesis
Groves' central claim is that the bias rule is no longer governed by rigid categories such as automatic disqualification for pecuniary interest. Australian law has moved to a single, context-sensitive standard focused mainly on apprehended bias: whether a fair-minded, reasonably informed observer might conclude that the decision-maker might not bring an impartial mind to the matter.

## Key Arguments

### 1. Actual bias matters less than apprehended bias
- **Actual bias** requires proof that the decision-maker had a closed mind or had prejudged the matter. Groves stresses that this is extremely difficult to prove in practice.
- **Apprehended bias** is therefore the main operative doctrine. It is assessed objectively and does not require a court to make a direct personal finding that the decision-maker was in fact biased.
- Exam point: if asked which form of bias usually matters in judicial review, lead with **apprehended bias**, unless a statute restricts review to actual bias.

### 2. The doctrine is grounded in fairness and impartiality
- The bias rule complements the hearing rule. The hearing rule protects the right to know and answer the case; the bias rule protects the right to have that case determined by an impartial decision-maker.
- Groves treats the rule as serving both:
  - **instrumental goals**: better decision-making by excluding irrelevant influences; and
  - **non-instrumental goals**: public confidence, respect, and the appearance of fairness.
- For judges, Groves notes a possible constitutional dimension via Chapter III, but treats the rule's main present foundation as common law.

### 3. Ebner replaces automatic disqualification with articulated reasoning
- Groves presents **Ebner** as the decisive Australian shift away from automatic disqualification, even for pecuniary interests.
- Under **Ebner**, the applicant must:
  1. identify what matter might lead the decision-maker away from deciding on the legal and factual merits; and
  2. articulate the logical connection between that matter and the feared departure from impartiality.
- The doctrinal consequence is that it is no longer enough merely to point to an "interest" or connection. The party must "join the dots."
- Exam point: if a problem question raises shares, relationships, prior involvement, or comments by the decision-maker, structure the answer around the **two-step Ebner articulation**.

### 4. Context is everything
- A major theme of the article is that the same bias standard applies across contexts, but its **content varies** with the decision-maker and statutory setting.
- **Jia** is Groves' leading example: ministers cannot be judged by the same standards of detachment as judges because their office necessarily involves political speech, accountability, and public advocacy.
- Groves suggests ministers sit at one end of the spectrum, while judicial officers remain the main reference point for strict impartiality.
- This matters because administrative decision-making is often non-adversarial, paper-based, and more procedurally flexible than court proceedings.

### 5. Older verbal formulae matter less than the objective structure of the test
- Groves explains the movement from tests such as "real likelihood," "reasonable suspicion," and "real danger" toward the modern language of the fair-minded lay observer.
- His key point is that the verbal differences should not distract from the real issue: courts are trying to assess the appearance of impartiality in an objective way.
- He also highlights a tension: the more detailed evidence courts attribute to the hypothetical observer, the closer that observer comes to resembling the decision-maker with hindsight rather than an outsider.

### 6. Bias can arise from multiple sources
Drawing on **Webb v R**, Groves groups typical bias claims into overlapping categories:
- **interest**;
- **conduct**, including statements;
- **association**; and
- **extraneous information**.

He also cautions against over-expansion of the doctrine through "judge shopping," referring to **Locobail**, while acknowledging that strong prior statements, writings, or relationships can still justify disqualification in the right case.

## Doctrinal Significance
- The article explains why Australian bias doctrine is now best seen as **flexible rather than rule-bound**.
- It positions **Ebner** as the central authority because it generalises the inquiry across different forms of interest while insisting on a concrete explanatory link.
- It shows that **administrative bias doctrine cannot simply copy judicial bias doctrine** without adaptation.
- It frames bias as a major part of procedural fairness, but one shaped by institutional role, statutory context, and practical realities of administration.

## Exceptions
Groves identifies three main exceptions:
- **Waiver**: a party who knows the facts and does not object in time may lose the point.
- **Statutory modification or abrogation**: legislation may alter the rule, but only with clear language.
- **Necessity**: a disqualified decision-maker may still act where the system could not otherwise function.

Exam point: necessity is narrow and usually strongest where no alternative decision-maker exists.

## Exam-Use Takeaways
- Start with **apprehended bias**, not actual bias, unless legislation clearly narrows the ground.
- Apply **Ebner** methodically: identify the disqualifying matter, then explain the logical connection to feared partiality.
- Always ask **who the decision-maker is**: judge, tribunal member, minister, or administrator.
- Use **Jia** to explain why ministers are judged in a more context-sensitive way than judges.
- If a question involves prior comments, relationships, or undisclosed interests, avoid conclusory language and spell out why those facts might or might not affect impartiality.
- Mention **waiver**, **statutory modification**, and **necessity** if the facts raise them.

## Bottom Line
Groves' article is valuable for exams because it turns the bias rule from a loose fairness slogan into a structured doctrine: bias is assessed objectively, chiefly through apprehended bias, under a flexible but demanding context-sensitive approach anchored by **Ebner**.
