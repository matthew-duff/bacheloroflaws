# Kioa v West

## Citation
*Kioa v West* (1985) 159 CLR 550 (HCA).

## Facts
- Jason and Fheodolena Kioa were Tongan citizens who entered Australia on temporary permits and remained after those permits expired.
- They sought to regularise their position and relied on compassionate circumstances, including the need to support family in Tonga after a cyclone.
- Their infant daughter, Elvina, was born in Australia and was an Australian citizen.
- A departmental submission recommended refusal of further permission to stay and recommended deportation orders under the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth).
- That submission included adverse comments suggesting Mr Kioa had not been genuine in seeking an extension and that his involvement with other Tongan illegal immigrants was "a source of concern" because they were seeking to circumvent Australian migration laws.
- Those adverse comments were not disclosed to the Kioas before the delegate made the deportation orders.

## Issues
1. Did the *Administrative Decisions (Judicial Review) Act 1977* (Cth) itself require natural justice for every decision to which it applied?
2. Were deportation decisions under s 18 of the *Migration Act* subject to procedural fairness despite earlier authority suggesting otherwise?
3. If so, did fairness require the Kioas to be told of, and allowed to answer, the adverse material in the departmental submission?
4. Did Elvina, as an Australian citizen child, have a separate right to be heard before her parents were deported?

## Held
1. No. The ADJR Act did not itself impose natural justice on every administrative decision within its scope.
2. Yes, by majority. In the statutory setting as it stood in 1983, deportation decisions of this kind were subject to procedural fairness unless clearly excluded.
3. Yes. The failure to give the Kioas an opportunity to answer the prejudicial material meant the deportation orders were invalid.
4. No. Elvina had no separate entitlement to be heard, although her citizenship and interests were relevant considerations for the decision-maker.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- Mason J stated that the common law had developed to recognise a duty to act fairly in administrative decisions affecting rights, interests and legitimate expectations, subject only to a clear contrary statutory intention.
- The Court treated "procedural fairness" as a flexible standard. What fairness requires depends on the statutory framework, the nature of the decision, and the interests affected.
- The majority held that the post-ADJR statutory framework no longer justified a blanket rule that deportation decisions under s 18 were exempt from natural justice.
- Mason J accepted that fairness does not always require advance notice merely because a person is a prohibited immigrant. But where the decision turns on adverse personal considerations or undisclosed information from another source, fairness may require disclosure and an opportunity to respond.
- The undisclosed allegations in the departmental submission were prejudicial because they suggested bad faith and improper association with persons said to be circumventing migration laws.
- Wilson J emphasised that it was enough that the undisclosed allegation was capable of prejudice; the appellants did not need to prove it actually changed the outcome.
- Deane J took a broader view: absent clear exclusion, a person facing deportation will ordinarily be entitled to an adequate opportunity to be heard before such an order is made because of the serious impact on status, rights and practical freedom.
- On Elvina's position, the Court held that any effect on her was indirect and consequential. She therefore had no separate hearing right, though the delegate still had to take her citizenship and welfare into account.
- Gibbs CJ dissented on the central procedural fairness issue, holding that the statutory scheme still excluded any obligation to give a further hearing before deportation under s 18.

## Significance
- Foundational High Court authority on procedural fairness in Australian administrative law.
- Marks the shift from narrow "natural justice" categories toward the broader and more flexible language of "procedural fairness".
- Establishes that serious administrative decisions cannot generally rely on undisclosed adverse material personal to the affected person.
- Frequently cited with *Applicant VEAL* for the proposition that adverse, prejudicial information must be put to the person affected before a decision is made.
- Important in migration law, but its reasoning extends well beyond migration to administrative decision-making generally.

## Exam Use
- Cite *Kioa v West* for the modern presumption that procedural fairness applies unless clearly excluded by statute.
- Use it where a decision-maker has before it adverse, personal, or prejudicial material that was not disclosed to the affected person.
- Use Mason J and Deane J to explain that the content of fairness is context-specific, but ordinarily includes a chance to answer critical matters that may count against the person.
- Distinguish between a person's own hearing right and indirect effects on third parties: Elvina's interests were relevant, but did not generate a separate right to be heard.
