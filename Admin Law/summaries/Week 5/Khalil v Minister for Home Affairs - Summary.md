# Khalil v Minister for Home Affairs [2019] FCAFC 151

## Citation
`Khalil v Minister for Home Affairs [2019] FCAFC 151` (Logan, Steward and Jackson JJ)

## Facts
- A delegate refused Mr Khalil's visa under `s 501(1)` of the `Migration Act 1958 (Cth)` because he did not pass the character test.
- Mr Khalil sought merits review in the AAT under `s 500`.
- On the morning of the listed hearing, his lawyer withdrew. Mr Khalil was in detention on Christmas Island, unrepresented, and had not yet received the hearing materials.
- The Tribunal granted only a 24-hour adjournment because it believed `s 500(6L)` required it to both decide the review and hand down written reasons within 84 days of notification of the delegate's decision.
- The Tribunal affirmed the delegate's decision on the last day of the 84-day period and gave written reasons the same day.

## Issues
- Did the Tribunal misconstrue `s 500(6L)` by thinking the 84-day period required written reasons as well as a decision?
- If so, was that error jurisdictional?
- Was the error material in the sense that compliance could realistically have produced a different outcome?
- Did the separate argument that the 84-day period had never started running matter?

## Held
- The appeal was allowed.
- Within the 84 days, the Tribunal only had to make its decision; it did not have to provide written reasons within that same period.
- The Tribunal's misunderstanding of that requirement was a jurisdictional error.
- The error was material because, without it, the Tribunal could realistically have granted a longer adjournment, giving Mr Khalil a better opportunity to obtain representation and present submissions.
- The Court did not uphold the separate argument that the 84-day period had not commenced to run.

## Key Reasoning
- The `AAT Act` distinguishes between making a decision under `s 43` and later giving reasons. A decision comes into operation upon being given, while reasons may be given orally or in writing and, if not given immediately in writing, can follow within a reasonable time.
- The Tribunal therefore misunderstood the nature of its task when it treated the statutory deadline as requiring a fully reasoned written decision by the last day.
- That misconception directly shaped the adjournment decision. The Tribunal repeatedly said it could not delay the hearing because the decision had to be "handed down" by Monday and it needed time to write reasons.
- In context, this was not a minor timing slip. The error infected the conduct of the hearing and therefore the ultimate review decision.
- Materiality was established because extra time could realistically have mattered. Even if Mr Khalil could not have introduced fresh evidence easily, he might have obtained representation and had better oral submissions made from the existing material.
- The Court accepted that a competent advocate could have made more of factors already in the papers, including family interests, rehabilitation material, and considerations bearing on the discretionary outcome.

## Why Ground 4 Failed
- Mr Khalil also argued the 84-day clock had not started because the original notification may not have complied with `s 501G(1)`.
- The Court did not decide that point conclusively.
- Even assuming there was something in it, the Tribunal acted prudently in proceeding on the basis that the period was expiring, given the Minister's reliance on that deadline and the obvious risk of being out of time.

## Orders
- The primary judge's orders were set aside.
- Certiorari issued quashing the Tribunal's decision of 26 February 2018.
- Mandamus issued requiring the Tribunal to determine the review application according to law.

## Significance
- `Khalil` is a useful authority on the distinction between a tribunal's decision and its reasons.
- It shows how a legal mistake about procedure or timing can become jurisdictional when it causes the decision-maker to misunderstand the nature of its task.
- It is also a strong materiality case: the applicant did not need to prove he would have won, only that compliance could realistically have made a difference.

## Exam Use
- Cite `Khalil` where a tribunal or administrator wrongly compresses a hearing because of a mistaken view of a statutory deadline.
- Use it for the proposition that a failure to afford a reasonable opportunity to present a case can be materially connected to the outcome even when the missing opportunity is better advocacy rather than new evidence.
- Pair it with `Hossain` and `SZMTA` on materiality, and with adjournment/procedural fairness authorities where the practical effect of the refusal is central.
