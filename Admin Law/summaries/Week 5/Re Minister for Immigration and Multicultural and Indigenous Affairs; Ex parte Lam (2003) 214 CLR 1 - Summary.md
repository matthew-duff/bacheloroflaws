# Re Minister for Immigration and Multicultural and Indigenous Affairs; Ex parte Lam

## Citation
*Re Minister for Immigration and Multicultural and Indigenous Affairs; Ex parte Lam* [2003] HCA 6; (2003) 214 CLR 1.

## Facts
- Lam held a transitional permanent visa but had a substantial criminal record, including a heroin trafficking conviction, so he failed the character test under s 501 of the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth).
- The Department notified him that cancellation of his visa was under consideration and invited submissions.
- Lam responded with detailed material, including submissions about his two Australian citizen children and a supporting letter from their carer, Ms Tran.
- A departmental officer later wrote saying the Department wished to contact the children's carer to assess Lam's relationship with them and the likely effect of visa cancellation on the children.
- Lam provided Ms Tran's contact details, but the Department never contacted her and did not tell Lam it would not do so.
- The Minister cancelled the visa under s 501(2). Lam sought certiorari and prohibition, arguing that the Department's failure to follow the announced procedure denied him procedural fairness.

## Issues
1. Did the Department's statement that it wished to contact the children's carer create a procedural fairness obligation to make that contact, or at least to tell Lam if it no longer intended to do so?
2. Is disappointed "legitimate expectation" enough to establish denial of procedural fairness?
3. Was Lam practically prejudiced by the Department's failure to contact the carer?

## Held
1. No. A representation about procedure may be relevant to procedural fairness, but it does not automatically bind the decision-maker to follow that procedure.
2. No. The critical question is whether the adopted procedure was unfair, not whether an expectation was disappointed.
3. No. Lam had already put detailed material before the Department about his children, including Ms Tran's views, and he was not deprived of any opportunity to provide further material.
4. The application failed; no breach of procedural fairness was established.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- The Court treated procedural fairness as a practical doctrine concerned with avoiding practical injustice, not enforcing good administration for its own sake.
- The Department's statement about contacting Ms Tran could be relevant to the content of fairness, but it did not create a form of procedural estoppel.
- Lam's case failed because he could not show that the broken representation caused any forensic disadvantage. He did not establish that he would have provided further material, taken any different step, or answered any adverse matter had he been told no further contact would occur.
- This was not a *Kioa*-type case involving undisclosed adverse information being relied on against the affected person. The Minister had before him Lam's own submissions, Ms Tran's letter, and other material about the children.
- Gleeson CJ emphasised that fairness is "essentially practical" and that no practical injustice had been shown.
- McHugh, Gummow, Hayne and Callinan JJ said the term "legitimate expectation" now has limited utility in Australian law, because the real inquiry is simply what procedural fairness requires in the circumstances.
- The Court doubted any broader approach that would require public authorities to fulfil every announced procedural step merely because it was announced.

## Significance
- Leading High Court authority limiting the independent force of "legitimate expectation" in Australian administrative law.
- Strong statement that procedural fairness requires practical injustice or loss of opportunity, not just disappointment when a public authority departs from an announced procedure.
- Important contrast case to authorities such as *Kioa* and *Applicant VEAL*, where undisclosed adverse material deprived the person of a meaningful chance to respond.
- Frequently used to show that not every administrative misstep or failure of good administration amounts to jurisdictional error.

## Exam Use
- Cite *Lam* when a problem question involves a decision-maker departing from a promised or foreshadowed procedure.
- Ask whether the departure actually deprived the person of a chance to present evidence, make submissions, or answer adverse material. If not, *Lam* points away from relief.
- Use the case to qualify reliance on "legitimate expectation": in Australian law it is not a free-standing doctrine that compels administrators to honour every procedural representation.
- Distinguish *Lam* from cases where undisclosed adverse information was used against the applicant. In *Lam*, the complaint was a disappointed expectation, not hidden prejudicial material.
