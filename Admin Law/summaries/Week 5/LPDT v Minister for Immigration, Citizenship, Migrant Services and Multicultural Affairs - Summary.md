# LPDT v Minister for Immigration, Citizenship, Migrant Services and Multicultural Affairs (2024) 280 CLR 321

## Citation
`LPDT v Minister for Immigration, Citizenship, Migrant Services and Multicultural Affairs` (2024) 280 CLR 321; `[2024] HCA 12`

## Facts
- LPDT, a Vietnamese national, held a spouse visa and had multiple convictions, including drug trafficking offences, for which he received terms of imprisonment.
- His visa was mandatorily cancelled under `s 501(3A)` of the `Migration Act 1958 (Cth)`.
- He sought revocation under `s 501CA(4)`. The AAT affirmed the delegate's refusal to revoke.
- Under `s 499(2A)`, the AAT had to comply with `Direction 90`, including relevant primary considerations.
- The AAT treated LPDT's offending as "very serious" and found para `8.1.1(1)(g)` directly relevant, even though the source material showed errors in how those provisions were applied.

## Issues
- Did the AAT fail to comply with `s 499(2A)` by misapplying mandatory considerations in `Direction 90`?
- If so, was that error material, so that it amounted to jurisdictional error?
- What is the correct approach to materiality in judicial review of an evaluative administrative decision?

## Held
- The appeal was allowed.
- The AAT committed jurisdictional error by failing to comply with `Direction 90` as required by `s 499(2A)`.
- The errors were properly characterised as one single failure to comply with the statutory direction, not three separate errors.
- The error was material because there was a realistic possibility that the decision actually made could have been different without the error.
- The Full Court erred by treating the problem as if the reviewing court could reconstruct how the AAT would have reasoned absent the error.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- The Court said materiality asks whether the decision that was in fact made could realistically, not necessarily would, have been different if the error had not occurred.
- "Realistic" excludes fanciful or improbable possibilities, but the threshold is not demanding.
- In this case, the AAT's mistakes all bore on Primary Consideration 1 and fed into the evaluative weighing exercise required under `s 501CA(4)`.
- Because the decision involved weighing mandatory considerations, the reviewing court could not safely sever the flawed reasoning and assume the same balance would have been struck.
- It is improper for a court on judicial review to speculate about how the administrator would have reasoned had the error not occurred, because that risks slipping into merits review.
- The source judgment treats the AAT's non-compliance with `Direction 90` as one statutory failure to perform the task required by `s 499(2A)`.

## Significance
- `LPDT` is now a leading High Court authority on materiality in administrative law.
- It clarifies that the usual question is whether there is a realistic possibility of a different outcome, not whether a different outcome was more likely than not.
- It is especially important where the original decision required an evaluative balancing of mandatory considerations.
- It also reinforces the distinction between judicial review and merits review: courts assess legality, not how they think the discretion should have been exercised.

## Exam Use
- Cite `LPDT` for the modern High Court formulation of materiality: a jurisdictional error is material if the decision actually made could realistically have been different absent the error.
- Use it where a decision-maker has misstated, misunderstood, or misapplied mandatory considerations fixed by statute or ministerial direction.
- Use it to resist an argument that a court can cure an evaluative error by speculating that the same result would have been reached anyway.
- Pair it with `Hossain`, `SZMTA`, and `Nathanson` when explaining the development of materiality doctrine.
