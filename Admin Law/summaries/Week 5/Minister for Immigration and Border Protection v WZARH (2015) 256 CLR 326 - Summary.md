# Minister for Immigration and Border Protection v WZARH

## Citation
*Minister for Immigration and Border Protection v WZARH* [2015] HCA 40; (2015) 256 CLR 326.

## Facts
- WZARH was a Sri Lankan Tamil who arrived at Christmas Island as an "offshore entry person" and sought recognition that Australia owed him protection obligations under the Refugees Convention.
- After an adverse Refugee Status Assessment, he requested Independent Merits Review (`IMR`).
- The first reviewer interviewed him and said she would conduct a fresh rehearing and make a recommendation to the Minister after considering his material and any further information.
- That reviewer later became unavailable. A second reviewer took over, but WZARH was not told of the change.
- The second reviewer did not interview WZARH, relied on written materials plus the recording and transcript of the first interview, formed an adverse view of his credibility, and recommended against recognition of protection obligations.

## Issues
1. Was WZARH denied procedural fairness when the IMR process changed from review by the interviewing reviewer to completion by a different reviewer without notice?
2. Did procedural fairness require a further oral hearing, or at least an opportunity to make submissions about how the altered review process should continue?
3. Should the analysis depend on WZARH having a "legitimate expectation" that the first reviewer would finish the review?

## Held
1. Yes. WZARH was denied procedural fairness.
2. Procedural fairness did not automatically require an oral hearing in every case, but it did require notice of the changed procedure and an opportunity to seek supplementation of submissions or further oral evidence.
3. No. "Legitimate expectation" is not the basis for determining either whether procedural fairness applies or what its content requires.
4. The Minister's appeal was dismissed with costs.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- Kiefel, Bell and Keane JJ said the correct question is not whether an expectation was disappointed, but what fairness required in the actual statutory and procedural setting.
- The Court reaffirmed that, absent clear contrary legislative intention, administrative decision-makers must accord procedural fairness to those affected by their decisions.
- There is no universal rule that procedural fairness always requires an oral hearing. The content of fairness is variable and depends on the practical requirements of the case.
- Here, however, the procedure initially adopted mattered. WZARH had been interviewed on the basis that the same reviewer would assess his claims and make the recommendation. When a different reviewer took over, the nature of the opportunity previously given changed in a significant way.
- The second reviewer decided credibility issues without personally observing WZARH. In refugee matters, especially where interpretation and credibility are important, demeanour, clarification, and direct questioning can materially affect a fair assessment.
- Because WZARH was not told of the change, he lost the opportunity to argue for a further hearing, to supplement written submissions, or to tailor his case to the new procedure.
- The Court distinguished *Lam*: unlike the applicant in *Lam*, WZARH did lose a real opportunity to advance his case. The question concerns fairness of the process, not whether the court thinks the reviewer probably reached the right substantive outcome.
- Gageler and Gordon JJ emphasised that when the procedure itself fails to afford a fair opportunity to be heard, denial of procedural fairness is established unless it can be shown that the failure did not deprive the person of the possibility of a successful outcome.

## Significance
- Important High Court authority on the content of procedural fairness in administrative decision-making.
- Confirms that "legitimate expectation" should not be used as the doctrinal touchstone for procedural fairness in Australian administrative law.
- Shows that even where no general right to an oral hearing exists, fairness may require notice and a chance to seek further participation if the decision-making process materially changes.
- Particularly important in migration and refugee contexts where credibility, interpretation, and the ability to respond to procedural changes can matter greatly.

## Exam Use
- Cite *WZARH* to show that the content of procedural fairness is practical and context-specific: ask what fairness required in the circumstances, not whether the person had a legitimate expectation.
- Use it where an agency changes procedure midstream without telling the affected person, especially if the person had already presented their case on the assumption that a different process would be followed.
- Pair it with *Kioa*, *Lam*, and *SZBEL* in problem questions about notice, opportunity to respond, and loss of a realistic opportunity to advance a case.
- Use Gageler and Gordon JJ's approach where you need to explain materiality in procedural fairness: relief is justified if the unfair procedure deprived the person of the possibility of a successful outcome.
