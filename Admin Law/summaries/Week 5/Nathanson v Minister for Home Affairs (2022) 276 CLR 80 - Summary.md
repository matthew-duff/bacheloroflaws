# Nathanson v Minister for Home Affairs (2022) 276 CLR 80

## Citation
`Nathanson v Minister for Home Affairs` (2022) 276 CLR 80; `[2022] HCA 26`.

## Facts
- Nathanson, a New Zealand citizen, held a Special Category visa and had his visa mandatorily cancelled under `s 501(3A)` of the `Migration Act 1958 (Cth)` after serious offending.
- A delegate refused to revoke the cancellation under `s 501CA(4)`.
- On review, the AAT had to apply `Direction 79`, which newly emphasised that violent crimes against women or children are viewed very seriously.
- Police material concerning prior domestic violence incidents had been raised before the Tribunal mainly in connection with the best interests of Nathanson's children.
- Nathanson appeared unrepresented. During closing submissions, the Minister relied on those domestic violence incidents as a new and serious factor bearing on protection of the Australian community under `Direction 79`.
- The Tribunal did not alert Nathanson to that new use of the material or give him an opportunity to address it, but then treated the incidents as serious in affirming non-revocation.

## Issues
1. Did the AAT deny Nathanson procedural fairness by failing to give him an opportunity to address the new adverse use of the domestic violence material under `Direction 79`?
2. If so, was that denial material so as to amount to jurisdictional error?
3. Did Nathanson need to identify the exact additional evidence or submissions he would have presented to prove materiality?

## Held
1. Yes. The Tribunal denied procedural fairness.
2. Yes. The denial was material and therefore jurisdictional error.
3. No. Nathanson did not have to articulate a specific course of action or precise additional evidence to establish a realistic possibility of a different outcome.
4. The appeal was allowed and the matter was remitted to the Tribunal for determination according to law.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- The plurality held that materiality in judicial review asks whether the decision actually made could realistically have been different if the legal condition had been complied with.
- In procedural fairness cases, where a person is denied a fair opportunity to present evidence or submissions on an issue requiring consideration, a realistic possibility of a different outcome will often be apparent from the record itself.
- The standard of `reasonable conjecture` is undemanding. It does not require the applicant to prove exactly how the lost opportunity would have been used.
- Here, the hearing became unfair when the Minister used the domestic violence material for a new purpose in closing submissions and the Tribunal failed to draw that shift to Nathanson's attention.
- Even if the underlying incidents could not realistically be denied, Nathanson might still have given further evidence, called his wife, or made submissions putting the incidents in context and arguing they should not be treated as very serious under `Direction 79`.
- Because the Tribunal's task involved evaluative weighing under `s 501CA(4)`, further evidence or submissions on the seriousness of the conduct could realistically have altered the Tribunal's assessment and the outcome.
- Gordon J treated a serious denial of an opportunity to be heard on an important issue in an evaluative decision as jurisdictional without any additional materiality burden. Edelman J likewise described the materiality burden, if applicable, as very slight.

## Significance
- `Nathanson` is a leading High Court authority on the relationship between procedural fairness and materiality.
- It confirms that courts should not demand a detailed reconstruction of what an applicant would have said had they been given a fair chance to respond.
- The case is especially important where an administrator changes the significance of adverse material during the hearing or uses known material for a new dispositive purpose without notice.
- It sits alongside `Hossain`, `SZMTA`, `MZAPC`, and `WZARH` in the modern law of materiality and procedural fairness.

## Exam Use
- Cite `Nathanson` where a decision-maker relies on adverse material for a new purpose without fairly alerting the affected person and giving them a chance to respond.
- Use it to argue that materiality is satisfied where the lost opportunity concerned an important evaluative issue and there was a realistic possibility of a different outcome.
- Use it against submissions that the plaintiff must prove exactly what extra evidence or submissions would have been made.
- Pair it with `Kioa`, `WZARH`, and `SZMTA` for notice-and-response problems involving migration decisions or other high-stakes administrative decisions.
