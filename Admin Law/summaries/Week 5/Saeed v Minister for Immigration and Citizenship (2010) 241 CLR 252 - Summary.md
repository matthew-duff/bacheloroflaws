# Saeed v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship

## Citation
*Saeed v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship* [2010] HCA 23; (2010) 241 CLR 252.

## Facts
- Saeed, a Pakistani citizen, applied offshore for a Skilled Independent visa.
- To obtain the visa, she needed to satisfy the decision-maker that she had worked in a relevant skilled occupation for at least 12 months in the previous 24 months.
- She provided documents showing employment as a cook at a restaurant in Rawalpindi.
- Australian immigration officers in Pakistan investigated and reported adverse information: no employee records were found and staff said no woman had worked in the kitchen.
- The Minister's delegate treated Saeed's employment evidence as false or misleading and refused the visa.
- Before refusing the application, the delegate did not tell Saeed about the adverse material or give her an opportunity to respond.

## Issues
1. Did s 51A(1) of the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth) exclude the common law hearing rule for this offshore visa application?
2. Did s 57, which required disclosure of certain adverse information, apply only to onshore applicants?
3. If the statutory code did not exclude fairness, was the delegate required to give Saeed an opportunity to answer the adverse information before refusal?

## Held
1. No. Section 51A(1) did not exclude procedural fairness for offshore visa applicants.
2. Yes. Section 57 dealt only with onshore applicants whose visas could be granted in the migration zone and whose refusals were reviewable.
3. Yes. The delegate was obliged to give Saeed an opportunity to comment on the adverse material before deciding the application.
4. The appeal was allowed, the refusal decision was quashed, and mandamus issued requiring the application to be reconsidered according to law.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- The Court reaffirmed that statutes are construed against a common law background of procedural fairness. Fundamental principles such as natural justice are not displaced except by plain words or necessary intendment.
- The phrase in s 51A making the subdivision an "exhaustive statement" applied only "in relation to the matters it deals with". That required close attention to what the operative provisions actually covered.
- Section 57 imposed mandatory disclosure obligations only for onshore visa applicants. Because Saeed was an offshore applicant, the provision of adverse information to her was not a "matter" dealt with by s 57.
- Since the subdivision did not deal with disclosure obligations for offshore applicants, s 51A did not exclude the common law hearing rule in that setting.
- The Court rejected heavy reliance on the Explanatory Memorandum and Second Reading Speech. Extrinsic materials could not override the enacted text, especially where fundamental rights were said to be displaced.
- Applying ordinary procedural fairness principles, the adverse verification material was central to the refusal and had to be put to Saeed before decision.
- The Court overruled *Minister for Immigration and Multicultural and Indigenous Affairs v Lat* to the extent it treated s 51A as excluding fairness more broadly.

## Significance
- Leading authority on statutory exclusion of procedural fairness in migration law.
- Strong modern statement of the principle of legality: unmistakable and unambiguous language is required to displace natural justice.
- Important for showing that an apparently exhaustive statutory procedure may still leave room for common law fairness where the statute does not actually deal with the particular situation.
- Often paired with *Kioa* and *VEAL* on the requirement to disclose adverse information that is central to the decision.

## Exam Use
- Cite *Saeed* where a statute is said to exclude procedural fairness. Argue that courts require clear textual indication, especially where fundamental common law protections are at stake.
- Use it when the decision-maker relies on undisclosed adverse material against a person who had no statutory right to be heard under the express code.
- In a problem question, compare the exact statutory provision with the factual setting and ask whether the statute really "deals with" that situation before concluding fairness is excluded.
