# Workshop 4 Study Guide Summary

## Core Structure
- **Merits review** asks for the correct or preferable decision; **judicial review** asks whether power was exercised lawfully.
- Judicial review is concerned with **legal error**, especially whether the decision-maker acted within power, followed fair procedures, and avoided jurisdictional error.
- Federal judicial review pathways mentioned in the guide are:
  - **Constitution s 75(v)** against officers of the Commonwealth.
  - **Judiciary Act s 39B** in the Federal Court.
  - **AD(JR) Act 1977 (Cth)** for statutory review of covered administrative decisions.
- At common law and under the Constitution, **jurisdictional error** is central. Under the AD(JR) Act, applicants usually plead one or more statutory grounds instead.

## Procedural Fairness: Exam Structure

### 1. Threshold question: when is procedural fairness implied?
- The common law presumes procedural fairness where a decision affects a person's **rights or interests** in a **direct and immediate** way, absent clear statutory exclusion.
- The source stresses that the duty is not confined to courts or quasi-courts; it extends to administrative decision-makers exercising statutory and sometimes non-statutory executive power.

### 2. Key threshold authorities
- **Ridge v Baldwin**: dismissal affecting office, pension rights, and reputation attracted natural justice.
- **FAI v Winneke**: refusal or non-renewal of a licence/approval can attract procedural fairness.
- **Kioa v West**: leading Australian authority; procedural fairness protects rights and interests and is presumed unless displaced by statute.
- **Plaintiff M61/2010E v Commonwealth**: even a process described as non-statutory may attract procedural fairness if it informs statutory power and directly affects liberty/interests.
- **Plaintiff S10/2011 v Minister for Immigration**: statutory powers apt to affect an individual's interests may attract the presumption.
- **Saraceni v ASIC**: no duty where the effect on the person is only indirect at an early investigative stage.

### 3. Statutory exclusion or modification
- Parliament can exclude or modify procedural fairness, but only by **plain words or necessary intendment**.
- This reflects the **principle of legality**: courts do not readily infer that Parliament intended to displace fundamental common law protections.
- **Re Minister for Immigration; Ex parte Miah**: a statutory procedural code was not treated as exhaustive.
- **Saeed v Minister for Immigration**: confirms the strong presumption against exclusion absent clear language.

### 4. Content question: what does fairness require?
- Content is **variable and context-specific**.
- The question is always what fairness required **in these statutory and factual circumstances**.
- Main fair hearing requirements from the guide:
  - adequate prior notice;
  - disclosure of the substance of the adverse case;
  - a reasonable opportunity to respond;
  - procedures adapted to the seriousness and complexity of the matter;
  - in some cases, oral hearing, representation, cross-examination, or adjournment.

## Fair Hearing Rules

### Prior notice
- A person usually must know:
  - the nature and purpose of the inquiry;
  - the issues to be considered;
  - the substance of adverse information that may be relied on.
- **VEAL v Minister for Immigration** and **Kioa v West** are central on disclosure of the case to be met.
- **Stowers v Minister for Immigration** is listed as a useful modern summary of notice principles.

### Disclosure of adverse material
- A decision-maker must disclose **credible, relevant, and significant** adverse material.
- The source emphasizes that fairness is not satisfied by withholding important adverse material merely because the decision-maker says it was given little weight.
- The decision-maker need not expose every provisional thought, but must identify any **critical issue not apparent** from the nature of the decision or statutory framework.

### Reasonable opportunity to respond
- Affected persons must have a real chance to answer the case against them.
- There is **no universal right to an oral hearing**, but one may be required where fairness cannot otherwise be achieved.
- An oral process may need to be adjusted where credibility is central, inconsistencies arise, the person is vulnerable, or comprehension is impaired.
- A refusal to adjourn can itself amount to unfairness, and in some contexts may also support an unreasonableness argument.

### Representation and cross-examination
- There is **no absolute right** to legal representation in administrative proceedings.
- Representation may be required if the matter is sufficiently serious or complex and the person cannot fairly present their own case.
- There is also **no general right to cross-examination**, but fairness may require it where credibility is central and an adverse witness is being relied on.

## Materiality
- Not every procedural error invalidates a decision.
- The current approach asks whether the denial of procedural fairness caused **practical injustice** and whether there was a **realistic possibility** of a different outcome.
- Key authorities listed in the guide:
  - **Minister for Immigration v WZARH**
  - **Minister for Immigration v SZSSJ**
  - **Minister for Immigration v SZMTA**
  - **LPDT v Minister for Immigration, Citizenship, Migrant Services and Multicultural Affairs**
- Exam point: once a procedural error is identified, ask whether compliance could realistically have produced a different result.

## Rule Against Bias
- Bias is the second branch of natural justice and concerns whether the case was considered **independently and impartially**.
- The source emphasizes the distinction between:
  - **actual bias**: hard to prove closed mind;
  - **apprehended bias**: whether a fair-minded lay observer might reasonably apprehend that the decision-maker might not bring an impartial mind.
- **Ebner v Official Trustee in Bankruptcy** supplies the standard two-step formulation for apprehended bias:
  - identify what might lead the decision-maker away from deciding on the merits;
  - articulate the logical connection between that factor and the feared departure from impartiality.
- **Minister for Immigration v Jia Legeng**: bias analysis is contextual and differs across judges, tribunal members, and ministers.
- **CNY17 v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection**: irrelevant and prejudicial material may support an apprehended bias argument.

## Problem-Question Checklist
1. Identify the **power**, decision-maker, and review pathway.
2. Ask whether the decision affected the person's **rights or interests directly and immediately**.
3. Check whether statute **expressly or impliedly excludes or modifies** procedural fairness.
4. Specify the precise **hearing requirement** said to be denied:
   - notice;
   - disclosure;
   - time to respond;
   - adjournment;
   - representation;
   - cross-examination;
   - impartiality.
5. Tie each alleged defect to the **context**:
   - seriousness of consequences;
   - vulnerability of the affected person;
   - complexity;
   - urgency/informality required by statute;
   - whether credibility was central.
6. Ask whether the withheld material or denied opportunity was **credible, relevant, and significant**.
7. Finish with **materiality**: could compliance realistically have produced a different outcome?

## Summerton Scenario: Compact Issue Spotter
- **Refusal of a short rest**: possible denial of a reasonable opportunity to present the case, especially given anxiety, PTSD, and self-representation.
- **New affidavit and oral evidence from the retired officer**: raises notice, disclosure, and opportunity-to-answer issues.
- **No cross-examination of the officer**: arguable unfairness because credibility and firsthand observation were central.
- **Use of blog material found by the tribunal associate**: possible unfairness if it was adverse and relevant but not properly put to the parties, even if said to carry little weight.
- **Finding that Summerton was unreliable**: strengthens the argument that procedural protections around adverse credibility material mattered.
- **Materiality**: ask whether proper notice, questioning, or response opportunities could realistically have affected the VRT's conclusion on whether PTSD was war-caused.
