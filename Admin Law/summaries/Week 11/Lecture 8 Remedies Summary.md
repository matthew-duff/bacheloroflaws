# Lecture 8 Remedies Summary

## Remedies taxonomy

Administrative law remedies in this lecture fall into four buckets:

- **Statutory remedies** under the *ADJR Act* and *JR Act* Part 3.
- **Common law writs**: prohibition, certiorari, mandamus, plus habeas corpus and quo warranto.
- **Equitable remedies**: declarations and injunctions.
- **Collateral challenge** as a separate way invalidity may arise.

Queensland point:

- Part 3 of the *JR Act* gives statutory review remedies.
- Part 5 of the *JR Act* incorporates remedies "in the nature of" the prerogative writs.
- Declarations and injunctions exist both within and outside Part 5.

## 1. Statutory remedies

### ADJR Act

- `s 16(1)` links remedies to review of a **decision** under `s 5`.
- `s 16(2)` links remedies to review of **conduct** under `s 6`.
- `s 16(3)` links remedies to **failure to decide** under `s 7`.

### JR Act Part 3

- The Supreme Court can quash a decision.
- It can backdate the quashing order to the date of the original decision, making the decision void ab initio in effect.
- It can remit the matter for reconsideration with directions.
- Relief remains confined by legality review principles, not merits review.
- There is **no general entitlement to damages** in Australian administrative law.

## 2. Writs / prerogative-style orders

### Core functions

- **Prohibition**: stops action affected by jurisdictional error before the decision-maker is functus officio.
- **Certiorari**: quashes a decision for jurisdictional error, and also for non-jurisdictional error of law on the face of the record.
- **Mandamus**: compels performance of a public duty; functionally similar to review for failure to decide.

Useful comparisons drawn in the slides:

- Prohibition roughly corresponds to review of **conduct** under *ADJR Act* `s 6` and *JR Act* `s 21`.
- Certiorari roughly corresponds to review of a **decision** under *ADJR Act* `s 5` and *JR Act* `s 20`.
- Mandamus roughly corresponds to **failure to decide** under *ADJR Act* `s 7` and *JR Act* `s 22`.

### When certiorari and prohibition are available

From *Electricity Commissioners*:

- The body must have legal authority to determine questions affecting rights.
- The body must be under a duty to act judicially.
- The body must act in excess of legal authority.

From *Ex parte Lain*:

- Statutory authority is **not essential**; prerogative or executive source of authority may still suffice.

### Legal effect requirement

The lecture distinguishes certiorari from prohibition:

- **Certiorari** requires a decision with legal effect or legal consequences.
- **Prohibition** may still be available prospectively even where the proposed outcome would not itself yet have legal effect.

Key cases:

- *Ainsworth*: adverse report had no legal effect, so certiorari unavailable; prohibition may have been available earlier for procedural unfairness.
- *Hot Holdings*: a recommendation or preliminary step can attract certiorari if it is a condition precedent to a later exercise of power affecting rights.

### Error of law on the face of the record

From *Craig v South Australia*:

- For this non-jurisdictional use of certiorari, the "record" is narrow.
- It does **not ordinarily include** transcripts or reasons for decision.
- This is narrower than statutory review under the *ADJR Act* and *JR Act*.

### Standing

- At common law, certiorari and prohibition traditionally required a "person aggrieved", though some strangers were allowed.
- Mandamus historically took a stricter approach.
- In Queensland, standing for Part 5 remedies is governed by `JR Act s 44`.

## 3. Declarations

### Nature and advantage

- A declaration states the legal position of the parties.
- It does **not** directly coerce compliance.
- If ignored, further relief such as an injunction may be needed.
- Courts often prefer declarations in public law because respondents are public bodies expected to obey the law.

### JR Act link

- `JR Act s 43(2)(a)` links declarations/injunctions to the same matters and persons to which the prerogative writs apply.
- `JR Act s 43(3)` links Part 5 relief to the Supreme Court's inherent jurisdiction.

### Limits

From *Ainsworth*:

- Declarations will not be granted for purely hypothetical questions.
- They will not be granted where no foreseeable consequences for the parties follow.

From *Gardner* and *Plaintiff M68*:

- Past conduct alone may not justify relief if nothing turns on it.
- But a declaration is still useful where it resolves whether government may lawfully repeat the conduct in future.

### Why declarations are especially useful

The lecture presents declarations as highly flexible because they can:

- be used for administrative, legislative, and judicial action;
- raise all common law legality grounds, including non-jurisdictional errors of law;
- operate in the Supreme Court's inherent jurisdiction, including against some non-government entities;
- be used pre-emptively to determine rights or statutory meaning, so long as the issue is real rather than hypothetical.

## 4. Injunctions

### Nature

- An injunction is coercive and enforceable by contempt.
- It may be **prohibitory** or **mandatory**.
- Interlocutory injunctions are functionally similar to stays pending final determination.

### Interlocutory test

From *ABC v O'Neill*:

- The applicant must show a **prima facie case**.
- The court then weighs the **balance of convenience**.

The slides also note the usual commercial requirement of an **undertaking as to damages**, while showing that public interest considerations can complicate that requirement in public law cases.

### Injunctions to enforce the criminal law

The lecture treats this as exceptional and cautionary:

- *Bateman's Bay*, *Onus*, and *Gouriet* stress that this jurisdiction must be used carefully.
- The concern is that civil injunctions may effectively strengthen criminal sanctions beyond what Parliament provided, and do so using civil procedure rather than criminal trial safeguards.

### Public interest and undertakings

*Central Queensland Speleological Society* illustrates:

- standing issues in public interest litigation;
- difficulty where the plaintiff cannot give an adequate undertaking as to damages;
- tension between public interest protection and abuse of process concerns.

## Exam use

When answering a remedies problem, work in this order:

1. **Identify the review pathway**: statutory review, prerogative-style order, declaration, injunction, or collateral challenge.
2. **Match the remedy to the procedural posture**:
   - no decision yet -> prohibition;
   - decision already made -> certiorari;
   - failure/refusal to act -> mandamus;
   - need flexible statement of legal position -> declaration;
   - need coercive restraint/preservation of position -> injunction or stay.
3. **Check threshold limits**: legal effect, functus officio, standing, hypothetical controversy, balance of convenience, undertaking as to damages.
4. **State the Queensland statutory hooks** where relevant: `ADJR Act s 16`, `JR Act Parts 3 and 5`, `JR Act ss 43-44`.
5. **Remember the bottom line**: remedies police legality, not merits, and there is no general damages remedy in administrative law.
