# Workshop 8: Standing

## Core idea
Standing asks whether the plaintiff's connection to the dispute is sufficient to let them invoke the court or tribunal's review jurisdiction. It is a threshold issue about who may challenge public power, not whether the challenge ultimately succeeds.

## Why standing matters
- Standing filters out claims by mere strangers, cranks, and busybodies.
- It helps ensure concrete adverseness and better-quality litigation.
- But overly strict standing can reduce access to justice and weaken the rule of law by insulating unlawful government action from review.

## 1. Declarations and injunctions: the common law starting point

### Attorney-General as traditional guardian of public rights
- Historically, public rights were vindicated by the Attorney-General, either `ex officio` or by relator action.
- Refusal of a fiat generally leaves private plaintiffs to establish their own standing.

### The `Boyce v Paddington` exceptions
A private plaintiff may sue without the Attorney-General where:
- a private right of the plaintiff is also interfered with; or
- the plaintiff suffers special damage from the interference with the public right.

### Modern Australian test: `special interest`
`Australian Conservation Foundation v Commonwealth` recasts the second `Boyce` exception. The plaintiff must show a `special interest in the subject matter of the action`.

Key propositions from `ACF`:
- the interest must be greater than that of an ordinary member of the public;
- it must be more than a mere intellectual or emotional concern;
- it need not be unique, proprietary, or purely pecuniary;
- the court asks whether success or failure affects the plaintiff in a practical way.

`ACF` itself failed because the Foundation's environmental concern and organisational objects, without more, were insufficient.

## 2. What counts as a special interest

### `Onus v Alcoa`
Standing was established because the plaintiffs had a close cultural and spiritual relationship to the relics and were affected in a significantly different way from the public at large.

Points to cite:
- no mechanical rule of thumb;
- intensity and proximity of the plaintiff's concern matter;
- community values may influence whether an interest is treated as sufficiently special;
- the plaintiff need only be affected to a substantially greater degree or in a significantly different manner, not uniquely.

### `Bateman's Bay`
- A likely adverse financial impact on the plaintiff's own funeral fund was enough.
- Gaudron, Gummow and Kirby JJ described standing as an enabling, not restrictive, procedural requirement.
- Their Honours were sceptical that the Attorney-General can always be relied on to protect the public interest.

### Liberal and restrictive examples
- `North Coast` and the later `ACF v Minister for Resources` show a liberalising trend for serious, active environmental bodies.
- `Right to Life`, `Friends of Castle Hill`, and `Lock the Gate Alliance` show that organisational concern alone still may not suffice.

## 3. Statutory judicial review: ADJR Act and JR Act

### The statutory formula
- ADJR Act: a `person aggrieved` may seek review.
- JR Act: same formula, defined as a person whose interests are adversely affected.

### Meaning of `person aggrieved`
Authorities such as `Tooheys` and `Marine Engineers` reject a narrow construction:
- no need to show a vested legal right;
- the grievance must be beyond that of an ordinary member of the public;
- the danger to the plaintiff's interests should be clear and imminent, not remote, indirect, or fanciful.

### Relationship with the common law
`Argos v Corbell` is the key modern authority:
- standing under the ADJR Act is determined by the statute itself;
- common law standing cases help identify relevant kinds of interests;
- the statutory language should not be artificially narrowed.

Exam position: after `Argos`, the best view is that the statutory `person aggrieved / adversely affected` test and the equitable `special interest` test have substantially converged in practice, even though they remain formally distinct sources.

## 4. Public interest organisations
When the plaintiff is an association or lobby group, courts often look cumulatively at:
- the body's objects and purposes;
- whether its activities relate closely to the subject matter;
- participation in the decision-making process;
- government recognition or consultation;
- whether it is a serious or peak body;
- geographic and factual proximity to the impact;
- whether the claimed effect is practical rather than ideological only.

No single factor is decisive. A cumulative case may be enough.

## 5. ART merits review
- Under the `Administrative Review Tribunal Act 2024 (Cth)`, a person whose interests are affected may apply for review.
- Section 15 is especially important for organisations: standing may exist where the decision relates to the organisation's objects or purposes.
- This is generally more flexible than common law standing and can be easier for public interest groups to satisfy.

## 6. Joinder and amici
- Joinder under the ADJR Act and JR Act turns on whether the applicant is sufficiently interested in the matter, using reasoning similar to standing analysis.
- Joinder is discretionary.
- An amicus curiae is not a party and appears only to assist the court on an issue that may otherwise be overlooked.

## 7. Exam-ready authority list
- `ACF v Commonwealth`: special interest required; mere intellectual or emotional concern is not enough.
- `Onus v Alcoa`: close cultural, spiritual, and practical connection can satisfy standing.
- `Bateman's Bay`: financial/commercial detriment can suffice; enabling approach.
- `Tooheys` and `Marine Engineers`: broad reading of `person aggrieved`.
- `Argos`: statutory standing is governed by the statute, but common law cases remain useful; avoid narrow glosses.
- `North Coast` / second `ACF`: liberalisation for credible environmental bodies.
- `Right to Life` / `Lock the Gate Alliance`: standing still depends on concrete effect and factual context.

## 8. Problem-question checklist
1. Identify the remedy sought.
- Declaration or injunction: ask whether the plaintiff has a `special interest`.
- JR Act / ADJR Act review: ask whether the plaintiff is a `person aggrieved` whose interests are adversely affected.
- ART review: ask whether the plaintiff's interests are affected, and for organisations check `s 15`.

2. Identify the plaintiff's interest.
- Private, commercial, financial, proprietary, cultural, environmental, organisational, or participatory.

3. Ask whether the effect is practical and sufficiently close.
- Is it direct, clear, and imminent?
- Or is it remote, speculative, ideological, or no different from the general public?

4. For organisations, build the cumulative case.
- Objects, activities, expertise, government recognition, submissions made, and closeness to the affected area or subject matter.

5. Compare the likely result under the statutory and equitable routes.
- In modern Australian law, outcomes will often be similar because the tests largely converge in substance.
- Still state the source of the test separately and apply each one carefully.

## 9. One-line answer to the policy debate
Standing rules aim to balance two competing values: preventing unmeritorious public law litigation while preserving access to justice and the rule of law by ensuring unlawful exercises of public power can still be challenged.
