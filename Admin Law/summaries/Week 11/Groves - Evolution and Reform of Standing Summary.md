# Thesis
Groves argues that standing remains a necessary threshold in Australian administrative law, but the orthodox `special interest` requirement is too restrictive. His core position is that standing should be retained in judicial review, yet relaxed so it does not obstruct meritorious challenges to unlawful administrative action, especially by environmental and public interest groups.

## Key arguments
- The traditional justification for standing is weakly explained. Courts invoke concerns about excluding `busybodies`, preserving adversarial quality, and relying on the Attorney-General as guardian of the public interest, but Groves argues these rationales are incomplete or outdated.
- There is a tension between restrictive standing and the constitutional role of judicial review. If courts exist to supervise unlawful executive action, a demanding standing threshold can leave illegality uncorrected.
- `Australian Conservation Foundation v Commonwealth` (`ACF`) remains the doctrinal starting point. It requires a `special interest`, meaning more than a mere intellectual or emotional concern, but that formulation disadvantages groups whose concern is public-regarding and widely shared.
- `Onus v Alcoa` shows that `special interest` is contextual rather than mechanical. A claimant need not be uniquely affected, but must be affected to a substantially greater degree or in a significantly different way from the public.
- Under the `ADJR Act`, the formula `person aggrieved` is generally treated as broad and remedial, not narrower than common law standing. Groves reads the case law as tending toward convergence between statutory and common law standing.
- Environmental cases softened `ACF` in practice. Lower courts increasingly accepted standing for environmental groups through a multi-factorial assessment of their objects, history, expertise, participation, and practical involvement, especially in `North Coast`.
- `Argos` confirms a broader view of affected interests. Economic interests can ground standing under the `ADJR Act`, and the case supports a wider understanding of what counts as an interest, even though it does not fully resolve standing doctrine.

## Doctrinal significance
- The article shows that `ACF` is still formally authoritative, but its restrictive force has been eroded by lower-court practice.
- It explains why environmental standing became the main site of doctrinal innovation: environmental harm is often diffuse, so orthodox tests based on distinct personal affectation work poorly.
- Groves treats `Argos` as important less for commercial standing alone and more because it reinforces a broad, practical conception of interests and supports further liberalisation.
- The article links standing doctrine to rule-of-law values. A standing rule focused too heavily on the claimant rather than the legality of executive action can undermine judicial review's supervisory function.

## Reform position
- Groves rejects full `open standing` as too radical and insufficiently justified.
- He is cautious about UK-style common law liberalisation because it relies heavily on judicial discretion and creates uncertainty.
- His preferred reform is statutory relaxation, particularly by adopting an approach similar to the `Administrative Appeals Tribunal Act`, where representative bodies can show standing by reference to their objects or purposes.
- He also treats the `EPBC Act` standing provisions as a partial codification of the more liberal environmental cases, though not a complete solution.

## Exam use takeaways
- Start with `ACF`: standing usually requires a `special interest`, not mere concern or ideological commitment.
- Use `Onus` to show that the test is comparative and contextual, not a demand for unique affectation.
- For environmental or representative groups, discuss the lower-court multi-factorial approach from `North Coast` and related cases.
- For statutory review, note that `person aggrieved` under the `ADJR Act` is broad and remedial, with `Argos` showing that practical economic effects can suffice.
- In essay questions, Groves is useful for the argument that standing should facilitate, not frustrate, the rule-of-law function of judicial review.
- In reform questions, his preferred position is: keep standing, but relax and clarify it by legislation rather than abolishing it or leaving reform entirely to judicial discretion.
