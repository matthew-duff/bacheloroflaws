# Argos Pty Ltd v Corbell

## Why this case matters
- Leading High Court authority on who is a `person aggrieved` under the `Administrative Decisions (Judicial Review) Act 1989 (ACT)`.
- Confirms that practical economic harm to an existing business can be enough for standing.
- Rejects an unduly narrow approach that treats commercial detriment as automatically too remote.

## Facts
- The ACT Minister approved a new commercial development at Giralang under the `Planning and Development Act 2007 (ACT)`.
- Nearby supermarket operators argued that the approval would reduce their turnover and profitability.
- Their landlord also claimed standing, arguing that harm to the tenant’s business might indirectly affect the lease.
- The issue was whether they were `persons aggrieved` under `s 5(1)`, read with `s 3B(1)(a)` of the ACT `AD(JR) Act`.

## Core holding
- The supermarket operators had standing because the evidence showed the approval would likely cause them a real loss in profitability.
- The landlord did not have standing on the majority view because its alleged loss depended on further contingencies, such as the tenant’s possible failure.
- The Court therefore treated the operators’ interests as sufficiently adversely affected, but the landlord’s interests as too indirect on the facts found.

## Key principles

### 1. Economic interests can found standing
- A person may be a `person aggrieved` where a decision is likely to cause practical commercial harm.
- Standing is not confined to interference with legal rights or proprietary interests.
- If a business can show likely loss of profitability caused by the decision, that can be enough.

### 2. No general rule against competitor standing
- The Court rejected the idea that `mere detriment to the economic interests of a business` will generally fail.
- The question is whether the applicant’s interests are adversely affected in a real and practical way, not whether the applicant is simply complaining about competition in the abstract.

### 3. "Remoteness" is descriptive, not a separate legal test
- Words like `directness`, `remoteness`, and `proximity` may describe the evaluative conclusion, but they are not controlling rules.
- Once the evidence established that the supermarket operators would suffer a non-insignificant loss in profitability, no further inquiry into remoteness was required.

### 4. Standing under the AD(JR) Act is not narrowed by the policy of the source statute
- The standing criterion is supplied by the `AD(JR) Act` itself.
- The statute under which the impugned decision was made may help identify the decision’s legal and practical operation.
- But courts should not shrink the AD(JR) standing test by asking whether the applicant’s interests coincide with the policy or objects of that other statute.

### 5. The applicant must show something beyond the position of the general public
- The required interest is one genuinely and adversely affected by the decision.
- The supermarket operators met that threshold because the predicted financial loss was specific to them.
- The landlord failed on the majority view because its alleged loss was only a possible downstream consequence.

## Majority and dissent
- Majority: French CJ, Hayne, Bell and Keane JJ held that the supermarket operators had standing, but the landlord did not.
- Gageler J dissented on the landlord issue and would have treated the landlord’s economic risk as sufficiently real to confer standing as well.

## Exam use
- Cite `Argos` when a problem question asks whether a competitor can challenge an administrative decision under a `person aggrieved` formula.
- Use it to argue that proven economic loss is enough even if the applicant has no private right to be free from competition.
- Use it to resist arguments that standing depends on the applicant advancing the same public interest pursued by the empowering statute.
- Distinguish applicants with direct evidence of adverse economic effect from applicants whose alleged loss depends on speculation or multiple contingencies.

## Bottom line
- `Argos` shows that under the AD(JR) Act, demonstrated practical economic harm can make a competitor a `person aggrieved`, but indirect or contingent commercial effects may still fall short on the facts.
