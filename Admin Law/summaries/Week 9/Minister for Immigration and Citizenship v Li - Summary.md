# Minister for Immigration and Citizenship v Li

## Citation
*Minister for Immigration and Citizenship v Li* [2013] HCA 18; (2013) 249 CLR 332 (HCA).

## Facts
- Ms Li applied for a skilled migration visa. A valid and favourable skills assessment was necessary at the time of decision.
- Her original skills assessment had been affected by false information provided by her former migration agent, so the Minister's delegate refused the visa.
- Ms Li sought merits review in the Migration Review Tribunal (`MRT`) and, through a new migration agent, obtained a fresh assessment process with Trades Recognition Australia (`TRA`).
- Before the MRT decided the review, Ms Li asked it to wait for the outcome of TRA's review of an adverse second assessment. Her agent identified specific errors in that assessment and indicated the matter was still being pursued.
- The MRT refused to wait, saying Ms Li had already been given enough opportunities to present her case and that it was not prepared to delay the matter further. It then affirmed the delegate's refusal.
- After the MRT decision, TRA in fact issued a successful skills assessment on 12 April 2010.

## Issues
- Was the MRT's refusal to adjourn or defer its decision legally unreasonable?
- Did that refusal amount to jurisdictional error?
- Did the circumstances also involve a denial of procedural fairness?

## Held
- Yes. The High Court dismissed the Minister's appeal.
- The MRT's refusal to defer its decision was unreasonable and therefore affected by jurisdictional error.
- French CJ also concluded that the refusal denied Ms Li a reasonable opportunity to obtain material central to her case, amounting to procedural unfairness.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- The power to adjourn in `s 363(1)(b)` of the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth) was discretionary, but not legally unconfined. Like any statutory discretion, it had to be exercised according to the statute's subject matter, scope and purpose.
- The plurality explained that legal unreasonableness is not limited to the old shorthand of a decision no reasonable decision-maker could make. The operative question is whether the exercise of power can be justified within the statutory framework.
- Their Honours stated that unreasonableness may be found where a decision lacks an "evident and intelligible justification". That formulation became the leading modern expression of legal unreasonableness in Australian administrative law.
- Here, the MRT focused only on the idea that Ms Li had already had "enough opportunities" to present her case. It did not meaningfully engage with the fact that the requested delay concerned material that went directly to a live visa criterion and might be obtained in the near future.
- The MRT gave no practical countervailing reason for refusing the short delay. Any general objective of efficiency had to be weighed against the statutory purpose of allowing an applicant a fair opportunity to present evidence and argument on the issues arising in the review.
- On the facts, the abrupt refusal to wait was arbitrary. The result itself supported an inference that the discretion had not been exercised according to law.
- French CJ additionally framed the problem as one of procedural fairness: the refusal denied Ms Li a reasonable opportunity to obtain and present the further skills assessment essential to her case.

## Significance
- `Li` is a leading High Court authority on legal unreasonableness as a ground of judicial review.
- The case shifts the focus away from treating `Wednesbury` as a rigid verbal formula and toward asking whether the decision has an evident and intelligible justification in light of the statute.
- It also shows that an apparently procedural case about refusing an adjournment can amount to jurisdictional error when the refusal cuts off a fair and rational opportunity to address a dispositive issue.

## Exam Use
- Use `Li` whenever a discretion has been exercised abruptly, arbitrarily, or without a convincing explanation tied to the statute.
- It is especially useful for adjournment or timing decisions, where a tribunal or administrator refuses a short delay even though the missing material goes directly to a criterion or issue that remains live.
- Quote the modern test as whether the decision lacked an "evident and intelligible justification", then connect that conclusion to the statutory purpose and the practical consequences of the decision.
- Pair `Li` with procedural fairness arguments where the unreasonable exercise of discretion also deprived the person of a fair opportunity to present important material.
