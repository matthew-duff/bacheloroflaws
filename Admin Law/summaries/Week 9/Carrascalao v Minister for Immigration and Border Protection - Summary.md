# Carrascalao v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection

## Citation
*Carrascalao v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection* [2017] FCAFC 107; (2017) 252 FCR 352 (FCFCA).

## Facts
- This was a joint Full Court judicial review challenge by Mr Carrascalao and Mr Taulahi to fresh ministerial decisions made on 14 December 2016 cancelling their visas under `s 501(3)` of the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth).
- Earlier that same day, the Full Court had set aside previous cancellation decisions and ordered both men be released from immigration detention.
- About four hours later, the Minister again cancelled both visas after receiving new departmental briefs shortly beforehand.
- Mr Carrascalao had lived in Australia since childhood, held a transitional permanent visa, had extensive family ties in Australia, and had a criminal record including a 2007 common assault conviction. The Minister also relied on his past involvement with the Bandidos OMCG.
- Mr Taulahi had lived in Australia for about 25 years, had a resident partner visa, Australian citizen family members, and a criminal history. The Minister relied principally on his association with the Lone Wolf OMCG and suspicion that the group was involved in criminal conduct.
- Both applicants argued that, given the volume of material and the very short time available, the Minister could not have genuinely considered the merits before acting without natural justice.

## Issues
- Did `s 501(3)` require the Minister to engage in an active intellectual process when deciding whether to cancel the visas?
- Was the Minister entitled to rely on departmental summaries rather than personally read every piece of underlying material?
- Did the Minister fail to consider the applicants' material or fail to consider whether it was in the national interest to proceed without natural justice?
- Did the Minister misconstrue or unreasonably apply the `national interest` criterion in `s 501(3)(d)`?

## Held
- Yes. The Minister was required to engage in an active intellectual process in assessing the merits before exercising the personal power under `s 501(3)`.
- Staff assistance and summaries are permissible, but only subject to important limits; a materially deficient summary may support an inference that the Ministerial process was legally inadequate.
- Ground 1 succeeded for both applicants: there was insufficient time for the Minister to undertake the legally required consideration.
- Grounds 2, 3 and 4 were rejected. In Mr Taulahi's case, grounds 7 and 8 were also rejected.
- The Full Court set aside both cancellation decisions and ordered the applicants' release from detention.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- In the judicial review context, to "consider" material means more than formal acknowledgment. It requires an active intellectual process directed to the merits of the particular case.
- The Court drew on *Tickner v Chapman* to explain that a Minister who must decide personally may be assisted by staff, but cannot substitute someone else's evaluation for his own consideration of the issues.
- Departmental summaries are not inherently impermissible. They can be used if they accurately and sufficiently convey the relevant material, especially factual material. But if a summary is materially deficient, or if the document itself indicates the Minister should personally inspect underlying material, reliance on the summary may be insufficient.
- On the facts, the Minister did not have enough time to do what his detailed reasons claimed he had done. The Court inferred that the required active intellectual process had not occurred because the Minister had, at most, only a very short period to review lengthy submissions, attachments, and draft reasons in both matters before deciding.
- The seriousness of visa cancellation, the absence of natural justice under `s 501(5)`, and the fact that the decisions were made personally by the Minister strengthened the need for careful engagement with the material.
- The Court rejected the separate argument that the Minister had failed to consider Mr Carrascalao's solicitor's further material: unlike Ground 1, that narrower complaint was not made out because the material was before the Minister and summarised without shown deficiency.
- On `national interest`, the Court held the concept is broad and largely political in character. It is for the Minister to decide which national interest factors are engaged in a given case, provided the satisfaction is reached reasonably.
- The Minister was not legally required to advert to every possible factor that might bear on the national interest. Therefore, the absence of an express reference to the best interests of children in the national-interest part of the reasons did not itself show misconstruction.

## Significance
- This is an important administrative law authority on when a court may infer that a personal Ministerial decision was not genuinely made through the legally required decisional process.
- It shows that "active intellectual process" is a practical judicial review standard: extreme time pressure, lengthy materials, and formulaic reasons can together support an inference of jurisdictional error.
- It also clarifies the relationship between personal Ministerial power and departmental assistance: staff may summarise, but they cannot replace the Minister's own decisional engagement.
- The case is also useful for showing the breadth of `national interest` in migration decisions and the limits of trying to turn every potentially relevant matter into a mandatory consideration.

## Exam Use
- Use this case where a statute requires a Minister personally to decide a matter: argue that the decision-maker must actually engage in an active intellectual process, not just sign off on a briefing.
- Use it when facts suggest implausibly short consideration time, especially where the record shows lengthy submissions, serious consequences, and detailed reasons that would have required real engagement.
- Pair it with *Tickner v Chapman* on the meaning of "consider" and the limits of relying on staff summaries.
- Use it against arguments that a broad criterion like `national interest` obliges the Minister to expressly address every conceivable factor; the question is whether the Minister's satisfaction was lawfully and reasonably formed, not whether every possible consideration was listed.
