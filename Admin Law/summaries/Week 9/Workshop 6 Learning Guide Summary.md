# Workshop 6 Learning Guide Summary

## 1. Doctrinal Structure

This workshop moves from **simple ultra vires** to **extended ultra vires**. The key ADJR hook is **improper exercise of power** under `ADJR Act s 5(1)(e)` and `s 6(1)(e)`, elaborated by `s 5(2)`.

Core grounds covered:

- **Irrelevant considerations**: taking into account matters that are not legally germane.
- **Relevant considerations**: failing to consider matters the statute requires to be considered.
- **Improper purpose**: using power for a purpose other than the one for which Parliament conferred it.
- **Legal unreasonableness**: exercise of power so unreasonable that it exceeds the bounds of lawful authority.

The recurring theme is **statutory construction**: what did Parliament authorize, for what purpose, and subject to what limits?

## 2. Wednesbury as the Starting Point

`Associated Provincial Picture Houses v Wednesbury Corporation` is the foundation case for judicial review of discretionary power.

Exam points:

- Courts are **not courts of appeal** in judicial review.
- A decision-maker must:
  - consider matters they are bound to consider;
  - disregard irrelevant matters;
  - exercise the discretion genuinely and according to law.
- "Unreasonableness" can be used broadly to capture multiple legal errors, but as an independent ground it sets a **very high threshold**.
- The court asks whether the decision is one **no reasonable authority could have made**, not whether the judge disagrees with it.

Use `Wednesbury` to frame the **legality/merits distinction** and the overlap between improper purpose, irrelevant considerations, bad faith, and unreasonableness.

## 3. Improper Purpose

### Rule

A power must be exercised **for the purpose for which it was conferred**. This is codified in `ADJR Act s 5(2)(c)`.

### How courts identify purpose

- Start with the statute's **text, structure, and title**.
- If purpose is not express, infer it from the Act as a whole.
- The court may use extrinsic materials where appropriate.
- The court then asks what purpose actually motivated the decision-maker.

### Authority: `Schlieske v Minister for Immigration`

Key point:

- Migration powers could be used for **immigration control**, but not as a disguised means of achieving **extradition** after extradition processes had failed.

Why it matters:

- It shows the distinction between a lawful outcome and an unlawful purpose.
- Even where the same practical result may later occur, the **power must be exercised for the right legal reason**.

Exam formula:

- Identify statutory purpose.
- Identify actual motivating purpose.
- Ask whether the latter is outside the former.

## 4. Relevant and Irrelevant Considerations

### Rule

Under `ADJR Act s 5(2)(a)-(b)`, a decision may be unlawful if the decision-maker:

- considered an irrelevant matter; or
- failed to consider a mandatory relevant matter.

### Method

- Determine what considerations actually influenced the decision.
- Determine, by **statutory construction**, which considerations were legally relevant or irrelevant.
- Distinguish between:
  - a matter that is legally mandatory to consider; and
  - the weight to be given to that matter, which is usually for the decision-maker.

### Authority: `Murphyores v Commonwealth`

Key point:

- Where the statute confers broad power and does not narrowly confine relevant criteria, a minister may be entitled to consider a wide range of matters, including environmental effects.

Exam takeaway:

- Relevancy grounds do not ask what seems sensible in the abstract.
- They ask what the **statute makes relevant**.

## 5. Legal Unreasonableness

### Rule

Legal unreasonableness rests on the idea that Parliament is **not taken to have intended** power to be exercised arbitrarily, irrationally, capriciously, or without an evident and intelligible justification.

### Wednesbury threshold

- Traditionally expressed as a decision so unreasonable that **no reasonable authority** could have made it.
- Applied cautiously because courts must avoid slipping into merits review.

### Modern Australian authority: `Minister for Immigration v Li`

Key point:

- The MRT acted unlawfully by refusing a short adjournment while Li sought review of a flawed skills assessment.
- The decision lacked an **evident and intelligible justification** and was therefore legally unreasonable.

Exam significance:

- `Li` modernizes the language of unreasonableness without abandoning the legality/merits distinction.
- French CJ's language of **lawfulness, fairness and rationality** is central.

## 6. Human Rights Through Administrative Law

Australia has no general federal bill of rights, so human rights arguments commonly enter administrative law through:

- **statutory interpretation**;
- **grounds of review**;
- sometimes **procedural fairness**.

### Principle of legality

Courts do not impute to Parliament an intention to curtail fundamental rights unless that intention is expressed with **unmistakable and unambiguous language**.

Authorities to cite:

- `Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth`
- `Coco v The Queen`
- `Evans v New South Wales`

Key function:

- If the text is open to more than one construction, courts prefer the rights-compatible one.

### International law and human rights

- There is a presumption that legislation is intended to conform with international law, where the statutory language permits.
- But unincorporated treaties do **not** automatically create domestic rights.
- `Teoh` recognized that treaty ratification may create a **legitimate expectation** for procedural fairness purposes.
- That reasoning was later heavily qualified by `Ex parte Lam`.

### Important caution

The source stresses that Australian courts are generally more comfortable protecting **common law rights** than directly using **international human rights law** as a free-standing source of administrative law obligations.

## 7. Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld)

### Main model

The Queensland HRA adopts a **dialogue model**:

- Parliament remains sovereign.
- Courts interpret legislation compatibly with rights where possible.
- Public entities must act compatibly with human rights.
- Parliament can still legislate incompatibly, including through override declarations.

### Protected rights and limits

- Human rights are listed in `ss 15-37`.
- Only **individuals**, not corporations, hold rights under the Act: `s 11`.
- Rights may be limited only by **reasonable limits demonstrably justified in a free and democratic society**: `s 13`.

For `s 13`, look for:

- the nature of the right;
- the importance of the limitation's purpose;
- rational connection between the limit and that purpose;
- whether less restrictive alternatives were available;
- overall proportionality.

### Public entity obligations: `s 58`

Public entities must:

- act and make decisions compatibly with human rights; and
- give **proper consideration** to relevant human rights.

Public entities include:

- **core public entities** such as government departments, police, ministers acting administratively, local governments;
- **functional public entities** when performing functions of a public nature.

Exceptions exist where, for example, the entity could not reasonably act differently because another law required the result.

### Piggy-back action: `s 59`

The HRA does **not** create a standalone cause of action.

Instead:

- a claimant must already have an independent cause of action or basis for relief;
- the HRA breach can then be added as a supplementary ground of unlawfulness;
- damages are unavailable.

### Legislative scrutiny and interpretation

- **Statements of compatibility**: `s 38`
- **Committee scrutiny**: `s 39`
- **Override declarations**: `ss 43-46`
- **Rights-compatible interpretation**: `s 48`
- **Declarations of incompatibility**: `s 53`

### Complaints to the QHRC

- Complain first to the relevant public entity.
- The entity has **45 business days** to respond: `s 65`.
- If unresolved, complain to the **Queensland Human Rights Commission**.
- The process is conciliatory and informal.
- If still unresolved, the Commissioner may report and publish information, but the HRA does not itself provide a complete judicial remedy pathway.

## 8. Authorities to Cite Fast

- `Wednesbury`: legality/merits distinction; relevant vs irrelevant matters; very high unreasonableness threshold.
- `Schlieske`: improper purpose; disguised extradition; power used for an ulterior purpose is unlawful.
- `Murphyores`: what counts as relevant depends on statutory construction and breadth of the power.
- `Li`: modern legal unreasonableness; absence of evident and intelligible justification.
- `Coco`, `Plaintiff S157`, `Evans`: principle of legality and rights-compatible interpretation.
- `Teoh`, `Lam`: treaty ratification and procedural fairness, but with major limits.

## 9. Compact Problem-Question Checklist

When answering a problem question, move in this order:

1. **Identify the source of power**.
   - What statute confers the power?
   - Is the challenge under common law, the ADJR Act, or both?

2. **Identify the exact ground**.
   - Improper purpose?
   - Relevant/irrelevant considerations?
   - Legal unreasonableness?
   - HRA unlawfulness?

3. **Construct the statute carefully**.
   - What purpose does the Act reveal?
   - What considerations are mandatory, permissible, or forbidden?
   - Is there language affecting rights?

4. **Apply the leading authority**.
   - `Schlieske` for ulterior purpose.
   - `Murphyores` for breadth of relevant considerations.
   - `Li` and `Wednesbury` for unreasonable discretion.
   - `Coco` / `Plaintiff S157` / `Evans` for rights-compatible interpretation.

5. **For HRA questions, ask separately**.
   - Is the respondent a **public entity**?
   - Which right is engaged?
   - Was the right properly considered?
   - Is any limitation justified under `s 13`?
   - Is there an **independent cause of action** to support a `s 59` piggy-back claim?

6. **Conclude on legality, not merits**.
   - The issue is not whether the decision was preferable.
   - The issue is whether the decision-maker stayed within the legal limits of the power.

## 10. One-Line Exam Thesis

Workshop 6 is about proving that discretionary power must remain tied to **statutory purpose, legally relevant reasoning, rational justification, and, in Queensland, human-rights-compatible administration**.
