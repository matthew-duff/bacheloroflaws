# Workshop 6: JR Grounds Continued – Illegality, Unreasonableness, and Human Rights

## 1. Extended Ultra Vires (Improper Exercise of Power)
Codified in **AD(JR)A 1977 (Cth) s 5(1)(e)** and **s 5(2)**. These grounds go beyond the text to examine the context and purpose of statutory powers.

### Key Grounds (s 5(2)):
- **(a) Irrelevant consideration**: Taking into account matters not germane to the decision.
- **(b) Relevant consideration**: Failing to take into account matters the decision-maker is bound to consider.
- **(c) Improper purpose**: Exercising power for a purpose other than that for which it was conferred.
- **(g) Legal unreasonableness**: An exercise of power so unreasonable that no reasonable person could have so exercised it (*Wednesbury*).

---

## 2. The *Wednesbury* Principles
*Associated Provincial Picture Houses v Wednesbury Corporation* (1948) established:
- **Non-Interference**: Courts must not substitute their own discretion for that of the authority (not a merits appeal).
- **The "Comprehensive" Sense**: Unreasonableness often acts as an umbrella term for failing to consider relevant matters, considering irrelevant ones, or acting in bad faith.
- **The "Overwhelming" Threshold**: To prove unreasonableness alone, the decision must be "so absurd that no sensible person could ever dream that it lay within the powers of the authority."

---

## 3. Improper Purpose
- **Rule**: Power must be used for its authorized purpose (*ADJR Act s 5(2)(c)*).
- **Identification**: Lawful purposes are implied from the Act's title, structure, and text.
- **Case Study: *Schlieske v Minister for Immigration* (1988)**:
    - **Facts**: Australian officials tried to deport Schlieske to Germany in a way that ensured his arrest by German police (after extradition failed).
    - **Outcome**: Held to be a **"disguised extradition"** (a sham). Deportation power under the *Migration Act* is for immigration control, not to aid foreign powers in bringing fugitives to justice.

---

## 4. Relevant and Irrelevant Considerations
- **Irrelevant Considerations**: Decision is invalid unless the consideration is insignificant.
    - *Murphyores v Commonwealth* (1976): The Minister could consider environmental impacts when deciding on export permits, even if not explicitly stated, because the power was broad and "open-textured."
- **Relevant Considerations**: Mandatory matters are identified through statutory construction (language, object, and purpose).

---

## 5. Legal Unreasonableness (Modern Application)
- **Presumption**: Parliament is presumed not to intend to authorize unreasonable power.
- **Case Study: *Minister for Immigration v Li* (2013)**:
    - **Facts**: The MRT refused to delay a hearing while Li sought a review of a flawed skills assessment, despite her migration agent's request.
    - **Outcome**: The High Court found the MRT's refusal was **legally unreasonable**. It was an arbitrary exercise of discretion that lacked an evident and intelligible justification.
    - **Core Values**: "Lawfulness, fairness and rationality" lie at the heart of administrative justice.

---

## 6. Judicial Review and Human Rights

### The Principle of Legality
- Courts will not interpret legislation as abrogating fundamental rights unless the intention is manifested by **"unmistakable and unambiguous language"** (*Plaintiff S157*, *Coco v The Queen*).
- *Evans v NSW* (2008): A regulation allowing police to stop conduct that "causes annoyance" at World Youth Day was invalid because it interfered with free speech without clear statutory authority.

### International Law
- Presumption that Parliament intends to legislate in accordance with international obligations (if the statute is ambiguous).
- *Teoh* (1995): Ratification of a treaty (e.g., CROC) can give rise to a **legitimate expectation** that the decision-maker will act consistently with it (though this has been significantly limited by *Ex parte Lam*).

---

## 7. Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld)

### Public Entity Obligations (s 58)
- **Duty**: Public entities must (1) act/make decisions compatibly with human rights, and (2) give **proper consideration** to relevant human rights.
- **Public Entity (s 9)**: Includes "core" entities (government departments, police, ministers) and "functional" entities (private bodies performing public functions like private prisons).

### The "Piggy-back" Action (s 59)
- The HRA does not provide a standalone cause of action.
- **Mechanism**: A person can only raise a human rights breach in court if they have an **independent** ground for legal challenge (e.g., a standard judicial review application).
- **Remedy**: No damages are available for human rights breaches under the HRA.

### Interpretation and Dialogue
- **s 48**: Courts must interpret Queensland laws compatibly with human rights "to the extent possible consistent with their purpose."
- **s 53**: The Supreme Court can issue a **Declaration of Incompatibility** if a law cannot be interpreted compatibly (this does not invalidate the law, but triggers a parliamentary response).
- **Override Declarations (s 43)**: Parliament can explicitly declare a law operates despite human rights (intended for exceptional circumstances).
