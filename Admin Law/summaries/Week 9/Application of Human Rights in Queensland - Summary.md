# Application of Human Rights in Queensland

## Core idea
Chapter 5 explains pt 3 of the `Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld)` as a **dialogue model**. Parliament, courts, and the executive each have a distinct role in protecting human rights, but **parliamentary sovereignty is preserved**. The HRA does not entrench rights constitutionally and does not let courts strike down legislation.

## Statutory framework

### 1. Parliamentary scrutiny of legislation
- `s 38`: every Bill must have a **statement of compatibility** saying whether it is compatible with human rights and, if not, the nature and extent of the incompatibility.
- The chapter treats `s 38` as a key dialogic mechanism because it forces early, reasoned, rights-based justification in the legislative process.
- Statements of compatibility are **not binding** on courts or tribunals, but may still matter as extrinsic material in later interpretation.
- `s 41`: subordinate legislation must be accompanied by a **human rights certificate**.
- `s 42` and `s 47`: failure to comply with these procedural requirements does **not affect validity**.

### 2. Override declarations
- `s 43`: Parliament may expressly declare that legislation operates despite incompatibility with human rights.
- Override declarations are intended only for **exceptional circumstances** such as war, emergency, or crisis.
- `s 44`: the member introducing such a Bill must explain the exceptional circumstances.
- `s 45`: the override disapplies the HRA to the extent of the declaration and generally expires after **5 years**, though `s 46` permits re-enactment.
- The chapter treats override declarations as politically significant but legally reflective of the fact that the HRA preserves parliamentary supremacy.

## Courts and interpretation

### 3. Interpretive obligation under `s 48`
- `s 48(1)`: all statutory provisions must be interpreted, **so far as possible consistently with their purpose**, in a way compatible with human rights.
- `s 48(2)`: if full compatibility is impossible, adopt the interpretation that is **most compatible** with human rights.
- `s 48(3)`: courts may consider international law and relevant domestic, foreign, and international jurisprudence.
- `s 48(4)`: incompatibility does **not** itself affect validity.
- `s 48(5)`: the section does not apply where an override declaration is in force.

### 4. How strong is `s 48`?
- The chapter stresses that `s 48` is **not a UK-style remedial power**. Courts cannot rewrite legislation contrary to statutory purpose.
- The preferred analogy is orthodox statutory interpretation under `Project Blue Sky`, but overlaid with the HRA's compatibility and proportionality requirements.
- If the ordinary meaning limits a right, the court asks whether that limit is **reasonable and demonstrably justifiable** under `s 13`.
- If several meanings are open, choose the one that best protects rights consistently with statutory purpose.

### 5. Authorities on interpretation
- `Momcilovic v The Queen`: major influence on the drafting of `s 48`; supports a restrained, non-remedial approach.
- `Slaveski v Smith` and `Nigro v Secretary, Department of Justice`: support the view that charter-style interpretation works through orthodox construction, not judicial rewriting.
- `Australian Institute for Progress Ltd v Electoral Commission of Queensland`: Queensland authority showing that `s 48` directs attention to statutory purpose plus compatibility, including `s 8` and `s 13`.
- `Christian Youth Camps Ltd v Cobaw Community Health Services Ltd`: used by analogy on non-retrospectivity concerns.

### 6. Referrals, interventions, declarations
- `s 49`: courts or tribunals may refer HRA questions to the Supreme Court if a party applies and referral is appropriate.
- `ss 50-52`: Attorney-General and QHRC may intervene; notice obligations support the institutional dialogue model.
- `s 53`: only the Supreme Court or Court of Appeal may make a **declaration of incompatibility** where a statutory provision cannot be interpreted compatibly with human rights.
- A declaration cannot be made if an override declaration is in force.
- The court must notify the Attorney-General and QHRC and give them a reasonable opportunity to intervene or make submissions.
- `s 54`: a declaration of incompatibility does **not affect validity**, create legal rights, or itself give rise to a civil cause of action.
- `ss 55-57`: the declaration is sent to the Attorney-General, then the responsible Minister and portfolio committee, preserving parliamentary control over any response.

## Public entities

### 7. Duties under `s 58`
- `s 58(1)(a)`: it is unlawful for a public entity to act or decide in a way that is **not compatible with human rights**.
- `s 58(1)(b)`: it is unlawful to fail to give **proper consideration** to a human right relevant to the decision.
- The chapter treats these as **distinct substantive and procedural duties**.
- `s 58(5)` explains proper consideration as including:
  - identifying the human rights that may be affected; and
  - considering whether the decision would be compatible with human rights.

### 8. Meaning of proper consideration
- Proper consideration requires a decision-maker to **seriously turn their mind** to the relevant rights, the impact of the decision, and countervailing interests.
- It is not meant to require public officials to reason like judges or produce elaborate legal analysis.
- But the obligation is more exacting than a superficial acknowledgment of rights.

### 9. Authorities on public entity obligations
- `Innes v Electoral Commission of Queensland (No 2)`: important Queensland authority on `s 58`, proportionality, and the possibility that a class-based effect is enough to engage human rights.
- `Owen-D'Arcy v Chief Executive, Queensland Corrective Services`: supports careful but practical use of comparative charter jurisprudence and addresses `s 58` obligations.
- `PJB v Melbourne Health` (`Patrick's Case`), `Castles`, and `Certain Children (No 1)`: Victorian authorities heavily relied on for the content of proper consideration and proportionality review.

### 10. Exceptions and consequences
- `s 58(2)`: no unlawfulness if the entity could not reasonably have acted differently because of another law.
- `s 58(3)`: exception for religious bodies in the specified circumstances.
- `s 58(4)`: no application to acts or decisions of a private nature.
- `s 58(6)`: breach does **not automatically invalidate** the act or decision and does not itself create an offence.
- The chapter suggests breach of `s 58(1)` is best understood as **unlawfulness**, but not necessarily jurisdictional error.

## Remedies and proceedings

### 11. The piggyback action under `s 59`
- `s 59` is the **only** way to seek relief on a ground of unlawfulness arising under `s 58`.
- There is **no stand-alone cause of action** for breach of human rights by a public entity.
- A claimant must already be able to seek some relief or remedy on another ground of unlawfulness.
- Once that condition is satisfied, the claimant may add the `s 58` unlawfulness point, even if the independent ground ultimately fails.
- `s 59(3)`: **no damages** are available merely for unlawfulness under `s 58`.
- `s 59(4)` preserves other public law remedies such as judicial review, declarations, injunctions, stays, and exclusion of evidence.

### 12. Review intensity
- The chapter argues that `ss 58-59`, read with `s 13`, introduce a form of **proportionality-based judicial review**.
- That does **not** collapse legality into merits review.
- Courts remain supervisory, not substitutionary, but may scrutinise more closely where rights reasoning is thin or absent.
- Better reasons and clearer proportionality analysis by the original decision-maker attract more judicial weight and latitude.

## Exam-use takeaways
- Start by identifying **which pt 3 institution is engaged**: Parliament, court, or public entity.
- If the issue is statutory meaning, apply `s 48`:
  - identify purpose;
  - ask whether a compatible interpretation is available;
  - if not, choose the most compatible interpretation consistent with purpose;
  - remember incompatibility does not itself invalidate legislation.
- If the issue is administrative action by a public entity, apply `s 58`:
  - was there a limit on a human right;
  - was the limit justified under `s 13`;
  - was there proper consideration;
  - do any exceptions in `s 58(2)-(4)` apply?
- If the claimant wants a remedy, ask `s 59` first:
  - is there an independent avenue for relief;
  - if yes, the human rights claim can piggyback;
  - damages are unavailable under `s 59(3)`.
- Always conclude with the structural point: the HRA is **rights-protective but not supreme**. Parliament remains sovereign, declarations of incompatibility do not invalidate laws, and many HRA mechanisms are designed to produce accountability and justification rather than automatic nullity.
