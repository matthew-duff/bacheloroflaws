# R v Toohey; Ex parte Northern Land Council

## Citation
*R v Toohey; Ex parte Northern Land Council* (1981) 151 CLR 170 (Gibbs CJ, Stephen, Mason, Aickin and Wilson JJ; Murphy J dissenting in part).

## Facts
- The Northern Land Council lodged a traditional land claim over a substantial part of the Cox Peninsula under `s 50(1)(a)` of the `Aboriginal Land Rights (Northern Territory) Act 1976 (Cth)`.
- Land in a `town` was excluded from claimable `unalienated Crown land`.
- After the claim was lodged, `reg 5` of the `Planning Regulations (NT)` treated a very large area, including most of the claimed land, as part of Darwin for town-planning purposes.
- Toohey J, acting as Aboriginal Land Commissioner, held that the relevant land was therefore `land in a town` and not claimable.
- The claimants argued that the regulation was invalid or open to challenge because the power had been used for an ulterior or extraneous purpose, namely to defeat the land claim, and sought production of government documents relevant to that issue.

## Issues
1. Could a court review delegated legislation made by the Administrator of the Northern Territory for improper purpose, or was that inquiry barred because the Administrator represented the Crown?
2. Did the Commissioner err in refusing to order production of documents relevant to the alleged improper purpose behind `reg 5`?
3. Was mandamus or certiorari available to correct the Commissioner's approach?

## Held
1. Yes. By majority, the Court held that unless judicial review is excluded, courts may determine whether delegated legislation is within the limits and purposes of the enabling statute, even where the regulation is made by a representative of the Crown.
2. Yes. The Commissioner erred in treating the regulation as effectively immune from that improper-purpose challenge and in refusing document production on that basis.
3. Yes. The matter was remitted to the Commissioner to proceed according to law; mandamus issued, and members of the Court also supported certiorari.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- The majority rejected any broad proposition that regulations made in the name of the Crown are beyond judicial scrutiny as to whether the statutory power has been exercised within legal limits.
- The key distinction was between questioning the political wisdom of legislation and asking a legal question: whether the delegated law was made for a purpose authorized by the statute.
- If a power is conferred for planning purposes, a regulation made for some alien purpose, such as defeating a land-rights claim, may be beyond power.
- Because that was a legally available argument, documents bearing on the purpose behind the regulation could be relevant and should not have been excluded merely because the regulation was made by the Administrator.
- The Commissioner's refusal to entertain that line of challenge distorted the course of the hearing and amounted to a constructive failure to exercise the jurisdiction conferred by the `Land Rights Act`.
- The Court did not finally determine that `reg 5` was invalid; instead, it held that the Commissioner had approached the question on the wrong legal footing and had to reconsider the matter according to law.
- Murphy J took a narrower view, holding that a regulation otherwise within the regulation-making power could not be invalidated merely for bad faith or ulterior purpose because courts do not invalidate legislative acts on that basis.

## Significance
- Major authority for the proposition that delegated legislation is reviewable for improper purpose and other ultra vires errors.
- Useful in distinguishing primary legislation, which courts do not invalidate for improper motive, from delegated legislation, which remains confined by the statute conferring the power.
- Also important on `constructive failure to exercise jurisdiction`: a decision-maker who wrongly excludes a legally available argument may commit jurisdictional error.
- Shows the continuing availability of public law remedies such as mandamus where an administrator misunderstands the scope of the legal inquiry they must undertake.

## Exam Use
- Cite this case when arguing that delegated legislation can be challenged for unauthorized purpose, even if made by high executive authority or in the name of the Crown.
- Use it where a tribunal or administrator wrongly refuses to consider evidence or arguments because they misunderstand the legal scope of review.
- Pair it with other improper-purpose and ultra vires authorities to show that subordinate legislation must stay within both the text and the purpose of the enabling Act.
