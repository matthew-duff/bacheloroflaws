# Innes v Electoral Commission of Queensland (No 2)

## Citation
*Innes v Electoral Commission of Queensland (No 2)* [2020] QSC 293; 5 QR 623 (Ryan J, Court of Disputed Returns).

## Facts
- Donald Innes unsuccessfully contested the 2020 Sunshine Coast Regional Council mayoral election, held during the early COVID-19 pandemic.
- He argued the election should have been postponed, or run differently, because in-person voting exposed voters to health risks and affected the fairness of the campaign.
- He also alleged the Electoral Commission of Queensland (`ECQ`) acted unlawfully by proceeding with in-person voting, cancelling electoral visitor voting, inadequately handling postal and telephone voting, and failing to properly consider human rights.
- Innes sought to quash the election result and also sought broad access to ECQ documents about the conduct of the elections.

## Issues
1. What is the scope of the Court of Disputed Returns' jurisdiction under `s 136` of the `Local Government Electoral Act 2011 (Qld)`?
2. Could `s 48` of the `Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld)` change the interpretation of the electoral provisions to support broader relief or document production?
3. Did the ECQ breach `s 58` of the `Human Rights Act` procedurally or substantively in the way it conducted the election during COVID-19?
4. If there was any irregularity, was it just and equitable to set aside the result?

## Held
1. No. The Court of Disputed Returns only has jurisdiction to hear disputes about the election of a person, not complaints about how local government elections generally should have been conducted.
2. No. Because `s 136` was not ambiguous, `s 48` of the `Human Rights Act` could not alter its meaning, and `s 140` only supported production of documents relating to the challenged election itself.
3. No. The applicant did not establish procedural or substantive unlawfulness by the ECQ under `s 58`.
4. No. The applicant failed to show that the election result did not reflect the free and deliberate choice of electors, so the application was dismissed.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- The Court emphasised that an election dispute under `Pt 7` is tightly confined: it is about the validity of the election actually held, not a general review of pandemic policy or electoral administration.
- Alleged "unfairness" is not enough by itself. It matters only if it stems from a recognised legal wrong such as breach of the electoral statute, fraud, corruption, intimidation, or similar unlawfulness.
- The election was presumed valid, and the applicant bore the onus of proving invalidity on the balance of probabilities.
- On interpretation, `s 48` of the `Human Rights Act` only operates where a constructional choice is genuinely open. It could not be used to rewrite clear limits in `s 136` or expand `s 140` into a general power to obtain documents about all local government elections.
- Ryan J accepted that the `Human Rights Act` may be engaged in court proceedings in several ways, including statutory interpretation and the "piggy-back" mechanism in `ss 58-59`, but those pathways did not enlarge the Court's jurisdiction.
- The Court adopted, without finally deciding broader doctrinal questions, a functional approach to identifying the human rights relevant to its own role. In that setting, `s 23` of the `Human Rights Act` (taking part in public life) was potentially relevant because the Court's function is to protect the free and deliberate choice of electors.
- The procedural challenge failed because the ECQ was not required to consult the Human Rights Commission, and the absence of express human-rights language in a media release did not show a failure to consider rights.
- The substantive challenge also failed. Where the ECQ's conduct was mandated by law, `s 58(2)` applied. Where decisions involved choice, any limits on participation rights were justified under `s 13` given the public health emergency, the need to protect health and safety, alternative voting methods, and practical limits on postal and telephone systems.
- Even assuming some irregularities, including issues around visitor voting, the applicant did not show they affected the outcome or made it just and equitable to grant relief.

## Significance
- Important authority on the narrow jurisdiction of the Queensland Court of Disputed Returns.
- Shows that the `Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld)` does not give a free-standing basis to relitigate election policy or expand clear statutory powers.
- Useful for the proposition that human-rights arguments in election litigation must still fit within the existing statutory cause of action and remedial structure.
- Illustrates judicial treatment of proportionality and justified limits on participation rights in the COVID-19 context.

## Exam Use
- Cite this case when a problem question tries to turn an election petition into a broader challenge to how an election should have been run.
- Use it to argue that `s 48` interpretation cannot overcome clear statutory text or enlarge jurisdiction where no real ambiguity exists.
- Use it on `ss 58-59` to show that public-entity human-rights obligations do not automatically invalidate conduct where the decision was legally required or any limit on rights was justified under `s 13`.
- Pair it with other election-dispute authorities when discussing the presumption of validity, onus of proof, and the requirement to show a result did not reflect the free and deliberate choice of electors.
