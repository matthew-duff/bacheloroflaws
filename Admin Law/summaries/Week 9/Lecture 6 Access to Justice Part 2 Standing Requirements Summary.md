# Lecture 6 Summary

## 1. Core idea

- Standing is the threshold question of **who is entitled to seek judicial review or related relief**.
- Per *Allan v Transurban*, standing describes the **interest required** to obtain common law, equitable, or constitutional remedies, separate from the substantive review grounds.
- The lecture treats standing across three contexts:
  - **declarations and injunctions**
  - **statutory judicial review under the ADJR Act and JR Act**
  - **public interest litigation**

## 2. Why standing is contested

- Standing reflects competing policy concerns.
- On one side, courts want **concrete adverseness** and to avoid abuse by strangers, busybodies, or cranks: *Kuczborski*; *Onus*.
- On the other side, there is a **public interest in lawful government action being challengeable in court**: *Kuczborski*.
- *Re McHattan* highlights the practical problem of **remoteness**: interests may be affected directly or indirectly, and the court must decide when the effect becomes too remote.
- *Truth About Motorways* says there is **no single universal standing criterion** across all public law remedies.

## 3. Declarations and injunctions: the general law test

### Attorney-General and public rights

- Traditionally, the Attorney-General may sue to enforce public rights and public duties.
- The slides note the distinction between:
  - **ex officio proceedings**
  - **relator actions**, brought with the Attorney-General's fiat
- *Bateman's Bay* is sceptical about assuming the Attorney-General will always protect public rights effectively.

### Private plaintiff standing

- Under *Boyce v Paddington*, a private plaintiff may sue without the Attorney-General where:
  1. a **private right** of the plaintiff is also interfered with; or
  2. the plaintiff suffers **special damage** from interference with a public right.
- In Australian law, *Australian Conservation Foundation* restates this in terms of a **special interest in the subject matter of the action**.

### Special interest

- The plaintiff must show an interest **greater than that of an ordinary member of the public**: *ACF*; *Onus*.
- A **mere intellectual or emotional concern** is not enough: *ACF*.
- The interest does not need to be unique, but it must be **practical, close, and real**.
- *Onus* emphasises there is **no mechanical rule of thumb**. The court evaluates:
  - the importance of the plaintiff's concern
  - the closeness of the plaintiff's relationship to the subject matter
- Brennan J's approach in *Onus* also supports the point that a plaintiff need not be **uniquely affected**.

### Liberalising suggestion

- *Bateman's Bay* contains obiter suggesting a less restrictive approach:
  - ask whether there is a **justiciable controversy**
  - deal with weak claims through **abuse of process, oppression, vexation, discretion, and costs**
- This does not abolish standing doctrine, but it points to a more flexible and less formalistic approach in some cases.

## 4. ADJR Act and JR Act standing

### Statutory formula

- Under the ADJR Act and JR Act, standing turns on whether the applicant is a **person aggrieved**.
- The slides state that this includes a person **whose interests are adversely affected by the decision**.

### Meaning of adverse effect

- The statutory test is concerned with whether the decision has a sufficiently serious effect on the applicant's interests.
- *Broadbridge v Stammers* is the lecture's example of a straightforward case:
  - the relevant danger or peril to the applicant's interests must be **clear and imminent**
  - not **remote, indirect, or fanciful**
  - the applicant's interest must be stronger than that of an ordinary member of the public

### Relationship with the general law

- *Australian Institute of Marine and Power Engineers* suggests it would be odd if the ADJR Act imposed a **narrower** standing test than the general law.
- *Argos v Corbell* confirms that common law standing authorities help explain the kinds of adverse effects relevant under the statute.
- Exam position: the tests remain formally distinct, but the lecture suggests they are **substantially converging in practice**.

## 5. Standing in public interest litigation

### Liberal authorities

- Some cases show a more liberal willingness to find standing for public interest bodies.
- *North Coast Environment Council* indicates that a serious environmental organisation may establish standing where its activities and connection to the issue are substantial.
- The slides identify factors suggesting standing such as:
  - being a **peak body**
  - **government recognition**
  - relevant **activities** tied to the subject matter
- *Nth Qld Conservation Council* treats peak status as relevant mainly because it shows the body is **serious and responsible**.
- *Animals' Angels* supports a broader view:
  - consider government recognition, activities, objects, and purposes
  - a special interest need not be the **strongest** or a **unique** interest

### Restrictive authorities

- Other cases are more restrictive.
- *Save the Ridge* expressed doubts about the liberal approach in *Nth Qld Conservation Council*.
- *Lock the Gate Alliance* expressed further doubts.
- *Right to Life Association* downplayed the significance of government recognition.
- *Friends of Castle Hill* applied a restrictive approach to a local environmental group.

### Practical takeaway

- Public interest standing remains **fact-sensitive and contested**.
- For organisations, courts look cumulatively at matters such as:
  - objects and purposes
  - actual activities
  - seriousness and responsibility
  - government recognition or participation
  - proximity between the body and the decision's practical impact

## 6. Exam-use section

### Short standing framework

1. Identify the **remedy sought**.
2. If seeking a **declaration or injunction**, ask whether the plaintiff has a **special interest** in the subject matter.
3. If proceeding under the **ADJR Act or JR Act**, ask whether the applicant is a **person aggrieved** whose interests are **adversely affected**.
4. Ask whether the claimed effect is **practical, close, and non-remote**, rather than ideological only.
5. For public interest bodies, build a cumulative argument from the organisation's **objects, activities, recognition, and factual connection** to the decision.
6. Cite both **liberal** and **restrictive** authorities if the facts are borderline.

### Authorities to use fast in an exam

- *ACF*: special interest required; mere intellectual or emotional concern is insufficient.
- *Onus*: no mechanical rule; intensity and closeness of the plaintiff's relationship matter.
- *Boyce*: classic two categories for private standing in public-right cases.
- *Broadbridge*: statutory standing requires a clear and imminent effect, not a remote one.
- *Argos*: statutory and general law standing analysis overlap in practice.
- *Bateman's Bay*: less restrictive, enabling view of standing and scepticism about relying on the Attorney-General.

### One-line takeaway

- The exam question is usually whether the plaintiff's connection to the decision is **sufficiently close and practical** to count as a **special interest** or an **adverse effect on interests**, with modern cases showing some convergence but no complete abandonment of limits on public interest standing.
