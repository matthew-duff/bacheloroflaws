# Minister for Immigration and Border Protection v Eden

## Citation
*Minister for Immigration and Border Protection v Eden* [2016] FCAFC 28; (2016) 240 FCR 158 (Allsop CJ, Griffiths and Wigney JJ).

## Facts
- Mr Eden, a New Zealand citizen living in Australia, pleaded guilty in 2011 to sexual assault committed while he was driving a taxi.
- He received a sentence of 12 months imprisonment, wholly suspended. That sentence was enough to enliven the character-cancellation power in `s 501(2)` because it met the statutory threshold of 12 months imprisonment.
- Nearly four years later, the Assistant Minister cancelled his visa on character grounds under `s 501(2)` of the `Migration Act 1958 (Cth)`.
- The Minister accepted that the risk of reoffending was low, but concluded that any similar future offending would cause significant harm and that protection of the Australian community outweighed countervailing factors such as Eden's family ties, business, and the best interests of his child.
- At first instance, Logan J set the cancellation aside as legally unreasonable, describing it as taking "a sledgehammer to crack a nut", with particular reliance on the objective seriousness of the offence, the delay, and the competing personal considerations.

## Issues
1. Did the primary judge wrongly substitute his own view of the seriousness of Eden's offending for the Minister's view?
2. Was the time elapsed between conviction and cancellation relevant to legal unreasonableness?
3. Was the cancellation decision, viewed as an outcome, outside the range of lawful outcomes and therefore legally unreasonable?

## Held
1. Yes. The primary judge erred by effectively remaking the decision on the basis of his own assessment of reasonableness.
2. Yes. The elapsed time since conviction was relevant to the assessment of risk and hardship, so delay could be considered in evaluating reasonableness.
3. No. The Minister's decision was within the range of lawful outcomes under `s 501(2)` and was not legally unreasonable.
4. The Minister's appeal was allowed, the primary judge's orders were set aside, and Eden's judicial review application was dismissed.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- The Full Court restated that legal unreasonableness is a question about the lawful exercise of power, not the correctness of the outcome on the merits. The court's role is supervisory.
- Under `Li`, `Singh`, and `Stretton`, the question is whether the decision falls outside the range of possible lawful outcomes having regard to the subject matter, scope, and purpose of the statute.
- The Minister's view that Eden's offending was "very serious" was open on the material before her. The fact that the sentence was wholly suspended did not mean the offence could not still be treated as serious or very serious for migration purposes.
- The primary judge was correct to treat the passage of time as relevant, because elapsed time bore both on the likelihood of reoffending and on the extent of hardship and strengthened family ties. But relevance of delay did not itself establish unreasonableness.
- The decisive point was that the Minister's core justification remained rational and available on the facts: even if the risk of reoffending was low, the potential harm from similar offending was grave, and the Minister could lawfully give decisive weight to community protection.
- Because that reasoning supplied an evident and intelligible justification, the cancellation decision was not beyond power even if another decision-maker, or a court, may have weighed the factors differently.
- The Full Court stressed that especially where the Minister personally exercises the power and Parliament provides no merits review, courts must be careful not to turn legal unreasonableness into disguised merits review.

## Significance
- This is a leading Federal Court authority on legal unreasonableness after `Li` in the context of migration character cancellations.
- It shows both that delay can be a relevant factual consideration and that relevance of delay does not mean a decision is unlawful.
- The case is especially useful for distinguishing a genuinely unlawful outcome from a merely harsh or contestable exercise of discretion.

## Exam Use
- Cite `Eden` when explaining that legal unreasonableness is not a licence for a court to rebalance competing factors.
- Use it with `Li` and `Stretton` to show that the real question is whether there is an evident and intelligible justification within the statutory framework.
- Use it where a problem question includes long delay before a coercive administrative decision: delay may be relevant to risk and hardship, but it is only part of the evaluative matrix.
- Use it to argue that a low risk of reoffending can still support intervention where the potential harm if the risk materialises is serious and the statute is directed to community protection.
