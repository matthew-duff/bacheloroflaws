# Minister for Aboriginal Affairs v Peko-Wallsend Ltd

## Citation
*Minister for Aboriginal Affairs v Peko-Wallsend Ltd* [1986] HCA 40; (1986) 162 CLR 24 (HCA).

## Facts
- Under the *Aboriginal Land Rights (Northern Territory) Act 1976* (Cth), the Aboriginal Land Commissioner was required to report on land claims and comment on detriment that might result if the claim were granted.
- The Commissioner recommended a grant that included 10 per cent of the Barote block. A valuable uranium deposit, Ranger 68, in fact lay wholly within that part of the block.
- The Commissioner did not know that fact. The evidence before him had suggested Ranger 68 was in the centre of the Barote block, not wholly within the area recommended for grant.
- After the report was issued, Peko-Wallsend and associated companies wrote to successive Ministers explaining that the report understated the detriment because Ranger 68 lay wholly within the land proposed to be granted.
- When the Minister later decided to recommend the grant, he had the Commissioner's report and a departmental brief summarising detriment, but the brief omitted the correcting information about Ranger 68.

## Issues
- What counts as a "relevant consideration" for the ground of review based on failure to take relevant considerations into account?
- Was the Minister bound to consider the Commissioner's comments on detriment under `s 50(3)` when deciding under `s 11(1)(b)` whether a grant should be made?
- Was the Minister also bound to consider later submissions in departmental possession correcting or updating the factual basis of those comments?
- Could the court intervene because the Minister failed to consider the matter, while leaving weight and ultimate policy judgment to the Minister?

## Held
- Yes. The appeal was dismissed and the Minister's decision was invalid.
- The Minister was bound to take into account the Commissioner's comments on detriment under `s 50(3)`.
- The Minister was also bound to consider material in departmental possession correcting or elucidating those comments where it showed the report proceeded on a false factual basis.
- The omitted information about Ranger 68 was material, not insignificant, so the Minister had failed to form the statutory satisfaction according to law.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- Mason J's leading reasons set out the classic propositions on relevant considerations:
  - the ground is made out only if the decision-maker failed to consider something they were **bound** to consider;
  - whether a matter is mandatory depends on construction of the statute;
  - if the statute does not specify the matter expressly, the implication must come from the statute's **subject matter, scope, and purpose**;
  - the court's role is supervisory, not merits-based.
- The Act's structure implied that detriment was a mandatory consideration for the Minister because the Commissioner had to comment on it, yet could not finally resolve how detriment should be weighed against the claim. The Minister was therefore the critical decision-maker for that issue.
- Once detriment was accepted as mandatory, the Minister had to consider it on the basis of the actual material available at the time of decision, not on a false factual assumption.
- It was not enough that the correcting letters sat somewhere in departmental files. Material in the Department's possession was treated as in the Minister's possession, and a deficient departmental summary could therefore produce legal error if it omitted a material fact the Minister was bound to consider.
- The Court distinguished between **failing to consider** a mandatory matter and the **weight** given to it. The Minister could lawfully decide that detriment should carry little or no weight, or decide on broad policy grounds, but only after properly considering the matter.
- The Court also stressed that judicial review does not let a court reweigh competing factors. Complaints about inadequate weight usually belong, if anywhere, under irrationality or unreasonableness rather than the relevant-considerations ground.

## Significance
- This is the leading Australian authority on mandatory relevant considerations.
- It provides the canonical test that mandatory considerations are identified by statutory construction, especially by reference to the statute's subject matter, scope, and purpose.
- It is also important on ministerial decision-making through departmental processes: a Minister may rely on summaries, but a materially deficient brief can invalidate the decision if it causes a mandatory fact to be overlooked.
- The case is often cited with the companion point that courts do not review the **weight** assigned to relevant considerations, because that would collapse legality review into merits review.

## Exam Use
- Use this case whenever a problem question raises failure to consider a mandatory matter. Start with the statute and ask whether the matter was expressly required or implied by text, context, and purpose.
- Use it to distinguish three categories clearly: mandatory relevant considerations, forbidden irrelevant considerations, and matters left to the decision-maker's evaluative choice.
- Use it against arguments that a court can review the adequacy of the weight given to a factor. `Peko-Wallsend` says the key legality question is usually whether the factor was considered at all, not whether the court agrees with its weight.
- Use it where a Minister relies on departmental briefs. If the brief omits a material fact in departmental possession that bears directly on a mandatory consideration, the Minister may not have exercised the power according to law.
