## Thesis
The chapter's central point is that the Australian Constitution contains only limited express administrative law guarantees, but it still shapes the whole field in a powerful way. The key influences are structural and normative rather than textual: the rule of law, the separation of judicial power, responsible government, constitutionalism, and the High Court's entrenched jurisdiction under `s 75(v)`.

## Core Constitutional Principles

### Rule of law
- Government action requires lawful authority; officials must act within the limits of statute and the Constitution.
- The Constitution is framed on an assumption of the rule of law, which gives courts a supervisory role over executive action.
- In admin law terms, the rule of law underpins judicial review, the principle that public power has legal limits, and the idea that the executive cannot authorise its own illegality.

### Separation of powers
- At Commonwealth level, the Constitution entrenches a tripartite structure between legislative, executive, and judicial power.
- This structure helps explain the legality-merits distinction: courts decide whether power was exercised lawfully, but they do not remake administrative decisions on the merits.
- The chapter uses `Dignan` to show that Parliament may delegate legislative power broadly to the executive, but `Boilermakers` to show that federal judicial power can only be exercised by Ch III courts and cannot be mixed with incompatible non-judicial functions.
- The chapter also links the separation of judicial power to the supervisory jurisdiction of State Supreme Courts through `Kable` and `Kirk`, especially review for jurisdictional error.

### Responsible government
- Ministers sit in Parliament and remain in office only while they retain the confidence of the lower house.
- Political accountability is important, but it is not a complete substitute for judicial or tribunal review.
- For admin law, responsible government helps explain why ministerial decisions are politically sensitive while still remaining legally reviewable.

### Constitutionalism
- Constitutionalism is the idea of limited government: public power is constrained by both rules and deeper constitutional values.
- Judicial review is the mechanism that enforces those limits.
- The chapter ties this to the classic idea from `Marbury v Madison` that it is the duty of courts to say what the law is, and to Australian authority recognising that courts declare and enforce the limits of statutory power.

## `s 75(v)` and Entrenched Review
- `s 75(v)` gives the High Court original jurisdiction to grant mandamus, prohibition, and injunction against officers of the Commonwealth.
- The chapter treats `s 75(v)` as the most important express constitutional protection for administrative law because it secures a basic element of the rule of law and an entrenched minimum provision of judicial review.
- `Plaintiff S157/2002` is the leading authority: Parliament may define the law officers must obey, but it cannot remove the High Court's jurisdiction to enforce legality.
- The chapter also explains that later cases such as `Bodruddaza` and `Graham` show that the constitutional minimum is not just formal jurisdiction. Legislation may also be invalid if it practically undermines effective review, for example by imposing an over-rigid time limit or blocking the court's access to critical evidence.
- The exact content of the "entrenched minimum provision of judicial review" remains unsettled. The safest exam position is that at minimum it protects supervisory review for jurisdictional error and some procedural conditions necessary for that review to remain effective.

## Exam-Use Takeaways
- If asked why administrative law has a constitutional dimension, say that the Constitution says little expressly but its structure and assumptions shape the whole system.
- If asked about legality versus merits, connect that distinction to the separation of judicial and executive power: courts supervise legality, while merits review is usually for tribunals or the executive.
- If asked about privative clauses or attempts to restrict review, start with `s 75(v)` and `Plaintiff S157/2002`, then add `Bodruddaza` and `Graham` to show that even procedural restrictions can be invalid if they impair effective review.
- If a problem concerns State courts, mention that `Kable` and `Kirk` prevent State legislation from undermining the institutional integrity and supervisory role of State Supreme Courts.
- If the facts involve detention, coercive executive action, or action without clear statutory authority, use the rule of law point that government must identify and stay within lawful authority.

## Bottom Line
For admin law exams, this chapter is best used as constitutional framing. It explains why judicial review is not merely a statutory convenience but a structural consequence of the Australian constitutional system, with `s 75(v)` providing the clearest entrenched protection.
