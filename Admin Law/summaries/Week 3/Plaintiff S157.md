[[Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth (2003) 211 CLR 476]]

**Citation:** *Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth* (2003) 211 CLR 476

### Facts
The plaintiff, a Bangladeshi citizen, was refused a protection visa by a delegate of the Minister for Immigration. The Refugee Review Tribunal (RRT) affirmed this decision. The plaintiff sought to challenge the RRT's decision in the High Court, alleging a denial of procedural fairness. However, ss 474 and 486A of the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth) posed significant legal obstacles. Section 474 was a "privative clause" purporting to make "privative clause decisions" final and not subject to judicial review (including the constitutional writs of mandamus, prohibition, and certiorari). Section 486A imposed a strict 35-day time limit on applications to the High Court for such review, with no power for the Court to extend time.

### Issue
1. Whether s 474 of the *Migration Act* was valid in light of s 75(v) of the Constitution, which confers original jurisdiction on the High Court to issue writs of mandamus, prohibition, or injunction against officers of the Commonwealth.
2. Whether s 486A was valid in imposing a non-extendable time limit on the High Court's constitutional jurisdiction.
3. The proper construction and effect of privative clauses in Commonwealth legislation.

### Held
1. **Section 474 is valid but limited:** It does not (and cannot) oust the High Court's jurisdiction under s 75(v) to review decisions involving **jurisdictional error**.
2. **Section 486A is valid but limited:** It only applies to "privative clause decisions" as defined. Since a decision involving jurisdictional error is "no decision at all" in law, it is not a "privative clause decision" under the Act, and thus the 35-day limit does not apply to challenges based on jurisdictional error.
3. **Jurisdictional Error:** A denial of procedural fairness constitutes jurisdictional error.

### Reasoning
*   **Constitutional Supremacy:** Section 75(v) of the Constitution introduces a "constitutional guarantee" of judicial review. Parliament cannot abrogate the High Court's power to ensure Commonwealth officers act within the law.
*   **Statutory Construction (The *Hickman* Principle):** The Court applied a strict construction to s 474. It held that the term "decision... made under this Act" in s 474(2) refers only to *valid* decisions. A decision involving jurisdictional error is not a decision made "under" the Act; it is a nullity. Therefore, the privative clause does not protect it.
*   **Reconciliation:** The Court must reconcile the privative clause with the rest of the Act. While a privative clause might signal that some minor procedural defects do not result in invalidity, it cannot protect against "imperative duties" or "inviolable limitations" (such as the requirement of procedural fairness).
*   **Rule of Law:** The Court emphasized that the Constitution is framed upon the assumption of the rule of law, which requires that the executive be kept within its legal bounds by the judiciary.

### Significance
*   **Protection of Judicial Review:** *Plaintiff S157* is a landmark case that entrenched the High Court's role in judicial review. It confirmed that s 75(v) creates a "constitutional minimum" of jurisdiction that Parliament cannot remove.
*   **Jurisdictional Error as the Key:** The case established jurisdictional error as the primary vehicle for bypassing privative clauses. If an error is "jurisdictional," the decision is a nullity and the privative clause has no application.
*   **Narrowing Privative Clauses:** It effectively rendered broad privative clauses inoperative against serious legal errors, particularly denials of procedural fairness.
*   **Constitutional Interpretation:** It demonstrated the Court's preference for reading down legislation to preserve constitutional validity rather than striking it down entirely.
