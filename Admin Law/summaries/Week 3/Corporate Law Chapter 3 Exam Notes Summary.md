# Corporate Law Chapter 3 Exam Notes Summary

## Source Note
The source is located in `Admin Law/sources/Week 3`, but its content is corporate law rather than administrative law. This summary follows the source as written.

## 1. Separate Legal Entity
- On registration, a company becomes a legal person separate from shareholders, directors, and founders.
- Under `Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) s 124(1)`, a company has the legal capacity and powers of an individual.
- Consequences: the company can own property, contract, sue, be sued, borrow, and employ in its own name.

### Salomon v Salomon & Co Ltd
- Authority for the separate legal entity doctrine.
- A validly incorporated company remains distinct from the person who formed and controlled it.
- Incorporation motives are legally irrelevant.
- Limited liability is available even in a small or effectively one-person company.

## 2. Limited Liability
- General rule: a shareholder need only contribute any unpaid amount on their shares (`s 516`).
- Policy reasons:
  - encourages investment and risk-taking
  - reduces shareholder monitoring costs
  - supports diversification
- Main downside: some business failure risk is shifted to creditors, especially involuntary creditors such as tort victims.

### Lee v Lee's Air Farming Ltd
- A person may occupy multiple roles in relation to a company.
- Because the company is separate, Mr Lee could be both governing director and employee under a valid contract of service.

### Andar Transport Pty Ltd v Brambles Ltd
- Reaffirms strict separation between an individual and their one-person company.
- The company can owe duties to the controller as employee, even where that controller is the company's directing mind.

## 3. Piercing the Corporate Veil
- Courts are highly reluctant to pierce the veil.
- `Briggs v James Hardie` indicates there is no single unifying principle; veil piercing is exceptional and fact-specific.

### Evasion of Existing Obligations
- `Gilford Motor Co Ltd v Horne`: veil pierced where a company was interposed to evade an existing restraint obligation.
- `Prest v Petrodel Resources Ltd`: distinguishes:
  - concealment principle: the court looks behind the structure to identify the true facts
  - evasion principle: true veil piercing where a company is used to defeat an existing legal right or obligation

### Fraud
- `Re Darby`: personal liability may follow where the corporate form is used as a sham or alias to perpetrate fraud.

### Agency
- Control alone does not establish agency.
- `Smith, Stone & Knight Ltd v Birmingham Corporation` is the classic implied agency case, but the approach is narrow and heavily criticized.

## 4. Statutory Inroads
- Common law exceptions are limited, so legislation creates direct personal or group liability in some situations.
- Insolvent trading: `s 588G` can make directors personally liable where the company incurs debts while insolvent and relevant suspicion grounds exist.
- Parent company liability: `s 588V` can make a parent company liable for subsidiary debts in insolvent trading situations.

## 5. Corporate Groups
- Each company in a group is treated as a separate legal entity.
- The law generally rejects treating a corporate group as a single economic unit.

### Walker v Wimborne
- Directors owe duties to the particular company they serve, not to the group as a whole.
- Intra-group transfers that prejudice one company can breach duties owed to that company.

### Tort Creditor Problem
- Asset-poor subsidiaries may bear liabilities while parent companies remain insulated.
- The source notes this as a major fairness concern, especially for involuntary creditors such as tort victims.
- Despite criticism, Australian law generally preserves corporate separateness for certainty and investment reasons.

## 6. Corporate Liability for Wrongs

### Criminal Liability at Common Law
- Corporate liability is attributed through natural persons.
- Under the directing mind theory, the mental state of senior officers can count as the company's mental state.
- `Tesco Supermarkets Ltd v Nattrass`: a local store manager was not the company's directing mind, so Tesco was not liable on that basis.
- `ABC Developmental Learning Centres v Wallace`: shows a more policy-sensitive approach to attribution in some statutory contexts.

### Criminal Liability Under the Criminal Code
- `Criminal Code Act 1995 (Cth)`, Part 2.5 applies to Commonwealth offences.
- Physical element (`s 12.2`): attributed where an employee, agent, or officer acts within actual or apparent authority.
- Fault element (`s 12.3`): attributed where the company authorized or permitted the offence, including through:
  - board or high managerial agent involvement
  - a corporate culture encouraging or tolerating non-compliance
  - failure to create and maintain a compliance culture

### Tort Liability
- Vicarious liability: company liable for torts committed by employees or agents in the course of employment.
- Direct liability: company liable where the wrongdoing is the company's own, such as corporate negligence, passing off, or defamation.

## Exam Takeaways
- Start with `Salomon`: incorporation creates a distinct legal person.
- Separate legal personality explains both limited liability and the possibility of multiple legal roles within one business structure.
- Veil piercing is exceptional; exam answers should prefer ordinary doctrines or statutory routes before arguing for piercing.
- In group company problems, identify which company owed the duty and who the claimant is.
- For corporate wrongdoing, distinguish attribution, vicarious liability, and statutory liability models.
