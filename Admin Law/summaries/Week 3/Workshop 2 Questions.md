## The Rule of Law and Access to Justice

**(1) Foundation for judicial review: the Commonwealth Constitution has few provisions of direct relevance for administrative lawyers: sections 73 and 75 are notable exceptions to that proposition. What are those provisions concerned with and what is the significance of s 73(ii), s 75(iii) and s 75(v)? Refer to Gageler’s article for assistance [[Gageler - Constitutional Context Summary]]**

Sections `73` and `75` are concerned with the jurisdiction of the High Court. Section `73` deals with the Court's appellate jurisdiction, including appeals from State Supreme Courts, while `s 75` deals with the Court's original jurisdiction in specified classes of matters. For administrative law, these provisions matter because they give the Constitution a direct role in maintaining judicial supervision of executive action, rather than leaving review entirely to statute or common law.

The significance of `s 75(v)` is central. It gives the High Court original jurisdiction where `mandamus`, `prohibition` or an `injunction` is sought against an `officer of the Commonwealth`. This has come to be understood as an entrenched minimum provision of judicial review for Commonwealth action, especially where there is jurisdictional error. Parliament cannot remove that minimum supervisory jurisdiction, so Commonwealth officers cannot be placed in an "island of power" immune from judicial review. Section `75(iii)` is also important because it gives the High Court original jurisdiction in matters in which the Commonwealth, or a person suing or being sued on behalf of the Commonwealth, is a party. Its significance is that it makes the Commonwealth itself amenable to judicial process and provides an additional constitutional footing for proceedings challenging unlawful executive action.

The significance of `s 73(ii)` is different but complementary. By guaranteeing appellate jurisdiction from State Supreme Courts, it constitutionally assumes the continuing existence of those courts as courts with their traditional supervisory role. As Gageler explains, this helps support the entrenched supervisory jurisdiction of State Supreme Courts to grant relief for jurisdictional error by State officials and inferior courts. Together, `s 75(v)` at the Commonwealth level and `s 73(ii)` at the State level underpin the constitutional foundation of judicial review and reinforce the rule-of-law idea that all public power must remain legally accountable.

**(2) Further to question one, identify a statute that confers a supervisory (judicial review) jurisdiction (comparable to the High Court’s original jurisdiction under s 75(v) Constitution) upon the Federal Court of Australia and Federal Circuit & Family Court (note: there is more than one statute, but which statute is most relevant for judicial review over administrative action?).**

The most relevant statute is the `Administrative Decisions (Judicial Review) Act 1977 (Cth)` (`ADJR Act`). It is the principal federal judicial review statute for Commonwealth administrative action, providing a statutory avenue in the Federal Court of Australia and the Federal Circuit and Family Court of Australia for review of decisions of an administrative character made under an enactment. It is the main judicial review statute because it codifies the grounds of review and offers a simpler and more flexible statutory alternative to relying only on constitutional writs or common law remedies.

It is also worth noting `s 39B` of the `Judiciary Act 1903 (Cth)`, which gives the Federal Court a jurisdiction substantially analogous to the High Court's jurisdiction under `s 75(v)`. However, for judicial review over administrative action specifically, the `ADJR Act` is the central statute.

**(3) Refer to Attorney-General (NSW) v Quin, and also your reading of Gageler’s article, and consider the relationship between the rule of law and judicial review over administrative action by government officials. Relatedly, what are the limits on judicial (review) powers under the Constitution?**

`Attorney-General (NSW) v Quin` and Gageler's article together present judicial review as a practical expression of the rule of law. Brennan J in `Quin` said that the court's role is to declare and enforce the law that fixes the limits of official power: government officials must act within legal authority, and courts ensure that they do. Gageler treats this as part of a broader constitutional conception of administrative law, in which judicial review exists so that public power is never legally unchecked.

At the same time, `Quin` shows that the rule of law does not turn courts into general supervisors of good administration. Judicial review is not a power to cure administrative injustice whenever a court thinks the result is unfair. The distinction is between legality and merits: courts may intervene where power has been exercised unlawfully, but they cannot substitute their own view of the preferable outcome, policy, or appointment decision. In `Quin`, that meant the Court could not compel the Attorney-General to apply the old policy simply because that seemed fairer to Quin.

Gageler explains that this supervisory role is constitutionally entrenched, but also constitutionally limited. At the Commonwealth level, `s 75(v)` secures a minimum provision of review against officers of the Commonwealth for jurisdictional error, and Parliament cannot remove that protection. At the State level, `s 73(ii)` supports the continuing supervisory jurisdiction of State Supreme Courts, so there can be no "islands of power" immune from judicial supervision. Yet the Constitution limits judicial review to judicial work: courts declare and enforce legal limits, especially jurisdictional limits, but they cannot exercise executive or merits-review functions themselves. The Constitution therefore both guarantees judicial review as a safeguard of legality and confines it to enforcing law, not remaking administrative decisions.

**Establishing legal authority to take administrative action: Church of Scientology v Woodward (1982) 154 CLR 25 (read the judgments of Mason and Brennan JJ).**

**Was ASIO subject to judicial review? And was there a statutory foundation enabling ASIO to investigate the affairs of the church?**

### Facts
The Church of Scientology and a member sued the Director-General of ASIO, the Attorney-General and the Commonwealth, alleging that ASIO had investigated them, continued gathering and communicating intelligence about them, and treated them as security risks even though initial inquiries had shown, or should have shown, that they were not relevant to security. The challenge covered the period before and after 1 June 1980, so both the `Australian Security Intelligence Organization Act 1956 (Cth)` and the `Australian Security Intelligence Organization Act 1979 (Cth)` were in play.

### Issues
Whether ASIO's activities were beyond the reach of the courts because they concerned national security, or whether the courts could judicially review whether ASIO had acted outside its legal powers. A second issue was whether there was a legal, and specifically statutory, foundation authorising ASIO to investigate the Church's affairs.

### Decision
ASIO was subject to judicial review, at least on the question whether it had acted beyond the limits of its statutory functions. Mason and Brennan JJ rejected the idea that national security placed ASIO outside judicial supervision. They treated `s 17` of the 1979 Act as stating objective limits: ASIO could obtain, correlate and evaluate intelligence only if it was relevant to security, and communicate it only for purposes relevant to security. Otherwise those limits would depend on the unreviewable opinion of the Director-General, which the Act did not provide.

There was also a statutory foundation for ASIO's investigation, but only within those statutory limits. Mason and Brennan JJ accepted that the ASIO legislation itself supplied the legal authority: before 1 June 1980 under the 1956 Act, and after that under the 1979 Act. In particular, `s 17` authorised ASIO to investigate persons or organisations where intelligence was relevant to security, including inquiries undertaken to determine whether a person or body was or was not a security risk. The Court did not accept that once initial inquiries suggested the Church was not a security risk, ASIO automatically lost power to continue investigating. The statutory foundation therefore existed, but ASIO remained reviewable if it strayed beyond the requirement of relevance to security.

Establishing legal authority to take administrative action: CPCF v Minister for Immigration (2015) 255 CLR 514.

Most administrative law cases are about statutory power: whether legislation authorised government actions, and the construction of the statute to determine the outer limits upon government power. But not all administrative power is sourced in statute: importantly s 61 of the Constitution supplies non-statutory executive power (similar to ‘prerogative powers’ at common law). A controversial case in which the Australian government relied on both statutory and non-statutory powers to validate its coercive action taken against asylum seekers at sea is CPCF.

**In light of your reading of CPCF, did the Maritime Powers Act 2013 (Cth) authorise the decision to; first, detain at sea, and secondly, take asylum seekers towards India? And, if not, did non-statutory executive power (or, ‘prerogative power’) supply an alternative basis for the Australian Government’s coercive actions?**

### Facts
`CPCF` concerned 157 asylum seekers on an Indian-flagged vessel intercepted in Australia's contiguous zone near Christmas Island. After the vessel became unseaworthy, they were transferred to a Commonwealth ship and detained there. The National Security Committee of Cabinet then decided they should be taken towards India, even though Australia had no prior agreement with India for their disembarkation. When negotiations with India failed, they were eventually taken to the Cocos (Keeling) Islands and detained under the `Migration Act`.

### Issues
The key questions were whether `s 72(4)` of the `Maritime Powers Act 2013 (Cth)` authorised, first, detention at sea and, secondly, taking the asylum seekers towards India despite there being no pre-existing agreement with India to receive them. If the statute did not authorise those steps, the further issue was whether `s 61` executive power, or prerogative-style non-statutory power, could nevertheless justify the coercive detention and attempted removal.

### Decision
On the statutory issue, the High Court majority held that `s 72(4)` did authorise both the detention at sea and the taking of the asylum seekers towards India. The majority read the power broadly: it permitted a maritime officer to detain a person and "take" that person to a place outside Australia, and that did not require a pre-existing diplomatic agreement guaranteeing immediate disembarkation. The power therefore extended to commencing the journey towards India and detaining the passengers while that course was being pursued.

The dissenting judges took a narrower view. They reasoned that `s 72(4)` did not authorise taking the passengers to India unless India was in truth a place where they could lawfully be discharged. Because no agreement or arrangement with India existed at the relevant time, detention for the purpose of taking them there was, on that view, beyond statutory power.

As to non-statutory executive power, there was no majority holding that it supplied an alternative basis. French CJ, Crennan J and Gageler J found it unnecessary to decide because they had already concluded that the statute authorised the detention and voyage towards India. Keane J alone held that, even if the Act had not authorised the conduct, `s 61` would have supported executive action to exclude non-citizens and to do what was reasonably necessary to prevent their entry, including detention and taking them towards India. By contrast, Hayne and Bell JJ, and Kiefel J, rejected that fallback argument. The better view of `CPCF` is therefore that the coercive actions were upheld because of the majority's construction of the `Maritime Powers Act`, not because the Court as a whole accepted a non-statutory executive power to detain and remove.

Note the second question was answered by only four members of the Court ([476]-[494], Keane J (majority), with Hayne and Bell JJ, [137]-[151] and Kiefel J, [258]-[286] (dissenting)).

Access to justice before the High Court of Australia: Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth (2002) 211 CLR 476

The approach of the HCA to privative clauses (‘ouster clauses’) demonstrates that the judiciary are suspicious about legislative attempts to restrict a person’s ability to challenge the legality of government action before the courts, as evidenced by Plaintiff S157/2002.

Plaintiff S157 is, arguably, one of the most significant contemporary decisions of the HCA. It concerned an attempt by the Federal Legislature to ‘oust’ the capacity of the High Court to review government action and use its remedial powers under s 75, vis-à-vis a ‘Commonwealth officer’.

**What did the High Court decide in respect of Parliament’s attempt to curtail the High Court’s supervisory function under s 75(v) Constitution? And what role did the ‘rule of law’ play in the reasoning of Chief Justice Gleeson (read carefully pages 482-494 of Plaintiff S157)?**

### Facts
The plaintiff, a Bangladeshi applicant for a protection visa, sought to challenge a Refugee Review Tribunal decision affirming the refusal of his visa. He alleged a denial of procedural fairness. Two provisions of the `Migration Act 1958 (Cth)` were said to obstruct review in the High Court: `s 474`, a broad privative clause declaring certain migration decisions "final" and not subject to judicial review, and `s 486A`, which imposed a strict 35-day time limit for seeking relief.

### Issues
Whether Parliament could, by `s 474`, prevent the High Court from exercising its entrenched supervisory jurisdiction under `s 75(v)` of the Constitution against officers of the Commonwealth, and whether a decision affected by jurisdictional error, including denial of procedural fairness, was really a decision "under" the Act protected by the privative clause. In Gleeson CJ's reasons, a further issue was how the rule of law informed the interpretation of the legislation and the constitutional position of the High Court.

### Decision
The High Court held that Parliament could not curtail the High Court's supervisory function under `s 75(v)` by enacting a privative clause in the broad terms of `s 474`. However, rather than striking `s 474` down in its entirety, the Court read it down. On its proper construction, `s 474` did not protect decisions affected by jurisdictional error, because such decisions were not truly decisions made "under" the Act at all. A decision made in breach of procedural fairness therefore fell outside the privative clause and remained reviewable by constitutional writs.

Gleeson CJ emphasised that `s 75(v)` secures a basic element of the rule of law: officers of the Commonwealth must act within the law, and Parliament cannot deprive the High Court of jurisdiction to ensure that they do so. The rule of law did not operate as a free-standing slogan detached from legal doctrine; rather, it explained why the Constitution entrenches judicial supervision and supported orthodox principles of statutory construction. In particular, Gleeson CJ treated the Constitution as framed on an assumption of the rule of law, invoked the presumption that Parliament does not intend to deprive people of access to the courts unless it uses unmistakably clear language, and rejected the idea that a privative clause could convert unfair or jurisdictionally defective decision-making into lawful authority.

As to `s 486A`, the Court held that it did not apply to decisions infected by jurisdictional error, because such decisions were not "privative clause decisions" in the first place. Parliament therefore failed in its attempt to insulate unlawful migration decisions from the High Court's supervisory review.
