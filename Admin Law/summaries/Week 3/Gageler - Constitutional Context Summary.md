[[Gageler's article on const context for admin law]]

**Reference:** Hon Stephen Gageler, "The Constitutional Dimension" in Matthew Groves (ed), *Modern Administrative Law in Australia* (Cambridge University Press, 2014) 165.

## Thesis
Gageler's central claim is that Australian administrative law can no longer be understood only as a patchwork of common law writs and statutory review mechanisms. Since 1990, the High Court has developed a constitutional conception of judicial review in which the rule of law requires courts to declare and enforce the legal limits on public power, especially through review for jurisdictional error.

## Key Arguments
- For much of the twentieth century, judicial review was treated mainly as a common law field built around prerogative writs, with `s 75(v)`, State Supreme Court jurisdiction, the `ADJR Act`, and later `s 39B` operating as separate pieces rather than one coherent constitutional structure.
- The modern shift begins with `Attorney-General (NSW) v Quin`, where Brennan J framed judicial review as enforcing the rule of law and insisted that courts may declare and enforce legal limits on power but may not remake administrative decisions on their merits.
- In `Re Refugee Review Tribunal; Ex parte Aala`, the High Court treated mandamus and prohibition under `s 75(v)` as constitutional writs and linked relief to jurisdictional error.
- In `Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth`, the Court held that `s 75(v)` entrenches a minimum provision of judicial review that Parliament cannot remove, at least where an officer of the Commonwealth commits jurisdictional error.
- In `Kirk v Industrial Court (NSW)`, the Court extended the constitutional logic to the States by treating the supervisory jurisdiction of State Supreme Courts as a defining constitutional characteristic supported by `s 73(ii)`.
- Gageler presents jurisdictional error as the minimum scope of entrenched review, but stresses that the label is conclusive rather than analytically precise. The hard question is not whether review exists, but how to identify the content of the legal limits that administrators have exceeded.

## Constitutional Significance
- `s 75(v)` entrenches High Court supervision of Commonwealth officers and prevents federal legislation from creating complete immunity from judicial review.
- `s 73(ii)` helps entrench the supervisory role of State Supreme Courts, so State Parliaments cannot strip those courts of review for jurisdictional error.
- Together, these provisions support the proposition that there can be no "islands of power" immune from judicial supervision.
- The constitutional dimension does not itself dictate an expansionist or restrictive approach to review. It guarantees supervision, but the content of review still depends on statute, common law method, and constitutional structure.

## Exam Takeaways
- Start with the rule-of-law proposition: judicial review exists to enforce the legal limits on public power, not to conduct merits review.
- Use `Quin`, `Aala`, `Plaintiff S157`, and `Kirk` as the key sequence showing how a piecemeal system became a constitutionally entrenched one.
- Explain the Commonwealth/State symmetry:
- Commonwealth level: `s 75(v)` plus constitutional writs against officers of the Commonwealth.
- State level: `s 73(ii)` plus the entrenched supervisory jurisdiction of State Supreme Courts.
- If asked about jurisdictional error, say it is the minimum constitutional ground of review, but its boundaries remain contested and must be worked out by identifying the statute's legal limits and the authority actually conferred.
