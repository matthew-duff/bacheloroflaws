# Robin Creyke, "Administrative Tribunals" in M. Groves and H.P. Lee (eds), Australian Administrative Law: Fundamentals, Principles and Doctrines (CUP, 2007) Ch 5

## Overview
A comprehensive chapter on administrative tribunals in Australia, covering their definition, classification, place in government, jurisdiction, procedures, and evidence rules. The chapter provides the foundational framework for understanding how tribunals (including the AAT/ART) operate.

## Key Themes

### What is a Tribunal?
- Difficult to define precisely — no single definition distinguishes tribunals from courts
- Key features: less formal, not bound by rules of evidence, generally cheaper and quicker than courts
- The merits review function is best committed to a tribunal because tribunals can examine whether a decision is **substantively correct** (considering law, facts, policy, and discretion)

### Categories of Tribunals
- **Specialist vs Generalist**: Specialist tribunals focus on one area (e.g., migration, veterans' affairs); generalist tribunals (like the AAT) have broad jurisdiction across government
- **Single vs Two Tier**: High-volume areas often have two tiers — a first-tier tribunal for efficient filtering, then a "superior" second-tier tribunal
- **Public vs Private**: Public sector tribunals review government decisions; private sector tribunals handle disputes between individuals (tenancy, consumer, employment)
- **Primary Decision-Maker vs Review Body**: Some tribunals make original decisions (e.g., guardianship); most review decisions already made by officials

### Tribunals in the System of Government
- Tribunals sit uneasily within the tripartite separation of powers — they are formally part of the executive but adjudicate quasi-judicially
- Possible placement: a **"fourth arm"** (integrity branch) of government — which gives due recognition to the need for impartiality and independence

### Merit Review — Key Principles
- **"Correct or preferable decision"**: The tribunal determines what the right decision is on all the evidence, not whether the original decision was "open" on the evidence (which is the judicial review standard)
- **"Stands in the shoes"**: The tribunal has the same powers and discretions as the original decision-maker (s 43 AAT Act)
- **De novo review**: Unless statute provides otherwise, review is on the facts, law, and policy at the **date of the tribunal's decision**, not the date of the original decision
- **Review of the decision, not the reasons**: The scope of review is not limited by the original decision-maker's statement of reasons — the tribunal considers the whole of the evidence and every aspect of the case

### Jurisdiction
- Tribunal jurisdiction is limited to **reviewable decisions** defined by statute
- "Decision" is defined broadly in the AAT Act (s 3(3)) — includes purported decisions that may be invalid
- Ensuring tribunals stay within jurisdictional limits is a prime function of judicial review

### Procedure and Evidence
- Tribunals are **not bound by formal rules of evidence** — the rules are "facultative, not restrictive" (Eshetu)
- Evidence must be **probative** — it must logically tend to show the existence or non-existence of relevant facts
- Proceedings must comply with **procedural fairness**
- Many tribunals operate in an **investigative** (inquisitorial) rather than adversarial manner, though Australian tribunals fall on a spectrum
- The AAT can "inform itself on any matter in such manner as it thinks appropriate" (s 33(1)(c) AAT Act)

## Significance
- Provides the essential textbook framework for understanding tribunals in Australia
- Covers the structural, procedural, and jurisdictional aspects that underpin Workshop 3 analysis
- Complements the case law (Drake, Pochi, Shi) with systematic academic analysis
