# The Administrative Review Tribunal Act 2024 (Cth)

## Purpose
- The Act establishes the **Administrative Review Tribunal** (`s 8`) and states its objective in `s 9`.
- Under `s 9`, the Tribunal must provide an **independent mechanism of review** that is fair and just, quick, informal, accessible, improves the transparency and quality of government decision-making, and promotes public confidence.

## Core Exam Point
- This Act is the framework statute for **Commonwealth merits review**.
- It does **not itself make every administrative decision reviewable**. Under `s 12`, a decision is reviewable only if another Act or legislative instrument confers review in the Tribunal.
- In an exam, start by asking:
  1. Is there a statutory pathway to ART review?
  2. Is the applicant a person whose interests are affected?
  3. What powers does the Tribunal have once review is engaged?

## Key Provisions

### Starting review
- `s 12`: a **reviewable decision** exists only where an Act or legislative instrument provides for Tribunal review.
- `s 17`: a person whose **interests are affected** may apply for review.
- `s 23`: within 28 days of notification, the decision-maker must give the Tribunal a **statement of reasons** and relevant documents.

### Procedure and powers
- `s 36`: the President may make **practice directions**.
- `ss 40-42`: a **guidance and appeals panel** may hear matters raising issues of significance, or certain internal appeal matters involving significance or possible material error.
- `s 49`: the Tribunal has procedural discretion, subject to the Act and rules.
- `s 50`: the Tribunal must act with as little formality and technicality as proper consideration permits.
- `s 51`: proceedings should be conducted accessibly, taking account of parties' needs.
- `s 52`: the Tribunal is **not bound by the rules of evidence**.
- `s 53`: in review proceedings, the Tribunal may control the scope of the review.
- `s 54`: the Tribunal may exercise **all powers and discretions of the original decision-maker**.
- `s 55`: each party must have a reasonable opportunity to present their case.
- `s 56`: parties and representatives must use their best endeavours to assist the Tribunal to make the **correct or preferable decision** and achieve the `s 9` objective.

### Hearings, outcomes, reasons
- `s 69`: hearings are generally **public**, unless practice directions or a Tribunal order require otherwise.
- `s 105`: on review, the Tribunal must either **affirm**, **vary**, or **set aside** the reviewable decision; if set aside, it may substitute a new decision or remit the matter for reconsideration with orders or recommendations.
- `s 111`: after deciding a review, the Tribunal must give written notice of its decision, a **statement of reasons**, and notice of further review/appeal rights, generally within 28 days if practice directions do not specify a time.

### Institutional safeguards
- `s 10`: the Tribunal consists of the President, Deputy Presidents, senior members, and general members.
- `s 209`: the Minister must establish one or more **assessment panels** to assess suitability for appointment to key Tribunal offices.
- `s 221`: non-judicial members may be terminated only on specified grounds, including indictable conviction, incapacity, serious misconduct, serious breach of the code or performance standard, conflicting outside work, or failure to disclose interests.

## Exam-Use Significance
- The Act shows that **merits review is statutory**: you cannot assume ART jurisdiction without a conferring provision.
- It reinforces the orthodox merits review idea that the Tribunal can stand in the shoes of the original decision-maker by exercising the same powers (`s 54`) and reaching the correct or preferable decision (`s 56`, `s 105`).
- It is also important for procedural fairness analysis because the Act combines informality (`s 50`), accessibility (`s 51`), flexible proof (`s 52`), public hearing as the default (`s 69`), and written reasons (`s 111`).
- The appointments and termination provisions matter in policy and essay answers because they are statutory safeguards for **independence, quality, and confidence** in the Tribunal system.

## Short Takeaway
- Use this Act in exams as the **roadmap statute** for Commonwealth merits review: identify the conferring enactment, confirm standing, explain the Tribunal's flexible but fair procedure, and finish with the remedial powers in `s 105`.
