# Robin Creyke, "Administrative Tribunals" - Summary

## Thesis
Tribunals occupy a distinctive place in Australian administrative law because they are institutionally better suited than courts to perform merits review: they can reconsider the substance of a decision, are procedurally flexible, and can substitute the correct or preferable outcome while still being constrained by statute, fairness, and jurisdiction.

## Key principles

### 1. Why tribunals matter
- Tribunals are generally quicker, cheaper, and less formal than courts.
- Their comparative advantage is merits review: deciding whether the substantive outcome is correct after considering law, facts, policy, and discretion.
- At Commonwealth level, constitutional separation of powers reinforces the use of tribunals because federal courts cannot exercise non-judicial merits review functions.

### 2. What a tribunal is
- Creyke stresses that tribunals are hard to define by a single formula.
- The better approach is functional rather than purely definitional: focus on what the body does and how it differs from a court.
- Common features include adjudicative procedures, some obligation to act judicially or fairly, statutory creation, and institutional flexibility in composition and powers.

### 3. Major tribunal categories
- Specialist tribunals deal with high-volume subject areas such as migration, veterans' matters, or social security.
- Generalist or multi-purpose tribunals, especially the AAT, bring together broad review jurisdictions in one body.
- Tribunals may be single-tier or two-tier, public or private, and either primary decision-makers or review bodies.
- Exam point: classification matters because it helps explain procedure, powers, and the kind of review available.

### 4. Where tribunals fit in government
- Tribunals do not sit neatly within the classic legislative-executive-judicial model.
- Creyke presents the strongest account as tribunals forming part of an "integrity" or fourth arm of government, reflecting their need for impartiality and independence.
- Even if not constitutionally entrenched as a separate branch, their institutional role helps explain why they review executive decisions yet operate with adjudicative features.

## Merits review doctrine

### 5. The tribunal's task: the "correct or preferable" decision
- Merits review asks whether the decision under review is substantively right, not merely legally valid.
- The tribunal stands in the shoes of the original decision-maker because it may exercise all the powers and discretions conferred on that decision-maker.
- The central contrast with judicial review is that courts usually ask legality questions, whereas tribunals ask what decision should now be made on the merits.

### 6. Merits review is usually de novo
- Unless the statute indicates otherwise, review is conducted afresh on the material before the tribunal.
- That means the tribunal may consider new evidence, changed facts, and sometimes changed law or policy.
- But de novo review is not absolute: the legislation may confine review by fixing the relevant time, limiting available material, or restricting the scope of reconsideration.

### 7. Review is of the decision, not just the reasons
- The tribunal is not confined to testing the adequacy of the original statement of reasons.
- Reasons remain important, but the tribunal may consider all relevant evidence and circumstances that emerge during review.
- This reinforces that merits review is practical and substantive rather than formalistic.

### 8. Jurisdiction remains statutory
- A tribunal can only review decisions that fall within the jurisdiction granted by legislation.
- Whether a matter is reviewable depends on the statutory definition of "decision" and on the subject-specific enactment conferring review rights.
- Courts retain the supervisory role of ensuring a tribunal has not exceeded jurisdiction or committed jurisdictional error.

## Procedure, evidence, and fairness

### 9. Flexible procedure does not mean lawlessness
- Tribunals are usually not bound by formal rules of evidence and are directed to proceed with as little formality and technicality as proper consideration permits.
- Even so, they must still act fairly and base findings on probative material.
- The absence of strict evidentiary rules widens the tribunal's fact-finding capacity, but does not turn the hearing into a free-for-all.

### 10. Investigative rather than purely adversarial
- Creyke describes many tribunal processes as lying on a spectrum between adversarial and inquisitorial models.
- In substance, merits review often expects the tribunal to help ensure relevant material is before it, especially where applicants are unrepresented.
- Still, the tribunal's investigative role is moderated by resources, statutory limits, and procedural fairness obligations.

### 11. No strict civil onus in the ordinary sense
- Strict notions of burden of proof do not fit neatly into merits review.
- In practice, an applicant still usually bears a practical burden of putting forward evidence to support their case.
- The better exam framing is that tribunals are not confined by rigid civil-litigation concepts, but they still require probative material sufficient to decide the facts.

## Policy and appeals

### 12. Government policy is relevant but not binding in the same way as statute
- Creyke treats the tribunal-policy relationship as a central tension in merits review.
- The chapter's account of the `Drake` litigation shows that tribunals should not apply policy mechanically.
- Policy may guide consistency and predictability, but a tribunal must still make its own assessment of the correct or preferable decision in the individual case.

### 13. Appeals from tribunals are usually limited to law
- Appeals to courts commonly lie on a question of law, not on the merits.
- This preserves the distinction between tribunal reconsideration of substance and curial supervision for legal error.
- Exam point: if a problem asks what happens after the tribunal's decision, identify whether the statute gives only a question-of-law appeal.

## Doctrinal significance
- The chapter supplies the underlying architecture of Australian merits review doctrine rather than a single narrow rule.
- Its main significance is to connect institutional design with doctrine: who decides, by what procedure, with what powers, and under what statutory limits.
- It also explains why tribunals are central to administrative justice even though they are not courts and do not produce binding precedent in the strict judicial sense.

## Exam-use takeaways
- Start with the tribunal's task: reach the correct or preferable decision, not merely test legality.
- Identify the statutory source of jurisdiction and whether the particular decision is reviewable.
- Ask whether review is de novo or whether the statute narrows the tribunal's powers.
- Explain that the tribunal stands in the shoes of the original decision-maker and may affirm, vary, set aside and substitute, or remit if the statute permits.
- Distinguish flexible procedure from absence of constraint: tribunals remain bound by fairness, probative evidence, and jurisdictional limits.
- If policy is raised, say it is relevant but cannot displace the tribunal's duty to reach the correct or preferable result in the individual case.
