# Re Drake and Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs (No 2)

## Citation
*Re Drake and Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs (No 2)* (1979) 2 ALD 634.

## Facts
- Drake, a US citizen living in Australia, was subject to a deportation order under s 12 of the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth) after conviction for offences connected with a large-scale cannabis cultivation operation at Wollogorang Station.
- The matter returned to the AAT after remittal from the Federal Court following the first *Drake* decision.
- The Minister had adopted a deportation policy stating that each case turned on its individual circumstances, but that the basic question was whether deportation was in the best interests of Australia, with particular concern about drug trafficking and corruption of young people.
- Drake relied on countervailing considerations including his Australian wife, his son Mark's deep ties to Australia, and the hardship deportation would cause the family.

## Issues
1. Is it lawful for the Minister to adopt and apply a general policy in exercising the deportation discretion?
2. What role should lawful ministerial policy play when the AAT is making the correct or preferable decision on review?
3. Applying those principles, should Drake's deportation order be affirmed?

## Held
- The Minister's policy was lawful.
- The AAT should ordinarily apply lawful ministerial policy unless the policy is unlawful or its application would produce injustice in the particular case.
- Drake's deportation order was affirmed.

## Reasoning / Ratio
- A policy is lawful if it guides rather than controls the discretion. It must remain consistent with the statute, permit consideration of all relevant circumstances, and not shut out arguments for departure in individual cases.
- Brennan J stressed that policy assists good administration because it promotes consistency, reduces arbitrariness, and provides a stable reference point where multiple decision-makers exercise the same discretion.
- Even so, the AAT's task under s 43 is to make the correct or preferable decision on the material before it. The Tribunal is independent and legally free to depart from ministerial policy.
- The Tribunal should nevertheless be cautious about departing from lawful ministerial policy because broad policy is politically accountable to Parliament, whereas the AAT's role is adjudicative rather than political.
- The Tribunal's ordinary practice should therefore be to apply lawful ministerial policy unless there are cogent reasons not to, especially where application of the policy would create injustice in the circumstances of the particular case.
- On the facts, Brennan J treated large-scale drug cultivation as gravely damaging to Australian society, found the prospect of recidivism was not small, and held that those considerations outweighed the hardship to Drake's wife and son.

## Significance
- This is the leading Australian merits review authority on the relationship between tribunal independence and executive policy.
- It establishes the classic position that policy may be highly persuasive without being binding.
- The case is routinely cited for the proposition that lawful policy should usually be followed for consistency, but not where doing so would produce injustice.

## Exam Use
- Use *Drake (No 2)* when asked how the AAT treats ministerial policy on merits review.
- Pair it with *Drake (No 1)*: *No 1* states the Tribunal's task is to reach the "correct or preferable" decision, while *No 2* explains how policy fits into that task.
- If a problem question raises fettering, distinguish between an unlawful rigid rule and a lawful policy that guides decision-making while leaving room for individual merits.
