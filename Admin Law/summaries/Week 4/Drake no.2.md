[[Re Drake and Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs (No 2) (1979) 2 ALD 634]]

**Citation**: *Re Drake and Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs (No 2)* (1979) 2 ALD 634

### Facts
This was a rehearing by the Administrative Appeals Tribunal (AAT) following a Federal Court remission in *Drake v Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs* (1979). The applicant, Daniel Drake, a US citizen, was subject to a deportation order under s 12 of the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth) following a conviction for possessing cannabis. The conviction arose from a large-scale cultivation operation at Wollogorang Station. The Minister had issued a policy statement outlining criteria for deportation, including the "best interests of Australia" and specific concern for drug trafficking.

### Issue
1. What role should Ministerial policy play in the AAT's review of a discretionary power?
2. Should the Tribunal apply the Minister's policy in determining the "correct or preferable" decision?
3. Was deportation the correct or preferable decision in Drake's specific circumstances?

### Held
The Tribunal affirmed the Minister's deportation order. 

### Reasoning
Brennan J (President) established the following principles regarding Ministerial policy:
- **Lawfulness of Policy**: It is desirable and lawful for a Minister to form and promulgate policy to guide the exercise of discretion, provided the policy is consistent with the statute and does not fetter the discretion (i.e., the decision-maker must still consider the individual merits of the case).
- **AAT's Relationship to Policy**: While the AAT is independent and must reach the "correct or preferable" decision, it should ordinarily apply lawful Ministerial policy unless there are "cogent reasons" to the contrary.
- **Consistency vs Justice**: Applying policy facilitates consistency in administrative decision-making, which is a requirement of justice. However, policy must not be applied so rigidly as to produce an unjust result in a particular case.
- **Political Responsibility**: Broad policy formulation is a political function for which the Minister is responsible to Parliament. The AAT, as an adjudicative body, should be reluctant to lay down broad policy itself.

In Drake's case, the policy regarding drug trafficking was consistent with the Act and the Tribunal's own values. Despite countervailing factors (Drake's family ties in Australia and his son's interests), the gravity of the offence and the risk of recidivism (linked to findings of prior involvement in cultivation) outweighed the reasons for him to remain.

### Significance
*Drake (No 2)* is the foundational case for the AAT's approach to Ministerial policy. It balances the Tribunal's independence with the need for administrative consistency. It establishes that while the AAT is not legally bound by executive policy, it will generally adopt and apply it to ensure that administrative justice is consistent across the government, provided the policy is lawful and does not lead to individual injustice.
