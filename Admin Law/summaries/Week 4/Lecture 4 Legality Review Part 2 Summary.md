# Lecture 4: Legality Review Part 2 Summary

## Overview
This lecture covers the second part of legality review grounds, focusing on unreasonableness, no evidence, jurisdictional facts, errors of law, fraud, bad faith, and jurisdictional error. It also explores the intersection between administrative law and the *Human Rights Act 2019* (Qld), particularly regarding proportionality and the impact of ouster (privative) clauses.

## Key points

### 1. Unreasonableness
*   **Wednesbury Unreasonableness:** Traditionally limited to cases of serious irrationality or capriciousness (*Associated Provincial Picture Houses Ltd v Wednesbury Corporation* [1948]).
*   **Modern Australian Approach:** *Minister for Immigration and Citizenship v Li* (2013) shifted the focus to whether a decision "lacks an evident or intelligible justification," relating to either the reasoning process or the outcome.
*   **Three Paradigms (Gummow J in *Fares Rural Meat*):**
    1.  Capricious selection of power invading common law rights.
    2.  Unjustified discrimination.
    3.  Exercise of power out of proportion to its scope.

### 2. No Evidence and Jurisdictional Facts
*   **Common Law vs. Statutory:** The common law "no evidence" ground is narrow (absence of any foundation in fact). The ADJR Act/JR Act ground is broader.
*   **Jurisdictional Facts:** Factual criteria that must be satisfied to enliven a power. Courts may take a subjective (*Plaintiff M70/2011* - French CJ) or objective approach (*Plaintiff M70/2011* - Majority).

### 3. Errors of Law
*   **Distinction:** Distinguishing errors of law from errors of fact remains complex. Errors in statutory interpretation are generally errors of law (*Agfa-Gevaert*).
*   **Face of the Record:** At common law, certiorari is available for non-jurisdictional errors of law only if they appear on the "face of the record" (*Craig v South Australia*).

### 4. Fraud and Bad Faith
*   **Standard of Proof:** Stricter standard due to the seriousness of the allegation (*Briginshaw*).
*   **Third-Party Fraud:** Can invalidate a decision if it adversely affects the exercise of power (*SZFDE*; *DUA16*).
*   **Bad Faith:** Requires actual bias or dishonesty; there is no "constructive" bad faith (*SBAN*).

### 5. Jurisdictional Error and Ouster Clauses
*   **Nature:** Jurisdictional error (JE) occurs when a decision-maker lacks, exceeds, or fails to exercise jurisdiction.
*   **Materiality:** An error is only jurisdictional if it is "material"—meaning there is a realistic possibility the outcome could have been different (*LPDT* (2024)).
*   **Ouster Clauses:** Provisions attempting to limit judicial review. Courts use JE to "read down" these clauses, as JE is constitutionally entrenched (*Plaintiff S157*; *Kirk*).
*   **Consequences:** JE usually makes a tribunal decision *void ab initio* (*Bhardwaj*), whereas a court decision remains valid until set aside (*Kable*).

### 6. Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld)
*   **Proportionality:** Section 13 introduces a more intensive standard of review than *Wednesbury* unreasonableness, though it stops short of merits review (*Owen-D'Arcy*).
*   **Public Entities:** Section 58 makes it unlawful for public entities to act incompatibly with human rights or fail to give proper consideration to them.

## Significance
This lecture is critical for understanding the "gravity" of legal errors required to trigger judicial intervention. It highlights the High Court's recent emphasis on **materiality** in jurisdictional error and the evolving role of **proportionality** under the Queensland *Human Rights Act*, which provides a more robust framework for challenging administrative decisions than traditional common law grounds.
