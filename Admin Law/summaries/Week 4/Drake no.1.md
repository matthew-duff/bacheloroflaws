[[Drake no.1]]

**Citation**: *Drake v Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs* (1979) 46 FLR 409

### Facts
Drake, an American citizen with permanent resident status in Australia, was convicted of possessing cannabis (intended for sale). He was sentenced to 12 months' imprisonment, but the magistrate directed his release after three months upon entering a $200 recognizance for good behaviour. The Minister for Immigration ordered his deportation under s 12 of the *Migration Act 1958* (Cth), which allowed for the deportation of aliens sentenced to "imprisonment for one year or longer". Drake appealed to the Administrative Appeals Tribunal (AAT), which was presided over by Davies J (a Federal Court judge). The AAT affirmed the Minister's decision, and Drake appealed to the Federal Court.

### Issue
1. **Constitutional**: Whether a Federal Court judge could validly sit as a member of the AAT (an administrative body) without violating the separation of powers.
2. **Statutory**: Whether a sentence of 12 months with a direction for release after three months constituted a sentence of "imprisonment for one year or longer".
3. **Administrative**: Whether the AAT erred by abdicating its independent review function by treating government policy as determinative.

### Held
1. **Constitutional**: Yes. There is no constitutional bar to a judge acting in their personal capacity in an administrative role (*persona designata*).
2. **Statutory**: Yes (Majority: Bowen CJ and Deane J). The "sentence" is the term imposed by the court, not the time actually served.
3. **Administrative**: Yes. The AAT erred by failing to exercise its own independent judgment on the merits, instead treating the Minister's policy as effectively binding.

### Reasoning
*   **Separation of Powers**: The Court held that while Ch III of the Constitution prevents the conferring of non-judicial power on a *court*, it does not prevent a *judge* from being appointed to an administrative office in their individual capacity.
*   **Merits Review Function**: The AAT's role is to determine the "correct or preferable" decision on the material before it. While the AAT can take government policy into account (and should do so for consistency), it must not abdicate its function by applying policy blindly. It must independently assess the propriety of the policy and its application to the specific facts of the case.
*   **Policy vs Law**: The AAT failed to make an independent assessment of whether the deportation was the "right" course in the best interests of Australia, appearing instead to merely "supervise" whether the Minister had reasonably applied his own policy.

### Significance
*   **AAT Function**: Establishes the core principle of merits review: the AAT must decide what is the "correct or preferable" decision, not merely whether the original decision was legally valid or reasonable.
*   **Use of Policy**: Clarifies that administrative tribunals may (and often should) consider government policy to promote consistency, but they must remain independent and prepared to depart from policy if the individual justice of the case requires it.
*   **Persona Designata**: Reaffirms that judges can perform non-judicial functions if appointed in their personal capacity.
