(Corporate Personality, Liability, Veil Piercing & Groups)

This guide synthesizes the Week 3 lecture materials and the critical judgments from *Prest v Petrodel* and *Meridian Global Funds*, preparing you for oral assessment questions on corporate fault, the corporate veil, and the entity vs. enterprise models.

## 🎯 PART A: CORE CONCEPTS & STATUTORY FRAMEWORK

### 1. Separate Legal Personality (SEP) & Corporate Capacity
*   **The *Salomon* Principle (1897):** A company is a separate legal entity (SEP) from its members and managers. This applies even to "one-person" companies. 
*   **Consequences of SEP:**
    *   **Limited Liability (s 9):** Shareholders are liable only for unpaid share capital.
    *   **Capacity (s 124):** A company has the legal capacity and powers of an individual.
    *   **Restrictions (s 125):** A company's constitution *may* contain an "objects clause" restricting powers, but acts outside those objects are not necessarily invalid against third parties (abolition of *ultra vires*).
    *   **Contractual & Property Rights:** A director can contract with their own company (*Lee v Lee’s Air Farming*). The company's property is not the shareholders' property (*Macaura*).

### 2. Corporate Fault & Attribution (How does a company "act"?)
Because a company is a fiction, the law must attribute human acts to it.
*   **Direct Liability (Alter Ego):** Where the person acting is the "directing mind and will" of the company (*Lennard’s Carrying Co*; *Tesco*).
*   **Vicarious Liability:** The company is liable for acts of its agents/employees in the course of employment (*Mousell Bros*).
*   ***Meridian Global Funds* Rules of Attribution (Hoffmann LJ):**
    1.  *Primary Rules:* Found in the constitution/board decisions.
    2.  *General Rules:* Agency and vicarious liability.
    3.  *Special Statutory Rules:* When a statute requires mental state/knowledge, you must ask: **"Whose knowledge did the statute intend to count as the company's?"** If the statute's policy would be defeated by only looking at the Board, you attribute the knowledge of the specific employee authorized to do the act.

### 3. Piercing the Corporate Veil (Exceptions to SEP)
Piercing the veil means disregarding the company's separate existence to impose liability directly on its controllers.
*   ***Prest v Petrodel* Framework (Sumption LJ):**
    *   **Concealment Principle (NOT Piercing):** Masking the true facts. The court uses existing trust or agency law to look behind the veil. E.g., The company holds the asset on resulting trust for the controller. The veil remains intact.
    *   **Evasion Principle (TRUE Piercing):** The *only* valid reason to pierce. Occurs when a person deliberately interposes a company to **evade a pre-existing legal obligation, restriction, or liability**. Narrowly construed; a "last resort" remedy.
*   **Tort Creditors:** Historically, courts resisted piercing the veil to help tort victims of underfunded subsidiaries (*Briggs v James Hardie*).

### 4. Corporate Groups
A group is a collection of related *separate* companies, not a single legal entity.
*   **Statutory Definitions:**
    *   **Subsidiary (s 46):** Parent controls board composition, OR >50% votes, OR >50% issued share capital.
    *   **Related Body Corporate (s 50):** Holding co, subsidiary, or subsidiary of the same holding co.
    *   **Controlled Entity (s 50AA):** Capacity to determine financial/operating policies.
*   **The Tension:**
    *   **Entity Model (Law):** Each company is separate. Directors owe duties to *their* individual company (*Walker v Wimborne*). 
    *   **Enterprise Model (Reality):** The group functions as a single economic unit. Courts sometimes struggle with the injustice of the Entity Model (e.g., *Qintex*).
*   **Statutory Exceptions for Groups:**
    *   **Insolvent Trading (s 588V-588X):** A holding company can be liable for the debts of an insolvent subsidiary if it knew or should have known of the insolvency.
    *   **Wholly Owned Subsidiaries (s 187):** A director of a wholly owned subsidiary can safely act in the best interests of the *holding company* IF the subsidiary's constitution allows it, it's done in good faith, and the subsidiary is solvent.

## 🗣️ PART B: ORAL ASSESSMENT PREP (THE "WHY")
*Practice discussing these highly testable policy areas.*

**Q: Explain the difference between Concealment and Evasion from *Prest*. Why did the court make this distinction?**
*Answer Checklist:*
*   **Evasion:** Using the corporate form to escape an *already existing* legal obligation (e.g., dodging an injunction by having a sham company do it). This justifies *piercing* the veil because the law must not be mocked.
*   **Concealment:** Merely hiding behind a corporate structure. This is common (e.g., nominee companies). It does *not* justify piercing the veil. Instead, the court applies standard equitable principles (like resulting trusts or agency).
*   **Why?** To heavily restrict veil piercing. Sumption LJ wanted to reaffirm the sanctity of *Salomon* and ensure piercing is an absolute last resort, preventing lower courts from piercing the veil merely because justice or "impropriety" seems to demand it.

**Q: How did *Meridian* change the way we attribute fault to companies compared to *Lennard’s Carrying Co*?**
*Answer Checklist:*
*   *Lennard’s* relied on the strict "directing mind and will" test. Only the very top executives were the company.
*   *Meridian* recognized this was too rigid for modern regulatory statutes. If a statute requires disclosure to the market (like the NZ Securities Act), and the company authorizes a mid-level manager to trade, the company "knows" what the manager knows.
*   Therefore, attribution is entirely contextual: it depends on interpreting the specific statute and ensuring its purpose is not frustrated by corporate structuring.

**Q: Discuss the tension between the "Entity Model" and the "Enterprise Model" in corporate groups. Which model does Australia follow?**
*Answer Checklist:*
*   **Australia strictly follows the Entity Model** (*Walker v Wimborne*). A parent company's assets belong to the parent; a subsidiary's assets belong to the subsidiary. Directors of the subsidiary cannot simply siphon funds to the parent without breaching their duties (*Qintex*), unless s 187 applies.
*   **The Enterprise Model** argues that groups operate as a single economic enterprise and should be treated as such for liability (e.g., making parents liable for subsidiary torts). 
*   **The Policy problem:** The Entity Model creates "moral hazard." Parent companies can quarantine risky activities (like asbestos mining in *James Hardie*) in undercapitalized subsidiaries, enjoying the profits while leaving tort victims uncompensated when the subsidiary goes bankrupt.

## ✍️ PART C: EXAM SCAFFOLDS (ISSUE SPOTTING & MIRAC)

### Scaffold 1: Can the Corporate Veil be Pierced?
*Use this if a shareholder/director is using a company to avoid a specific debt or injunction, or hiding assets.*
1.  **Issue:** Can [Claimant] pierce the corporate veil of [Company] to hold [Controller] personally liable (or reach their assets)?
2.  **Rule:** Under *Salomon*, the company is a separate legal entity. The veil can only be pierced under the **Evasion Principle** (*Prest v Petrodel*): where [Controller] deliberately evades an *existing* legal obligation/restriction by interposing a company.
3.  **Application:**
    *   Is it evasion? (Was the obligation pre-existing, and was the company specifically used to frustrate its enforcement?) *If yes -> Veil Pierced.*
    *   Is it merely concealment? (Did they just buy property through the company? Was the company acting as an agent or holding property on trust?) *If yes -> Veil remains intact, apply trust/agency law.*
    *   Statutory exceptions? Check **s 588G** (Director insolvent trading) or **s 588V** (Holding company insolvent trading).
4.  **Conclusion:** The court will likely look through the veil (evasion) OR declare a trust relationship (concealment), resulting in [Controller's] assets being exposed.

### Scaffold 2: Attributing Employee Knowledge/Acts to the Company
*Use this if a middle-manager commits statutory fraud or fails a disclosure requirement, and the question asks if the COMPANY itself has breached the statute.*
1.  **Issue:** Can the knowledge/acts of [Employee] be attributed to [Company] so as to render the company liable under [Statute]?
2.  **Rule:** The doctrine of attribution (*Meridian Global Funds*). Where primary rules (constitution) or general rules (agency/vicarious) do not provide the answer, the court applies a **Special Rule of Attribution**.
3.  **Application:**
    *   Identify the intended policy and purpose of the statute.
    *   Determine if [Employee] was the person authorized by the company to perform the relevant act.
    *   Ask: *Would the policy of the statute be defeated if the employee's knowledge was not attributed to the company?* (Like the disclosure requirement in *Meridian*).
4.  **Conclusion:** The knowledge is attributed, and [Company] is primarily liable for the statutory breach.

### Scaffold 3: Intra-Group Transfers & Director Duties
*Use this if a subsidiary director moves cash to help a struggling parent company.*
1.  **Issue:** Has [Director of Sub] breached their duty to act in the best interests of [Sub] by transferring funds to [Parent/Sibling Co]?
2.  **Rule:** Under the strict Entity Model (*Walker v Wimborne*), directors must act in the interests of *their* specific company, not the corporate group as a whole.
3.  **Exceptions to the Rule:**
    *   **Charterbridge Test (Equiticorp):** Could an intelligent, honest director reasonably believe the transaction benefited the *subsidiary* (e.g., saving the parent saved the sub's reputation/funding)?
    *   **s 187:** If [Sub] is a *wholly owned subsidiary*, the director may act in the interests of [Parent] IF:
        1. Sub's constitution expressly permits it;
        2. Director acts in good faith;
        3. Sub is solvent and remains so after the act.
4.  **Conclusion:** The director has/has not breached their duty, depending on whether s 187 elements are satisfied or the Charterbridge test is met.
