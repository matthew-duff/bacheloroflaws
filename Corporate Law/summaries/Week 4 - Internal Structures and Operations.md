# Week 4 - Internal Structures and Operations

## Key Statutes
* **s 134 CA**: Internal Governance Rules (IGRs)
* **s 135 CA**: Replaceable rules don't apply to 1-person companies
* **s 136 CA**: Adoption and alteration of constitution (Requires 75% Special Resolution)
* **s 140 CA**: Constitution acts as a Statutory Contract
* **s 141 CA**: Table of Replaceable Rules
* **s 198A CA (RR)**: Business managed by/under direction of directors
* **s 201A CA**: Minimum directors (Pty: 1, Public: 3 with 2 resident)
* **s 249D CA**: Members requesting a meeting
* **s 249H CA**: Notice of meeting (21 days usually, 28 for listed)
* **s 250J-JA CA**: Voting by poll or show of hands
* **s 1322 CA**: Procedural irregularities in meetings

## 1. Internal Governance Rules (IGRs)
*   **What are they?** Rules managing the internal workings of the company. Can be the *Corporations Act* Replaceable Rules, a customised constitution, or a combination (**s 134**).
*   **Replaceable Rules (RRs):** Default rules in the CA (**s 141**). Apply unless modified by a constitution.
*   **Constitution:** Customised rules. Obligatory for No Liability companies, guarantees, and listed companies (ASX-LR).
*   **Adoption & Alteration:** Must be adopted by all members on registration, or by 75% Special Resolution after (**s 136**). Dissenters are bound unless the change demands more money/shares from them (**s 140(2)**).

## 2. The Statutory Contract (s 140)
*   **Concept:** The constitution and RRs act as a statutory contract between:
    1.  Company & Member
    2.  Company & Director / Company Secretary
    3.  Member & Member
*   **Enforcement Limits:** Enforceable only by parties acting in their capacity as members (**_Eley v Positive Government_**; **_Bailey v NSW Medical Defense Union_**). Only personal rights can be enforced by a member; collective rights belong to the company (**_MacDougall v Gardiner_**; **_Foss v Harbottle_**).
*   **Remedies:** Usually declarations & injunctions. Damages are NOT available for breach of statutory contract (**_Webb Distributors_**).

## 3. Division of Powers
*   **Two primary organs:**
    1.  **Board of Directors:** Management rights.
    2.  **General Meeting (Shareholders):** Control and existential rights.
*   **General Rule:** Organs are sovereign within their spheres. The general meeting cannot override the board's management decisions unless the constitution says otherwise (**_Automatic Self-Cleansing Filter Syndicate_**; **_ACCR v CBA_**).
*   **s 198A (RR):** Power lies with directors to manage the business.

## 4. Members and the General Meeting
*   **Member Powers:**
    *   *Existential:* Changing name, type, constitution.
    *   *Personnel:* Removing/appointing directors, auditor.
    *   *Financial:* Significant/conflicted transactions, capital reductions.
    *   *Reserve Powers:* If the board is deadlocked or unable to act, power reverting to members.
*   **Meetings:**
    *   **AGMs** (Annual) and **EGMs** (Extraordinary).
    *   **Notice:** Generally 21 days for unlisted, 28 for listed (**s 249H**). Must contain specific matters (**s 249L**).
    *   **Calling a meeting:** Directors (**s 248C**), or members with 5% votes or 100 members (**s 249D**).
    *   **Quorum:** Usually 2 members (**s 249T**).
    *   **Voting:** Ordinary resolution (>50%) or Special Resolution (≥75% of voters). Passes by show of hands or by poll.

## 5. The Board of Directors
*   **Composition:** Directors (Exec, Non-Exec, Independent, Chair, Nominee/Alternate).
*   **Committees:** The board can delegate to committees (Audit, Remuneration, Risk Management, Nomination) (**s 198D**).
*   **Director's Rights:** Rights to inspect financial records at all times (**s 290**) and other books for certain situations (**s 198F**).
*   **Meetings:** Quorum is 2 directors (**s 248F**). Resolutions pass by majority (**s 248G**). "Paper meetings" allowed via written resolution (**s 248A**).

## 6. Curative Provisions (s 1322)
*   Procedural errors (like defective notice or lack of quorum) don't automatically invalidate a meeting.
*   The court can declare proceedings valid if the irregularity was procedural, there was honest intent, and it is just and equitable (no "substantial injustice" caused).
