# Exam Summary: The Sociological Concept of Law
**Author:** Roger Cotterrell (1983)

## 1. Core Objective
Cotterrell explores the transition from **jurisprudential** definitions of law to **sociological** concepts of law. He argues that for sociology, a "concept of law" is not an end in itself (as in jurisprudence) but a preliminary tool for organizing empirical research.

---

## 2. Key Distinction: Normative vs. Empirical Legal Theory

| Feature | Normative Legal Theory (Jurisprudence) | Empirical Legal Theory (Sociology of Law) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Focus** | Internal logic of legal doctrine. | Law as a social phenomenon. |
| **Method** | Explains law via rules, principles, and values within the doctrine. | Explains law via its social effects, power structures, and institutional practices. |
| **Goal** | Achieving a "scientific" definition or model of law. | Formulating a framework for empirical testing and social analysis. |

---

## 3. Typology of Sociological Concepts of Law
Cotterrell identifies three main ways sociologists conceptualize law:

### A. Law as State Law (The Dominant View)
*   **Definition:** Law is defined by its association with the state (e.g., Black’s view: "Law is governmental social control").
*   **Critique:** Often too narrow; it ignores non-state forms of regulation and can be "intimidated" by professional legal definitions.

### B. Legal Pluralism
*   **Definition:** Law exists at multiple levels of social interaction, independent of the state.
*   **Focus:** Normative orders within groups, organizations, or communities.
*   **Critique:** Can struggle to explain the specific political dimension and power of state law.

### C. Law as "Legality" or Doctrine
*   **Definition:** Focuses on the integrity of doctrine and the "essence" of the legal (procedural prerequisites).
*   **Focus:** How legal ideas and ideologies function to coordinate social life and formalize power.

---

## 4. Key Arguments for Exams

### 1. The Role of Ideology
*   **Argument:** Legal doctrine is not just technical; it is **ideological**.
*   **Function:** Ideology "cloaks" power and class relations while protecting them. It provides the authority for dispute processing (e.g., courts appearing "impersonal" while applying law).
*   **Quote:** "Defence of the so-called abstract foundations of the legal system is the most general form of defence of bourgeois class interests" (referencing Pashukanis).

### 2. Law as a Precision Instrument of Power
*   **Argument:** Law formalizes the exercise of power, turning it into a "precision instrument of control" across various levels of social life.
*   **Agenda Setting:** Power includes the ability to set the agenda of debate, which is heavily influenced by the ideological conditions created by law.

### 3. Beyond "Lawyers' Law"
*   **Argument:** To understand the changing social significance of law, sociologists must look at forms of **bureaucratic regulation** and control that exist outside traditional state law.
*   **Professional Boundary:** The legal sociologist must "become a lawyer" (understand doctrine) to effectively challenge or go beyond the lawyer's conception of law.

---

## 5. Summary of Significance
*   **For Sociology:** The concept of law is a "starting point" that influences research agendas.
*   **For Law Students:** Cotterrell highlights that law is not just a set of neutral rules but a social institution deeply embedded in power dynamics and ideological structures.
