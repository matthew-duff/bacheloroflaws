# Case Summary: Re Wakim; Ex parte McNally (1999) 198 CLR 511

## Court and Citation
*   **Court:** High Court of Australia
*   **Citation:** (1999) 198 CLR 511; [1999] HCA 27
*   **Judges:** Gleeson CJ, Gaudron, McHugh, Gummow, Kirby, Hayne, and Callinan JJ

## Overview
*Re Wakim* is a landmark constitutional law case that struck down the "cross-vesting" scheme, which allowed State Parliaments to vest State judicial power in federal courts (and the Commonwealth to consent to such vesting).

## Key Facts
1.  **The Scheme:** The *Jurisdiction of Courts (Cross-vesting) Act 1987* (Cth and States) and the *Corporations Act 1989* (Cth) established a system where federal courts could exercise State jurisdiction and vice versa.
2.  **The Challenge:** The prosecutors challenged the validity of these laws, arguing that the Commonwealth Constitution does not permit federal courts to exercise jurisdiction other than that specified in Chapter III.
3.  **Prior Case:** A similar challenge in *Gould v Brown* (1998) had failed because the High Court was equally divided (3-3).

## Issues
1.  Can a State Parliament validly confer State judicial power on a federal court?
2.  Can the Commonwealth Parliament validly consent to the conferral of State judicial power on a federal court?
3.  Does Chapter III of the Constitution exhaustively define the jurisdiction that can be conferred on federal courts?
4.  Does the "accrued jurisdiction" of federal courts allow them to hear non-federal claims that are part of the same "matter"?

## Ratio Decidendi (Reasons for Decision)
*   **Exhaustive Nature of Chapter III (6-1 Majority, Kirby J dissenting):**
    *   Chapter III of the Constitution is an **exhaustive statement** of the original jurisdiction that may be conferred on federal courts.
    *   Sections 75 and 76 define the "matters" in which federal courts can have jurisdiction. State judicial power is not one of these matters.
    *   Section 77(iii) allows the Commonwealth to invest State courts with federal jurisdiction, but there is no reciprocal provision allowing States to invest federal courts with State jurisdiction.
*   **Separation of Powers:**
    *   The Constitution is based on a separation of powers. The Commonwealth Parliament cannot expand the jurisdiction of federal courts beyond the limits set by Chapter III.
    *   "Co-operative federalism" is a political concept, not a legal source of power. Parliaments cannot, by agreement, bypass constitutional limitations.
*   **Accrued Jurisdiction:**
    *   Federal courts **can** hear non-federal claims if they are part of a single "matter" (a "justiciable controversy").
    *   A "matter" exists where the federal and non-federal claims arise out of a **common substratum of facts**.
    *   In *Re Wakim*, the claims against the solicitors were held to be within the Federal Court's accrued jurisdiction because they were part of the same controversy as the bankruptcy proceedings.

## Legal Significance
1.  **Invalidation of Cross-vesting:** The decision effectively ended the general cross-vesting of State jurisdiction into federal courts, leading to the "Corporations Law crisis" and the eventual referral of powers by the States to the Commonwealth (resulting in the *Corporations Act 2001*).
2.  **Strict Interpretation of Chapter III:** Reaffirmed the principle from *In re Judiciary and Navigation Acts* (1921) that federal judicial power is strictly limited to the "matters" listed in the Constitution.
3.  **Clarification of Accrued Jurisdiction:** Provided a practical test for when federal courts can exercise jurisdiction over State law claims (the "common substratum of facts" test).
4.  **Territories Power (s 122):** Confirmed that the Commonwealth can confer jurisdiction on federal courts in relation to Territory matters, as this is a "law made by the Parliament" under s 76(ii).

## Quick Revision Table
| Concept | Principle in *Re Wakim* |
| :--- | :--- |
| **State to Federal Cross-vesting** | **Invalid.** States cannot confer power on federal courts. |
| **Federal to State Cross-vesting** | **Valid.** Expressly permitted by s 77(iii). |
| **Chapter III** | **Exhaustive.** Limits federal court jurisdiction to ss 75 & 76. |
| **Accrued Jurisdiction** | **Valid.** If claims arise from a "common substratum of facts". |
| **Co-operative Federalism** | Cannot override constitutional prohibitions. |
| **Territories (s 122)** | Federal courts **can** exercise jurisdiction over Territory laws. |
