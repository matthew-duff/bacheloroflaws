# Case Summary: Re Wakim; Ex parte McNally (1999) 198 CLR 511

## Court and Citation
*   **Court:** High Court of Australia
*   **Citation:** (1999) 198 CLR 511; [1999] HCA 27
*   **Judges:** Gleeson CJ, Gaudron, McHugh, Gummow, Kirby, Hayne, and Callinan JJ

# Contextualising to Corporate Law
## Context
Before 2001, Australia did not have a single, unified federal corporate law. Instead, it operated on a "cooperative scheme" where the Commonwealth enacted a model _Corporations Law_ for the Australian Capital Territory, and each State passed legislation applying that exact same law within its own borders.

To make this system efficient and avoid forcing litigants to bounce between different state courts, the cross-vesting scheme allowed the Federal Court of Australia to hear these state-based corporate disputes.
## The _Re Wakim_ Bombshell
When the High Court ruled in _Re Wakim_ that the States could not constitutionally confer their jurisdiction onto federal courts, the cooperative corporate law scheme instantly fractured.

- **Loss of Jurisdiction:** The Federal Court of Australia suddenly lost the power to hear the vast majority of corporate law cases, as these matters technically arose under State law.
- **Insolvency Chaos:** Major corporate proceedings, including winding-up orders, liquidations, and director examinations that were currently before the Federal Court were thrown into legal limbo. (In fact, one of the specific matters bundled into the _Re Wakim_ judgment involved the winding up of a company, Amann Aviation, under state law).
- **Forum Shopping:** The decision threatened to return Australia to a fragmented system where corporate litigants would have to carefully navigate different State Supreme Courts depending on where a company was incorporated or operating.

Subsequently, to prevetn economic and legal chaos, a permanent fix was required. **The Corporations Act 2001**. Because the Commonwealth did not have the constitutional power to enact a comprehensive national corporate law on its own, the States agreed to use [[Constitutional Section 51(xxxvii)]] of the Constitution to formally refer their corporate law-making powers to the Commonwealth.

# Case Details
## Overview
*Re Wakim* is a landmark constitutional law case that struck down the "cross-vesting" scheme, which allowed State Parliaments to vest State judicial power in federal courts (and the Commonwealth to consent to such vesting).

## Key Facts
1.  **The Scheme:** The *Jurisdiction of Courts (Cross-vesting) Act 1987* (Cth and States) and the *Corporations Act 1989* (Cth) established a system where federal courts could exercise State jurisdiction and vice versa.
2.  **The Challenge:** The prosecutors challenged the validity of these laws, arguing that the Commonwealth Constitution does not permit federal courts to exercise jurisdiction other than that specified in Chapter III.
3.  **Prior Case:** A similar challenge in *Gould v Brown* (1998) had failed because the High Court was equally divided (3-3).

## Issues
1.  Can a State Parliament validly confer State judicial power on a federal court?
2.  Can the Commonwealth Parliament validly consent to the conferral of State judicial power on a federal court?
3.  Does Chapter III of the Constitution exhaustively define the jurisdiction that can be conferred on federal courts?
4.  Does the "accrued jurisdiction" of federal courts allow them to hear non-federal claims that are part of the same "matter"?

### **The Majority Judgments**

**Gummow and Hayne JJ (with Gleeson CJ and Gaudron J agreeing)**

- **Chapter III is Exhaustive:** Their Honours concluded that Chapter III of the Australian Constitution exhaustively defines and limits the jurisdiction that can be conferred on federal courts. Because State jurisdiction is not listed in Chapter III, it cannot be given to a federal court.
    
- **No Power to "Consent":** They rejected the argument that the Commonwealth's "consent" to the States conferring jurisdiction was valid under incidental constitutional powers. They held that no amount of "cooperative federalism" can supply constitutional power where none inherently exists.
    
- **Territory Jurisdiction Exception:** They upheld the validity of cross-vesting involving the Australian Capital Territory (ACT), noting that Section 122 of the Constitution explicitly allows the Commonwealth Parliament to make laws for the government of the Territories.
    
- **Accrued Jurisdiction:** They affirmed that federal courts can still hear non-federal (state) claims if they are part of a "single justiciable controversy" alongside a legitimate federal claim.
    

**McHugh J**

- **Strict Constitutional Interpretation:** Justice McHugh emphasized that the judiciary's role is to give effect to the Constitution's text, regardless of how politically or practically convenient the cross-vesting scheme might be.
    
- **Negative Implications:** He argued that because Chapter III explicitly states what matters federal courts _can_ hear, it carries a "negative implication" that expressly forbids them from hearing anything else. He found the idea that States could pour unlimited non-federal jurisdiction into federal courts to be "bizarre" and constitutionally baseless.
    

**Callinan J**

- **Historical Context:** Justice Callinan reviewed the framing of the Constitution and found zero historical intent to allow federal courts to be repositories for State judicial power.
    
- **Support for the Majority:** He completely agreed with McHugh J and the joint judgment of Gummow and Hayne JJ, concluding that there is no constitutional foundation for the States to invest federal courts with their jurisdiction.
    

---

### **The Dissenting Judgment**

**Kirby J**

- **Constitutional Adaptability:** Justice Kirby fiercely dissented, arguing that the Constitution should not be subjected to "Laocoönian constraints" but must be adaptable to the practical needs of a modern, cooperative federation.
    
- **Cooperative Federalism:** He pointed out that the Constitution does not explicitly forbid cross-vesting. He argued that since the Constitution encourages inter-governmental cooperation, striking down a universally supported and highly efficient legal scheme based on "negative implications" is overly rigid.
    
- **Implied Nationhood and Incidental Powers:** He concluded that the Commonwealth Parliament possessed the power to consent to the scheme through its "incidental powers" (ensuring the federal judiciary runs efficiently) and the "implied nationhood power" (coordinating a complex national society).
- 
## Legal Significance
1.  **Invalidation of Cross-vesting:** The decision effectively ended the general cross-vesting of State jurisdiction into federal courts, leading to the "Corporations Law crisis" and the eventual referral of powers by the States to the Commonwealth (resulting in the *Corporations Act 2001*).
2.  **Strict Interpretation of Chapter III:** Reaffirmed the principle from *In re Judiciary and Navigation Acts* (1921) that federal judicial power is strictly limited to the "matters" listed in the Constitution.
3.  **Clarification of Accrued Jurisdiction:** Provided a practical test for when federal courts can exercise jurisdiction over State law claims (the "common substratum of facts" test).
4.  **Territories Power (s 122):** Confirmed that the Commonwealth can confer jurisdiction on federal courts in relation to Territory matters, as this is a "law made by the Parliament" under s 76(ii).

## Quick Revision Table
| Concept | Principle in *Re Wakim* |
| :--- | :--- |
| **State to Federal Cross-vesting** | **Invalid.** States cannot confer power on federal courts. |
| **Federal to State Cross-vesting** | **Valid.** Expressly permitted by s 77(iii). |
| **Chapter III** | **Exhaustive.** Limits federal court jurisdiction to ss 75 & 76. |
| **Accrued Jurisdiction** | **Valid.** If claims arise from a "common substratum of facts". |
| **Co-operative Federalism** | Cannot override constitutional prohibitions. |
| **Territories (s 122)** | Federal courts **can** exercise jurisdiction over Territory laws. |
