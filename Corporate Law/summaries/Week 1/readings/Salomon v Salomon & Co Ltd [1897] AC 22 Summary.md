# Case Summary: Salomon v Salomon & Co Ltd [1897] AC 22

## Case Details
- **Court:** House of Lords
- **Date:** 16 November 1896
- **Judges:** Lord Halsbury LC, Lord Watson, Lord Herschell, Lord Macnaghten, Lord Morris, and Lord Davey.
- **Key Area of Law:** Corporate Personality; Limited Liability; Separate Legal Entity.

---

## Context / Facts
1. **Aron Salomon** was a successful sole trader (boot manufacturer) for 30 years.
2. He formed a limited company, **A. Salomon & Co. Ltd**, to transfer his business to.
3. **Statutory Compliance:** The Companies Act 1862 required 7 shareholders. Salomon, his wife, and 5 children each took 1 share. Salomon later held 20,001 shares; the other 6 family members held 1 share each.
4. **The Transaction:** The company purchased the business for ~£39,000. Payment included:
   - £20,000 in fully paid £1 shares.
   - £10,000 in debentures (secured by a floating charge over assets).
   - ~£9,000 in cash/debt discharge.
5. **Insolvency:** The company fell into financial difficulty. In liquidation, assets were sufficient to pay the secured debenture holder (Salomon) but not the unsecured creditors.
6. **Liquidator’s Claim:** The liquidator argued the company was a "sham," an "alias," or an "agent" for Salomon, and that Salomon should be personally liable for the company's debts.

---

## Ratio Decidendi (The Legal Rule)
**The "Salomon Principle":** Once a company is validly incorporated under the statute, it becomes a **separate legal entity** distinct from its shareholders and directors.

- **Separate Existence:** The company is not an agent or trustee for its members, even if one person holds virtually all the shares and controls the business.
- **Statutory Compliance:** If the formal requirements of the Act are met (e.g., 7 signatories), the motives of the founders are irrelevant. The law recognizes the "artificial creation" regardless of whether it is a "one-man company."
- **Limited Liability:** Shareholders are only liable to the extent of their shareholding; they are not responsible for the company's debts simply because they own or control it.

---

## Key Principles & Quotes
- **Corporate Veil:** The "veil of incorporation" separates the company from its members.
- **Lord Halsbury LC:** "Once the company is legally incorporated it must be treated like any other independent person with its rights and liabilities appropriate to itself... the motives of those who took part in the promotion... are absolutely irrelevant."
- **Lord Macnaghten:** "The company is at law a different person altogether from the subscribers to the memorandum... the company is not in law the agent of the subscribers or trustee for them."
- **Fraud:** Overvaluation of assets sold to the company is not fraud if all shareholders (the family) were fully aware of and consented to the price.

---

## Legal Significance (Exam Focus)
1. **Foundation of Company Law:** Establishes the doctrine of **separate legal personality**.
2. **Validation of Private/One-Man Companies:** Confirmed that individuals can gain the benefit of limited liability by forming a company, even if they retain total control.
3. **Security for Founders:** Validated the practice of a vendor taking secured debentures as part of the purchase price, giving them priority over unsecured creditors in liquidation.
4. **"Piercing the Veil":** This case sets a very high bar. The "veil" is only pierced in exceptional circumstances (e.g., fraud or sham to evade existing legal obligations), which were not found here despite the "one-man" nature of the business.

## Quick Revision Checklist
- [ ] **Entity:** Company ≠ Shareholders.
- [ ] **Control:** 99% ownership does not create an agency relationship.
- [ ] **Debentures:** Founders can be secured creditors of their own companies.
- [ ] **Motive:** Incorporating to limit personal liability is a legitimate use of the Act, not a "scheme" or "fraud."
