# Exam Summary: Schmitthoff, ‘The Origin of the Joint-Stock Company’ (1939)

## 1. Overview & Core Thesis
Schmitthoff examines the competing theories regarding the origin of the joint-stock company using a **comparative law approach**. He rejects the "receptionist" view (Italian origin) in favor of an **"evolutionist" theory**, arguing that the English joint-stock company is an indigenous development from medieval commercial structures.

---

## 2. Competing Theories of Origin

| Theory | Proponents | Core Argument | Schmitthoff's Critique |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Receptionist (Italian)** | Holdsworth, Gierke | Originated in Italy (e.g., Bank of St. George, 1419). Principle "received" by Northern Europe. | **Rejected.** Italian models evolved from **loans/debt** (state creditors), whereas Northern models evolved from **active trade**. |
| **Evolutionist (English)** | Scott, Schmitthoff | Indigenous development from the **Regulated Company** and medieval guilds. | **Supported.** It fits the structural transition from collective trading to joint-stock management. |
| **Shipping (Dutch)** | Lehmann | Developed from shipping partnerships (*Reederei*). | Seen as a parallel or subset of the evolutionist path. |

---

## 3. The Italian Model (The "Myth" of Reception)
*   **Key Institution:** *Casa di San Giorgio* (Genoa).
*   **Nature:** Originally a body of state creditors (*compera*). The state's debt was divided into shares (*luoghi*).
*   **Transition:** In 1419, it moved from a fixed interest rate to a dividend based on profits.
*   **Schmitthoff’s View:** This was a "barren" phenomenon. It was a **money-lending device** that never emancipated into a general commercial form. It lacked the "creative principle" of active trading found in the North.

---

## 4. The English Evolution (The "Regulated Company" Path)
Schmitthoff argues the joint-stock company is an offshoot of the **Regulated Company** (e.g., Merchant Adventurers).

### A. Structural Evolution
1.  **The Guild Roots:** Early social/religious guilds established the idea of "corporateness" (common property, internal tribunals, "brotherhood").
2.  **The Regulated Company:** A corporation where members traded on their *own* capital but followed common rules and enjoyed a monopoly.
3.  **Sub-organizations:** Within Regulated Companies, members began forming smaller "partnerships" or "companies" to pool capital for specific voyages (e.g., 2/3 for partner, 1/3 for me).
4.  **The Shift:** These sub-groups eventually supplanted the individual trading of the parent body. The "joint stock" became the permanent capital of the corporation itself.

### B. Legal Characteristics
*   **Corporateness:** Derived from English townships and boroughs, not Roman law.
*   **Limited Liability:** Evolved "insensibly" through the practice of these sub-organizations rather than being a sudden legal invention.

---

## 5. The Continental Development (Dutch Model)
*   **Dutch East India Company (1602):** The first "modern" joint-stock company on the continent.
*   **Structure:** A "compulsory shipping cartel" formed by uniting smaller pre-existing companies.
*   **Link to England:** Schmitthoff suggests the Dutch model either directly continued the English line or arose from identical economic conditions (the "creative principle of commercial corporateness").

---

## 6. Key Exam Takeaways
*   **Primary Origin:** Indigenous evolution from **commercial corporateness** and **regulated companies**, not a transplant of Italian banking practices.
*   **Structural Difference:** Italian models were **debt-based** (passive); English/Dutch models were **trade-based** (active).
*   **The "Creative Principle":** Law follows economic expansion; the joint-stock form was the legal expression of the need for concentrated capital in age-of-discovery exploration.
