# Summary: Criminal Code Act 1899 (Qld) - Corporate Law Context

**File:** `Corporate Law/sources/Week 1-Materials to L1/readings/Criminal Code Act 1899 (Qld).md`
**Focus:** Key provisions relevant to corporate criminal responsibility and liability of company members/officers.

---

## 1. Criminal Responsibility of Members (s 34)
**Section 34: Offences by partners and members of companies with respect to partnership or corporate property**
*   **Principle:** A member of a corporation or joint stock company is criminally responsible for acts or omissions regarding corporate property to the same extent as if they were not a member.
*   **Exam Significance:** This section clarifies that corporate membership does not provide a shield against criminal liability for offences involving the entity's property (e.g., stealing or fraud against the company by a shareholder).

## 2. Secret Commissions (Chapter 42A)
This chapter deals with corruption involving agents, which often includes company directors and officers.

### s 442G: Liability of director etc. acting without authority
*   **Principle:** Any director, manager, or officer of a company who knowingly takes part in, or is privy to, doing any act without authority which would contravene the "Secret Commissions" chapter, commits a crime.
*   **Exam Significance:** Highlights the specific criminal exposure of corporate officers for unauthorized corrupt dealings.

### s 442I: Penalty on conviction
*   **Corporations:** Liable to a penalty of **3,400 penalty units**.
*   **Individuals:** Liable to **7 years imprisonment**.
*   **Additional Liability:** The court may order the return of the value of the secret commission received or given.

### s 442M: Custom of itself no defence
*   **Principle:** It is **not a defence** to show that receiving or offering a secret commission is "customary" in a particular trade or business.
*   **Exam Significance:** Prevents the "industry standard" argument from being used to excuse corrupt corporate behavior.

## 3. General Principles of Responsibility (Chapter 5)
While applicable to all persons, these sections form the basis of the "identification doctrine" and "vicarious liability" discussions in corporate law:
*   **s 23 (Intention—motive):** Subject to express provisions, a person is not criminally responsible for an act or omission which occurs independently of the exercise of the person’s will, or for an event which occurs by accident.
*   **s 7 (Principal offenders):** Defines parties to an offence, including those who "procure" or "aid" the commission of an offence—relevant for attributing acts of employees/agents to the corporation or its controllers.

## 4. Fraud and Falsification (Part 6)
*   **s 408C (Fraud):** The primary fraud provision used in corporate contexts (e.g., directors using company funds dishonestly).
*   **s 441 (Fraudulent falsification of records):** Specifically targets clerks or servants (including company officers) who falsify books or accounts with intent to defraud.

---
**Revision Note:** In the context of "Week 1: Companies and Company Law", these sections illustrate that the "separate legal personality" of a corporation does not immunize the individuals behind it from criminal law, and that the Code specifically contemplates the corporation as a subject of criminal penalties (e.g., s 442I).
