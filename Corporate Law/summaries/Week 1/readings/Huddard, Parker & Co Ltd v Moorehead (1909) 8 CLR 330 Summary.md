# Exam Summary: Huddart, Parker & Co Ltd v Moorehead (1909) 8 CLR 330

## Case Overview
*   **Court:** High Court of Australia
*   **Significance:** A foundational (though later partially overruled) case on the **Corporations Power** (s 51(xx)) and the **Trade and Commerce Power** (s 51(i)) of the Australian Constitution. It established the "Reserved State Powers" doctrine, which was later overturned in the *Engineers' Case* (1920).

---

## Key Facts
*   The Commonwealth passed the *Australian Industries Preservation Act 1906* (Cth), which aimed to prevent monopolies and restrictive trade practices.
*   Sections 5 and 8 of the Act specifically prohibited foreign, trading, and financial corporations from engaging in certain restrictive trade practices within a State (intra-state trade).
*   Huddart, Parker & Co (a Victorian company) refused to answer questions from the Comptroller-General of Customs regarding alleged anti-competitive behavior in the coal trade, challenging the validity of the Act.

---

## Ratio Decidendi (The Legal Rule)
1.  **Scope of s 51(xx):** The High Court held that s 51(xx) does **not** grant the Commonwealth power to create corporations; it only applies to corporations already "formed" (either foreign or under State law).
2.  **Reserved State Powers (The "Old" View):** The majority (Griffith CJ, Barton, O'Connor, and Higgins JJ) held that the power to regulate intra-state trade was "reserved" to the States. Therefore, the Commonwealth could not use the corporations power to regulate the internal trading activities of companies within a single State.
3.  **Invalidity of ss 5 and 8:** Because these sections attempted to regulate the *conduct* of corporations in intra-state trade (a field deemed reserved to the States), they were held to be *ultra vires* (beyond power) and invalid.
4.  **Investigative Power (s 15B):** The Court held that the power to compel answers to questions (s 15B) was valid as an administrative inquiry and did not constitute an exercise of judicial power.

---

## Key Principles & Judicial Reasoning
*   **Griffith CJ & Barton J:** Argued that s 51(xx) allows the Commonwealth to set conditions for a corporation to enter a field of trade, but once the corporation is legally "in" the State, its actual trading operations are governed by State law.
*   **O'Connor J:** Viewed s 51(xx) as a power to "recognize" corporations as legal entities, not to regulate their business conduct.
*   **Isaacs J (Dissenting):** Provided a broader interpretation of s 51(xx), arguing that the power should allow the Commonwealth to regulate the conduct of the specified corporations in their dealings with the public. This dissent eventually became the basis for the modern, broader interpretation of the corporations power in *Strickland v Rocla Concrete Pipes Ltd* (1971).

---

## Legal Significance for Exams
*   **Historical Context:** Represents the peak of the "Reserved State Powers" doctrine.
*   **Overruled Status:** While the specific holding regarding the invalidity of the trade practices provisions was **overruled** by *Strickland v Rocla Concrete Pipes Ltd* (1971), the principle that the Commonwealth cannot *create* corporations under s 51(xx) remains law (confirmed in the *[[Incorporations Case (NSW v Commonwealth (1990) 169 CLR 482)]]* (1990)).
*   **Modern Application:** In an exam, use this case to show the evolution of s 51(xx). Contrast the narrow "status-based" view in *Huddart Parker* with the modern "activities-based" or "object-of-command" view established in *Rocla Pipes* and *WorkChoices* (2006).
