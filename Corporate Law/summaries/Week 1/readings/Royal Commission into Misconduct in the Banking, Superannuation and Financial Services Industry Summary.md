# Exam Summary: Royal Commission into Misconduct in the Banking, Superannuation and Financial Services Industry (Hayne Royal Commission)

## 1. Core Mandate & Findings
*   **Purpose:** To inquire into whether conduct by financial services entities amounted to misconduct or fell below community standards and expectations.
*   **Key Finding:** Misconduct was widespread, driven by the pursuit of profit (entity) and gain (individual). Providing service to customers was relegated to second place; "sales became all-important."
*   **Primary Responsibility:** Lies with the **entities** and their **boards/senior management**. They are responsible for the culture, governance, and remuneration practices that allowed misconduct.

## 2. The Four Key Observations (Root Causes)
1.  **Connection between Conduct and Reward:** Incentive and commission schemes measured sales/profit, not compliance or proper standards. Rewarding misconduct drove illegal or unethical behavior.
2.  **Asymmetry of Power and Information:** Marked imbalance between entities (who set terms) and consumers (who lacked knowledge and negotiating power).
3.  **Conflicts between Duty and Interest:** Intermediaries (e.g., mortgage brokers) often act in their own interest or the provider's interest rather than the client's. "Self-interest will almost always trump duty."
4.  **Lack of Accountability:** Entities were not properly held to account. Compensation is not the same as punishment; misconduct must be detected, denounced, and punished to be deterred.

## 3. The Six Norms of Conduct (Fundamental Principles)
These are the "pillars" for all financial services behavior:
1.  **Obey the law.**
2.  **Do not mislead or deceive.**
3.  **Act fairly.**
4.  **Provide services that are fit for purpose.**
5.  **Deliver services with reasonable care and skill.**
6.  **When acting for another, act in the best interests of that other.**

## 4. General Rules for Reform
*   **Apply and Enforce the Law:** Shift from "managing" risks to ensuring compliance.
*   **Industry Codes:** Should be enforceable. Key promises in codes should be treated as statutory breaches if broken.
*   **Ban on "Hawking":** Prohibit unsolicited offering of financial products (especially superannuation and insurance) to retail clients to prevent preying on the unsuspecting.
*   **Intermediaries:** Must act for the person who *pays* them. They owe a duty to act only in the interests of that person.
*   **Conflicted Remuneration:** Eliminate exceptions (e.g., **grandfathered commissions**). There is no justification for maintaining payments that influence advice.
*   **Culture and Governance:** Must focus on **non-financial risks** (compliance, conduct, reputation) as much as financial risks.

## 5. Legislative Approach: Simplification
*   **Reduce Exceptions:** Complication in the law often comes from "carve-outs" for special interests. These should be eliminated to prevent "gaming" the system.
*   **Explicit Connections:** Legislation should explicitly link specific rules to the six fundamental norms of conduct to clarify the law's purpose.

## 6. Key Exam Terms
*   **Misconduct:** Conduct that breaks the law or falls below community expectations.
*   **Conflicted Remuneration:** Benefits that could reasonably be expected to influence the choice of product or advice given.
*   **Grandfathered Commissions:** Commissions on old products that were exempt from the original FOFA (Future of Financial Advice) reforms; the Commission recommended their total abolition.
*   **Hawking:** Unsolicited selling of financial products.
*   **Efficiently, Honestly, and Fairly:** The overarching statutory obligation for AFSL and ACL holders (s 912A Corporations Act; s 47 NCCP Act).
