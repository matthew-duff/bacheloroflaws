# Shaffer, ‘How Businesses Shape Law: A Socio-Legal Framework’ (2009) Summary

## Overview
Gregory Shaffer provides a **socio-legal framework** to analyze the mechanisms through which business interests shape law. He argues that law is not autonomous but is in a **dynamic, reciprocal relationship** with business. Business shapes law through public institutions and by creating its own private legal systems.

---

## 1. Business and the Public Legal System
Business holds a "privileged position" in public lawmaking due to its structural importance (jobs, investment) and superior resources.

*   **Legislation:** Business uses monetary resources, lobbying, and "think tanks" to influence statutes. Legislators often include "loopholes" or ambiguous language to satisfy business interests while appearing to serve the public.
*   **Administration (Agencies):** Agencies are subject to "capture" or "co-optation." Constant interaction between regulators and the regulated (the "revolving door") leads to a "negotiated legal order" where business provides the very information used for its regulation.
*   **Courts:** Business acts as a **"Repeat Player"** (Galanter’s theory).
    *   **Advantages:** Economies of scale, better lawyers, ability to absorb costs.
    *   **"Playing for Rules":** Businesses settle cases likely to create bad precedent and litigate those likely to create favorable rules.
    *   **Shadow of the Law:** Most disputes are settled in the "shadow" of potential litigation costs and judicial outcomes, where well-resourced parties can induce favorable settlements.

---

## 2. The Private Legal Sphere
Business creates its own legal systems to govern transactions, often bypassing or transforming public law.

*   **Lex Mercatoria (Private Merchant Law):** Centralized private regimes (e.g., ICC rules like UCP for letters of credit) that national courts often enforce.
*   **Relational/Market Norms:** Decentralized reliance on reputation and long-term relations rather than formal law (Macaulay/Macneil).
*   **Internalization (Corporate Policies):** Businesses create internal compliance departments.
    *   **Expanding Law:** Can spread "rights consciousness" (e.g., civil rights/diversity policies).
    *   **Curtailing Law:** Can "managerialize" law, redefining compliance to suit business goals and using internal grievance procedures to preempt external judicial intervention.

---

## 3. Dynamic Interaction (Public-Private)
Public and private systems are **interpenetrated**.
*   **Public in Shadow of Private:** Legislators often codify existing business customs (e.g., Uniform Commercial Code). Courts may defer to internal corporate grievance procedures as evidence of "reasonable" behavior.
*   **Private in Shadow of Public:** Private standards are often created to preempt potential government regulation.

---

## 4. Global and Transnational Context
*   **Transnational Lawmaking:** Business enlists powerful states (e.g., the US) to export their preferred legal models via international treaties (WTO, UNCITRAL).
*   **Differential Reception:** Transnational law acts as a **"legal irritant"** (Teubner). Its impact varies by country based on:
    *   **Legal Culture:** Attitudes toward law (e.g., US "adversarial legalism" vs. Asian preferences for informal resolution).
    *   **Institutional Path Dependency:** Existing structures that mediate how new global rules are implemented.
*   **Recursivity:** Lawmaking is a cycle where national experiences influence global norms, which then constrain national law (Halliday & Carruthers).

---

## Key Revision Concepts for Exams
*   **Repeat Players vs. One-Shotters:** Why "haves" come out ahead in litigation.
*   **Managerialization of Law:** How internal corporate policies transform the meaning of public legal mandates.
*   **Adversarial Legalism:** The US-specific style of lawyer-dominated, litigious policy implementation.
*   **Recursivity of Law:** The three-way cycle between national lawmaking, global norm-making, and their intersection.
*   **Legal Autonomy vs. Responsiveness:** The tension between law appearing independent and being socially relevant.
