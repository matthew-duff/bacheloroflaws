# Case Summary: R v Hughes (2000) 202 CLR 535

## 1. Case Details
- **Court:** High Court of Australia
- **Date:** 3 May 2000
- **Citation:** [2000] HCA 22; 202 CLR 535
- **Coram:** Gleeson CJ, Gaudron, McHugh, Gummow, Kirby, Hayne, and Callinan JJ

## 2. Context & Facts
- **The Scheme:** The case concerned the "national scheme" for corporate regulation (the Corporations Law), where Commonwealth legislation applied in the ACT and was adopted by each State as its own law (e.g., *Corporations (Western Australia) Act 1990* (WA)).
- **The Prosecution:** The Commonwealth Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) prosecuted Hughes in WA for offences against the *Corporations Law of Western Australia* (specifically relating to "prescribed interests" offered in an investment scheme involving offshore transactions in the USA).
- **The Challenge:** Hughes challenged the power of the Commonwealth DPP to prosecute State offences, arguing that the legislative "cross-vesting" of executive power was invalid.

## 3. Key Legal Issues
1. Whether a State can validly confer executive functions/powers on a Commonwealth officer (the DPP).
2. Whether the Commonwealth can validly authorize its officers to accept and exercise those State functions/powers.
3. Whether the Commonwealth can impose a **duty** on its officers to exercise State powers.

## 4. Ratio Decidendi (The Principles)
- **Conferral of Power:** A State can confer powers on a Commonwealth officer, and the Commonwealth can permit its officers to accept those powers (cooperative federalism).
- **The Requirement for a Head of Power:** For the Commonwealth to authorize or impose a **duty** on its officers to exercise State functions (especially those affecting the rights of individuals, like criminal prosecution), there must be a **nexus to a Commonwealth head of power** under the Constitution (e.g., s 51).
- **Incidental Power (s 51(xxxix)):** The Commonwealth's power to legislate in aid of the executive power (s 61) supports the DPP's role, but only where the underlying subject matter falls within federal legislative competence.
- **Application to Facts:** The prosecution was valid because the specific offences (offshore investment schemes) fell under the **Trade and Commerce power (s 51(i))** and the **External Affairs power (s 51(xxix))**.

## 5. Legal Significance (Exam Focus)
- **Limits on Cooperative Schemes:** *Hughes* qualified the earlier broad view of cooperative federalism in *R v Duncan* (1983). It established that the Commonwealth cannot simply "adopt" State executive functions without a constitutional basis.
- **Duty vs. Permission:** While the Commonwealth can *permit* officers to perform State functions, if the scheme involves a *duty* to act (which the Corporations Law did), a specific head of power is required.
- **Constitutional Vulnerability:** This decision exposed the "Hughes gap"—the risk that many parts of the Corporations Law (those not clearly under s 51(xx) or other powers) might be unprosecutable by the Commonwealth DPP.
- **Resulting Reform:** This case (along with *Wakim*) led directly to the **referral of powers** by the States to the Commonwealth under s 51(xxxvii), resulting in the *Corporations Act 2001* (Cth).

## 6. Key Sections Cited
- **Constitution:** ss 51(i), 51(xx), 51(xxix), 51(xxxix), 61, 109.
- **WA Law:** *Corporations (Western Australia) Act 1990*, ss 29, 31.
- **Cth Law:** *Corporations Act 1989*, s 47; *DPP Act 1983*.
