# Case Summary: New South Wales v Commonwealth (1990) 169 CLR 482
*(The Incorporation Case)*

## ⚖️ Case Details
- **Court:** High Court of Australia
- **Citation:** (1990) 169 CLR 482; [1990] HCA 2
- **Key Constitutional Provision:** Section 51(xx) — The Corporations Power

---

## 📝 Context & Facts
The Commonwealth Parliament enacted the *Corporations Act 1989* (Cth), which sought to establish a national system of company law. Specifically, sections 114–125 purported to allow the Commonwealth to **incorporate** (create) trading and financial corporations. Several States challenged the validity of these provisions, arguing that the Commonwealth's power under s 51(xx) was limited to regulating *existing* corporations, not creating them.

---

## 💡 Key Principles & Ratio Decidendi

### 1. The Scope of s 51(xx)
The High Court (6:1 majority, Deane J dissenting) held that **s 51(xx) does not confer power on the Commonwealth to legislate for the incorporation of trading and financial corporations.**

### 2. Textual Interpretation: "Formed"
The majority focused on the specific phrasing of s 51(xx): *"Foreign corporations, and trading or financial corporations **formed** within the limits of the Commonwealth"*.
- **Past Participle:** The word "formed" is a past participle used adjectivally. 
- **Temporal Meaning:** It describes corporations that have *already* undergone the process of formation. 
- **Conclusion:** The power is one with respect to "formed corporations," thereby excluding the process of incorporation itself.

### 3. Historical & Precedent Support
- **Huddart, Parker & Co Pty Ltd v Moorehead (1909):** The Court reaffirmed the textual conclusion reached in *Huddart Parker*—that the power is confined to corporations already in existence—despite the "reserved powers" doctrine from that case having been long discarded (*Engineers' Case*).
- **Constitutional Convention Debates:** Historical records indicated that the framers intended for the states to retain the power of incorporation, while the Commonwealth would have power over their "status" or "recognition" once formed.

### 4. Comparison with s 51(xiii)
The Court noted that s 51(xiii) (the Banking power) expressly includes "the incorporation of banks." The absence of similar language in s 51(xx) supported the inference that a general power of incorporation was not intended.

---

## 🏛️ Legal Significance
- **State vs. Federal Power:** Confirmed that the power to create (incorporate) general trading and financial corporations remains with the States.
- **Limits of s 51(xx):** While the corporations power is broad in terms of *regulating* the activities of existing corporations (as established in *Strickland v Rocla Concrete Pipes*), it is not a power to bring those corporations into existence.
- **The "Corporations Law" Scheme:** This decision necessitated the "cooperative scheme" (the *Corporations Act 2001*), where States referred their powers to the Commonwealth to allow for a truly national regulatory framework.

---

## 🎓 Exam Quick Revision
- **Question:** Can the Commonwealth use s 51(xx) to create a new company?
- **Answer:** **No.** (*NSW v Commonwealth*).
- **Reasoning:** The word "formed" in s 51(xx) implies the corporation must already exist. The power is over "formed" entities, not the act of "forming" them.
- **Dissent:** Deane J argued for a "plenary" and "liberal" construction, suggesting "formed" was merely a descriptive label to distinguish local from foreign corporations.
