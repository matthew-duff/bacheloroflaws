> [!IMPORTANT]
> This chapter is foundational. Exam questions frequently test: the **registration process**, the **proprietary vs public distinction**, the **four liability structures**, and the **entity model vs enterprise model** in corporate groups.

## 1. What Is a "Corporation"?

### s 57A Definition (Non-Exhaustive)

The term **"corporation"** under the Act includes (but is not limited to):

| # | Type | Key Detail |
|---|------|------------|
| 1 | **Company** | Registered under the Act (s 9) — the primary focus |
| 2 | **Body corporate** | Any body with corporate personality (general law) |
| 3 | **Unincorporated body** | That may sue/be sued or hold property in the name of its secretary/office-holder |
| 4 | **ATSI corporation** | Registered under the *Corporations (Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander) Act 2006* (Cth) |

### Two Specific Exclusions (s 57A(2))

1. **Exempt public authority** — a body corporate that is a "public authority" or "instrumentality/agency of the Crown"
   - *FCT v Bank of Western Australia*: must perform a **traditional or inalienable government function** with government authority; a private body for profit cannot qualify
2. **Corporation sole** — a largely historical concept; the occupant of a particular office given corporate status (e.g. the King, a bishop — per Blackstone)

> [!IMPORTANT]
> A **company** is a *subset* of "corporation." Some Act provisions use "corporation" (broader) while others use "company" (narrower). Know which term a section uses.

## 2. The Registration Process

Four main steps to create a company under the Act:

### Step 1 — Reserve the Company Name

- Apply to **ASIC** to reserve a name (s 152)
- **Part 2B.6** governs name availability and restrictions
- Can register with **ACN only** as the name (s 148(1))
- Prohibited/restricted words include: *Trust*, *Incorporated*, words suggesting government/Royal connection, and *Sir Donald Bradman*
- Once registered: name + ACN must appear on all **public documents and negotiable instruments** (s 153)

### Step 2 — Internal Rules and Consents

- Decide: rely on **replaceable rules** or adopt a **constitution** (or a combination)
- If constitution → each proposed member must **agree in writing** to its terms (s 136(1))
- **Consents required** from each person who will be a member, director, or company secretary (s 120)
- **Director requirements:**
  - Proprietary: min **1 director**, ordinarily resident in Australia (s 201A(1))
  - Public: min **3 directors**, at least **2** ordinarily resident in Australia (s 201A(2))
- **Secretary:**
  - Public: must have at least **1 secretary** (ordinarily in Aus)
  - Proprietary: **not required** to have a secretary (s 204A)
- If share capital → members consent to take up shares and pay the amount required on registration

### Step 3 — Lodge Application with ASIC

Application under **s 117(2)** must include:
- Type of company
- Proposed name (unless ACN is used)
- Name and address of each proposed member
- Present name, former names, DOB, place of birth of each proposed director/secretary
- Address of proposed registered office
- If share capital — number of shares + amount to pay
- If limited by guarantee — amount of guarantee
- Public companies must attach a **copy of the constitution** (if any)
- Can lodge online or in hard copy

### Step 4 — Registration by ASIC

- ASIC **"may"** assign an ACN, register the company, and issue a **certificate of registration** (s 118)
- Certificate includes: name, ACN, company type, date of registration
- **s 1274(7A)**: Certificate is **conclusive evidence** that all registration requirements were complied with
  - Exception: ASIC itself is not bound — it can challenge validity of registration
- Company comes into existence at the **beginning of the day** on which it is registered

## 3. Consequences of Registration

Upon registration, the company is a **body corporate** (s 119). Key consequences:

| Consequence | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| **Sue and be sued** in own right | The company (not members) has standing — **proper plaintiff principle**. Problems arise when the wrongdoer controls the company (→ Ch 14) |
| **Perpetual succession** | Continues regardless of changes in members, directors, or company name/type. Only ends by **winding up** or **cancellation of registration** |
| **Property capacity** | Can acquire, hold, and dispose of property independently. Members/directors have **no legal or equitable interest** in company property — *Gramophone & Typewriter Ltd v Stanley* |

> [!CAUTION]
> A frequent exam issue: ignoring the company's separate existence — especially in small family businesses. Owning all shares does **not** make the company's assets yours.

## 4. Other Corporate Forms (Outside the Act)

### 4.1 Incorporated Associations
- Each state/territory has an **Associations Incorporation Act**
- For **non-profit** organisations (community, sporting groups)
- Requires ≥ 5 members; cannot trade for members' pecuniary gain (trading to fund non-profit activities is OK)
- Has body corporate features: perpetual succession, limited liability, property capacity
- Must include **"Inc."** in name
- Annual accounts must be prepared, audited, and presented to AGM

### 4.2 Co-operatives
- Governed by **Co-operatives National Law (CNL)** (adopted by state/territory legislation)
- Becomes a corporation once registered (CNL s 28)
- Seven **co-operative principles** (s 10): autonomous, self-help, democratically controlled, limited capital compensation
- CNL incorporates/mirrors many Corporations Act provisions (especially director duties)

### 4.3 Statutory Corporations
- Created by **specific Acts of Parliament** (e.g. ABC, Australian Human Rights Commission, universities)
- Fall **outside** the Corporations Act unless their creating legislation provides otherwise
- Can be privatised by converting to a company under the Act (e.g. Commonwealth Bank in 1990)
- Significant policy issues re accountability when governments hold interests in companies under general incorporation legislation

## 6. Proprietary vs Public Companies

### 6.1 Proprietary Companies — Five Requirements

| # | Requirement | Authority |
|---|-------------|-----------|
| 1 | Must have a **share capital** | s 112(1) |
| 2 | Min **1 member** (s 114), max **50 non-employee shareholders** (s 113). Unlimited employee shareholders allowed | s 113, 114 |
| 3 | Min **1 director** ordinarily resident in Australia | s 201A(1) |
| 4 | **Cannot** engage in public fundraising (Ch 6D); can only offer shares to existing members/employees | s 113(3) |
| 5 | Must include **"Proprietary Limited" / "Pty Ltd"** at end of name | ss 148(2), 149 |

**Key cases & points:**
- *Re Dungowan Manly Pty Ltd (in liq)*: s 114 minimum of 1 member must not be disregarded even though no penalty is prescribed
- A **company can be a member** of another company (s 2C, *Acts Interpretation Act*)
- A body corporate **cannot** be appointed as a director (s 201B(1))
- A company can be a **"shadow director"** for liability purposes — *Standard Chartered Bank v Antico*
- One person can be **sole member and sole director** (→ one-person company)

### 6.2 Large vs Small Proprietary Companies

**Three criteria** (s 45A) — a company is **large** if it meets ≥ 2 of the thresholds for a financial year (specific amounts in Corporations Regulations). **Small** = falls below any 2 of the 3 criteria.

**Small proprietary company advantages:**
- **No obligation** to prepare financial report/directors' report unless directed by shareholders (≥ 5% votes) or ASIC (ss 293–294)
- If directed to prepare, **no audit required** unless shareholders (≥ 5% votes) direct it or ASIC orders it (ss 293, 301(2), 294)

> [!NOTE]
> The large/small classification can **change year to year** as business expands or contracts, altering financial reporting obligations.

### 6.3 Public Companies (Residual Category)

A public company is **any company that is not proprietary** (s 9).

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Members | Min **1** (s 114); **no statutory maximum** |
| Directors | Min **3**, at least **2** ordinarily in Australia (s 201A(2)) |
| Fundraising | May offer securities to the public (Ch 6D); may seek listing on a securities exchange |
| Share capital | Not required — can be a company limited by guarantee |

### 6.4 Key Differences: Proprietary vs Public

**Replaceable rules specific to proprietary companies:**
- **s 194** — Directors can vote on contracts where they have a declared interest
- **s 254D** — Pre-emption rights for existing shareholders on new share issues
- **s 254W(2)** — Directors can pay dividends as they see fit
- **s 1072G** — Directors can refuse to register a share transfer for any reason

**Rules specific to public companies:**
- **s 201E** — Each director must be appointed by **separate resolution** (unless unanimous agreement)
- **s 203D** — Directors can be removed by **ordinary resolution** regardless of constitution
- **s 195** — Directors with a material interest must **not vote** or be present during discussion (unless permitted by non-affected directors)
- **Ch 2E** — Closely regulates financial benefits to **related parties**

### 6.5 Changing Status (s 162(1))

- Proprietary ↔ Public conversion by **special resolution** (≥ 75% of votes: s 250MA) + lodging required documents with ASIC (s 163)
- Change of status **does not** create a new legal entity (s 166(1))
- ASIC can **force** a proprietary company to become public if it breaches s 113 requirements (s 165)

## 7. Liability of Members — Four Structures (s 112)

### 7.1 Company Limited by Shares

- Capital divided into shares; member liable only for **unpaid amount** on their shares (ss 9, 516)
- Once fully paid → **liability discharged** (for that shareholder and future holders)
- Must include **"Limited" / "Ltd"** at end of name (s 148(2))
- Can be proprietary or public
- **Proprietary share ownership** = primarily about allocating liability, power, and control
- **Public share ownership** = primarily a function of **investment**

### 7.2 Company Limited by Guarantee

- **No share capital**; no power to issue shares (s 124(1))
- Members guarantee a specified amount if the company is wound up and assets are insufficient (ss 9, 517)
- Liability only arises **on winding up**; no capital required during operation
- **Cannot pay dividends** to members (s 254SA)
- Must be a **public company** (because no share capital)
- Include **"Limited" / "Ltd"** at end of name (s 148(2))
- Common for welfare, social, **not-for-profit** organisations
- s 150: ASIC may allow omission of "Limited" if registered as a **charity** under the *Australian Charities and Not-for-profits Commission Act 2012* (Cth), constitution prohibits fees to directors, and directors approve all other payments

**Key case:** *National Safety Council of Australia (Vic Division)* — one of Australia's largest collapses was a guarantee company. 300 employees, losses ~$65.6M from CEO fraud. Resulted in significant decision on **duties of care of non-executive directors**.

### 7.3 Unlimited Liability Company

- Members liable **without limit** for company debts if assets insufficient (s 9)
- Must have a **share capital** (member pays unpaid on shares first, then further contributions)
- Can be proprietary or public
- **No requirement** to indicate unlimited liability in name (but unlimited proprietary must include "Proprietary": s 148(3))
- **Key advantage**: not subject to restrictions on **share capital reductions** (s 258A) — shareholders can have capital returned while company still operates
- Commonly used by **professional practices** (lawyers, accountants)

### 7.4 No Liability Company

- No contractual right to recover amounts unpaid on shares from shareholders (ss 9, 112(2), 254M(2))
- Shareholder may **forfeit shares** rather than pay called amounts
- **Restricted to** companies with share capital whose **sole object is mining purposes** (s 112(2))
- Cannot engage in activities outside mining purposes (s 112(3))
- Must include **"No Liability" / "NL"** at end of name (s 148(4))
- Must be a **public company** (s 112(1))
- Historical origin: *Mining Companies Act 1871* (Vic), introduced to combat "dummying" — using fictitious names to avoid paying

## 8. Change of Company Status (Part 2B.7, ss 162–167)

- Pass a **special resolution** → lodge with ASIC
- Where change may **increase member obligations/liabilities** (e.g. limited → unlimited, guarantee → shares), additional documentation required (s 163)
- Change effective when ASIC publishes notice (s 164)
- Change **does not** create a new legal entity; does not affect existing property, rights, obligations, or legal proceedings (s 166(1))
- *Windsor v National Mutual*: sections dealing with change of type are intended to **cover the field** — cannot change status by another method (e.g. scheme of arrangement)

## 9. Holding Companies, Subsidiaries and Corporate Groups

### 9.1 Subsidiary Test (s 46)

A company is a **subsidiary** of another if the first company:

1. **Controls the composition** of the subsidiary's board (including power to appoint/remove all or majority of directors — s 47); **OR**
2. Can **cast or control** > 50% of maximum votes at a general meeting; **OR**
3. Holds > **50% of issued share capital**

**Exclusions:** Does not apply where shares/power are held in a **fiduciary capacity** (s 48(2)).

**Key case — *Mount Edon Gold Mines v Burmine*:**
- The test requires **legal power**, not mere practical/de facto control
- White J's example: If shareholder A holds 80% but is habitually absent, and shareholder B (15%) customarily appoints directors, Company is still **not** B's subsidiary because A could exercise its legal power at any time

### 9.2 Key Definitions

| Term | Definition | Authority |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Holding company** | A body corporate that has a subsidiary | s 9 |
| **Wholly owned subsidiary** | No members other than holding company or its nominees/other wholly owned subsidiaries | s 9 |
| **Related body corporate** | A subsidiary and its holding company, and subsidiaries of same holding company | s 50 |
| **Ultimate holding company** | The top-level parent in a chain of subsidiaries | s 9 |

Chain rule: if C is a subsidiary of B, and B of A, then **C is also a subsidiary of A** (s 46(b)).

### 9.3 The Entity Model vs Enterprise Model ⭐

> [!IMPORTANT]
> This is a **major exam theme**. Understand the tension between these models and the case law.

#### Entity Model (Prevailing Australian Position)
- Each company in a group is a **separate and independent legal entity**
- Directors must act in the interests of **their** company, not the group
- **Leading authority**: *Walker v Wimborne* (1976, HCA) — Mason J affirmed "fundamental principles" of separate legal entity status
- Consequence: creditors deal with individual companies; assets/liabilities cannot be redistributed across the group

#### Enterprise Model
- Recognises the **economic unity** of the group
- "The profitability of any one constituent corporation is largely irrelevant ... except as it contributes to the overall effort"
- More aligned with commercial reality — Rogers J in *Briggs v James Hardie*: entity model "pays scant regard to commercial reality"

### 9.4 Key Cases on Corporate Groups

| Case | Facts & Principle |
|------|-------------------|
| ***Walker v Wimborne*** (1976, HCA) | Companies managed by common directors; Mason J: each company is separate and independent. The **foundational** entity model authority |
| ***Qintex* (ANZ v Qintex)** | Qintex borrowed $185.5M, defaulted. ANZ tried to obtain guarantees from ~90 wholly owned subsidiaries. Court refused — enforcing guarantees would breach subsidiary directors' duties. Cannot arbitrarily redistribute assets/liabilities across group |
| ***Re Spargos Mining NL*** | Spargos was profitable until absorbed into IRL group and used as a "cash box" to fund other group activities. Court found **oppressive conduct** (predecessor of s 232). Murray J: group philosophy tends to take little account of individual company interests and small shareholders |
| ***Equiticorp Finance v Bank of New Zealand*** | Hawkins (director of multiple group companies) used funds from two group companies to repay another's bank debt. Majority (Clarke & Cripps JJA) applied the **Charterbridge test**: Could an intelligent, honest director reasonably have believed the transaction benefited the company? Here, yes — "welfare of the group was intimately tied up with welfare of individual companies" |
| ***Charterbridge Corp v Lloyds Bank*** (UK) | Pennycuick J's test: whether an **intelligent and honest** director could reasonably have believed the transaction was for the benefit of the company. Allows regard to wider group interests |

### 9.5 Section 187 — Statutory Recognition of Group Interests

A director of a **wholly owned subsidiary** may act in the best interests of the **holding company** if:
1. The subsidiary's **constitution expressly authorises** this
2. The director acts in **good faith** in the best interests of the holding company
3. The subsidiary is **not insolvent** at the time, and does not become insolvent because of the act

### 9.6 Other Group Provisions

- **s 588V**: Holding company may be liable for **insolvent trading** of its subsidiary
- **AASB 10**: Consolidated financial statements required based on concept of **control** (power to direct activities that significantly affect investment returns)
- **s 323**: Director/officer of controlled entity must provide information for consolidated financial statements
- **Ch 2E**: Regulates financial transactions between public companies and **related parties** (s 228); control defined in **s 50AA(1)** — capacity to determine outcome of decisions about financial and operating policies

## 10. Other Classifications

### 10.1 Closely-Held vs Widely-Held Companies

| Feature | Closely-Held | Widely-Held |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Membership | Small; close association between members and directors | Large, diverse membership |
| Director-member relationship | Members often are the directors (or family ties) | Directors practically and legally separated from members |
| Corporate form | Typically proprietary (but not synonymous) | Typically listed public companies |
| Legal recognition | Australian law has been **slow** to recognise the implications; no distinct jurisprudence |

> [!NOTE]
> "Closely-held" ≠ "proprietary" and "widely-held" ≠ "public." These are **organisational** descriptions, not legal categories.

**Key case — *Ebrahimi v Westbourne Galleries* (HL):**
- Business previously a partnership, then incorporated
- Relations of mutual confidence broke down
- Lord Wilberforce: a company is "more than a mere legal entity" — there is room to recognise individuals' rights, expectations, and obligations that are "not necessarily submerged in the company structure"
- Court ordered **winding up on just and equitable grounds**
- Concept of **"quasi-partnership"** companies — followed in some Australian courts

### 10.2 One-Person Companies

- Permitted: one person as **sole director and sole shareholder** of a proprietary company
- **Rationale 1**: Avoids forcing others to be involved just to meet formal requirements (particularly relevant for women being coerced into co-directorship — Lavarch reforms, mid-1990s)
- **Rationale 2**: Extends protection and benefits of incorporation to **sole traders**

**Special provisions:**
- If sole director/shareholder dies, becomes incapacitated, or bankrupt → personal representative/trustee may appoint a new director (ss 201F(2)–(3))
- Director can exercise **all company powers** except those allocated to general meeting by the Act or constitution (s 198E)
- Director's resolution: record and sign in minute books (s 248B)
- Member's resolution: record and sign in minute books (s 249B)
- Can appoint another director by recording and signing in records (s 201F(1))

## 11. Registration Patterns and Statistics

- **2022–23**: ~3.2 million companies registered in Australia
- ~**99%** are proprietary companies; **87%** of those have only 1–2 shareholders
- Public companies limited by shares: ~**1%** of registrations
- Next most popular: companies limited by guarantee
- Of Australia's ~2.5 million active businesses (June 2023): **41%** structured as companies; the rest as partnerships, trusts, or sole proprietorships
- A 2007 study found ~**88%** of listed companies were structured as part of a **corporate group**

## 12. Registrable Australian Bodies, Foreign Companies and CCIVs

| Type | Key Features |
|------|-------------|
| **Registrable Australian body** | Body corporate created outside the Act's jurisdiction (e.g. incorporated associations, co-ops). Must register to carry on business in the jurisdiction (s 601CA). "Carrying on business" definition: s 21 |
| **Foreign company** | Body corporate incorporated outside Australia. Must register under pt 5B.2 div 2. Must appoint a **local agent** who is personally liable for penalties from contraventions (ss 601CF–CJ) |
| **CCIV** (Ch 8B, added 2022) | Company limited by shares operating as an umbrella investment vehicle with distinct sub-funds. Can have only **one director** (must be a public company): s 1224. Response to 2009 recommendation |

## 13. Factors Influencing Choice of Incorporation

### Advantages of Incorporation

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Limited liability** | Most obvious advantage; but may be eroded by **personal guarantees** demanded by creditors |
| **Financial instruments** | Capacity to issue shares, debentures, circulating security interests (s 124) — not available to partnerships |
| **Taxation** | Corporations enjoy beneficial marginal tax rates; evidence suggests tax is a major factor in small business incorporation |

### Disadvantages

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Regulatory compliance costs** | Maintaining registers, preparing accounts, holding AGMs, submitting annual returns |
| **Agency costs** | In widely-held companies: shareholders face monitoring costs; managers bear costs of signalling alignment with shareholder interests (→ Ch 2) |

### The s 115 Prohibition

- **s 115**: An association/partnership with the **object of acquiring gain** and consisting of **> 20 persons** must be incorporated in some form
- Higher limits for professional partnerships: accountants 1,000; legal practitioners 400; architects/pharmacists/vets 100; actuaries/doctors/patent attorneys/brokers 50
- Rationale: prevent the mischief of large trading bodies where persons don't know with whom they contract
