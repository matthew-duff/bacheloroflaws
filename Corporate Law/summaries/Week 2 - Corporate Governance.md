## 🎯 Core Themes
Corporate governance concerns the rules, practices, and processes by which a company is directed and controlled. 
It essentially deals with the balance of power between shareholders, directors, and management.

## ⚖️ The Central Problem: Separation of Ownership and Control
A fundamental concept in modern corporate law. As companies grow (especially public companies), the people who *own* the company (the shareholders) are rarely the same people who *manage* the company (the directors and executive officers).

### 📖 Key Theorists & Readings 
*   **Berle and Means, *The Modern Corporation and Private Property***: The foundational text observing that as share ownership becomes dispersed, shareholders lose effective control over the corporation to professional managers.
*   **Fama and Jensen, *‘Separation of Ownership and Control’***: Exploring agency theory—the costs and conflicts that arise when principals (shareholders) delegate authority to agents (managers).

## 🏢 "For Whom Are Corporate Managers Trustees?" (The Purpose Debate)
Who do directors actually serve? If they hold power in trust, who are the beneficiaries?
This debate asks whether a company should maximize shareholder wealth exclusively or consider broader stakeholder interests.

### 📖 The Classic Debate:
*   **A. Berle (1931/1932):** Argued that corporate powers are held in trust *for shareholders*. The primary duty of managers is generating returns for the owners of the capital.
*   **M. Dodd (1932):** Argued that the corporation is an economic institution with a social service function. Managers are trustees not just for shareholders, but for *employees, consumers, and the wider public*.

### 📖 Modern Perspectives:
*   **Milton Friedman (1970):** Famous for the essay asserting "the social responsibility of business is to increase its profits" (aligning generally with shareholder primacy theory).
*   **Modern Corporate Governance Principles (e.g., ASX Corporate Governance Council):** While typically focused on shareholder value, modern recommendations increasingly stress ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) factors and "social license to operate."

## 📜 Key Governance Reports & Frameworks
*   **The Cadbury Report (1992):** A landmark UK report on the financial aspects of corporate governance. It introduced the "comply or explain" approach—companies should comply with best practice codes or explain to shareholders why they haven't. This heavily influenced Australian governance regimes (like the ASX Principles).
*   **ASX Corporate Governance Principles and Recommendations (4th ed, 2019):** Non-binding recommendations for listed entities in Australia (operating on the "if not, why not" disclosure model under listing rule 4.10.3).
