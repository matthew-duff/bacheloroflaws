## Key Concepts at a Glance

| Concept | Core Idea |
|---|---|
| **Corporate governance** | The system of rules, practices, and processes by which companies are directed and controlled — addresses *who* controls, *how* they are accountable, and *to whom* |
| **Berle & Means problem** | Separation of ownership (shareholders) and control (directors) → governance gap |
| **Agency costs** | Costs arising when management pursues its own interests rather than maximising shareholder wealth |
| **Hard law** | Binding legislation & case law (e.g. Corporations Act, common law) |
| **Soft law** | Non-binding best-practice standards (e.g. ASX Corporate Governance Principles) |
| **Hybrids** | Private rules given legal force (e.g. accounting standards, ASX Listing Rules) |

## 1. What Is Corporate Governance?

### Etymology
- *Governance* derives from Greek — **steering of a ship**.
- Encompasses both **strategic direction** (the captain) and **operational management** (the helmsman).
- Increasingly also covers **corporate culture**.

### Three Pillars

| Pillar | Focus | Primary Discipline |
|---|---|---|
| **Control rights** | Who gets to make decisions? Allocation of powers to directors, shareholders, regulators | Law |
| **Accountability** | How are decision-makers held responsible? Financial reporting, disclosure, fiduciary duties | Accounting |
| **Efficiency / Effectiveness** | Does the governance structure make companies perform better? | Economics |

### Company Law vs Corporate Governance
- **Corporate governance ⊂ Company law:** Company law covers the *entire lifecycle* (creation → management → winding up); corporate governance focuses on management and control — "the interesting bit in the middle."
- **Company law ⊂ Corporate governance:** Corporate governance is the *bigger universe*; company law is just one regulatory tool within it.

## 2. The Berle & Means Problem (1932)

### The Observation
- As outlined in [[Berle and Means, The Modern Corporation and Private Property (1932)]], Berle (lawyer) and Means (economist) studied the US corporate landscape in the late 1920s–early 1930s.
- Finding: large publicly listed companies are characterised by a **separation of ownership and control**.
  - Those who **control** the company (directors/management) do **not own** it. (A tiny fraction directs a massive portion of industry).
  - Those who **own** it (shareholders) have **no effective control** over it. (Power is concentrated through legal devices).
- This separation opens the door for society to demand that the modern corporation serve not just owners or managers, but society as a whole, raising a profound problem of "governmental technique."

### Why Does This Happen?
1. **Dispersed shareholding** — thousands of shareholders each holding tiny percentages.
2. **Collective action problem** — coordinating many small shareholders is impractical.
3. **Rational apathy** — for a small investment, it is cheaper to **sell** ("Wall Street walk") than to mount a governance challenge.
4. **Diversified portfolio strategy** — investors spread risk, reducing incentive to monitor any single company.

### Berle's Solution and Theoretical Nuance
- **Corporate Powers in Trust:** In [[A. A. Berle-CorporatePowersPowers-1931]], Berle argues that all corporate powers are held in trust for the ratable benefit of all shareholders. Every action must pass a "two-fold test": a technical/legal test and an equitable test.
- **Strict Fiduciary Duty:** In [[A. A. Berle-CorporateManagersTrustees-1932]], Berle argued that until a framework for broader social accountability is created, managers must strictly remain fiduciaries for shareholders. Otherwise, abandoning shareholder primacy grants managers unchecked, absolute power over wealth.
- Embrace management freedom → directors become **technocratic, publicly minded "princes of industry"** who act for the benefit of society.
- Critique: sounds deeply naïve today, though in context of 1930s (stock market crash, rise of managed economies) it was less so.

> [!important] Exam Relevance
> The Berle & Means separation of ownership and control is **the foundational corporate governance problem**. Almost every governance mechanism studied in this course is a response to it.

## 3. Agency Theory & the Nexus of Contracts (1970s–80s)

### Key Scholars
- Eugene **Fama** & Michael **Jensen** — applied Coasian economics to the firm.
- **Jensen & Meckling** — described the firm as a *"nexus of contracting relationships … characterised by the existence of divisible residual claims on the assets and cash flows of the organization."*
- **Easterbrook & Fischel** — *"The 'personhood' of a corporation is a matter of convenience rather than reality."*

### The Corporation as a "Nexus of Contracts"
- From an economic perspective, the corporation (a legal concept) is analysed as a type of **firm** (an economic concept).
- A firm = a series of **transactions or contracts** between individual actors: investors, managers, employees, creditors, customers.
- "Contract" here ≠ strict legal contract — it highlights the **voluntary and adaptive** nature of arrangements.
- This framework **denies** the corporation as a separate entity; it is merely shorthand for a multiplicity of private, consensual relationships.
- The only relevant function of a company = **maximise returns to individual investors**.

### The Refinement: Agency Costs
- Recasts the Berle & Means problem as one of **agency costs**:
  - Directors/management (agents) are likely to **prefer their own self-interest** over the interests of the company and its shareholders (principals).
  - Separating management from capital contribution allows specialists to manage — but creates risk of self-dealing.

| Cost Type | Who Bears It | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Monitoring costs** | Investors | Keeping an eye on managers; analysing financial data; attending general meetings |
| **Bonding costs** | Managers | Assuring investors that their interests will not be harmed; performance guarantees |
| **Agency costs** | Both (combined) | The total divergence from shareholder wealth maximisation |

- Specific manifestations of agency costs include:
  - **Empire-building** (growing the company for prestige, not efficiency)
  - **Perquisites** (e.g. corporate jets, lavish offices)
  - **Shirking** — simply not working hard enough

### Why Monitoring Fails
- Investors have **limited time and knowledge** for negotiations and monitoring.
- In firms with widespread ownership → **collective action among investors is small**.
- Transaction costs of negotiating effective contracts are **high**; prospects of effective monitoring are **low**.

### Corporate Law as "Off-the-Rack" Terms
- Easterbrook & Fischel: *"Corporate law is a set of terms available off-the-rack so that participants in corporate ventures can save the costs of contracting … [it] fills in the blanks and oversights with the terms that people would have bargained for had they anticipated the problems."*
- Key features of corporate law (SEP, limited liability, directors' duties) operate as **standard contractual terms** read into each corporate constitution.
- Corporate law **supplements but never displaces** actual bargains → individuals should have freedom to opt out.

### The Goal
- Corporate governance exists to **reduce agency costs** as far as possible.

> [!note] Two Strands of Economic Theory
> **Agency theory** addresses the separation of ownership and control. **Transaction-cost economics** asks *why* different organisational forms (firms vs markets) are used to structure production. Both complement each other and together explain the design of corporate governance mechanisms.

## 4. Mechanisms of Corporate Governance

### 4.1 Hard Law

| Mechanism | Example |
|---|---|
| **Fiduciary duties** | **s 181 CA** — directors must exercise powers in what they believe to be the best interests of the company |
| **Conflict of interest rules** | Prohibition on secret profits; disclosure of personal interests |
| **Mandatory financial reporting** | Directors must cause accounts to be prepared and made available to shareholders and ASIC |
| **Disclosure regimes** | Transparency obligations to discourage misconduct |

### 4.2 Soft Law

- **The Cadbury Report (1992)** ([[cadbury-report.md]]):
  - A pioneering UK report that established foundational standards for corporate governance via a voluntary "Code of Best Practice."
  - Introduced the concept of "comply or explain" regarding disclosure.
  - Emphasized openness, integrity, and accountability, and heavily stressed the crucial role of independent Non-Executive Directors (NEDs) in reviewing performance and managing conflicts of interest.

- **ASX Corporate Governance Principles and Recommendations** (4th ed, 2019) ([[cgc-principles-and-recommendations-fourth-edn.md]]):
  - Based around 8 central principles (including laying solid foundations for management, instilling an ethical culture, safeguarding corporate reports, making timely disclosure, respecting security holders, managing risk, and remunerating responsibly).
  - Not legally binding — failure to comply is not *unlawful*.
  - Operates through **corporate peer pressure** — an "if not, why not" regime (under ASX Listing Rule 4.10.3).
  - Companies must include a Corporate Governance Statement detailing their compliance.
  - Banks, investors, and regulators use compliance as a benchmark → practical consequences for non-compliance.

### 4.3 Hybrids (Private Rules with Legal Force)

| Hybrid | How It Works |
|---|---|
| **Accounting standards** | Set by international and Australian private bodies; given legal effect by CA which mandates accounts be prepared in accordance with them |
| **ASX Listing Rules** | Private contractual rules between ASX and listed companies; incorporated into CA (e.g. **s 793C**) and enforceable by ASIC and aggrieved persons |

### 4.4 Market Mechanisms

Economic theory identifies **three competitive markets** that constrain managerial misuse of power (reducing the need for direct investor monitoring):

| Market | How It Disciplines | Theoretical Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Market for corporate securities** | In an efficient capital market, **share price reflects all available information** — including quality of shareholder protections → market does the monitoring job | Efficient Market Hypothesis |
| **Market for corporate control (takeovers)** | Underperforming companies → low share price → attractive takeover target → incumbent board replaced → disciplines managers to perform | Easterbrook & Fischel |
| **Market for company managers** | Competition for managerial talent, *inside and outside* the company, incentivises directors to maximise value or risk not being re-elected/re-appointed | Jensen & Meckling |

Additional market-based mechanisms:

| Mechanism | How It Disciplines |
|---|---|
| **Executive remuneration** | Pay-for-performance structures align management self-interest with shareholder wealth maximisation |
| **Wall Street walk** | Shareholders sell shares in poorly governed companies → depresses share price → signals market disapproval |

> [!warning] Gaming Remuneration Metrics
> Directors can manipulate performance metrics (e.g. deferring costs, laying off employees before measurement dates) to trigger bonuses without improving underlying performance. This is a persistent **cat-and-mouse** dynamic.

### 4.5 Regulator Guidance
- **ASIC** issues guidance and regulatory expectations — not law, but the corporate community complies to avoid regulatory scrutiny.

## 5. Boom–Bust–Regulate Cycle

| Event | Response |
|---|---|
| **Tulip Mania** (1600s) | Early regulatory awareness |
| **South Sea Bubble** (1720) | **Bubble Act** — companies banned outright |
| **Stock Market Crash** (1929) | Berle & Means analysis; New Deal regulation |
| **Enron scandal** (2001) | **CLERP 9** (Australia); **Sarbanes-Oxley** (US) |
| **GFC** (2008) | Wave of mandatory regulation |
| **Australian Banking Royal Commission** (2018–19) | Ongoing regulatory reform |

> [!note] Pattern
> Each crisis triggers a wave of **mandatory regulation**. Over time, regulation is **watered down** in favour of growth and facilitation → until the next crisis. The intractability of these problems should temper expectations about what corporate governance can achieve.

## 6. Mandatory vs Facilitative Regulation

| Characteristic | Mandatory | Facilitative |
|---|---|---|
| **Approach** | "Thou shalt / shalt not" | Provides a legal shell; enables collective action |
| **Example** | Director's duty provisions; financial reporting | Replaceable rules; simple registration process ($450, ~5 mins) |
| **When dominant?** | Post-crisis periods | Periods of economic growth / deregulation |

- **Responsive regulation**: modern trend — builds structures and processes rather than blunt prohibitions. More sophisticated but *much* wordier (what "thou shalt not kill" says in 4 words takes tens of pages in responsive regulatory form).

---

## 7. Justiciability

- Many corporate governance mechanisms (soft law, ASX principles, regulator guidance) are **not justiciable** — courts cannot rule on them.
- This means a significant portion of corporate governance operates **outside traditional judicial accountability**.
- Decisions increasingly happen in boardrooms, behind closed doors, or in negotiations between the board and ASIC — reducing **public accountability**.

---

## 8. Organisation & Audience of the Corporations Act

### Structure
- Australia uses an **omnibus statute** — the CA includes everything (creation, management, capital, insolvency, financial markets).
- Contrast: UK, Canada, NZ have separate statutes for insolvency, financial markets, etc.
- Australia is the **only common law jurisdiction** that puts insolvency rules inside the corporate statute.

### Audience
- The CA is written **for lawyers**, not company directors.
- Assumption: everything is mediated by professional advisors.
- Contrast: NZ and Canada deliberately addressed their companies legislation to *directors* in plain English.
- Practical consequence: most directors derive their understanding of obligations from **soft sources** (ASX governance code, ASIC guidance) rather than the Act itself.

### Responsibility
- Corporate law policy shifted from the **Attorney-General** to **Treasury** — reflects the company being seen as an *economic* instrument, not just a *legal* one.

## 9. Is the Berle & Means Problem Relevant for Australia?

| Factor | US (Berle & Means model) | Australia |
|---|---|---|
| **Shareholding pattern** | Hugely dispersed, small individual shareholders | More concentrated — institutional investors (superannuation, insurance, banks) hold substantial chunks |
| **Number of large listed companies** | Many | ~200 (ASX 200) out of ~2 million registered companies |
| **Typical governance problem** | Directors beyond shareholder accountability | **Shareholder vs shareholder disputes** (especially in family and closely held companies) |

- Australia **aspires** to the US model and has built its governance regime around the Berle & Means problem.
- But the regime is really solving a problem for the **tiny minority** (~200 listed companies) while the vast majority of companies face a **different governance problem** (shareholder disputes, not director accountability).

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Be prepared to critically evaluate whether the Australian corporate governance framework, modelled on the Berle & Means problem, is fit for purpose given Australia's concentrated ownership landscape.

## 10. Competing Corporate Theories — Context for Governance

The corporate governance debate sits within a broader theoretical landscape. Each theory takes a different position on the **four perennial tensions** (group vs individual; management vs ownership; facilitation vs intervention; public vs private):

| Theory | What Is the Corporation? | Governance Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Concession theory** | Artificial entity, created by the state as a privilege | State *must/may* regulate; corporations are public actors — supports ESG obligations |
| **Aggregate theory** | Contractual association of individuals | Law should *facilitate* only; corporations are private — managerial power derives from contract |
| **Economic (nexus of contracts)** | Shorthand for a web of private contracts | Default rules + market forces; shareholder primacy — the framework underpinning modern governance |
| **Team production** (Blair & Stout) | Privately constructed "team" with multiple inputs | Board = independent **mediating hierarchy**, not agents of any single group — challenges shareholder primacy |
| **Natural entity** | Real, natural product of human initiative | Corporations should not be regulated more than individuals — but if they are "persons", they owe social responsibilities (Dodd) |
| **Organisational** | Complex decision-making environment | Internal dynamics (information distortion, diffuse control) shape outcomes — boards may have limited actual impact |
| **Feminist** | Inherently gendered institution | Challenges nexus-of-contracts model; firm = "web of relationships"; care ethics should inform directors' duties |
| **Stakeholder** | Both private and public simultaneously | Directors owe broader duties beyond shareholders; public regulation is justified |

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> When answering a governance question, explicitly identifying which *theory* underpins your argument (e.g., "from a concession perspective, the state is justified in mandating ESG reporting because …") demonstrates sophisticated analysis.

### Key Theoretical Debates in Governance

| Debate | Position A | Position B |
|---|---|---|
| **Shareholder primacy vs stakeholderism** | Directors' sole duty = maximise shareholder wealth (Friedman; Easterbrook & Fischel) | Directors must consider employees, creditors, communities, environment (Dodd; Blair & Stout) |
| **Facilitation vs intervention** | Corporate law = "off-the-rack" default rules; parties can opt out (economic theory) | Mandatory rules needed to correct market failures and protect public interests (concession theory) |
| **Who monitors the monitors?** | Market forces (securities, takeovers, managerial labour) are sufficient | Markets are imperfect; legal accountability mechanisms are essential |

## 11. Corporate Purpose, ESG & Stakeholder Theory

### The Central Question
> Should corporations be confined to profit maximisation for shareholders, **or** should they have regard to environmental and social impacts?

### The ESG Framework

| Component | Generally Includes |
|---|---|
| **Environmental** | Sustainability, climate-change matters |
| **Social** | Ethical standards in company operations; modern slavery; human rights |
| **Governance** | Board accountability, transparency, diversity |

### The Spectrum of Views

| Position | Key Argument | Key Scholar |
|---|---|---|
| **Shareholder primacy** | Corporation's *sole* function = maximise returns to shareholders; altruism only if consistent with that goal | Milton Friedman ([[A Friedman doctrine‐- The Social Responsibility of Business Is to Increase Its Profits - The New York Times]]) |
| **Enlightened shareholder value** | Directors must act for shareholders, but take a **long-term view** considering stakeholder interests as a *means* to shareholder benefit | UK Companies Act 2006 |
| **Stakeholder theory** | Corporations have **direct responsibilities** to a wide range of constituencies beyond shareholders | E. Merrick Dodd ([[Dodd, ‘For Whom are Corporate Managers Trustees?’ (1932)]]); Kent Greenfield |
| **Team production** | Board mediates among *all* team members' interests, not just shareholders | Blair & Stout |

### E. Merrick Dodd (1932) — The Social Responsibility Argument
- As detailed in [[Dodd, ‘For Whom are Corporate Managers Trustees?’ (1932)]], Dodd challenged the traditional maximum-profit-for-stockholders legal doctrine.
- Shareholders in modern public companies = **"absentee owners"** separated from management.
- Directors should not be regarded as mere agents of shareholders.
- The corporation is a "factual entity/institution"—more than an aggregate of its stockholders. As an institution, it has social responsibilities as a **"good citizen"** — directors are justified in favouring employees, consumers, communities, environment without it being a breach of trust.
- Dodd aligns with growing public/business opinion that modern capitalism cannot survive if it ignores the economic security of workers.

### Milton Friedman (1970) — The Shareholder Primacy Argument
- As argued in [[A Friedman doctrine‐- The Social Responsibility of Business Is to Increase Its Profits - The New York Times]], the sole social responsibility of a business is to increase its profits within the rules of the game.
- **Artificial Responsibilities:** A corporation is an artificial person; only individuals can have true responsibilities. A corporate executive is an agent of the owners (principals).
- Spending corporate money on social causes equates to "unadulterated socialism" and bypassing democratic political mechanisms (functioning as a civil servant levying a "tax").
- Extending the "social responsibility" doctrine unnecessarily extends the coercive political mechanism (based on conformity) into the voluntary market sphere (based on unanimity). Actions branded as social responsibility are often just self-interested "window dressing".

### Enlightened Shareholder Value — UK Companies Act 2006, s 172(1)
> A director must act in a way that he considers, in good faith, would be most likely to **promote the success of the company for the benefit of its members as a whole**, having regard to:

| Factor | Description |
|---|---|
| **(a)** | Likely consequences of any decision **in the long term** |
| **(b)** | Interests of employees |
| **(c)** | Relationships with suppliers, customers and others |
| **(d)** | Impact on **community and environment** |
| **(e)** | Maintaining a reputation for **high standards** of business conduct |
| **(f)** | Acting **fairly between members** |

- Maintains shareholder primacy but provides a **broader context** for fulfilling that duty.
- **Criticism:** all it requires is that directors "think about" wider interests — courts have affirmed the weighing of factors is a **commercial decision for directors**, not the court.

### Australian Position — No Equivalent of UK s 172
- **Senate Committee (1989):** requiring directors to consider social impact = "an extension of Australian company law fraught with most important consequences."
- **CAMAC Report (2006):** "a non-exhaustive catalogue of interests … serves little useful purpose for directors."

**Two narrow exceptions:**

| Provision | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **s 299(1)(f) CA** | Annual directors' report must detail performance re **environmental regulation** |
| **Treasury Laws Amendment Act 2024** | Companies with financial reporting requirements must publish annual **"sustainability report"** on climate-related matters |

- **ASX Corporate Governance Principle 3:** listed entities must act lawfully, ethically, and responsibly — "if not, why not" approach to ESG considerations.

### Stakeholder Theory
- Non-shareholder stakeholders (employees, creditors, customers, communities) are **unlikely or unable** to protect their interests by private contract.
- Corporations' conduct has significant **public ramifications** → public regulation is needed.
- Corporations are **"both private and public simultaneously"** — Kent Greenfield.
- Challenges: should directors' duties extend beyond shareholders? Should non-shareholders be on the board?

### Global Developments

| Initiative | Year | Nature | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises** | 1976 (revised) | Voluntary, non-binding | MNEs should contribute to sustainable development and respect human rights |
| **UN Global Compact** | 2000 | Non-binding | 10 principles on human rights, labour, environment, anti-corruption |
| **UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights** | 2011 | Non-binding | Businesses must "avoid causing or contributing to adverse human rights impacts" — a **global standard of expected conduct** |
| **Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Cth)** | 2018 | Binding (reporting) | Some corporations must report annually on modern slavery risks — 2023 review found it is "not being taken seriously enough" |

> [!important] Global Context
> The UN Guiding Principles' corporate "responsibility" to respect human rights does **not** equate to a legal obligation — but it "poses a challenge for corporate governance theory" because shareholder-centric models may be inadequate.

### Modern Governance Trends

| Trend | Detail |
|---|---|
| **ESG reporting** | Boards increasingly accountable for environmental and social impact (e.g. BHP incidents, climate litigation, modern slavery supply chains) |
| **Broader accountability** | Shift from "maximise shareholder wealth" → accountability to **society as a whole** |
| **Board gender diversity** | Norway: minimum gender representation since 2006; evidence that "women on boards ask more questions" improves monitoring |
| **Efficiency & productivity focus** | Corporate governance should *facilitate* productivity, not just impose accountability |
| **Responsive regulation** | Building regulatory structures and processes rather than blunt prohibitions |
| **One-size-fits-all model** | Australia (like UK) uses the same company form for sole traders and BHP; contrast with Europe (GmbH vs AG) and US (LLCs) |
