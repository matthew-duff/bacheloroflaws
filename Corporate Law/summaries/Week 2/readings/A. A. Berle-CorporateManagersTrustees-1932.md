# Summary: For Whom Corporate Managers Are Trustees: A Note (1932)

**Author:** A. A. Berle, Jr.

## Core Argument
While acknowledging the economic reality that corporations impact various stakeholders (workers, consumers, the community), Berle argues that corporate managers must strictly remain fiduciaries for their shareholders (security holders) until a clear, enforceable legal framework is developed to hold them accountable to wider society. Abandoning the shareholder primacy rule prematurely would simply grant managers unchecked, absolute power over corporate wealth without any real accountability to anyone.

## Key Points
- **The Debate with Professor Dodd:**
  - Professor E. Merrick Dodd argued that public opinion increasingly views the corporation as an economic institution with social responsibilities (e.g., to employees, customers, and the public), not just an entity existing solely for the profit of shareholders.
  - Berle agrees with Dodd's underlying *economic and social theory*. He acknowledges that industrial society is highly concentrated in large corporations, and that multiple groups have legitimate claims on the corporate wealth stream (security owners, management, patrons, workers, and the community).
- **The Danger of Unchecked Managerial Power:**
  - Berle's primary objection is legal and practical: if the law stops requiring managers to maximize shareholder returns before establishing new legal mechanisms of accountability to other stakeholders, it effectively grants "uncontrolled power to corporate managers."
  - This would result in a "social-economic absolutism of corporate administrators." Even if benevolent, such concentrated and unchecked power is dangerous and an unsound basis for an economic commonwealth.
- **The Role of Private Property:**
  - Berle emphasizes that the American system relies heavily on private property to support individuals during periods when they cannot work (childhood, old age, sickness). 
  - The corporate system absorbs the bulk of society's savings. If managers can freely divert these funds for "social" purposes without strict legal duties to the investors, it threatens the financial security of individuals who depend on those corporate securities. It risks creating a system of "economic civil war."
- **The Conclusion - Protect What We Know:**
  - Until society develops a rigorous system of required accountability that balances the needs of investors, workers, customers, and the community, the law must cling to the existing rule.
  - "As lawyers, we had best be protecting the interests we know [shareholders], being no less swift to provide for the new interests as they successively appear."
