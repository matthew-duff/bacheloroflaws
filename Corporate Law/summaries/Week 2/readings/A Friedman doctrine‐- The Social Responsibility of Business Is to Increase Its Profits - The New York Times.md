# Summary: A Friedman doctrine - The Social Responsibility of Business Is to Increase Its Profits

**Author:** Milton Friedman (Sept. 13, 1970)

## Core Argument
The sole social responsibility of a business is to use its resources and engage in activities designed to increase its profits, provided it stays within the rules of the game (open and free competition without deception or fraud). The concept that businesses have broader "social responsibilities" (e.g., providing employment, eliminating discrimination, avoiding pollution) is inherently misguided and equates to "unadulterated socialism."

## Key Points
- **Artificial Responsibilities:** A corporation is an artificial person, and therefore can only have artificial responsibilities. Only individuals (people) can have true responsibilities. 
- **The Role of the Corporate Executive:** 
  - An executive is an employee (agent) of the owners (principals) of the business.
  - Their primary direct responsibility is to conduct the business according to the owners' desires—usually to make as much money as possible while conforming to societal rules (law and ethical custom).
  - If an executive acts on a "social responsibility" that reduces returns to stockholders, increases prices to customers, or lowers wages for employees, they are effectively spending someone else's money without their consent.
- **Taxation and Undemocratic Processes:**
  - When executives spend corporate money on social causes, they are acting as a public employee/civil servant—simultaneously deciding whom to tax, by how much, and for what purpose.
  - Doing this bypasses the democratic political mechanisms (constitutional, parliamentary, judicial) established to handle taxation and public expenditure. 
  - To impose taxes and expenditures for social objectives, administrators should be selected through a political process, not by private enterprise stockholders.
- **Consequences and Inefficiencies:**
  - Even if an executive wanted to discharge a "social responsibility" (e.g., fighting inflation), they lack the expertise to know how their corporate actions will actually solve complex macroeconomic or social problems.
  - They risk being fired by stockholders or abandoned by customers/employees who do not agree with the imposed costs.
- **Individual vs. Corporate Responsibility:** 
  - As an individual (acting as a principal), a corporate executive can freely devote their *own* money, time, and energy to any social cause they choose.
  - Proprietors (sole owners) can also choose to spend their own money on social goals without issue, as it is their own wealth being expended.
- **"Window Dressing":** Sometimes, actions labeled as "social responsibility" are actually long-term strategic moves justified by corporate self-interest (e.g., investing in the local community to attract employees). Friedman views calling this "social responsibility" as hypocritical "window dressing" that harms the foundations of a free society.
- **Political Principles - Unanimity vs. Conformity:**
  - The market mechanism is based on *unanimity*: voluntary cooperation where no individual coerces another.
  - The political mechanism is based on *conformity*: the individual must serve a more general social interest and conform even if overruled. 
  - Extending the "social responsibility" doctrine to business unnecessarily extends the coercive political mechanism into the voluntary market sphere.
