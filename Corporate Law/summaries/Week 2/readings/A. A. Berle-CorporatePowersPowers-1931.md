# Summary: Corporate Powers as Powers in Trust (1931)

**Author:** A. A. Berle

## Core Thesis
All powers granted to a corporation, its management, or any controlling group (whether derived from statute or the corporate charter) are held in trust and are "exercisable only for the ratable benefit of all the shareholders as their interest appears." 

Berle argues that because of this, every corporate action must pass a "two-fold test":
1. **Technical/Legal Test:** Have the technical rules regarding the existence and proper exercise of the power been met?
2. **Equitable Test:** Was the power exercised fairly for the benefit of all shareholders, analogous to how a trustee must act for a beneficiary (*cestui que trust*)? Even if a grant of power is absolute in its terms, its use is subject to equitable limitation if exercised to the detriment of shareholder interests.

## Key Examples of Equitable Limitations in Practice
Berle points out that courts have already applied this underlying equitable principle across several areas of corporate law, even if they haven't explicitly articulated a universal rule:

- **The Power to Issue Stock:** While management typically has the legal power to issue stock, courts restrict this power in equity to ensure new stock is issued for fair value (e.g., property or cash) so existing shareholders are not unfairly diluted ("watered down").
- **The Power to Declare (or Withhold) Dividends:** Directors have broad discretion over dividend policy, but equity requires that dividends be withheld only for legitimate business reasons, not to indulge the personal or private motives of the controlling group.
- **The Power to Acquire Stock in Other Corporations:** This power must be used to benefit the corporation as a whole, and not to forward the personal enterprises of the managers or subserve special interests within or without the corporation.
- **The Power to Amend the Charter:** The reserved legal power to amend the corporate charter must be exercised equitably to distribute benefits or sacrifices fairly. Courts routinely step in to prevent majority shareholders from amending the charter in ways that oppress minority shareholders (for example, attempting to cancel vested rights to accrued cumulative dividends).
- **The Power to Transfer the Enterprise:** In mergers, stock exchanges, or the sale of assets, the controlling group must ensure that the rights and interests of all classes of shareholders are substantially protected and not confiscated.

## Conclusion
Instead of viewing these as isolated, disjointed rules, Berle synthesizes them into a single, fundamental principle: corporate powers are never absolute; they are held in a fiduciary capacity. Recognizing this overarching equitable limitation provides courts with the flexibility to substitute or adopt new remedies as needed to protect passive investors from the unrestrained power of corporate management.
