# Summary: Review of 'The Modern Corporation and Private Property' by Berle and Means (1932)

**Source Document:** A 1934 book review in the journal *Public Administration* by W. I. Jennings.

## Core Theme: The Divorce of Ownership and Control
The review highlights the central thesis of Adolf A. Berle and Gardiner C. Means' seminal book: the fundamental separation of ownership from control in modern American industry. 

## Key Points
- **Concentration of Power:** Due to the massive growth of large corporations, an extremely tiny fraction of the population (2,000 persons out of 125,000,000) is in a position to control and direct half the industry in the United States.
- **Divorce Between Ownership and Control:** While the actual ownership of corporate stock has spread more widely among the general public, the *control* of that property has become concentrated in the hands of a few through various legal devices. The power resulting from ownership is exercised by a comparatively few individuals rather than the actual owners.
- **The Social Consequence:** Since the control groups have separated themselves from the owners, they have opened the door for wider claims on the corporation by all of society. The community is now in a position to demand that the modern corporation serve not just the owners or the controlling managers, but society as a whole.
- **The Problem of Governmental Technique:** The reviewer, writing from a public law perspective, notes that the book proves the facts of this concentration but leaves a huge open question for the future: *How* are these massive corporations to be controlled by society? By socialism? By the corporate state? It poses a fundamental problem of governmental technique that has yet to be solved.
