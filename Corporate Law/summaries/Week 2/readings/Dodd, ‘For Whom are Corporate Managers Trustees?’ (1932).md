# Summary: For Whom Are Corporate Managers Trustees? (1932)

**Author:** E. Merrick Dodd, Jr.

## Core Argument
Dodd challenges the traditional legal doctrine (defended by A. A. Berle) that a business corporation exists *solely* to maximize profits for its stockholders, and that managers are strictly trustees for the stockholders. Dodd argues instead that public opinion and modern business practices are increasingly viewing the corporation as an economic institution endowed with a social service function and social responsibilities extending beyond the pursuit of profit.

## Key Points
- **Agreement and Disagreement with Berle:**
    - Dodd completely agrees with Berle's desire to establish legal controls that prevent managers from diverting corporate profits into their own pockets.
    - However, Dodd disagrees with Berle's assertion that the only way to do this is by strictly enforcing the "maximum-profit-for-stockholders" formula. Restricting managers exclusively to shareholder profit ignores the changing role of the corporation in society.
- **The Shift in Public and Business Opinion:**
    - Society and corporate leaders are increasingly recognizing that business has a social responsibility.
    - Prominent business figures (like Owen D. Young and Gerard Swope of General Electric, and Dean Donham of Harvard Business School) have begun explicitly stating that managers must administer corporate wealth fairly in the interests of employees, customers, the general public, and stockholders alike.
    - Dodd points to the growing expectation that corporations contribute to local charities (unemployment relief, community chests) as evidence that society no longer views businesses as existing solely to enrich owners.
- **The Needs of the Modern Economy:**
    - Dodd argues that modern capitalism cannot survive if it ignores the economic security of the worker. 
    - Future industrial planning designed to stabilize production and protect employees will inevitably require modifications to the strict "maximum-profit" formula.
- **The Corporation as a Factual Entity/Institution:**
    - To legally justify managers acting for the social good without stockholder consent, Dodd relies on the concept of the corporation as a real, distinct entity—a "factual unit" that is more than just an aggregate of its stockholders.
    - If the corporation is a real entity functioning in society, it can be viewed as an "institution" or a "person" that is subject to the evolving ethical and professional standards of the business world.
    - Therefore, when managers use corporate funds for social purposes (like charity or employee welfare), they are not committing a breach of trust; rather, they are appropriately fulfilling the social obligations of the corporate institution they represent. Managers are "trustees for an institution rather than attorneys for the stockholders."
