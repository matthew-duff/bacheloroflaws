# The Corporation The Pathological Pursuit of Profit and Power

### 1. The Psychopathic Entity

Bakan uses the diagnostic criteria of the World Health Organization and the DSM-IV to analyze corporate behavior. Because corporations are designed solely to pursue profit for their shareholders, they systematically exhibit traits such as:

- **Callous unconcern for the feelings of others:** Layoffs, poor working conditions, and exploitation.
- **Incapacity to maintain enduring relationships:** Fleeing communities when cheaper labor is found elsewhere.
- **Reckless disregard for the safety of others:** Ignoring environmental or consumer safety standards if the financial penalty is cheaper than fixing the problem.
- **Inability to experience guilt:** Viewing fines and legal settlements merely as "costs of doing business" rather than moral failings.
    

### 2. The Legal Mandate (Fiduciary Duty)

The book explains that this "psychopathic" behavior isn't necessarily because the humans running the corporations are evil. Instead, it is a structural, legal requirement. Corporate executives have a legally binding **fiduciary duty** to maximize shareholder value. Legally, they cannot prioritize social responsibility, environmental protection, or employee welfare _unless_ doing so ultimately serves the goal of increasing profits.

### 3. The Externalisation Machine

Bakan highlights how corporations are designed to be "externalizing machines." An externality is a cost generated by a business that is pushed onto someone else. For example, a factory polluting a local river avoids the cost of waste disposal (benefiting shareholders), while the local community pays the price in health issues and environmental cleanup. The corporation’s goal is to externalize as many costs as possible to maximize internal profits.

### 4. Democracy Ltd. and Regulatory Capture

The book explores how corporations use their immense wealth to influence and overpower democratic institutions. Through lobbying, campaign contributions, and public relations, corporations actively shape the laws that are supposed to regulate them. Bakan argues that this "regulatory capture" threatens democracy itself, as public interests are subordinated to private corporate interests.

### 5. The Myth of Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

Bakan is highly skeptical of Corporate Social Responsibility initiatives. He argues that CSR is mostly a public relations tactic used to preempt government regulation and mask the inherently self-interested nature of the corporation. Since the legal mandate to maximise profit remains, CSR can only ever be a facade.


### 6. The Macro Consequences: Where the Corporate Mandate Leads

When the corporate structure operates at scale over decades, Bakan argues it creates severe macroeconomic and geopolitical consequences.

- **The Frankenstein Analogy and Western Success:** The book does not deny that the corporate model—specifically the combination of limited liability and transferable shares—was the great engine of Western success. It allowed for the massive pooling of capital that funded the Industrial Revolution. However, Bakan uses the analogy of Frankenstein's monster: society created a brilliant, highly efficient machine for generating wealth, but that creation has now overpowered its creators and operates entirely on its own cold, profit-driven logic, devoid of human constraints.
    
- **The Vanishing Act of Moral Responsibility:** The corporate structure effectively engineers moral responsibility out of existence. Limited liability protects investors' assets and acts as a moral shield, while executives are legally required to compartmentalize and ignore their personal ethics to fulfill their fiduciary duties. When systemic harms occur, the corporation absorbs the fine as a business cost, and individual human accountability evaporates.
    
- **The Tragedy of the Commons:** Because corporations function as externalizing machines, shared public resources like clean air, oceans, and local environments are treated as free dumping grounds. The legal mandate to maximize profit guarantees they will deplete these public commons unless stopped by external government force.
    
- **The Race to the Bottom (Nation-State Level):** In a globalized economy, highly mobile corporations force nation-states into a mutually destructive competition. Countries actively dismantle their own public wealth, lowering corporate tax rates and gutting environmental and labor protections just to appease stateless corporate entities and attract investment.
    
- **The Legal Obligation to Exploit:** Systemic labor exploitation, such as offshoring to sweatshops, is not a glitch in the system; it is a legally mandated feature. If an executive can manufacture a product for significantly less in a developing nation with weak labor laws compared to a Western nation with union protections, choosing to pay a living wage locally becomes a violation of their fiduciary duty to shareholders.

### The Conclusion and Call to Action

Ultimately, Bakan concludes that the corporation is a legal fiction created by the government, and therefore, it should be subordinate to the public good. He advocates for reclaiming democratic control over corporations through stricter regulations, dismantling corporate personhood rights, and strengthening public institutions to protect society from corporate harms.
