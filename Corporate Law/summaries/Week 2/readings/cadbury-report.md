# Summary: Report of the Committee on the Financial Aspects of Corporate Governance (The Cadbury Report, 1992)

**Author:** Sir Adrian Cadbury (Committee Chairman)

## Core Subject
The Cadbury Report is a highly influential UK report that established foundational standards for corporate governance, specifically focusing on financial reporting, accountability, and the proper structure of corporate boards.

## Key Points
- **The Code of Best Practice:** 
    - The centerpiece of the report is a voluntary "Code of Best Practice" directed at the boards of all listed companies.
    - Companies are required by the London Stock Exchange to state whether they comply with the Code and to publicly disclose and explain any areas of non-compliance.
- **Underlying Principles:**
    - **Openness:** Disclosure of information builds confidence and promotes the efficient working of the market economy.
    - **Integrity:** Financial reporting must be honest, complete, and present a balanced picture of the company's affairs.
    - **Accountability:** Boards are accountable to shareholders. Good governance clarifies the respective responsibilities of directors, shareholders, and auditors.
- **Board Structure and Effectiveness:**
    - The report supports the UK unitary board system but stresses the need for a strong balance of power.
    - **Separation of Roles:** It strongly recommends that the role of Chairman and Chief Executive be separated to prevent an unfettered concentration of power in one individual.
- **The Crucial Role of Non-Executive Directors (NEDs):**
    - NEDs must bring independent judgment to bear on strategy, performance, and standards of conduct.
    - They play two vital roles: (1) reviewing the performance of the board and executives, and (2) taking the lead where conflicts of interest arise (e.g., directors' pay, takeovers, succession).
    - Boards should have a minimum of three NEDs, with at least two being completely independent of management.
- **Voluntary Compliance vs. Legislation:**
    - The Committee believes a voluntary code, coupled with strict public disclosure, is more effective and flexible than statutory legislation, which might encourage companies to merely comply with the letter of the law rather than its spirit.
