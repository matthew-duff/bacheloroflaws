## Why Corporate Theory Matters

Corporate theory addresses **three foundational questions**:
1. **What is the corporation**, and from where does it gain political and social legitimacy?
2. **What is the purpose** of the corporation, and whose interests should it serve?
3. **How should corporate power be regulated**, and by whom?

These questions map onto the **four perennial tensions** identified in Chapter 1:

| Tension | Spectrum |
|---|---|
| **Group vs Individual** | Is the corporation a collective entity or just a bundle of individual rights? |
| **Management vs Ownership** | How far should directors operate independently of shareholders? |
| **Facilitation vs Intervention** | Should law merely enable incorporation, or actively regulate corporate conduct? |
| **Public vs Private** | Are corporations private enterprises or public actors? |

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Theories are rarely expressly stated in legislation or judgments — they remain **submerged beneath the text**. Identifying which theory underpins a statute or decision is a powerful analytical move in exam answers.

## 1. Concession Theory

> *The corporation is an artificial entity created by the state — corporations exist "only because governments say so."*

### Core Claim
Corporate status is a **concession (privilege) granted by the state**. The corporation is an *artificial* (not fictitious) legal construct, capable of bearing rights and duties separately from the individuals who populate it.

### Two Versions

| Version | State's Role | Regulatory Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong** | Corporate status must be **earned on a continuing basis**; it is a privilege that can be withdrawn | Government **must** regulate corporations |
| **Mild** | Corporate status is **presumed** once basic statutory prerequisites for incorporation are met | Government **may** regulate corporate activity |

### Key Cases

| Case | Principle |
|---|---|
| ***Nicholson v Permakraft (NZ) Ltd* [1985] 1 NZLR 242** | Cooke J: limited liability is a "privilege … open to abuse" — courts must be alert to irresponsible corporate structuring (strong version) |
| ***Salomon v Salomon & Co Ltd* [1897] AC 22** | Lord Halsbury: company is an "artificial creation of the legislature" but once properly incorporated has "real existence" (mild version) |
| ***EPA v Caltex Refining Co Pty Ltd* (1993) 178 CLR 477** | HCA: corporations **not entitled** to privilege against self-incrimination — corporation is an "artificial entity" and "creature of the law" (McHugh J) |
| ***Hale v Henkel* (1906) 201 US 43** | Corporation is "a creature of the State … incorporated for the public … holds [privileges] subject to laws of the State" |
| ***Northside Developments Pty Ltd v Registrar-General* (1990) 170 CLR 146** | Brennan J: company's "existence, capacities and activities are only such as the law attributes to it" |
| ***Ashbury Railway Carriage & Iron Co v Riche* (1875)** | Ultra vires doctrine — companies could only carry out activities specified in their Memorandum of Association |

### Two Key Claims

1. **Metaphysical status:** The corporation is an *artificial* entity — a legal construction, not a natural person. As Gageler J explained, referring to an "artificial" legal person implies nothing more than a unit in respect of which legal personality has been conferred — *CEPU v Queensland Rail* (2015) 256 CLR 171 [53].
2. **Public/private status:** By tying the corporation to the state, concession theory preserves the political primacy of the individual and emphasises the **wider public interest** over private interests.

### Historical Context
- Concession theory was especially prominent during the **first half of the 19th century**, when liberal political theory was taking root.
- The tension: how to accommodate corporations within an ideology centred on the state and *individual* citizens.
- The ultra vires doctrine exemplified this — transactions outside the corporation's stated objects were invalid.
	- Ultra vires has since been **abolished** in Australia, but concession reasoning still surfaces in judicial decisions.

### Criticisms

| Criticism | Response |
|---|---|
| **Irrelevant in an era of simple registration** — no longer grant-based | State's role shifted from Parliament to the executive; the state still creates corporations |
| **Corporate attributes can be replicated by contract/trust** | Even if some features can be contractually mimicked, **separate corporate status** lies beyond the capacity of ordinary contract |
| **Too authoritarian** — subjects corporations to "the whims of government" | A more nuanced approach recognises the role of self-regulation |
| **Does not address internal corporate relations** | Aggregate theory fills this gap |

### Significance
Concession theory underpins arguments for **corporate social responsibility** and the idea that corporations are **social enterprises** with obligations beyond profit-making.

## 2. Aggregate Theory

> *The corporation has no existence or identity that is separate and apart from the natural persons in the corporation.*

### Core Claim
The corporation is an **association (aggregation) of individuals** joined by mutual contractual agreement. Corporate law should reflect individual rights and private initiative.

### Key Case

| Case | Principle |
|---|---|
| ***Automatic Self-Cleansing Filter Syndicate Co Ltd v Cuninghame* [1906] 2 Ch 34** | Company constitution (Memorandum & Articles) forms a **contract between members** that regulates internal affairs |
| ***Ashbury Railway Carriage & Iron Co v Riche*** | Cairns LC: inside the area defined by the memorandum, members "may make such regulations for their own government as they think fit" — contractual view accommodated within mild concession theory |

### Two Versions

| Version | Claim |
|---|---|
| **Mild** | Corporation is a contractual association, but some external legal constraints may be justified |
| **Strong** | "The authority of the sovereign toward the corporation … is no greater and no less than its authority toward any other private agreement among contracting parties" — corporations should not be subject to specialised regulatory regimes |

### Key Features
- Emphasises the **primary status of individuals** and the **private status** of the corporation.
- Law's role → confined to **facilitating** the formation of contractual relationships.
- Jennifer Hill: "company law in Australia has thus always had a strong contractual element to it."
- The corporate constitution continues to be understood as a set of contractual relations specifying the rights and powers of members and directors.
- **Freedom of contract** is used to legitimate managerial power: directors' authority stems from the contractual agreement of the owners.

### Link to Economic Theories
Since the 1970s, aggregate theory's preoccupation with justifying managerial autonomy led to the development of **law and economics** theories — the next major strand.

## 3. Economic Theories

> *The firm is a "nexus of contracts" between individual actors.*

### Two Principal Strands

#### 3.1 Agency Theory
- Addresses problems from the **separation of ownership (investment) and control (management)** in large corporations.
- The firm = a **nexus of contracting relationships** characterised by divisible residual claims on assets and cash flows (*Jensen & Meckling*).
- "Contract" here is **not** used in the strict legal sense — it highlights the *voluntary and adaptive* nature of arrangements.
- **Denies** the existence of the organisation as a separate entity: *"The 'personhood' of a corporation is a matter of convenience rather than reality"* — Easterbrook & Fischel.
- Only relevant function = **maximise returns to individual investors**.

##### Agency Costs

| Concept | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Monitoring costs** | Costs incurred by *investors* to keep an eye on managers |
| **Bonding costs** | Costs incurred by *managers* to assure investors their interests are safe |
| **Agency costs** | The combined total of monitoring + bonding costs |

- Contracts between managers and investors aim to **minimise** these agency costs.
- But contracting is **imperfect**: investors have limited time/knowledge; collective action among dispersed shareholders is unlikely → monitoring is weak.

#### 3.2 Transaction-Cost Economics
- Examines **why** different organisational forms (firms vs markets) are used to structure production.
- Complementary to agency theory — together they explain the design of internal corporate governance mechanisms.

### Two Mechanisms for Reducing Transaction Costs

#### A. Market Forces

| Market | How It Constrains Misuse |
|---|---|
| **Market for corporate securities** | In an efficient capital market, **share price reflects all available information**, including quality of shareholder protections → market monitors the company |
| **Market for corporate control** | Underperforming companies face **takeover** → incentivises current managers to perform or risk displacement |
| **Market for company managers** | Competition for managerial talent constrains abuse — managers who fail to maximise value will not be re-elected/re-appointed |

#### B. Corporate Law Rules
- Key features (SEP, limited liability, directors' duties of care and loyalty) operate as **standard "off-the-rack" contractual terms** read into each corporate constitution.
- Easterbrook & Fischel: corporate law "fills in the blanks and oversights with the terms that people would have bargained for had they anticipated the problems and been able to contract costlessly in advance."
- Corporate law **supplements but never displaces** actual bargains.
- Some law and economics writers accept that corporate law should prescribe **some mandatory rules** to correct agency relationship problems.

### Criticisms

| Criticism | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Over-reliance on "contract"** | Reduces complex corporate relationships to binary exchanges; ignores the idea of the corporation as an *organisation* |
| **Treats corporations as purely private** | "Because corporations are seen as private creations, corporate law is insulated from politics and concerns about the public interest" — Kent Greenfield |
| **Ignores public/social impact** | Disregards the significant social externalities many corporations create (human rights, environment) |

## 4. Team Production Theory

> *The board of directors serves as a "mediating hierarchy" — not as agents of shareholders alone.*
> — Blair & Stout

### Core Claim
When individual contracts between team members are not feasible, corporate law provides a **"second best" solution**: team members hand over rights (including rights over joint output) to the corporation as a separate entity. The board of directors then manages these assets as an **independent mediating hierarchy**.

### The Problem Team Production Solves

| Problem | Description |
|---|---|
| **Shirking** | Individual team members free-ride on others' efforts, knowing they'll still receive a share of benefits |
| **Rent-seeking** | Team members spend excessive effort and group resources arguing about their share of outputs |

### How It Differs from Agency Theory

| Feature | Agency Theory | Team Production Theory |
|---|---|---|
| **Who are directors' principals?** | Shareholders (directors = agents of shareholders) | No single group — directors serve **all** team members |
| **Team members** | Shareholders + managers | Shareholders, managers, employees, creditors, possibly others |
| **Director oversight** | Shareholders monitor directors | Unclear who monitors directors (a **weakness**) |
| **Shareholder status** | Primary/privileged | One input among many |

### Key Propositions
- Directors are there to "protect the enterprise-specific investments of **all** members of the corporate 'team'" — not just shareholders.
- Explains the otherwise "puzzling" independence of the board: if directors were merely agents of shareholders, why are shareholder control rights so limited?
- Shares common ground with **stakeholder theory** (wider constituency) but uniquely argues directors are under **no group's control**.

### Criticisms

| Criticism | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Who monitors the directors?** | If shareholders are not the primary constituency, their monitoring incentive decreases — creates a **governance gap** |
| **Balancing diverse interests → rent-seeking** | Board must balance competing team member interests, which ironically incentivises the very rent-seeking behaviour the theory seeks to avoid |

## 5. Natural Entity Theory

> *Corporations are a natural product of human interaction — corporate law does not create them; it merely gives them formal recognition.*

### Two Claims

| Claim | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Corporations are natural, not state-created** | They arise from the voluntary actions and desires of their corporators; their political and legal status is **independent** of the state |
| **Separate existence from members** | The corporation is a "group-person" possessing its own will and capacity for action, distinct from individuals (Otto von Gierke) |

### Organic Theory (Variant)
- Uses the **human body** as analogy: directors = the **mind** of the company.
- Allows legal concepts premised on individual action (e.g., **mens rea**) to be applied to corporations → important for **criminal/civil liability** of corporations.

### Relationship to Other Theories

| Comparison | Detail |
|---|---|
| **vs Aggregate / Economic theory** | Treats the corporation as more than a set of individual contractual relations — the sum is **greater than its parts** |
| **vs Concession theory** | Agrees corporation has separate existence; **disagrees** that this depends on the state → corporation is *real*, not artificial |
| **Similar conclusion to aggregate theory** | Both resist heavy regulation — if corporations are natural entities, no reason to regulate more closely than individuals or partnerships |

### Implications
- If corporations are natural entities analogous to natural persons → they should be entitled to the **same rights and privileges**, and held to the **same standards and responsibilities**, as natural persons.
- This supports E. Merrick Dodd's argument (1932) that corporations have **social responsibilities** beyond profit-making.

## 6. Organisational Perspective

> *Corporations are complex decision-making environments whose internal dynamics shape individual behaviour.*

### Core Insight
Corporate law treats the corporation as a collection of individuals, but this is a **"black box"** view. Organisational theorists argue we must understand the internal workings:
- Formal and informal networks of communication, behaviour patterns, and understandings link corporate insiders.
- The larger the organisation → the more **information distortion** and **diffuse control**.
- The board may have only a **limited impact** on actual decision-making.

### Decision-Making Assessment Criteria (Bottomley)

| Criterion | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Accountability** | Officeholders must explain their use of power, including conflicts of interest |
| **Deliberation** | Decisions should consider all relevant arguments |
| **Contestability** | Realistic possibility that decisions can be challenged where they conflict with the corporation's interests |

### Ethical Implications
- Robert Jackall's study of large US corporations (1980s): "Bureaucracy transforms all moral issues into immediately practical concerns" — the etiquette of authority relationships **supersedes** ethical considerations.
- Raises the question: does the organisational setting **enhance or diminish** individual ethical responsibility?
- There will often be a **divergence** between legal design and organisational reality.

## 7. Feminist Perspectives

> *The corporation is "inherently gendered."*

### Three Categories (Lahey & Salter)

| Category | Focus | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| **Liberal feminism** | Gender equality *within* existing corporate structures; how structures affect individual behaviour | Enforce individual rights to equal treatment and equal pay (does not restructure power) |
| **Socialist feminism** | Effect of corporatism on the *larger culture*; women as workers | Recognises that corporations dominate individual action; women's lives are "doubly devalued" as workers in both corporation and home |
| **Radical feminism** | Why the corporation was developed in the first place; how it serves "liberal and capitalist patriarchy" | Challenges fundamental male values embedded in dichotomies (public/private, individual/group, self-interest/cooperation); emphasises *care, responsibility, and responsiveness* |

### Key Feminist Contributions to Corporate Law
- **Critiques shareholder primacy** — argues for a wider constituency of interests.
- The firm as a **"web of relationships"** (not just a nexus of contracts).
- **Feminist care ethics** can give substantive content to director and officer **duties**.
- Analysis of corporate power's disproportionate effect on **women**.

### Board Gender Diversity
- Evidence that gender diversity positively affects monitoring: **"women on boards ask more questions"**.
- **Norway** (since 2006): all public companies must have a minimum of both genders on boards.
- Peta Spender: "the participation of women on boards is a measure of economic citizenship and democratic leadership."
- Counter-concern: quotas may lead to **token appointments** or misperceptions.

## 8. Corporate Purpose — ESG and CSR

### The Central Question
> Should corporations be confined to profit maximisation for shareholders, **or** should they have regard to environmental, social, and governance (ESG) concerns?

### The ESG Framework

| Component | Generally Includes |
|---|---|
| **Environmental** | Sustainability, climate-change matters |
| **Social** | Ethical standards in company operations |
| **Governance** | Board accountability and transparency |

### E. Merrick Dodd (1932)
- From a **natural entity** perspective: corporations are not merely aggregations of shareholders.
- Shareholders in modern public companies are "absentee owners" separated from management.
- Directors must make decisions in the interests of the corporation as a **"good citizen"** — this may not align with shareholder interests.
- Proposed: representatives of interests other than shareholders (e.g., employees) could sit on the board.

### The Spectrum of Opinion

| Position | Key Argument |
|---|---|
| **Shareholder primacy** | Corporation's primary function = maximise returns to shareholders; altruism only if consistent with that goal |
| **Enlightened shareholder value** | Directors must act for shareholders, but should take a **long-term view** considering stakeholder interests as a means to shareholder benefit |
| **Stakeholder theory** | Corporations have direct responsibilities to a **wide range of constituencies** beyond shareholders |

### Milton Friedman's Critique
- *"The Social Responsibility of Business is to Increase its Profits"*
- Requiring directors to consider broad social responsibilities → vague, undefined standard of conduct → impedes effective decision-making.
- Cynical view: ESG/CSR = corporate *"window dressing"* that deflects from deeper concerns about misuse of corporate power.

## 8.1 Enlightened Shareholder Value

### UK Companies Act 2006, s 172(1)

> A director must act in a way that he considers, in good faith, would be most likely to **promote the success of the company for the benefit of its members as a whole**, having regard to:

| Factor | Description |
|---|---|
| **(a)** | Likely consequences of any decision **in the long term** |
| **(b)** | Interests of the company's **employees** |
| **(c)** | Need to foster relationships with **suppliers, customers and others** |
| **(d)** | Impact on **community and environment** |
| **(e)** | Maintaining a reputation for **high standards of business conduct** |
| **(f)** | Need to act **fairly between members** |

### Criticisms of s 172
- All it requires is that the director should **"think about"** interests beyond members — no substantive obligation.
- Courts have affirmed: weighing of factors is a **commercial decision** for the directors, not the court, "except in a clear case."

### Australian Position
- **No equivalent** of UK s 172 in the *Corporations Act 2001* (Cth).
- **Senate Committee (1989):** requiring directors to consider social impact = "an extension of Australian company law fraught with most important consequences."
- **CAMAC Report (2006):** "a non-exhaustive catalogue of interests … serves little useful purpose for directors."

#### Two Exceptions in Australian Law

| Provision | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **s 299(1)(f) CA** | Annual directors' report must detail the company's performance re any **environmental regulation** applying to its operations |
| **Treasury Laws Amendment (Financial Market Infrastructure and Other Measures) Act 2024** | Companies with financial reporting requirements must publish an annual **"sustainability report"** on climate-related matters |

#### ASX Corporate Governance Principles
- **Principle 3:** listed entities must act lawfully, ethically, and responsibly.
- Listed companies must include a **corporate governance statement** in their annual report (if-not-why-not approach).
- Expectations to preserve reputation with key stakeholders: customers, employees, suppliers, creditors, regulators.

## 8.2 Stakeholder Theory

> *Corporations are comprised of important constituencies beyond shareholders — employees, creditors, customers, communities — whose interests are unlikely to be protected by private contracts alone.*

### Core Propositions
- Non-shareholder stakeholders are **unlikely or unable** to protect their interests via private contracts.
- Therefore, a system of **public corporate regulation** is justified because corporate conduct has significant **public ramifications**.
- Avoids the binary: corporations are **both private and public simultaneously**.

### Range of Versions

| Version | Key Idea |
|---|---|
| **Enlightened shareholder value** | Shareholder primacy, but with a long-term lens considering stakeholder interests |
| **Communitarian theory** | "All those involved in the corporation are potentially members of one community" — divergent interests but also shared goals and bonds |

### Challenge to Corporate Law
Stakeholder theory challenges:
- **Directors' duties** — should they be owed to more than just shareholders?
- **Shareholder rights** — what rights should flow from share ownership?
- **Corporate governance mechanisms** — should non-shareholders be on the board? Should general meetings be open to non-shareholders?

> [!note] Key Quote
> "Corporations really are both private and public simultaneously. They are not states … At the same time, corporations are not purely private individuals. They are institutions that sometimes act as quasi-governments and … take actions that affect every aspect of people's lives, including people who have no formal contractual relationship with them." — Kent Greenfield

## 9. Global Developments

### Three Key International Initiatives

| Initiative | Year | Nature | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises** | 1976 (revised) | Voluntary, non-binding | MNEs should "[c]ontribute to economic, environmental and social progress" and "respect … internationally recognised human rights" |
| **UN Global Compact** | 2000 | Non-binding | 10 principles on human rights, labour, environment, anti-corruption — drawn from UDHR, ILO Declaration, Rio Declaration, UNCAC |
| **UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights** | 2011 | Non-binding | States should set clear expectations; businesses must "avoid causing or contributing to adverse human rights impacts" — a "global standard of expected conduct" |

### Modern Slavery
- **Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Cth):** some corporations must report annually on modern slavery risks in operations and supply chains.
- 2023 review found: reporting is "not being taken seriously enough" — seen as a "tick-box exercise."

### Criticisms
- UN Global Compact: does not specify **how** to implement standards → companies may enhance reputations merely by signing.
- UN Guiding Principles: the corporate "responsibility" does not equate to a **legal obligation** → "poses a challenge for corporate governance theory."

## 10. Summary — Theories Compared

| Theory | What is the corporation? | Public or Private? | Role of Law | Internal Governance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Concession** | Artificial entity, state-created | Public (tied to the state) | Must/may regulate | Little to say |
| **Aggregate** | Contractual association of individuals | Private | Facilitate only | Contractual relations between members/directors |
| **Economic (law & economics)** | "Nexus of contracts" | Private | Supplement private bargains; default rules | Focus on agency costs; shareholder primacy |
| **Team Production** | Privately constructed "team" | Private (but broader constituency) | Recognise corporate entity as owner of team assets | Board = independent mediating hierarchy |
| **Natural Entity** | Real, natural product of human initiative | Private | Facilitate, not regulate | Group-person with its own will |
| **Organisational** | Complex decision-making environment | Less concerned with this | Less concerned with this | Focus on internal dynamics, information flows, ethics |
| **Feminist** | Inherently gendered institution | Externally critiques both private and public aspects | Challenges existing structures | Highlights hierarchy, power, gendered impacts |
| **Stakeholder / ESG** | Both private and public | Both | Substantive regulation needed | Directors owe broader duties beyond shareholders |

> [!important] The Four Perennial Tensions — Theory Mapping
> Each theory takes a different position on the tensions from Chapter 1:
> - **Group vs Individual:** Concession, Natural Entity, and Stakeholder theories emphasise the *group*; Aggregate and Economic theories emphasise the *individual*.
> - **Management vs Ownership:** Economic theory focuses on this separation; Team Production theory justifies board independence.
> - **Facilitation vs Intervention:** Aggregate, Economic, and Natural Entity theories favour *facilitation*; Concession and Stakeholder theories favour *intervention*.
> - **Public vs Private:** Concession and Stakeholder theories treat corporations as *public*; Aggregate, Economic, and Natural Entity theories treat them as *private*.

## Exam Preparation Prompts

> [!tip] Oral Assessment — Key Discussion Questions
> 1. What is the difference between the "strong" and "mild" versions of concession theory? Why does it matter?
> 2. How does aggregate theory justify managerial power within the corporation?
> 3. What are "agency costs" and how do market forces reduce them?
> 4. Why does team production theory argue that directors are *not* merely agents of shareholders?
> 5. How does natural entity theory arrive at a similar policy conclusion (less regulation) to aggregate theory, but via a different route?
> 6. What is the practical difference between "stakeholder theory" and "enlightened shareholder value"?
> 7. Should Australia adopt a provision equivalent to UK s 172? What are the arguments for and against?
> 8. How do feminist perspectives challenge the "nexus of contracts" model?
