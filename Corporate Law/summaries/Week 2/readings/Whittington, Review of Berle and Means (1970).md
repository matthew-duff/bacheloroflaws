# Summary: Review of 'The Modern Corporation and Private Property' (Revised Edition)

**Author:** G. Whittington (The Economic Journal, March 1970)

## Core Subject
This is a book review of the 1968 Revised Edition of A. A. Berle and G. C. Means' classic 1932 book, *The Modern Corporation and Private Property*. The reviewer evaluates the enduring legacy of the original work as well as the new additions provided by the authors.

## Key Points
- **Enduring Importance and Inter-disciplinary Success:**
    - Whittington notes that the book's central thesis—the divorce between corporate ownership and control—is now a foundational concept in the theory of the firm, heavily influencing later economists.
    - The work's strength lies in its successful blend of law and economics: the law explains *how* ownership is separated from control, while economics reveals the *scale* of this phenomenon (e.g., the dominance of the 200 largest firms) and the deficiencies in traditional economic theories that assumed profitability was the only control mechanism.
- **Berle's New Contributions (1968 Preface):**
    - Berle highlights the changing nature of property relationships, specifically the increasing role of the state as a supplier of "technical property" (technology) and as a constraint on corporations.
    - He discusses the massive growth of "passive property"—institutional shareholdings like pension funds. Berle optimistically suggests that this allows society to redistribute wealth without interrupting the actual control and production of the corporations.
- **Means' New Contributions (1968 Statistical Appendix):**
    - Means provides updated statistical data showing that since 1929, economic concentration within the U.S. non-financial corporate sector has *increased*, and share ownership has become even *more dispersed*.
    - Despite past criticisms of his statistical methodology concerning specific years, the new data (comparing 1962 with 1929) confirms that the overall trend in corporate concentration is definitively upwards.
- **Conclusion:**
    - Whittington concludes that the new prefaces are "stimulating and provocative" and that the book remains a classic work that still "repays re-reading."
