# Tutorial 1: Building on Content from Lecture 1 (Companies and Company Law)

## Question

Sergio and Elisa are siblings with complementary but different personalities. Sergio has people skills and a flair for management but is risk averse. Elisa, who studies engineering, is introverted, hands-on and a risk taker. Through incredible hard work and abstemiousness, Sergio has saved enough cash to put down a deposit on a unit in the outer suburbs of Brisbane. His idea is to buy the flat, live in it, and then sell it later for a profit. Sergio is prepared to ‘flip’ the property by himself or to cut in Elisa. If the latter, they’ve discussed that she could use her ‘handy woman’ skills to help fix up the unit and contribute to the repayments. 

Meantime, Elisa (an engineering student) has completed a promising project in one of her courses. Together with two classmates, she managed to refine a technology that prevents mid-air collisions between drones. Elisa’s lecturer says that there is considerable potential to commercialise the refined technology, including for use in pilot-less planes. He has offered to put Elisa and her friends in touch with a venture capitalist. 

Advise Sergio and Elisa on which business structure they should use to pursue each of these two ideas. Include in your advice a discussion of the relative advantages and disadvantages of that structure in the circumstances, and some tips on establishing that structure so that it is best suited to their situation.

### **Advice to Sergio**

For Sergio's unit-flipping idea, the best starting point is **not** a company, but either:

1. a **sole trader / personal ownership structure** if he proceeds alone; or
2. a **small, closely held partnership or co-ownership arrangement** if Elisa joins him.

The main reason is that this is a **small-scale, closely held venture** centred on one property, with no obvious need for outside investors, perpetual succession, or transferable ownership. Week 1 shows that the company form is most attractive where the parties need **separate legal personality (SEP)**, **limited liability**, **continuity**, and the ability to divide ownership through shares. Those advantages exist here, but they are less important than in Elisa's second venture.

If Sergio proceeds **alone**, personal ownership is the simplest option. It avoids registration costs, ongoing compliance, and governance formalities. He keeps direct control over the property and decision-making. The downside is that there is **no SEP** and **no limited liability**: Sergio would be personally exposed to debts and liabilities. By contrast, if he incorporated, the company would own the property as a separate legal person under **s 119**, and Sergio would not own the asset personally merely because he owned the shares. That follows the **Salomon** principle. But for a one-person property venture, that separation may be more inconvenient than helpful.

If Elisa joins, a **partnership-style arrangement** is probably still more suitable than an immediate company. Week 1 notes that a partnership is an unincorporated association of persons carrying on business in common with a view to profit. That seems close to what Sergio and Elisa are considering: pooled contributions, shared work, and a shared profit on resale. The advantage is simplicity and flexibility. The disadvantage is significant: **each participant is personally liable**, and there is no corporate veil.

If they are worried about personal exposure, they could instead use a **proprietary company limited by shares**. That would give them:

- **SEP**: the company, not Sergio or Elisa, would contract, borrow, and hold the property.
- **Limited liability**: members are generally only liable up to any unpaid amount on their shares.
- **Continuity**: the venture could continue even if one wants to exit.
- **Clearer allocation of power and profit**: ownership can be divided through shares rather than informal understandings.

But in these circumstances a company also has real disadvantages:

- **Compliance costs and formality** outweigh the benefits for a very small venture.
- Lenders often require **personal guarantees**, which can erode the practical value of limited liability.
- In a close family-style venture, the real risk is often not the Berle and Means separation of ownership and control, but **shareholder disagreement**. Week 2 notes that, in Australia, the more common governance problem for small companies is disputes between participants in closely held businesses.

So the better advice is:

- **Sergio alone:** buy and manage the property personally.
- **Sergio with Elisa:** use a carefully documented **co-ownership or partnership arrangement** unless they expect the activity to expand into an ongoing business with multiple projects.

If they do decide to incorporate, they should form a **small proprietary company limited by shares** rather than a public company. Under Week 1's framework, that suits a small private venture because it requires only **one member**, at least **one Australian-resident director**, cannot raise funds from the public, and is designed for closely held businesses.

Tips if Sergio and Elisa work together:

- Put the arrangement in writing from the start.
- Record who contributes what: deposit, repayments, renovation labour, and sale proceeds.
- Decide whether ownership and profit-sharing are equal or reflect different contributions.
- Decide who manages day-to-day decisions and whether both must approve a sale, refinance, or major renovation.
- If they use a company, adopt a **constitution or shareholder agreement** dealing with share transfers, deadlock, exit rights, and director decision-making.

Overall, Sergio's venture is the kind of small, personal, low-scale enterprise for which a **company is possible but not necessary**. The simpler unincorporated form is likely preferable unless liability concerns become acute or the project grows into a repeat commercial operation.

### **Advice to Elisa**

For Elisa's drone-collision technology venture, the strongest recommendation is a **proprietary company limited by shares**.

This venture is very different from Sergio's property idea. It involves:

- multiple founders;
- potentially valuable **intellectual property**;
- higher commercial risk;
- possible outside investment from a **venture capitalist**; and
- a realistic prospect of growth beyond the personal involvement of the original founders.

Those features make the company form much more suitable.

The main advantages are:

- **Separate legal personality**: under **s 119**, the company becomes a legal entity distinct from Elisa and the other founders. It can own the intellectual property, enter licensing and investment agreements, sue and be sued, and continue independently of changes in personnel.
- **Limited liability**: this is especially important in a technology commercialisation venture, where product, regulatory, contractual, and financing risks may be significant.
- **Share-based ownership**: the founders can divide control and economic interests through shares, which is essential if a venture capitalist is to invest.
- **Perpetual succession and transferability**: investors usually want an entity that survives changes in membership and can issue or transfer equity.
- **Governance structure**: the company form creates a framework for allocating decision-making power between shareholders and directors.

Week 2 makes this recommendation even stronger. Once investors come in, the venture is likely to face the classic **corporate governance** issue: separation of ownership and control. The company form is built for that problem. Corporate law supplies the governance architecture, and the founders can supplement it with a constitution and private agreements.

A partnership would be much less suitable. It lacks SEP, exposes all partners to personal liability, and is not a good vehicle for outside equity investment. A sole trader structure is impossible because Elisa is already working with two classmates. A public company is also inappropriate at this stage because it carries much heavier governance and disclosure obligations and is designed for public fundraising. If the venture scales substantially, the company could later convert from proprietary to public status.

The most important practical tips for Elisa are:

- **Incorporate early**, before serious negotiations with investors or customers.
- Ensure the **IP is assigned to the company**, not left with Elisa personally, her classmates, or the university by default.
- Issue shares in a way that reflects contributions and expected future roles.
- Decide from the beginning who will be **directors**, who will manage the business, and which decisions require shareholder approval.
- Use a **constitution and/or shareholders agreement** to regulate transfer of shares, pre-emption rights, founder exits, deadlock, appointment of directors, and future capital raising.
- Be careful with **pre-registration contracts**. If deals are made before incorporation, the promoters may be personally liable unless the company is registered and ratifies the contract under **s 131**.

There is also a governance point from Week 2. If the founders simply rely on friendship and informal trust, they risk agency and control problems later. A venture capitalist will usually want formal protections precisely because corporate governance exists to reduce those risks. So even at a start-up stage, Elisa should think not only about incorporation, but about **who controls the company, to whom they are accountable, and how disputes will be resolved**.

Overall:

- **Sergio's property idea**: personal ownership or a simple partnership/co-ownership model is probably best, with a company only if liability protection becomes especially important.
- **Elisa's technology venture**: a **proprietary company limited by shares** is the most suitable structure from the outset.
