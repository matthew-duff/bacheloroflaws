# Case Summary: Tesco Supermarkets Ltd v Nattrass [1972] AC 153

![Case image](/assets/pixabay/case_summary_tesco_supermarkets_ltd_v_nattrass_1972_ac_153_pixabay_1850082.jpg)

*Image credit: [Pixabay](https://pixabay.com/photos/hangers-clothing-shopping-market-1850082/) by [Pexels](https://pixabay.com/users/Pexels-2286921/). Query: `store`*


## 1. Case Overview
*   **Court:** House of Lords
*   **Key Issue:** Whether a company can be held liable for the acts of a store manager under the *Trade Descriptions Act 1968* (UK), and the scope of the "directing mind and will" doctrine.

## 2. Facts
*   Tesco advertised a "flash pack" of Radiant washing powder at a discounted price (2s. 11d.) via posters.
*   A shop assistant (Miss Rogers) put out packs marked at the normal price (3s. 11d.) because the discounted stock was empty.
*   The store manager (Mr. Clement) failed to notice this error and marked his daily return as "all special offers O.K."
*   A customer was charged the higher price and complained. Tesco was prosecuted under s 11(2) of the *Trade Descriptions Act 1968*.
*   **Defense:** Tesco relied on s 24(1), arguing the offence was due to the "act or default of another person" (the manager) and that the company had taken "all reasonable precautions and exercised all due diligence."

## 3. Ratio Decidendi
*   **Identification Doctrine:** A corporation is only liable for the acts of those who are its "directing mind and will." 
*   **Status of Managers:** A store manager of one branch in a large chain is **not** the "directing mind and will" of the corporation. He is "another person" for the purposes of the statutory defense.
*   **Due Diligence:** The company (the board of directors/senior management) had exercised due diligence by setting up an efficient system of control and supervision. They were not vicariously liable for the manager's failure to implement that system.

## 4. The "Directing Mind and Will" Doctrine
*   **Lord Reid's Formulation:** A corporation acts through living persons. Some represent the company as its "hands," while others act **as** the company.
*   **Who qualifies?** Only those who are in actual control of the operations of the company, having its "directing mind and will" (typically the board of directors, the managing director, or perhaps other very superior officers who carry out functions of management and speak and act as the company).
*   **Delegation vs. Chain of Command:** There is a distinction between delegating a function (which might make the delegate the company) and merely setting up a chain of command where the board retains control. In this case, the manager was part of the chain of command, not a delegate of the board's functions.

## 5. Legal Significance
*   **Primary Liability:** Established the "Identification Doctrine" in English Law, clarifying when a company is primarily (not vicariously) liable for criminal acts.
*   **Narrow Scope:** It significantly narrowed the range of employees whose actions can be attributed to the company, making it harder to prosecute large corporations for the failings of mid-level or low-level management.
*   **Contrast with Vicarious Liability:** Reaffirmed that in the absence of specific statutory wording, a company is generally not vicariously liable for the criminal acts of its servants in "mens rea" or "due diligence" style offences.

## 6. Exam Tips: Quick Revision
*   **Key Quote:** "He is an embodiment of the company... his mind which directs his acts is the mind of the company." (Lord Reid)
*   **Distinction:** Store Manager $\neq$ Directing Mind. Board of Directors $=$ Directing Mind.
*   **Application:** Use this case when arguing whether a company is liable for a regulatory breach caused by a local employee despite having a corporate compliance policy.
