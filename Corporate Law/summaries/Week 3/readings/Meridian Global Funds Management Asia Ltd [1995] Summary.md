# Exam Summary: Meridian Global Funds Management Asia Ltd v Securities Commission [1995] 3 All ER 918

## Case Overview
*   **Court:** Privy Council (on appeal from NZ)
*   **Key Judge:** Lord Hoffmann
*   **Core Issue:** When should the knowledge or acts of an individual employee be attributed to a company, especially regarding statutory disclosure requirements?

---

## 1. The "Rules of Attribution" (Lord Hoffmann's Framework)
Lord Hoffmann moved away from a singular "metaphysical" search for a company's "directing mind and will." Instead, he proposed three levels of attribution:

1.  **Primary Rules of Attribution:** Found in the company's constitution (Articles of Association) or implied by company law (e.g., decisions of the Board or unanimous shareholder agreements).
2.  **General Rules of Attribution:** Principles of **Agency** and **Vicarious Liability** (e.g., a company is liable for a driver's negligence in the course of employment).
3.  **Special Rules of Attribution:** Required when a specific legal rule (usually statutory) cannot be satisfied by primary or general rules. This is a matter of **statutory interpretation/construction**.
    *   **The Question:** "Whose act (or knowledge, or state of mind) was for this purpose intended to count as the act etc of the company?"
    *   **The Answer:** Determined by the language, content, and policy of the specific rule.

---

## 2. Ratio Decidendi
The knowledge of an employee is attributed to the company if that employee is the person who, with the authority of the company, had the relevant knowledge or performed the relevant act, and the **policy of the statute** would be defeated if that knowledge were not attributed.

*   **Application to Facts:** 
    *   The investment manager (Koo) had authority to "do the deal."
    *   The policy of the *Securities Amendment Act 1988* (NZ) was to ensure immediate transparency in share ownership.
    *   If the company only "knew" when the Board knew, the Act's purpose would be frustrated by management's ignorance or lack of supervision.
    *   Therefore, Koo's knowledge was attributed to Meridian, even though he acted for a corrupt purpose and hid the deal from the Board.

---

## 3. Legal Significance & Exam Points
*   **Decline of the "Directing Mind and Will" Doctrine:** Hoffmann clarified that the "directing mind and will" (from *Lennard’s Carrying Co*) is not a universal test but a specific attribution rule for a specific statute. It is often unnecessary and can be misleading.
*   **Context-Specific Attribution:** Attribution is not "all or nothing." A person might be the "company" for one statutory purpose (e.g., reporting shares) but not for another (e.g., manslaughter).
*   **Policy-Driven Interpretation:** In exams, always ask: *What is the purpose of this statute? Would that purpose be defeated if we didn't attribute this person's knowledge to the company?*
*   **Contrast with *Tesco v Nattrass*:** 
    *   In *Tesco*, the statute provided a "due diligence" defense, implying that the negligence of a low-level manager should *not* be attributed to the company if the Board had a system in place.
    *   In *Meridian*, the statute's focus on market transparency required attribution to the person actually authorized to trade.

## 4. Quick Revision Checklist
- [ ] **Primary Rules:** Board/Shareholders.
- [ ] **General Rules:** Agency/Vicarious Liability.
- [ ] **Special Rules:** Interpreting the specific statute's policy.
- [ ] **Key Quote:** "There is in fact no such thing as the company as such... only the applicable rules [of attribution]."
- [ ] **Outcome:** Meridian was liable for failing to disclose because the manager who did the trade knew about it.
