# Exam Summary: Jones v Lipman [1962] 1 WLR 832

## 1. Case Overview
*   **Court:** Chancery Division, UK
*   **Judge:** Russell J
*   **Key Concept:** Piercing the Corporate Veil — The "Sham" or "Facade" Exception.

## 2. Material Facts
*   **The Contract:** Lipman (D1) entered into a binding contract to sell freehold land to Jones (P) for £5,250.
*   **The Evasion:** To avoid specific performance of the contract, Lipman acquired a shelf company, **Alamed Ltd (D2)**, with a nominal capital of £100.
*   **The Transfer:** Lipman transferred the land to Alamed Ltd for £3,000 (significantly undervalued). Alamed Ltd was entirely controlled by Lipman (he and a clerk were the sole directors/shareholders).
*   **The Defence:** Lipman argued he could no longer complete the sale because he no longer owned the land, and the plaintiffs were only entitled to damages for breach of contract.

## 3. Ratio Decidendi
The court will "pierce the corporate veil" where a company is used as a **"mere cloak or sham"** to evade an existing legal obligation or to defeat the rights of a third party.

*   **The "Creature" Principle:** The company was described as the "creature of the first defendant, a device and a sham, a mask which he holds before his face in an attempt to avoid recognition by the eye of equity."
*   **Control & Ability:** Because Lipman had absolute control over the company, he was in a position to cause the contract to be completed.
*   **Equitable Remedy:** Equity will not allow the corporate form to be used to practice a fraud or evade a contractual obligation.

## 4. Legal Significance (Exam Focus)
*   **Piercing the Veil:** This is a classic "exception" to the rule in *Salomon v Salomon*. It establishes that the separate legal personality of a company cannot be used as a shield for inequitable conduct.
*   **Specific Performance:** The court granted specific performance against **both** Lipman and the company, ordering the company to transfer the land to the plaintiffs.
*   **Comparison with *Gilford Motor Co Ltd v Horne [1933]*:** *Jones v Lipman* extends the *Gilford* principle (which dealt with restrictive covenants/injunctions) to the realm of real property and specific performance.
*   **Modern Context (*Prest v Petrodel [2013]*):** Lord Sumption later categorised *Jones v Lipman* under the **"Evasion Principle"**—where a person is under an existing legal obligation which they deliberately evade by interposing a company under their control.

## 5. Quick Revision Catchphrases
*   "A device and a sham."
*   "A mask... to avoid recognition by the eye of equity."
*   "The evasion principle" (interposing a company to avoid an existing legal duty).
