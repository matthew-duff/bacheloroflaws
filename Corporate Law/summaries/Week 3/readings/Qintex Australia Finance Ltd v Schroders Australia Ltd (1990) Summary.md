# Exam Summary: Qintex Australia Finance Ltd v Schroders Australia Ltd (1990) 3 ACSR 267

## 1. Case Overview
*   **Court:** Supreme Court of NSW (Commercial Division)
*   **Judge:** Rogers CJ
*   **Key Issue:** Identifying which specific subsidiary in a corporate group was the contracting party when the group treasury operated as a single entity ("Qintex") without distinguishing between individual companies.

## 2. Facts
*   The **Qintex Group** operated a centralized treasury. Dealers for both Qintex and the defendant (**Schroders**) treated the client simply as "Qintex".
*   A forward exchange contract was entered into on 1 August 1989, resulting in a loss of ~$1.37m.
*   Schroders attempted to set off this loss against funds held in the account of **Qintex Australia Finance Ltd (QAFL)**.
*   QAFL argued it was *not* the contracting party; the candidates were **Qintex Television Ltd (QTL)** or **Qintex Australia Ltd (QAL)**.
*   **Evidence of Confusion:**
    *   Schroders' records used a single code (`QTLBR1`) for all Qintex transactions.
    *   Qintex staff told Schroders: "It does not matter to us. Choose whichever company you feel the most comfortable with."
    *   The proceeds of the initial "spot" transaction were paid into QAFL's account (an objective fact pointing to QAFL), but the documentation (deal slips/faxes) often referred to QTL or "Qintex Group".

## 3. Ratio Decidendi (The Legal Rule)
The identity of a contracting party within a corporate group must be determined by an **objective assessment** of the facts and surrounding circumstances known to both parties at the time of the contract.

*   **Application:** Despite the "commercial reality" that the group acted as one, the court must still identify a *single* legal entity as the party to the contract.
*   **Result:** Rogers CJ found that, although the result was "wholly artificial," the documentation and objective facts (specifically Schroders' own records and confirmations) pointed to **QTL**, not QAFL. Therefore, Schroders could not set off the debt against QAFL's funds.

## 4. Legal Significance & "Separate Legal Personality"
### A. Rigid Adherence to *Salomon*
*   The case reaffirms the principle in **Industrial Equity Ltd v Blackburn (1977)**: the law maintains a rigid demarcation between subsidiaries and holding companies, even if they operate as a single economic unit.
*   The "separate legal personality" of each subsidiary must be respected, even if the parties themselves ignored it during the "rush and bustle of commercial life."

### B. Tension Between Law and Commercial Reality
*   Rogers CJ highlighted a "tension" between the law and modern business practice.
*   In practice, conglomerates often ignore internal boundaries. In law, those boundaries are critical, especially in **insolvency**, where identifying the correct debtor determines which creditors get paid.

### C. Call for Law Reform
*   Rogers CJ famously suggested that Parliament should consider reform to allow the assets and liabilities of parent and subsidiary companies to be **aggregated** in certain insolvency situations (often called "pooling").
*   He noted that the current "rigid" approach leads to unproductive legal costs and "windfalls for some, and an unfair loss to others."

## 5. Exam Tips: How to use this case
*   **Use for:** Problems involving **corporate groups** where it is unclear which company signed a contract.
*   **Key Quote:** "There is today a tension between the realities of commercial life and the applicable law... the law [requires] a rigid demarcation between wholly owned subsidiaries."
*   **Contrast with:** Cases where the "corporate veil" is pierced (e.g., *Gilford Motor Co Ltd v Horne*). *Qintex* is **not** about piercing the veil; it is about the difficulty of identifying the correct legal person within a group.
*   **Policy Argument:** Use *Qintex* to argue that the *Salomon* principle can be commercially "artificial" and may require legislative reform to allow for "pooling" in group insolvencies.
