# Case Summary: DHN Food Distributors Ltd v Tower Hamlets LBC [1976] 3 All ER 462

## Quick Overview
- **Court:** Court of Appeal (UK)
- **Key Judges:** Lord Denning MR, Goff LJ, Shaw LJ
- **Core Concept:** Single Economic Entity / Piercing the Corporate Veil
- **Status:** Historically significant but **heavily qualified** (see *Woolfson* and *Adams v Cape*)

---

## 1. Facts
- **Corporate Structure:** DHN Food Distributors (Parent) operated a grocery business. It had two wholly-owned subsidiaries:
    1. **Bronze Investments Ltd:** Owned the land (premises).
    2. **DHN Food Transport Ltd:** Owned the vehicles.
- **Operation:** All three companies had the same directors and shareholders. The business was run as a single integrated unit.
- **The Event:** Tower Hamlets LBC compulsorily acquired the land owned by Bronze.
- **The Dispute:** DHN (Parent) claimed compensation for **business disturbance**. The Council refused, arguing that DHN had no legal interest in the land (it only had a license from Bronze), and Bronze (the owner) suffered no disturbance as it didn't run the business.

## 2. Issue
Could the court "pierce the corporate veil" to treat the parent and subsidiaries as a **single economic entity**, allowing the parent to claim compensation for a loss technically suffered by its subsidiary's asset?

## 3. Ratio Decidendi
The Court of Appeal held that the group should be treated as a **single economic entity** for the purpose of compensation.

- **Lord Denning MR:** "These three companies should, for present purposes, be treated as one... They are as tied together as can be." He compared them to a partnership where the companies are partners.
- **Goff LJ:** Focused on the fact that DHN had complete control over Bronze. Bronze was a "shell" with no business of its own; its only asset was the land used by DHN.
- **Shaw LJ:** Emphasized the "community of interest" and that the separation was merely a matter of "technical form."

## 4. Legal Significance (Exam Focus)
### The "Single Economic Entity" Principle
- **Definition:** The idea that a group of companies may be treated as a single legal unit where they are functionally integrated and under common control.
- **Departure from *Salomon*:** This case represents a rare, liberal departure from the strict separate legal entity principle established in *Salomon v Salomon & Co Ltd*.

### Current Standing & Limitations
- **Criticism (*Woolfson v Strathclyde Regional Council* [1978]):** The House of Lords doubted *DHN*, stating the veil should only be pierced where the corporate structure is a **"mere façade"** concealing the true facts.
- **Refinement (*Adams v Cape Industries plc* [1990]):** Confirmed that there is **no general principle** of a "single economic entity." The court will not ignore the separate legal personality of group members just because it seems "fair" or "just" to do so.
- **Modern Rule (*Prest v Petrodel Resources Ltd* [2013]):** Piercing is a last resort, limited to the "evasion principle" (where a person is under an existing legal obligation which they deliberately evade by interposing a company).

## 5. Summary for Revision
| Feature | Detail |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Main Rule** | Companies in a group *can* be treated as a single economic entity (Denning's view). |
| **Application** | Compulsory land acquisition / compensation context. |
| **Why it matters** | It's the high-water mark for the "single economic entity" argument. |
| **Counter-point** | *Adams v Cape* effectively killed the broad application of this principle. |
