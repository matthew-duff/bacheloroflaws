# Case Summary: Smith, Stone & Knight Ltd v Birmingham Corporation [1939] 4 All ER 116

**Court:** King’s Bench Division  
**Judge:** Atkinson J  
**Key Concept:** Piercing the Corporate Veil — Agency Exception

---

## 1. Facts
*   **Parent Company (SSK):** Owned a factory and business premises.
*   **Subsidiary (Birmingham Waste Co Ltd):** SSK formed this subsidiary to carry out a specific part of its business (waste paper).
*   **Structure:** SSK held 497/502 shares (the other 5 held in trust for SSK). All directors of the subsidiary were directors of SSK. SSK provided all capital, kept all books, and took all profits. The subsidiary had no staff of its own and paid no rent (it was a book entry).
*   **Issue:** The Birmingham Corporation compulsorily acquired the premises. SSK claimed compensation for "disturbance" to the business. The Corporation argued the business belonged to the subsidiary (a separate legal entity), and since the subsidiary was only a yearly tenant, it wasn't entitled to the same compensation.

## 2. The "Agency" Test (The 6 Questions)
Atkinson J identified six requirements to determine if a subsidiary is carrying on business as an **agent** for the parent (allowing the veil to be pierced):

1.  **Profits:** Were the profits of the subsidiary treated as the profits of the parent?
2.  **Appointment:** Were the persons conducting the business appointed by the parent?
3.  **Head & Brain:** Was the parent company the "head and brain" of the trading venture?
4.  **Control/Governance:** Did the parent company govern the adventure, decide what should be done, and what capital should be embarked on the venture?
5.  **Skill & Direction:** Did the parent company make the profits by its skill and direction?
6.  **Effectual Control:** Was the parent company in effectual and constant control?

**Application in this case:** All six questions were answered in the **affirmative**. The subsidiary was a "simulacrum" or "tool" of the parent.

## 3. Ratio Decidendi
The corporate veil can be pierced where a subsidiary company does not carry on business on its own behalf but acts as an **agent** for the parent company. In such cases, the occupation and business loss of the subsidiary are legally the occupation and loss of the parent.

## 4. Legal Significance (Exam Focus)
*   **Exception to *Salomon*:** This is a leading case for the "agency" exception to the doctrine of separate legal entity.
*   **High Threshold:** While SSK won, modern courts (e.g., *Adams v Cape Industries*) have made it much harder to argue agency just because of 100% ownership. You need to show the subsidiary has **no independent existence** (no staff, no separate books, no independent management).
*   **Distinction:** Contrast with *Salomon v Salomon & Co Ltd*—here, the court looked past the form to the substance of the control relationship to prevent an "unjust" result in compensation law.

---
**Quick Revision Tip:** If a problem scenario shows a subsidiary with *no employees*, *no separate bank account*, and *directors who never meet*, apply the **SSK 6-point test** to argue the parent is the real principal.
