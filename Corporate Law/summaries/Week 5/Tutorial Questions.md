## Question 1

The directors of International Ltd and Local Ltd agreed to commence a new business to be owned by a new company, Alpha Pty Ltd (‘Alpha’), whose share capital would be divided into two classes as follows: 

Ordinary shares – $1 issue price:
* 500,000 – International Ltd
* 250,000 – Local Ltd 

Preference shares – $2 issue price
* 100,000 – Betty (director and 50% shareholder of International Ltd)
* 100,000 – Charles (director and 50% shareholder of International Ltd)
* 100,000 – Rachel (director and 50% shareholder of Local Ltd) 

It was agreed that Betty, Charles, and Rachel would be the first directors of Alpha, with Rachel also appointed as secretary. International Ltd and Local Ltd also agreed that the internal governance rules of Alpha should provide that: 

a) A person nominated by Local must be the chair of Alpha and that the chair should have both a vote as well as a casting vote at directors’ meetings; 
b) A person nominated by International Ltd must be the managing director of Alpha and the person so appointed cannot be removed from that position unless International Ltd specifically agreed; 
c) Alpha’s ordinary shareholders would have six votes per share and preference shareholders, one vote per share at shareholders’ meetings; and 
d) An Alpha shareholder can only transfer their shares to existing shareholders. The transfer price must be the market price as determined by an independent valuer. 

Advise whether Alpha should rely on the replaceable rules in the above circumstances or whether a constitution would be more appropriate. 

## Question 2

Traction Shoe Pty Ltd (‘Traction’) operated a bulldozer manufacturing and export business from premises at Hole Street. The directors of Traction were Tilly and William. The 2,500 issued shares in Traction were originally all owned by Construction Pty Ltd (‘Construction’), a company also owned and controlled by Tilly and William. The day-to-day operation and management of Traction were undertaken by Tilly and Traction’s operations manager, Nigel. 

Tilly asked her close personal friend, Bruce, if he was interested in investing $1,000,000 in return for 40% of the shares in Traction. Tilly told Bruce that Traction needed the money for the expansion of its business. There was detailed discussion as to the expansion envisaged. Bruce instructed Tilly to look for new premises for the expansion, especially as Bruce had heard rumours of the sale of a rival business, Digger Pty Ltd. Digger owned premised at Caterpillar Court. 

Bruce received a written sale contract on Construction letterhead recording that Construction had received $500,000 from Bruce, on behalf of Buffet Enterprises Ltd, for the purchase of 20% (500 shares) in Traction. This meant Construction retained 80% (2,000 shares). Bruce said he would consider a further $500,000 investment if Construction was willing to sell more shares, depending on the progress of expansion plans. 

a) If Bruce acquired a stake of 20% of the shares in Traction in his own name, outline the extent to which he could influence the day-to-day management of the company. 
b) Would your answer to a) be any different if Bruce acquired an 80% stake? 
c) As a further inducement for Bruce to invest in the company, Tilly said that if Bruce acquired 20% of the shares, Bruce could appoint one director to the board of directors. In what circumstances, and on what basis, would such an arrangement be enforceable by Bruce?
